Austro-Hungarian Empire

Vienna, Prague, Budapest — the Habsburg Jewish world

The Austro-Hungarian Empire hosted one of the most culturally vibrant Jewish communities in modern history. Vienna was home to Herzl, Freud, Mahler, Wittgenstein, and Stefan Zweig; Prague's Maharal created the legendary Golem and its Old-New Synagogue has stood since the 13th century; Budapest built the world's largest synagogue. The Chatam Sofer in Bratislava founded the Orthodox resistance to modernity. Galicia, encompassing Kraków, Lviv, and hundreds of shtetlach, was the heartland of Hasidism and the great yeshiva world. The empire's collapse in 1918 left its Jews exposed to the nationalisms that would culminate in the Holocaust.

Historical Arc

The Austro-Hungarian Empire at its territorial height in the late nineteenth century encompassed an extraordinary diversity of Jewish communities: the sophisticated and largely acculturated Jews of Vienna and Budapest, the Yiddish-speaking masses of Galicia (southern Poland, western Ukraine), the mountain farmers of Carpathian Ruthenia, the merchants of Bohemia and Moravia, the yeshiva communities of Pressburg and Sighet, and the Zionist pioneers of czernowitz and Lemberg. This political entity — unwieldy and multinational by the time of its collapse after World War I — incubated the major movements that defined modern Jewish history: the Haskalah, the Reform and Orthodox movements, Hasidism, political Zionism, secular Jewish nationalism (the Bund), and Jewish integration into European professional and cultural life.

Vienna in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries was, after New York, perhaps the most culturally productive and creatively explosive Jewish city in history: Freud, Herzl, Mahler, Schnitzler, Kraus, Hofmannsthal, Zweig, and the entire Vienna School of psychoanalysis all flourished in the same cultural moment. Budapest produced similarly disproportionate contributions: Theodor Herzl was born here; the father of modern Hungary's economy was the Jewish financier Baron Moritz von Kuffner; Budapest was the birthplace of Zionism's first great leader, Max Nordau. Eastern regions of the empire, particularly Galicia and Bukovina, were the heartlands of traditional Yiddish-speaking Ashkenazic Jewry, the territory that produced the great Hasidic dynasties (Belz, Bobov, Klausenberg, Sanz) and the great Lithuanian-style yeshivot.

The Holocaust virtually annihilated this world. The Jews of Vienna (some 200,000 in 1938) were deported to the East in 1941–1943, with only a few thousand surviving in hiding or through mixed marriages. Hungarian Jewry — the largest community in Axis-aligned Europe, numbering perhaps 800,000 — experienced the fastest and most complete destruction of any Jewish community in history: in eight weeks in the spring of 1944, the Eichmann machinery and Hungarian authorities deported over 400,000 Jews to Auschwitz. Galician Jewry was largely destroyed earlier in the war. By 1945, the Austro-Hungarian Jewish world existed only in memory, in memoirs and literary works, and in the postwar rebuilt communities of Vienna and Budapest and the emigrant communities of Israel and America.

Key Facts & Milestones

Vienna's Jewish community peaked at 200,000 (10% of the population) before the Nazi annexation in 1938

Prague's Old-New Synagogue, built c. 1270, is the oldest active synagogue in Europe

Galicia, the eastern Habsburg province, was the heartland of both Hasidism and the Lithuanian yeshiva tradition

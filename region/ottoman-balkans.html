Ottoman Empire & Balkans

The Ottoman refuge and the Balkan mosaic

When Spain expelled its Jews in 1492, Sultan Bayezid II of the Ottoman Empire welcomed them with open arms, reportedly saying the Spanish king had 'impoverished his own country and enriched mine.' The Ottoman Empire became the great refuge of Sephardic Jewry — Salonika became a majority-Jewish city where Ladino was the language of the marketplace, Istanbul's Jewish community thrived under the millet system, and communities from Sarajevo to Rhodes developed rich multicultural identities. The Romaniote Jews of Greece, however, predate even the Sephardic arrival, representing the oldest continuous Jewish community in Europe.

Historical Arc

When Spain expelled its Jews in 1492, Sultan Bayezid II of the Ottoman Empire reportedly commented that Ferdinand and Isabella had impoverished their kingdom while enriching his. His offer of refuge to the Sephardic exiles — and the Ottoman Empire's subsequent tolerance, which extended to Jews throughout its vast domains — made the Ottoman world the most important Jewish center of the early modern era. For several centuries following the expulsion, more Jews lived under Ottoman rule than under any other sovereign power on earth. From Constantinople (Istanbul) to Cairo, from Salonika to Aleppo, from Rhodes to Baghdad, the Ottoman millet system protected Jewish religious and communal autonomy while integrating Jews into imperial commerce, medicine, and diplomacy.

The Ottoman Empire's Jewish geography encompassed extraordinary diversity: Sephardic exiles who brought Iberian culture, language (Ladino), and printing technology; Romaniote Jews — Greek-speaking communities whose presence in the Balkans predated Christianity and whose distinct tradition had developed over more than fifteen centuries; Ashkenazic immigrants from Hungary and the German lands; Mizrahi communities of Syria, Egypt, and Mesopotamia; and the ancient Jewish communities of Anatolia. This diversity made Ottoman Jewry a mosaic of rites, languages, and traditions, held together by Ottoman imperial governance and the internal authority of the chief rabbi (Hakham Bashi) recognized by the state.

The twentieth century brought catastrophe. The collapse of the Ottoman Empire after World War I, the rise of Balkan nationalism, Nazi occupation of Greece and the Balkans in World War II, and the founding of the State of Israel transformed this world beyond recognition. The Jews of Salonika — who had made that city a majority-Jewish metropolis and the "Jerusalem of the Balkans" — were almost entirely murdered in Auschwitz in 1943. Turkish Jews emigrated to Israel in large numbers after 1948. By the end of the twentieth century, communities that had numbered in the hundreds of thousands had been reduced to thousands or tens of thousands.

Key Facts & Milestones

Salonika (Thessaloniki) was a majority-Jewish city for much of the 16th–19th centuries, with Ladino as its commercial language

The Ottoman Empire absorbed an estimated 150,000 Jewish refugees after the 1492 Spanish expulsion

Sarajevo's Haggadah (c. 1350) is one of the most beautiful illuminated Hebrew manuscripts ever produced

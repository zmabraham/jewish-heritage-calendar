Holy Land & Ancient Israel

Where Jewish history began — and endures

The cradle of Jewish civilization, from the ancient kingdoms of Israel and Judah through the glories of the First and Second Temple periods, the Roman exile, the Byzantine and Crusader eras, and the extraordinary modern return to Zion. For three thousand years, the cities, towns, and villages of the Land of Israel have anchored Jewish prayer, yearning, and identity. Even in centuries of exile, communities from Safed to Tiberias to Hebron maintained an unbroken Jewish presence in the ancestral homeland.

Historical Arc

The Land of Israel — stretching from the headwaters of the Jordan at Dan in the north to the wells of Be'er Sheva in the south, and encompassing both banks of the Jordan River — is the geographic and spiritual heart of Jewish civilization. Bounded by the Mediterranean Sea to the west, the Negev desert to the south, and the Transjordanian plateau to the east, this compact strip of territory has generated more religious, literary, and historical significance per square mile than perhaps any other region on earth. Its landscape — the Galilee's forested hills, the Jezreel Valley's breadbasket, the Judean highlands, the Jordan Rift, and the Negev — shaped the rhythms of biblical life, and the names of its cities echo through three millennia of sacred text.

Jewish presence in this land is uninterrupted from roughly 1800 BCE to the present day, even through the most catastrophic of destructions. When the Assyrians shattered the northern kingdom in 722 BCE and the Babylonians razed Jerusalem in 586 BCE, communities survived. When Rome crushed the great revolts of 66–73 CE and 132–135 CE and forbade Jews from entering Jerusalem, the Galilee became the new center of Jewish life, nurturing the Mishnah, the Jerusalem Talmud, and the great rabbinic academies. Through Byzantine persecution, Arab conquest, Crusader violence, and Ottoman rule, Jews remained — in Jerusalem, Safed, Tiberias, Hebron, and scattered villages — maintaining an unbroken thread that the Zionist movement would weave into a modern state.

The Land of Israel is not merely a geographic fact but a theological category in Jewish thought. The concept of Eretz Yisrael — the Land of Israel — carries covenantal weight in Torah, halakhic significance in rabbinic law, and existential urgency in modern Jewish politics. It is the place where prophecy flourished, where the Temple stood twice, where the Sanhedrin convened, where the Kabbalah crystallized in Safed, and where, in the twentieth century, millions of survivors and pioneers rebuilt a Jewish commonwealth.

The Americas

The New World — refuge, reinvention, and the largest Jewish communities on earth

The Americas became the great refuge and reinvention of the Jewish people. The first Jews arrived in Recife, Brazil in 1630; when the Dutch lost Brazil to Portugal in 1654, twenty-three refugees sailed north to New Amsterdam — planting the seed of the New York Jewish community that would eventually number two million. Newport, Rhode Island's Touro Synagogue (1763) received George Washington's famous letter promising liberty of conscience. A century later, the Lower East Side of Manhattan became the most densely Jewish neighborhood in history, as two million Eastern Europeans fled the pogroms. Today, North and South America together host more Jews than any other region of the world.

Historical Arc

The Jewish experience in the Americas — from the earliest settlement of Recife by Portuguese Marranos in the 1630s, through the establishment of the first permanent Jewish community in North America in New Amsterdam in 1654, to the complex multicultural landscape of contemporary American Jewry — represents both the newest and in some ways the most successful chapter in diaspora Jewish history. The United States, home to the largest Jewish diaspora community in the world (numbering roughly 7 million, second only to Israel), achieved the Jewish emancipation that Europe only haltingly and incompletely approached: the First Amendment's free exercise clause and its ban on religious establishment made America the only nation in Jewish history whose foundational legal principles explicitly protected religious equality.

The American Jewish story is fundamentally an immigrant story: the Sephardic merchants who arrived in the colonial period; the German Jews who came in the mid-nineteenth century and established the Reform movement and the institutions of communal life; the massive wave of Eastern European Jews (1881–1924) fleeing pogroms and poverty who created the Yiddish-speaking urban culture of the Lower East Side, the Garment District, and the sweatshop economy; and the refugee migrations of the 1930s and 1940s (European Jews fleeing Nazism) and the postwar period (Holocaust survivors and refugees from Soviet communism, the Middle East, and elsewhere). Each wave added new layers to the complex palimpsest of American Jewish culture.

Latin America and the Caribbean Jewish communities, though smaller than their North American counterparts, reflect the diversity of the Sephardic diaspora: the Marrano refugees who settled in Dutch Brazil, Curacao, and Suriname; the German and Eastern European Jews who arrived in Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries; the Holocaust survivors who found haven in South America; and the complex history of Jewish communities under authoritarian regimes. Argentina's Jewish population (roughly 300,000 in 1945) was the largest in the Spanish-speaking world; the 1994 AMIA bombing remains the worst antisemitic attack in the diaspora since World War II.

Key Facts & Milestones

New York City's Jewish population peaked at approximately 2 million in the 1950s — the largest Jewish community in all of history

The Touro Synagogue in Newport, RI (1763) is the oldest surviving synagogue building in the United States

Argentina's Baron Hirsch agricultural colonies, established 1891, resettled 35,000 Russian Jewish refugees on the pampas

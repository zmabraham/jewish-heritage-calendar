Eastern Europe — Poland & Lithuania

The great heartland of Ashkenazic Jewry

Poland and Lithuania formed the core of the Ashkenazic world for five centuries. The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, at its height, sheltered the largest Jewish population on earth — a world of yeshivot, Hasidic courts, Bundist politics, Hebrew newspapers, Yiddish theater, and vibrant urban kehillot. Vilna was the 'Jerusalem of Lithuania,' home to the Vilna Gaon and a center of rabbinic scholarship and secular Jewish culture alike. Warsaw's Jewish community of 370,000 was the second largest in the world. The Holocaust destroyed this world in three years, but its legacy — in language, law, literature, and religious life — shapes Jewish civilization to this day.

Key Facts & Milestones

Poland hosted 3.3 million Jews on the eve of WWII — the largest Jewish population in Europe

The Volozhin Yeshiva, founded 1803, became the model for all modern Lithuanian-style yeshivot worldwide

Yiddish, the daily language of Ashkenazic Jews, reached its highest literary and cultural development in Poland

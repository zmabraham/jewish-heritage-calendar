# Russia, Ukraine & Eastern Pale of Settlement

## Overview

The Pale of Settlement — the western borderlands of the Russian Empire where Jews were legally required to reside from 1791 to 1917 — encompassed the densest concentration of Jews in the modern world. At its height, the Pale contained approximately five million Jews, living in hundreds of shtetlakh (small towns) and major cities stretching from the Baltic coast through Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Moldova to the Black Sea. Within this vast territory, two radically different but equally world-historical Jewish movements were born in the eighteenth century: Hasidism in the south (Ukraine), transforming Jewish spirituality, and the Enlightenment-influenced secular movements of the north, producing Zionism, socialism, and the vast Jewish immigration to America. Both movements, in their different ways, were responses to the same conditions: poverty, legal restriction, periodic violence, and the question of whether Jewish life in Russia had a future.

The Pale was defined by paradox. In it lived some of the poorest Jews in the world and some of the most learned; some of the most pious and the most radically secular; communities of extraordinary internal richness — with their own theaters, newspapers, schools, political parties, and religious institutions — existing within a framework of legal discrimination and intermittent murderous violence (pogroms). The pogroms of 1881–1884, 1903–1906, and 1918–1921 each sent waves of emigrants fleeing westward to America or, for the ideologically committed, southward to Palestine.

Ukraine holds a particular place in this story. The birthplace of Hasidism (the Ba'al Shem Tov lived in Medzhibozh in Podolia), the site of some of the worst anti-Jewish massacres in history (the Chmielnicki massacres of 1648, the Petliura pogroms of 1918–1921, and the Nazi Einsatzgruppen massacres of 1941–1942), and the homeland of some of the most culturally productive Jewish communities, Ukraine was simultaneously the source of some of the most vibrant Jewish creativity and the site of its most horrific destruction.

## Historical Arc

### Jewish Settlement and the Pale of Settlement (1300–1800)

Jewish settlement in Ukrainian and Belarusian territories expanded dramatically in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries as part of the overall eastward expansion of Polish-Lithuanian Jewry, described in the Poland-Lithuania section. These eastern territories — Podolia, Volhynia, Lithuania proper, and eventually Belarusian lands — were part of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth until the partitions of 1772–1795. The Chmielnicki massacres of 1648–1649 struck most severely in the Ukrainian territories, destroying dozens of communities. When the Russian Empire absorbed the eastern Polish territories, Catherine the Great in 1791 established the Pale of Settlement, limiting Jewish residence to these former Polish territories (and the newly acquired Crimea and Novorossiya), effectively confining Russia's Jewish population to the imperial periphery.

### The World of the Shtetl (1800–1881)

The shtetl — the small Jewish market town of Eastern Europe — became the defining setting of Ashkenazic Jewish life. In the shtetl's social ecology, Jews occupied the commercial and artisanal roles: merchants, innkeepers, blacksmiths, tailors, wheelwrights, and above all traders and middlemen between the Polish or Ukrainian agricultural peasantry and the urban market economy. The shtetl was organized around the synagogue, the market square, the study house (beit midrash), and the rabbi's court — if it was a Hasidic town — or the rabbi's academy if it was a Lithuanian one. The internal culture was rich: Yiddish was the universal vernacular; Hebrew was the language of prayer and Torah; religious practice organized daily, weekly, and annual time; the entire community participated in the rhythms of the Jewish calendar. This was the world that Sholem Aleichem's Tevye the Dairyman inhabited, and that Marc Chagall painted from memory in Paris decades after leaving Vitebsk.

### Pogroms and Political Awakening (1881–1914)

The assassination of Tsar Alexander II in 1881 — falsely attributed to Jewish conspirators — triggered the first wave of modern pogroms across the Pale. The violence of 1881–1884 killed hundreds, destroyed thousands of homes, and sent the first mass wave of Jewish emigration to America. The government's response, the "May Laws" of 1882 restricting Jewish residence even within the Pale, intensified the sense that there was no future for Jews in Russia. The Kishinev pogrom of 1903, in which 49 Jews were killed in an organized massacre with tacit government support, shocked the Jewish world; Chaim Nachman Bialik's poem "In the City of Slaughter" became the definitive literary response to pogrom violence. The 1905 revolution and its aftermath brought a new wave of pogroms, killing thousands. These conditions produced the great Jewish political awakening: Zionism (Hovevei Zion, founded 1884; the Zionist Organization, 1897) drew one constituency; the Bund (General Jewish Labour Bund, founded Vilna 1897), committed to Jewish autonomy within a transformed Russia, drew another; and mass emigration to America drew a third.

### The Civil War Pogroms (1918–1921)

The collapse of the Russian Empire in 1917 brought liberation from the Tsarist anti-Jewish laws but opened a period of catastrophic violence. As Red Army, White Army, Ukrainian nationalist (Petliura) armies, and various bandit forces fought across Ukrainian territory, Jewish communities were caught between all sides. Estimates of Jews killed in the Ukrainian civil war pogroms of 1918–1921 range from 50,000 to 200,000 — the worst mass killing of Jews in history since the seventeenth century and a direct predecessor in form and location of the Holocaust that followed.

### Soviet Jewry (1917–1989)

The Bolshevik Revolution of 1917 formally abolished the Pale and all anti-Jewish legislation, and in the early Soviet period many Jews rose to prominent positions in the party, the Red Army, and cultural institutions (Trotsky, Zinoviev, Kamenev, Sverdlov). The Soviet state, while hostile to religion, supported Yiddish secular culture: Yiddish schools, newspapers, theater, and the Yevsektsiya (Jewish section of the Communist Party) promoted a secular Jewish identity. Birobidzhan, a territory in the Far East, was designated a Jewish Autonomous Region in 1934. However, Stalin's purges from 1936 onwards disproportionately targeted Jewish Communists; the Doctors' Plot (1952–1953) seemed the prelude to mass deportation, averted only by Stalin's death. Soviet Jews gradually lost Yiddish language, religious practice, and communal institutions, becoming highly educated but culturally assimilated. The Six-Day War of 1967 and the campaign of Soviet Jews demanding the right to emigrate ("Let My People Go") eventually produced a massive emigration: approximately 1.2 million Soviet Jews emigrated to Israel between 1990 and 2001.

### The Holocaust in the East (1941–1944)

The Nazi invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941 brought the most efficient mass murder in history. The Einsatzgruppen — mobile killing units following the German armies — murdered Jews community by community. Babi Yar, the ravine outside Kiev, holds the bodies of 33,771 Jews murdered in two days in September 1941. By the end of 1942, most of Ukrainian, Belarusian, and Russian Jewry had been murdered. Communities that had existed for centuries were liquidated in days.

## Major Communities

**Odessa** — The "Pearl of the South," founded in 1794, Odessa was the most cosmopolitan and Europeanized city in the Pale. Its Jewish community, which numbered over 100,000 by 1900, was uniquely secular, producing Zionist leaders (Ahad Ha'am, Jabotinsky, Bialik), gangsters, and a distinctive hybrid culture. The Odessa pogroms of 1871, 1881, and 1905 tempered this cosmopolitan milieu with violence.

**Kiev** — The capital of Ukraine had a Jewish community formally excluded from the city until 1861; the subsequent community was prosperous and acculturated. The Beilis Affair (1911–1913), in which a Jewish worker in Kiev was falsely accused of ritual murder in the last major blood libel trial in Europe, brought international attention.

**Vitebsk** — Marc Chagall's hometown and a vibrant center of Yiddish culture in Belarus. Under Soviet power, Vitebsk briefly hosted a remarkable school of modern art.

**Medzhibozh** — The small Podolian town where the Ba'al Shem Tov lived and died, which remains a pilgrimage destination for Hasidic Jews.

**Uman** — Home to the grave of Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (1772–1810), which draws tens of thousands of Hasidic pilgrims on Rosh Hashanah annually, making it one of the largest Jewish pilgrimages in the world.

**Lubavich** — The small Belarusian town for which the Chabad Hasidic movement is named (Lubavitch). From 1813 to 1915, the Chabad rebbes held court here, producing the system of Hasidic thought and outreach that is today the world's most geographically extensive Jewish movement.

**St. Petersburg / Petrograd / Leningrad** — Formally outside the Pale, St. Petersburg was nonetheless home to a Jewish community of wealthy merchants and professionals given special dispensation to reside in the capital, who played major roles in Russian cultural and intellectual life.

**Vilna / Minsk / Pinsk / Grodno** — The major cities of the Lithuanian and Belarusian portions of the Pale, each hosting major Jewish communities with distinct political and cultural profiles.

## Notable Figures

**Ba'al Shem Tov (c. 1698–1760)** — See Poland-Lithuania section; the Besht lived and worked in Podolia (modern Ukraine) and is buried in Medzhibozh.

**Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (1772–1810)** — Great-grandson of the Besht, Nachman's profound mystical teachings and his remarkable fairy tales (transmitted by his disciple Reb Noson) represent the most psychologically and philosophically sophisticated expression of Hasidic thought; his grave in Uman is a major pilgrimage site.

**Chaim Nachman Bialik (1873–1934)** — The national poet of Hebrew literature, born in Volhynia, whose poems — from the tender lyrics of childhood memory to the wrathful "In the City of Slaughter" written after Kishinev — shaped Hebrew poetry and Zionist consciousness.

**Leon Trotsky (1879–1940)** — Born Lev Bronstein in Yanovka, Ukraine, the revolutionary leader whose role in the Bolshevik seizure of power and organization of the Red Army made him one of the most consequential political figures of the twentieth century.

**Vladimir Jabotinsky (1880–1940)** — Born in Odessa, the founder of Revisionist Zionism and the Irgun militia, whose vision of Jewish self-defense and his demand for a Jewish state on both banks of the Jordan made him the ideological ancestor of the Israeli right.

**Marc Chagall (1887–1985)** — Born in Vitebsk, Chagall's paintings of the shtetl — floating fiddlers, village rooftops, Sabbath candles — created the defining visual image of Eastern European Jewish life and remain among the most beloved works in twentieth-century art.

**Sholom Aleichem (1859–1916)** — See Poland-Lithuania section; though Ukrainian-born, his work traverses the entire Eastern European Jewish world.

## Cultural Legacy

The Russian and Ukrainian Jewish experience produced the three most consequential movements of modern Jewish history: mass emigration to America (creating the American Jewish community), Zionism (creating the State of Israel), and the Soviet Jewish experience (which contributed over a million citizens to Israel and communities to the United States, Germany, and elsewhere). Klezmer music, Yiddish literature, the language of millions — these were the cultural products of the Pale. The Holocaust ended this civilization in its original setting, but the Soviet aliyah of the 1990s brought Russian Jewish culture back to Israel in a massive wave, permanently transforming Israeli society and culture.

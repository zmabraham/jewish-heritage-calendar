# Eastern Europe — Poland & Lithuania

## Overview

The vast expanse of Poland-Lithuania — the Commonwealth that united the Polish crown and the Grand Duchy of Lithuania from 1569 to 1795 — constituted for several centuries the world's largest and most important center of Jewish civilization. By 1600, more Jews lived in Poland-Lithuania than in all the rest of the world combined; by 1900, despite the partitions of Poland and the eastern territories' incorporation into the Russian and German empires, this region still housed the demographic and spiritual heartland of Ashkenazic Jewry. It was the land that produced the Council of Four Lands, the most autonomous Jewish self-governing body in diaspora history; the Vilna Gaon, perhaps the greatest Talmudic mind of the modern era; the Hasidic movement that transformed Jewish spirituality; and the Lithuanian yeshiva network that preserved classical Torah scholarship across the upheavals of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

The cities of this region are roll-call of Jewish civilization: Vilna — "the Jerusalem of Lithuania" — with its academies, printing presses, and street-level Torah culture; Warsaw, capital of Polish Jewry and the largest Jewish urban community in Europe by 1900; Cracow (Krakow) with the Rema synagogue and the memory of Rabbi Moses Isserles; Lublin, the "Jewish Oxford" of the sixteenth century where the Council of Four Lands convened; Volozhin, whose yeshiva founded in 1803 established the model for the modern Lithuanian academy; Mir, whose yeshiva survived the Holocaust by fleeing to Shanghai; and Brest-Litovsk, ancient community and site of the fateful 1918 Treaty.

The destruction of Polish-Lithuanian Jewry in the Holocaust was the worst concentrated devastation in the history of any people. Of approximately 3.3 million Jews in Poland in 1939, approximately 90 percent were murdered by the Nazis and their collaborators. The shtetlakh — the small market towns that defined Eastern European Jewish life — were emptied. The great yeshivot ceased to exist in their original locations. The Yiddish language, mother tongue of the overwhelming majority of these victims, lost most of its native speakers in a few years. What had been the heartland of the Jewish world became what Claude Lanzmann called "a graveyard without gravestones."

## Historical Arc

### Polish Golden Age (1300–1648)

Jewish settlement in Poland is documented from at least the eleventh century, and Poland's attraction for Jewish immigrants increased dramatically following the expulsions from Germany (thirteenth to fourteenth centuries), France (1306, 1394), and other Western European countries. King Casimir III "the Great" (1310–1370) issued charters protecting Jewish rights and became so closely associated with Jewish welfare that folk memory attributed his philo-Semitism to a love affair with a Jewish woman named Esther. The union of Poland and Lithuania in 1569 created the largest Jewish territory in the world. Jewish settlement spread throughout the Commonwealth, with Jews serving as merchants, innkeepers, leaseholders, and commercial intermediaries in the economy of the Polish nobility. The Council of Four Lands (Vaad Arba Aratzot), established in the mid-sixteenth century, met twice yearly at the great fairs of Lublin and Jaroslav, collected taxes, adjudicated disputes, and governed Jewish communal life with remarkable autonomy — the most self-governing body in diaspora history. The sixteenth and seventeenth centuries saw the flourishing of Polish-Lithuanian Talmudic scholarship: Shulchan Aruch author Joseph Karo's work was adapted for Ashkenazic practice by Rabbi Moses Isserles (the "Rema," 1520–1572) of Cracow, whose glosses (Mappa) made the code equally authoritative for Ashkenazim. The academies of Lublin, Poznan, and Cracow trained thousands of students.

### Cossack Massacres and Recovery (1648–1750)

The Chmielnicki Uprising of 1648–1649, in which Ukrainian Cossack armies under Bohdan Chmielnicki massacred the Polish nobility, peasants, and Jews who served them, brought catastrophic violence to the Jewish communities of Ukraine and eastern Poland. Contemporary sources record the destruction of hundreds of communities and the killing of tens of thousands of Jews — some estimates range as high as 100,000 dead. The "Gezeyres Takh ve-Tat" (the decrees of 5408–5409 in the Hebrew calendar) became the benchmark catastrophe by which Ashkenazic Jews measured suffering until the Holocaust. Recovery was gradual but real: new communities were rebuilt, and the eastern Jewish world slowly reconstituted itself.

### Hasidism (1740–1820)

In the second quarter of the eighteenth century, a religious and social revolution began in the Polish-Ukrainian borderlands. Rabbi Israel ben Eliezer, known as the Ba'al Shem Tov ("Master of the Good Name," c. 1698–1760), preached a Judaism of ecstatic prayer, divine joy, and spiritual accessibility that appealed to ordinary Jews who felt excluded from the scholar-dominated elite of traditional Jewish practice. His disciples spread the Hasidic movement rapidly: Rebbe Dov Ber (the "Great Maggid" of Mezeritch), Rabbi Levi Yitzhak of Berditchev, and dozens of other charismatic teachers established their own courts across Poland, Ukraine, and Belarus. The movement spread northward into Lithuania and was met there by fierce opposition from the Mitnagdim (opponents), led by the Vilna Gaon (Rabbi Elijah of Vilna, 1720–1797), who issued bans of excommunication against the Hasidim. The dispute between Hasidim and Mitnagdim, which divided Ashkenazic Jewry for generations, paradoxically produced two great revitalizations of Jewish piety and learning.

### Partition, Haskalah, and Nationalism (1795–1914)

The partitions of Poland in 1772, 1793, and 1795 divided the Jewish heartland among Russia, Prussia, and Austria. The Jews of Galicia came under Austrian rule; those of Poland's central territories under Prussian then Russian rule; the majority under Russian rule in the newly created Pale of Settlement. Lithuanian Jews (in the Russian Empire from 1795) developed the great yeshiva culture of the nineteenth century: the Volozhin yeshiva, founded by Rabbi Hayyim of Volozhin (1749–1821), the Vilna Gaon's leading disciple, established the model for the modern Lithuanian yeshiva with full-time intensive Talmud study divorced from direct community service. Mir, Slabodka, Telz, Slobodka, and other yeshivot followed. The Warsaw community grew explosively through the nineteenth century to become the largest in Europe, producing a vibrant Hebrew and Yiddish press, theater, literature, and political movements including the Bund (Jewish socialist workers' movement) and the various Zionist factions.

### Interwar Period and Holocaust (1918–1945)

The reconstitution of independent Poland in 1918 brought approximately 3 million Jews into a nationally conscious but economically fragile and often antisemitic state. The interwar period (1918–1939) saw an extraordinary flourishing of Yiddish cultural life: the YIVO Institute for Jewish Research (founded in Vilna, 1925), Yiddish theater and film, Hebrew schools (the Tarbut network), and the full spectrum of Jewish political parties and institutions. The German invasion of September 1939 and the Soviet occupation of eastern Poland (including Vilna) began the destruction. The Nazi occupation systematically murdered Polish Jewry: Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Auschwitz — the death camps were all on Polish soil. The Warsaw Ghetto Uprising (April–May 1943), the most significant Jewish armed revolt, ended with the ghetto's destruction. By 1945, approximately 300,000 of Poland's 3.3 million Jews had survived.

## Major Communities

**Vilna (Vilnius)** — "The Jerusalem of Lithuania," Vilna was the intellectual capital of Ashkenazic Judaism, home to the Vilna Gaon, dozens of yeshivot, and a dense network of Jewish cultural institutions. The city's Jewish population of nearly 60,000 (1939) was almost entirely murdered between 1941 and 1943.

**Warsaw** — The largest Jewish community in Europe by the late nineteenth century, Warsaw's 350,000 Jews (1939) inhabited a world of extraordinary cultural vitality — Yiddish theater, Hebrew schools, political parties, publishing houses — before their murder in the Warsaw Ghetto and Treblinka.

**Cracow (Krakow)** — The seat of the Rema (Moses Isserles), the "Cracow school" of Talmudic scholarship, and the Wawel district's Jewish quarter. The Rema synagogue and cemetery remain major Jewish heritage sites.

**Lublin** — The seat of the great Lublin Yeshiva (founded 1515 by Rabbi Jacob Pollak and developed by Rabbi Shalom Shakhna), one of the earliest and most prestigious academies of Jewish learning in Eastern Europe, and the meeting place of the Council of Four Lands.

**Volozhin** — A small Lithuanian town whose yeshiva (1803–1892) became the model for the modern Lithuanian yeshiva and the grandfather of dozens of successor institutions worldwide.

**Mir** — The Mir Yeshiva, founded in 1816, gained renown as one of the elite Lithuanian academies; its entire student body (approximately 400 students) escaped to Shanghai in 1941 via Curacao visas, surviving the Holocaust intact and eventually reconstituting the yeshiva in Jerusalem and Brooklyn.

**Brest-Litovsk (Brisk)** — Home to Rabbi Joseph Babad, author of *Minchat Chinuch*, and Rabbi Chaim Soloveitchik, whose analytic method ("Brisker method") revolutionized Talmudic study in the twentieth century.

**Lodz** — The industrial city whose Jewish community of 230,000 (1939) established the Lodz Ghetto, the second largest after Warsaw, which was liquidated in August 1944.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Moses Isserles (Rema, 1520–1572)** — The Cracow legal authority whose glosses to the Shulchan Aruch made that code authoritative for Ashkenazic Jews. His grave in Cracow is a major pilgrimage site.

**Vilna Gaon (Rabbi Elijah of Vilna, 1720–1797)** — Perhaps the greatest Talmudic mind of the modern era, the Gaon authored hundreds of works on Talmud, Kabbalah, and Jewish thought, rejected the Hasidic movement, and inspired the Lithuanian yeshiva tradition.

**Rabbi Israel Ba'al Shem Tov (Besht, c. 1698–1760)** — The founder of Hasidism, whose teachings of divine immanence, joyful prayer, and spiritual accessibility transformed the landscape of Jewish religious life.

**I.L. Peretz (1852–1915)** — The Warsaw writer who modernized Yiddish literature, turning it from a folk medium into a serious literary art form, and whose Hasidic tales represent some of the finest short fiction in any language.

**Sholem Aleichem (1859–1916)** — The "Jewish Mark Twain," born in Ukraine and associated with the entire Eastern European Yiddish world, whose character Tevye the Dairyman inspired *Fiddler on the Roof*.

**Mordecai Anielewicz (1919–1943)** — The commander of the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising, who at age 24 led the first major uprising against the Nazis in occupied Europe, dying in the Mila 18 bunker.

## Cultural Legacy

Eastern European Ashkenazic Jewry was the cultural matrix from which most contemporary diaspora Jews descend. Yiddish — the Judeo-German vernacular of the Ashkenazic masses — was the mother tongue of perhaps eleven million people in 1939 and produced a rich literature, theater, film, journalism, and music. The Lithuanian yeshiva tradition, transplanted to Israel and the United States, remains the dominant form of Orthodox Jewish learning worldwide. Hasidism, born in the Polish-Ukrainian borderlands, transformed Jewish spirituality globally and gave the world klezmer music, the niggun (wordless melody), and a theology of immanent holiness. The Holocaust destroyed this world physically, but its cultural legacy — preserved in YIVO archives, in the works of Peretz, Sholem Aleichem, and Singer, in the surviving yeshivot, and in the diaspora communities built by survivors — continues to define contemporary Jewish life.

# British Isles, India & Asia

## Overview

The Jewish communities of the British Isles, the Indian subcontinent, and Asia represent three distinct histories bound together partly by the expansion of British commercial and imperial power and partly by the Sephardic mercantile networks that preceded the British Empire. In England, Judaism was formally absent for 366 years (1290–1656) before Oliver Cromwell's tacit readmission of Jews created the foundation for what would become one of the world's most significant Diaspora communities. In India, Jewish communities of extraordinary antiquity — the Bene Israel of the Malabar Coast and Bombay, and the Cochini Jews of Kerala — maintained their identity for centuries or millennia in isolation from the mainstream Jewish world. And across East Asia — in China's ancient Jewish community of Kaifeng, in the Baghdadi Jewish merchants of Shanghai and Bombay, and in the refugees who found haven in Shanghai during the Holocaust — Jewish presence was scattered, fragile, and often remarkable.

England's Jewish story is in many ways the story of modernity itself: the 1656 readmission coincided with the beginning of commercial capitalism; the Sephardic merchant community that first established London Jewish life was followed by Ashkenazic immigrants whose descendants would produce Benjamin Disraeli, Isaiah Berlin, Jonathan Sacks, and generations of British Jewish civic life. The Manchester textile industry and the Gateshead yeshiva, the Chief Rabbinate and the Bevis Marks Synagogue, the Board of Deputies and the Zionist Federation — all represent different facets of a community that has made remarkable contributions to British and world culture while maintaining its Jewish identity.

India's Jewish communities offer the most extreme test case of isolated Jewish continuity: the Bene Israel of Maharashtra province, who may have arrived in India over two thousand years ago, maintained Sabbath rest, dietary laws, and prayer in a form recognizable to the Jewish world when Portuguese Jews "rediscovered" them in the sixteenth century. The Cochini Jews of Kerala, similarly ancient, produced liturgical manuscripts, synagogue art, and a community life adapted to the Kerala environment while remaining distinctly Jewish.

## Historical Arc

### Ancient India: Bene Israel and Cochini Jews (c. 500 BCE–1500 CE)

The origins of India's two ancient Jewish communities remain debated. The Bene Israel ("Sons of Israel") of Maharashtra province — concentrated in villages of the Konkan Coast south of Bombay and later in Bombay itself — preserved a tradition of descent from shipwrecked Jews who arrived millennia ago; the most common scholarly estimate places their arrival between 1,000 and 2,500 years ago. Before contact with other Jewish communities, they observed the Sabbath, kept certain dietary laws, circumcised their sons, celebrated Passover (known to them as "Elijah's holiday"), and used certain Hebrew prayers, but had lost many other practices. They were integrated into Hindu caste society as the "Shanwar Telis" (Saturday oil-pressers), indicating their refusal to work on the Sabbath, and were respected within the local social order. Portuguese Jewish contacts in the sixteenth century and the arrival of Cochini and Baghdadi Jews in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries gradually reintegrated them into broader Jewish practice.

The Cochini Jews (or Malabar Jews) of Kerala trace their settlement to ancient times — some traditions claim origin at the time of Solomon's trade with Ophir, and a copper plate grant from the local Hindu king, dated 1000 CE, documents Jewish rights and privileges in Cranganore (Kodungallur). The Cochini community split into "Black Jews" (of mixed Indian ancestry) and "White Jews" (claiming pure Jewish descent from later arrivals), a distinction maintained with considerable social tension into the twentieth century. The "Paradesi" (foreign) synagogue in Cochin, built in 1568 near the Maharajah's palace, is the oldest surviving synagogue in India and a major heritage site.

### Medieval England: Settlement, Persecution, and Expulsion (1066–1290)

Jews were introduced to England following the Norman Conquest of 1066, when William the Conqueror brought Jewish merchants from Rouen. Medieval English Jewry was concentrated in major towns — London, York, Lincoln, Bristol, Canterbury — and primarily engaged in moneylending, an occupation largely forbidden to Christians and economically essential for royal finance and aristocratic borrowing. The community existed under royal protection, theoretically "owned" by the king as property of the crown. This precarious status was accompanied by periodic violence: the massacre of York's Jews in Clifford's Tower in 1190, during the Third Crusade's fever, killed approximately 150 people. Blood libel accusations (Norwich 1144, the prototype for all subsequent such accusations; Lincoln 1255, Hugh of Lincoln) resulted in executions. Edward I's Edict of Expulsion in 1290, following a currency manipulation trial and general anti-Jewish pressure, expelled all Jews from England — the first national expulsion in European history.

### Readmission and Sephardic Foundation (1656–1800)

During the Commonwealth period, Oliver Cromwell's de facto readmission of Jews (1656) was driven by commercial and theological motives: the belief that Jews' participation in English commerce would benefit the economy, and millenarian Protestant belief that Jews must be gathered in England before the Final Redemption. Menasseh ben Israel, the Amsterdam rabbi and publisher, lobbied Cromwell and published *The Hope of Israel* to support readmission. The first community established in the readmission era was Sephardic — Dutch Marranos who had returned to Judaism in Amsterdam and established trading networks connecting Amsterdam, London, Curacao, Suriname, and Barbados. The Bevis Marks synagogue, built in 1701 in the City of London, served this community and remains the oldest synagogue in Britain still in use. Ashkenazic immigrants began arriving in the early eighteenth century, establishing a separate Great Synagogue in Aldgate.

### Victorian Jewish Life and Emancipation (1800–1900)

The long campaign for Jewish emancipation in England culminated in the Jewish Disabilities Removal Act of 1858, which allowed Jews to sit in Parliament without swearing a Christian oath. Lionel de Rothschild, elected to Parliament in 1847, waited eleven years before the oath was changed. Benjamin Disraeli, who had been baptized as a child, served as Prime Minister twice (1868 and 1874–1880) and is the only person of Jewish birth to have led a British government. Victorian Anglo-Jewry developed sophisticated communal institutions: the Board of Deputies of British Jews, the United Synagogue, the Chief Rabbinate, and major charitable organizations. The large-scale immigration of Russian and Polish Jews following the pogroms of 1881–1906 transformed Anglo-Jewry, creating working-class communities in the East End of London, Leeds, Manchester, and Glasgow.

### Baghdadi Jews in India and Asia (1800–1950)

A third Jewish community shaped the Asian Jewish landscape: the Baghdadi Jews, Iraqi Jewish merchants who established commercial networks across the British Empire in Asia. The Sassoon family, originally from Baghdad, arrived in Bombay in 1832 and built a commercial empire that stretched from Mumbai to Manchester to Shanghai. The Sassoons established synagogues, schools, and charitable institutions in Bombay, Poona, Rangoon, Singapore, and Shanghai, creating a network of Sephardic communities linked by family, language (Judeo-Arabic), and the infrastructure of British imperial commerce. Shanghai's Baghdadi community, centered on the Ohel Rachel synagogue (built 1920 by Jacob Schiff in memory of his wife) and the Ohel Moishe synagogue, was the most significant Asian Jewish community outside India.

### Kaifeng: Chinese Jewry

China's Jewish community of Kaifeng — an isolated outpost of probably Silk Road traders who settled in the Northern Song Dynasty capital (tenth to twelfth centuries) — represents the most extreme case of Jewish isolation and assimilation in history. At its height in the sixteenth century, the Kaifeng community maintained a synagogue, Torah scrolls, and Jewish practice. But by the nineteenth century, they had largely assimilated into Chinese society, losing Hebrew literacy and most Jewish practice while retaining the memory of Jewish identity. The synagogue fell to a Yellow River flood in 1849; the community of perhaps a thousand people who identify as Jewish descendants of Kaifeng today are attempting revival with assistance from Israeli and diaspora organizations.

### Shanghai Refuge (1933–1950)

The most dramatic episode in Asian Jewish history was the refuge offered by Shanghai to approximately 18,000–20,000 European Jewish refugees between 1938 and 1941. Shanghai was unique among major cities in requiring no visa for entry; when every other door was closed, Shanghai remained open. The refugees — primarily German, Austrian, and Polish Jews — created a Jewish city within the city in the Hongkew (Tilanqiao) district, maintaining newspapers, theaters, schools, and communal life in extraordinary circumstances. Under Japanese occupation after Pearl Harbor, the refugees were confined to a designated area but survived largely intact, emigrating to Israel, America, and elsewhere after 1948.

## Major Communities

**London** — The center of Anglo-Jewry since the readmission, London's community today numbers approximately 150,000 (greater London area about 200,000), organized in three major synagogue bodies (United Synagogue/Orthodox, Masorti/Conservative, Liberal/Reform) with the Bevis Marks Synagogue as its historic monument.

**Manchester** — The second community, Manchester's textile industry attracted Sephardic and then Ashkenazic immigrants; Chaim Weizmann lived and worked here as a chemist before becoming Israel's first president.

**Gateshead** — A small northeastern English town that houses one of the leading yeshivot and Jewish girls' schools outside Israel, drawing students from across the world and maintaining an authentically traditional community culture.

**Cochin (Kochi)** — The Kerala city whose White Jews occupied the synagogue lane adjacent to the Maharajah's palace. The community, which never numbered more than a few hundred, maintained extraordinary liturgical traditions. Today, only a handful remain; most emigrated to Israel after 1948.

**Bombay (Mumbai)** — The great metropolis became the center of Indian Jewry, gathering Bene Israel, Cochini, and Baghdadi communities. The Magen David synagogue and the Keneseth Eliyahoo synagogue represent different communities' histories.

**Calcutta (Kolkata)** — The Baghdadi Jewish center in eastern India, whose community of several thousand at its peak maintained magnificent synagogues (Magen David and Beth El) and an important communal culture.

**Shanghai** — The Baghdadi commercial center and Holocaust refugee haven, whose Jewish history stretches from the nineteenth-century Sassoon commercial empire through the mid-twentieth-century European refugee community.

**Kaifeng** — The ancient Chinese Jewish community, now reduced to a small group of descendants attempting to reconnect with Jewish heritage.

## Notable Figures

**Benjamin Disraeli (1804–1881)** — Though baptized in childhood, Disraeli's Jewish origin shaped his identity and politics; he was twice Prime Minister of Britain and the Earl of Beaconsfield, the most politically successful person of Jewish background in nineteenth-century Europe.

**David Sassoon (1792–1864)** — The Baghdad-born patriarch who established in Bombay the commercial empire that made the Sassoons the dominant Jewish family in Asia, funding synagogues, schools, and charitable institutions across the British Empire.

**Chaim Weizmann (1874–1952)** — Born in Russia, educated in Germany, worked in Manchester, Weizmann's chemistry research (acetone for cordite) gave him access to Arthur Balfour; his diplomacy produced the Balfour Declaration and eventually the establishment of Israel, of which he became the first president.

**Menasseh ben Israel (1604–1657)** — The Amsterdam rabbi and publisher who lobbied Cromwell for the readmission of Jews to England, writing *The Hope of Israel* and conducting the famous negotiations that resulted in the de facto readmission of 1656.

**Lord Jonathan Sacks (1948–2020)** — Chief Rabbi of the United Hebrew Congregations of the Commonwealth from 1991 to 2013, Sacks was the most prominent Jewish intellectual voice in British public life, writing works of theology, ethics, and Jewish thought that reached a global audience.

## Cultural Legacy

Anglo-Jewry punched well above its demographic weight in British intellectual, artistic, and political life: Disraeli, Isaiah Berlin, Karl Popper, Harold Pinter, Simon Schama, Antony Blinken's British cousins — the list is disproportionate to a community of perhaps 300,000. The Bevis Marks Synagogue and the London Jewish Museum preserve Sephardic and Ashkenazic heritage respectively. Indian Jewry bequeathed to Israel the Cochini liturgical tradition (remarkably preserved in the synagogue of Moshav Nevatim in the Negev) and Bene Israel customs. The Shanghai refuge represents a unique and largely unknown chapter of Holocaust rescue. The Kaifeng story raises profound questions about the boundaries of Jewish identity and the power of cultural memory to persist across centuries of isolation.

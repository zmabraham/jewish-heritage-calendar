# Western Europe (France, Germany, Netherlands, Italy)

## Overview

Western European Jewry encompasses one of the most creative, turbulent, and ultimately tragic chapters in Jewish history. From the Rhineland communities of medieval Germany — whose synthesis of learning and martyrdom gave Ashkenazic civilization its foundational identity — to the Enlightenment capitals of Paris and Amsterdam where Jewish emancipation was first theorized and then achieved, to the cultural capitals of Vienna, Frankfurt, and Rome where modern Jewish thought was born, this region produced an intellectual and cultural legacy of the first order while simultaneously experiencing some of the worst violence ever directed against Jews.

The ShUM communities of Mainz, Worms, and Speyer — whose Hebrew acronym gives Ashkenazic custom its name — established the template of Ashkenazic Jewish life in the eleventh and twelfth centuries, producing Rashi, the greatest Torah commentator in history, and the Tosafists who built on his work. The Crusades brought the first major organized massacres of European Jews. The Black Death (1348–1350) triggered accusations of well-poisoning that resulted in the destruction of hundreds of German Jewish communities. Expulsions from England (1290), France (1306, 1394), and numerous German cities pushed Jewish populations eastward into Poland and Lithuania, where Ashkenazic civilization would flourish for centuries before the Holocaust.

Italy presents a contrasting narrative: a land where Jewish communities persisted without expulsion from Roman times through the modern era, producing the Venice ghetto (both the first formal ghetto and a surprisingly vital cultural center), the printing houses of Venice and Mantua, and the brilliant synthesis of Italian Renaissance culture with Jewish scholarship. The Netherlands, particularly Amsterdam, became in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries the most tolerant Jewish haven in Europe, host to Sephardic exiles and Ashkenazic immigrants alike.

## Historical Arc

### Ancient Presence and Roman Foundation (c. 50 BCE–500 CE)

The Jewish presence in Western Europe begins with Roman-era communities following trade routes and legions. Rome's Jewish community, one of the oldest in continuous existence, dates from at least the second century BCE and was augmented by Jewish slaves and captives after the Jerusalem destructions of 70 and 135 CE. Synagogue inscriptions and catacombs in Rome document a vibrant community. Jewish communities spread along Roman trade routes to Lyon, Cologne, and other centers; the Cologne community is attested from 321 CE, making it one of Europe's oldest documented Jewish communities.

### Early Medieval and Carolingian Period (500–1000)

Under the Frankish and Carolingian kingdoms, Jews occupied a distinctive niche as merchants, especially in the long-distance trade between Christian Europe and the Islamic world. Charlemagne and his successors granted Jews royal protection and commercial privileges, valuing their commercial networks. Jewish communities established themselves in the Rhineland cities of Mainz, Worms, Speyer, and Trier during this period, laying the groundwork for the ShUM communities that would define Ashkenazic culture.

### ShUM Communities and the Crusade Era (1000–1300)

The communities of Mainz (Maguncia in Hebrew, hence "M"), Worms (Varmayza, "V" for the Hebrew letter Vav — actually "U/W"), and Speyer (Shpira, "SH") formed the ShUM alliance whose communal statutes, liturgical practices, and legal decisions became normative for all of Ashkenazic Jewry. From Worms came Rashi (Rabbi Solomon ben Isaac, 1040–1105), whose commentaries on the Torah and Talmud remain the standard tools of Jewish study to the present day, printed in every traditional edition. The First Crusade (1096) brought catastrophe: crusading armies, en route to the Holy Land, attacked the Rhineland Jewish communities, demanding conversion or death. The communities of Mainz, Worms, and other cities were largely massacred; many Jews chose death — including mass suicide and killing their own children — rather than forced conversion, an act of martyrdom (Kiddush Hashem) that defined Ashkenazic religious identity for centuries. The Second and Third Crusades brought renewed violence. Yet scholarship continued: the Tosafists (twelfth to fourteenth centuries), working primarily in France and Germany, produced elaborate critical commentary on the Talmud that remains foundational to Talmudic study.

### Expulsions and Black Death (1300–1500)

England expelled its Jews in 1290 under Edward I, in what proved to be a template for subsequent European expulsions. France expelled Jews in 1306 under Philip IV and again in 1394. The Black Death (1347–1351) triggered a wave of accusations that Jews had poisoned wells, resulting in the murder or expulsion of Jewish communities from hundreds of German cities. Jews who survived expelled northward and eastward, carrying Ashkenazic culture into Poland and Lithuania. By 1500, Jewish presence in the western German lands, France, and England was greatly reduced, though communities survived in certain territories, particularly those under imperial protection.

### Italy and Venice: Renaissance and Ghetto (1300–1700)

Italy, which had no national Jewish expulsion, maintained continuous Jewish communities in Rome, Venice, Florence, Mantua, Ferrara, and elsewhere. Italian Jews participated in the Renaissance as financiers (the only lending permitted to Jews in many Italian cities), physicians, and scholars. The Venetian ghetto, established in 1516 (giving the world the word "ghetto" from the Venetian word for the foundry district where Jews were confined), was paradoxically a site of vibrant cultural creativity: Leon Modena preached there; Italian Jewish composers wrote madrigals; the printing presses of Venice and other Italian cities produced the first complete editions of the Talmud (Soncino press, 1483–94; Daniel Bomberg's Venetian edition, 1520–23, establishing the standard pagination still used today). Mantua's court Jews and Ferrara's Marrano refugees produced extraordinary cultural syntheses.

### Haskalah, Emancipation, and Reform (1700–1900)

The Enlightenment brought the "Jewish question" — whether Jews could and should be granted full civil equality — to the center of European political debate. Moses Mendelssohn (1729–1786), the "German Socrates," became the embodiment of Jewish Enlightenment: a philosopher respected in German intellectual society who translated the Torah into German, argued for civil rights, and launched the Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment) movement. The French Revolution (1789) granted French Jews full civil equality, and Napoleon's emancipatory policies spread across Europe. The nineteenth century saw Jewish emancipation in Germany, Austria, Italy, and eventually England, alongside the emergence of the Reform movement (born in Hamburg and Frankfurt), Neo-Orthodox response (Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch in Frankfurt), and the beginnings of Zionism (Theodor Herzl, though Viennese, observed the Dreyfus Affair in Paris). The great wave of industrialization and urbanization drew Jews into the German and French middle classes, producing extraordinary cultural contribution — Heine, Marx, Freud, Kafka, Mahler, Mendelssohn.

### Holocaust (1933–1945)

The Nazi seizure of power in Germany in 1933 began the systematic persecution that culminated in the murder of six million Jews. German, French, Dutch, Italian, and Belgian Jewish communities were decimated. The Amsterdam Jewish community that had produced Spinoza was 80 percent murdered. Rome's ancient community saw hundreds deported to Auschwitz in the October 1943 roundup. The Rhineland communities whose origins stretched back to Roman times were destroyed. Western European Jewry survived in greatly reduced numbers; many survivors emigrated to Israel or the United States.

## Major Communities

**Mainz (Maguncia)** — The "M" of ShUM, Mainz was the largest and most influential Rhineland Jewish community, seat of major rabbinic authorities including Rabbenu Gershom (Me'or HaGolah, "Light of the Exile," c. 960–1028), who enacted foundational Ashkenazic legal reforms.

**Worms** — Rashi studied here and the community maintained a great yeshiva. The Worms synagogue, repeatedly destroyed and rebuilt, was restored in 1961 and stands today as a monument to Rhineland Jewish history.

**Frankfurt am Main** — The Judengasse (Jews' Lane) of Frankfurt was for centuries a major Ashkenazic community, birthplace of the Rothschild family and site of the famous Frankfurt ghetto whose conditions were described by Goethe in his autobiography.

**Amsterdam** — The "Dutch Jerusalem" of the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries, Amsterdam's Sephardic community (the Portuguese Congregation) and its Ashkenazic community (two separate communities) made the city the most tolerant and culturally productive Jewish center in Europe. Spinoza, the Pinto family, and later Heinrich Heine and the diamond workers' union all contributed to its legacy.

**Venice** — The first European ghetto and a site of extraordinary intellectual and artistic creativity, including the first standard Talmud edition, the first Jewish newspaper (Gazeta de Venecia), and major figures in Hebrew printing, music, and literature.

**Rome** — The oldest Jewish community in continuous existence in Western Europe, with roots going back to at least the second century BCE. Rome's Jewish community survived two millennia of papal restrictions, ghetto confinement, and fascist persecution.

**Paris** — The intellectual capital where the Dreyfus Affair played out (inspiring Herzl) and where Jewish emancipation was first legislated (1790–1791). The largest Jewish community in Western Europe today outside of Israel.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbenu Gershom ben Judah (c. 960–1028)** — "Light of the Exile," Rabbenu Gershom's synods issued foundational enactments for Ashkenazic Jewry including the ban on polygamy and the prohibition on reading others' letters without permission, establishing the framework of communal governance.

**Rashi (Rabbi Solomon ben Isaac, 1040–1105)** — The greatest Jewish biblical and Talmudic commentator, whose explanations of the Torah and Talmud, written in lucid Hebrew with occasional French glosses (providing a unique record of medieval French vocabulary), remain the standard entry point for every student of Jewish texts.

**Moses Mendelssohn (1729–1786)** — The philosophical bridge between traditional Judaism and European Enlightenment, whose translation of the Torah into German, advocacy for Jewish civil rights, and philosophical respectability launched the era of Jewish emancipation.

**Samson Raphael Hirsch (1808–1888)** — The founder of Neo-Orthodoxy in Frankfurt, whose synthesis of Torah observance with secular culture ("Torah im derekh eretz") created the modern Orthodox movement and the institutions — separate day schools, Orthodox community organizations — that characterize it today.

**Heinrich Heine (1797–1856)** — The poet who converted to Christianity as "an entry ticket to European culture" while never ceasing to engage with Jewish identity, whose lyric poetry and political journalism make him one of the greatest figures of German literature.

**Sigmund Freud (1856–1939)** — The Viennese founder of psychoanalysis, whose Jewish identity and marginality as a Jew in Habsburg Vienna have been extensively explored as influences on his revolutionary theory of the unconscious.

## Cultural Legacy

Rhineland Jewry gave Ashkenazic civilization its foundational legal and liturgical texts. Rashi's commentaries, the Tosafot, the Sefer Hasidim, and the liturgical poetry of the Crusade period define the spiritual profile of Ashkenazic Judaism. The Haskalah and emancipation produced the first generation of secular Jewish cultural giants in modern European culture. The Reform and Neo-Orthodox movements, both born in Germany, defined the denominational divisions that structure modern Jewish religious life globally. Italian Jewry produced the standard Talmud edition and centuries of Hebrew printing. Dutch Jewry produced Spinoza, whose work marks a watershed in the history of Western philosophy. Tragically, much of this heritage now exists primarily in archives, memories, and the communities that the survivors built in Israel and America.

# Ottoman Empire & the Balkans

## Overview

When Spain expelled its Jews in 1492, Sultan Bayezid II of the Ottoman Empire reportedly commented that Ferdinand and Isabella had impoverished their kingdom while enriching his. His offer of refuge to the Sephardic exiles — and the Ottoman Empire's subsequent tolerance, which extended to Jews throughout its vast domains — made the Ottoman world the most important Jewish center of the early modern era. For several centuries following the expulsion, more Jews lived under Ottoman rule than under any other sovereign power on earth. From Constantinople (Istanbul) to Cairo, from Salonika to Aleppo, from Rhodes to Baghdad, the Ottoman millet system protected Jewish religious and communal autonomy while integrating Jews into imperial commerce, medicine, and diplomacy.

The Ottoman Empire's Jewish geography encompassed extraordinary diversity: Sephardic exiles who brought Iberian culture, language (Ladino), and printing technology; Romaniote Jews — Greek-speaking communities whose presence in the Balkans predated Christianity and whose distinct tradition had developed over more than fifteen centuries; Ashkenazic immigrants from Hungary and the German lands; Mizrahi communities of Syria, Egypt, and Mesopotamia; and the ancient Jewish communities of Anatolia. This diversity made Ottoman Jewry a mosaic of rites, languages, and traditions, held together by Ottoman imperial governance and the internal authority of the chief rabbi (Hakham Bashi) recognized by the state.

The twentieth century brought catastrophe. The collapse of the Ottoman Empire after World War I, the rise of Balkan nationalism, Nazi occupation of Greece and the Balkans in World War II, and the founding of the State of Israel transformed this world beyond recognition. The Jews of Salonika — who had made that city a majority-Jewish metropolis and the "Jerusalem of the Balkans" — were almost entirely murdered in Auschwitz in 1943. Turkish Jews emigrated to Israel in large numbers after 1948. By the end of the twentieth century, communities that had numbered in the hundreds of thousands had been reduced to thousands or tens of thousands.

## Historical Arc

### Pre-Ottoman Jewish Communities (c. 300–1453)

Jewish presence in the Balkans and Anatolia is ancient. Romaniote Jews — Greek-speaking Jews following a Byzantine rite distinct from both Sephardic and Ashkenazic practice — lived in Constantinople, Salonika, Ioannina, and throughout Greece from at least the Hellenistic period. Paul's epistles in the New Testament document Jewish communities in Salonika, Corinth, and Athens, and Jewish inscriptions from Byzantine Greece span centuries. Under Byzantine rule, Jews experienced periodic persecution including forced conversions under various emperors, but community life persisted. When the Ottoman Turks conquered Constantinople in 1453 under Mehmed II, the sultan actively encouraged Jewish settlement to repopulate and rebuild the city.

### Ottoman Welcome and Sephardic Influx (1453–1550)

Ottoman policy toward religious minorities was generally pragmatic: the millet system granted recognized religious communities (dhimmis) internal autonomy — their own courts, schools, and charitable institutions — in exchange for loyalty and tax payment. This framework, while clearly hierarchical and discriminatory in some respects, provided conditions far more favorable than contemporary European practice. When the 1492 expulsion came, the Ottoman Empire was thus a natural destination. Tens of thousands of Sephardic Jews settled in Istanbul, Salonika, Izmir, Adrianople (Edirne), Bursa, and the Syrian cities of Aleppo and Damascus. They brought with them printing technology (the first Hebrew press in the Ottoman Empire was established in Istanbul in 1493, less than forty years after Gutenberg), the textile industry, medical knowledge, and commercial networks linking the Ottoman world to Europe. Their linguistic legacy — Ladino, or Judeo-Spanish — transformed Sephardic communities across the empire and remained the primary Jewish vernacular in the Balkans into the twentieth century.

### Golden Age of Ottoman Jewry (1550–1700)

The sixteenth century was the apogee of Ottoman Jewish prosperity and influence. Don Joseph Nasi (c. 1524–1579), a Portuguese Marrano who had returned to Judaism and settled in Istanbul, rose to become Duke of Naxos and one of the most powerful figures in Ottoman foreign policy, using his international commercial and diplomatic connections on behalf of both the Ottoman Empire and Jewish interests worldwide. Gracia Mendes Nasi (c. 1510–1569), the remarkable businesswoman and philanthropist who preceded him, organized an underground railroad for Marranos fleeing the Inquisition and channeled enormous wealth into Jewish communal and scholarly causes. The rabbi and legal codifier Rabbi Joseph Karo, who had settled in Safed (under Ottoman rule), completed the Shulchan Aruch in 1563; its acceptance across the Jewish world was facilitated partly by Ottoman printing and distribution networks. Salonika became the world's largest Jewish city, with Jews constituting more than half the total population and dominating the city's textile industry, porters' guild, and commercial life. Notably, the port of Salonika ceased operation on the Jewish Sabbath because so many workers were Jewish.

### Sabbatai Zevi and Messianic Disruption (1665–1720)

In 1665, a Jewish mystic from Izmir named Sabbatai Zevi (1626–1676) was proclaimed the Messiah by the prophet Nathan of Gaza, triggering the most significant messianic movement in post-biblical Jewish history. Communities across the Jewish world — from Yemen to England, from Morocco to Poland — were swept up in the movement, with many Jews selling their property in anticipation of imminent redemption. When Ottoman authorities arrested Sabbatai Zevi in 1666 and offered him the choice of death or conversion to Islam, he converted. The resulting crisis shattered many communities but did not entirely end the movement: a secret sect of followers known as Dönmeh (Turkish for "converts") continued to practice a crypto-Jewish form of Sabbateanism while outwardly Muslim, surviving in Salonika into the twentieth century.

### Tanzimat, Nationalism, and the Long Nineteenth Century (1839–1912)

The Ottoman Tanzimat reforms of the nineteenth century formally abolished the dhimmi system and granted Jews (and Christians) equal civil status. This modernization brought both opportunities and challenges: Jewish communities gained new access to Ottoman civil life but also faced the pressures of European nationalism, which the Ottoman Empire's Balkan territories were generating in increasingly dangerous forms. The Alliance Israélite Universelle, a French Jewish organization founded in 1860, established schools throughout the Ottoman Empire that gave Sephardic Jewish youth French-language education, connecting them to Western European culture and increasingly to Zionist politics.

### Catastrophe and Displacement (1912–1950)

The Balkan Wars of 1912–1913 transferred Salonika from Ottoman to Greek control, fundamentally altering the political context of its Jewish majority. World War I, the collapse of the Ottoman Empire, and the emergence of modern Turkey under Ataturk, the population exchanges between Greece and Turkey, and the fragmentation of the Balkans into nation-states all disrupted Jewish communities. The Nazi occupation of Greece (1941–1944) proved catastrophic: between March and August 1943, approximately 50,000 Salonikan Jews — nearly the entire community — were deported to Auschwitz-Birkenau, where most were murdered. Bulgarian Jews fared better due to Bulgarian refusal to deport its own citizens, though Bulgarian-administered Jewish communities in Macedonia and Thrace were not protected. Turkish Jews emigrated to Israel in large numbers after 1948 under the Law of Return.

## Major Communities

**Istanbul (Constantinople)** — The Ottoman imperial capital and the largest Jewish community in the empire. Istanbul's Sephardic community, augmented by Romaniote and Ashkenazic populations, numbered in the tens of thousands and produced major rabbinic figures, merchants, and community institutions.

**Salonika (Thessaloniki)** — The "Jerusalem of the Balkans," Salonika was the world's largest Sephardic city for four centuries. Its Jewish majority, Ladino-speaking culture, and textile industry made it unique in world history. The community was almost entirely annihilated in 1943.

**Izmir (Smyrna)** — The great Aegean port city, birthplace of Sabbatai Zevi, and home to a prosperous Sephardic community of merchants and scholars. Izmir's Jews were prominent in the Levant trade.

**Aleppo (Aram Tzova)** — The Syrian commercial capital with one of the oldest Jewish communities in the world. Aleppo's Jews maintained a distinctive rite and tradition centered on the famous "Aleppo Codex," the most authoritative manuscript of the Hebrew Bible, produced in the early tenth century.

**Edirne (Adrianople)** — The former Ottoman capital of Thrace hosted a significant Sephardic community and was a center of Ottoman Jewish publishing.

**Rhodes** — The island community, under Ottoman then Italian rule, maintained a distinctive Judeo-Spanish culture until the almost total deportation of its 1,700 Jews to Auschwitz in July 1944.

**Sofia and Bulgarian Jewry** — Bulgaria's Jewish community, concentrated in Sofia and other cities, survived the Holocaust largely intact due to domestic Bulgarian political resistance to deportation, though Jews in Bulgarian-administered territories perished.

**Ioannina** — The capital of Greek Epirus maintained an ancient Romaniote community following the Byzantine rite. Though small, the Ioannina community preserved extraordinary examples of medieval Jewish liturgy, costume, and practice until the deportation of most of its 1,850 members in 1944.

## Notable Figures

**Gracia Mendes Nasi (c. 1510–1569)** — The Portuguese-born businesswoman who organized a clandestine network helping Marranos escape the Inquisition, eventually settling in Istanbul where she funded Jewish scholarly publications, established yeshivot, and attempted to organize an economic boycott of Ancona after its burning of Marranos.

**Don Joseph Nasi (c. 1524–1579)** — Her nephew, the Duke of Naxos and one of the most powerful Jewish figures of the sixteenth century, who deployed Ottoman diplomatic and commercial resources in service of Jewish interests including attempts to establish a Jewish settlement in Tiberias.

**Sabbatai Zevi (1626–1676)** — The Izmir-born mystic whose messianic proclamation in 1665 sparked the largest and most consequential messianic movement in Jewish history since the destruction of the Second Temple.

**Rabbi Joseph Caro (1488–1575)** — See Iberian section; operating in Ottoman Safed, his Shulchan Aruch was printed and distributed throughout the Ottoman network, making Ottoman printing infrastructure critical to its universal acceptance.

**Rabbi Samuel de Medina (Maharashdam, 1506–1589)** — The leading halakhic authority of Salonika whose thousands of responsa addressed the legal challenges faced by the post-expulsion Sephardic diaspora.

**Abraham Danon (1857–1925)** — Ottoman Jewish scholar, educator, and historian who documented Sephardic language and culture in the twilight of Ottoman Jewry.

## Cultural Legacy

The Ottoman Sephardic world created a distinctive Judeo-Spanish (Ladino) culture of extraordinary richness: the romancero tradition of medieval ballads preserving Spanish literary forms; the melos (music) of Ottoman Sephardic synagogues combining Spanish, Turkish, and Greek elements; a printing tradition that made Istanbul and Salonika major centers of Hebrew publishing for centuries. The Romaniote tradition preserved ancient Jewish-Greek customs, piyyutim, and a connection to the earliest diaspora communities. Turkish Jewish cooking — a synthesis of Sephardic, Ottoman, and Mediterranean traditions — is one of the richest Jewish culinary traditions. Ladino as a language, though severely reduced by the Holocaust, survives in communities in Israel, the United States, and Turkey, and its preservation has become an important cultural project.

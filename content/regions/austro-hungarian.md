# Austro-Hungarian Empire

## Overview

The Austro-Hungarian Empire at its territorial height in the late nineteenth century encompassed an extraordinary diversity of Jewish communities: the sophisticated and largely acculturated Jews of Vienna and Budapest, the Yiddish-speaking masses of Galicia (southern Poland, western Ukraine), the mountain farmers of Carpathian Ruthenia, the merchants of Bohemia and Moravia, the yeshiva communities of Pressburg and Sighet, and the Zionist pioneers of czernowitz and Lemberg. This political entity — unwieldy and multinational by the time of its collapse after World War I — incubated the major movements that defined modern Jewish history: the Haskalah, the Reform and Orthodox movements, Hasidism, political Zionism, secular Jewish nationalism (the Bund), and Jewish integration into European professional and cultural life.

Vienna in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries was, after New York, perhaps the most culturally productive and creatively explosive Jewish city in history: Freud, Herzl, Mahler, Schnitzler, Kraus, Hofmannsthal, Zweig, and the entire Vienna School of psychoanalysis all flourished in the same cultural moment. Budapest produced similarly disproportionate contributions: Theodor Herzl was born here; the father of modern Hungary's economy was the Jewish financier Baron Moritz von Kuffner; Budapest was the birthplace of Zionism's first great leader, Max Nordau. Eastern regions of the empire, particularly Galicia and Bukovina, were the heartlands of traditional Yiddish-speaking Ashkenazic Jewry, the territory that produced the great Hasidic dynasties (Belz, Bobov, Klausenberg, Sanz) and the great Lithuanian-style yeshivot.

The Holocaust virtually annihilated this world. The Jews of Vienna (some 200,000 in 1938) were deported to the East in 1941–1943, with only a few thousand surviving in hiding or through mixed marriages. Hungarian Jewry — the largest community in Axis-aligned Europe, numbering perhaps 800,000 — experienced the fastest and most complete destruction of any Jewish community in history: in eight weeks in the spring of 1944, the Eichmann machinery and Hungarian authorities deported over 400,000 Jews to Auschwitz. Galician Jewry was largely destroyed earlier in the war. By 1945, the Austro-Hungarian Jewish world existed only in memory, in memoirs and literary works, and in the postwar rebuilt communities of Vienna and Budapest and the emigrant communities of Israel and America.

## Historical Arc

### Medieval Austrian Jewry (1200–1620)

Jewish communities are documented in Austria from the twelfth century, with Vienna's Jewish community established by the late thirteenth century. The "Judenstrasse" (Jews' Street) in medieval Vienna housed a substantial community that produced liturgical poets and legal scholars, Rabbi Yom Tov Lipmann Heller among them. However, medieval Austrian Jewry was repeatedly expelled: in 1420, Duke Albrecht V expelled Vienna's Jews, confiscated their property, and burned many at the stake; in 1469-70, Emperor Frederick III expelled Jews from Austrian cities; the 1496 expulsion of Jews from Styria and Carinthia; and the 1670 expulsion under Leopold I that depopulated Vienna's Jewish community for decades. Only in the late eighteenth century under Maria Theresa and Joseph II did a permanent community begin to reestablish itself.

### Josephinian Reforms and Early Modern Community (1780–1867)

Empress Maria Theresa's reign (1740–1780) was hostile to Jewish interests, but her son Joseph II (1780–1790) issued the Edict of Tolerance (1782), granting Jews rights to attend school, engage in trades, and worship — provided they attend German-language schools and adopt "useful" occupations. These Josephinian reforms created the foundation of the modern Austro-Hungarian Jewish community, triggering mass migration from Bohemian, Moravian, and Galician communities to Vienna. The 1848 revolutions, which brought Jewish emancipation, and the 1867 compromise establishing Austria-Hungary as a dual monarchy with full civil equality for Jews, unleashed the rapid urbanization and acculturation of Central European Jewry.

### Viennese Jewish Renaissance (1867–1914)

Vienna's Jewish population exploded from about 40,000 in 1867 to 175,000 by 1910, making it the largest Jewish community in the empire and one of the largest in Europe. The Jewish contribution to Viennese culture was extraordinary: literature (Arthur Schnitzler, Stefan Zweig, Joseph Roth), philosophy (Otto Weininger, Karl Popper), journalism (Karl Kraus), music (Gustav Mahler, Arnold Schoenberg, Alban Berg), and above all psychoanalysis (Sigmund Freud, Alfred Adler, Otto Rank). Viennese Jews were overrepresented in law, medicine, journalism, the arts, and the professions, yet still faced pervasive antisemitism, which defined Austrian politics from the Christian Social movement of Karl Lueger to the Anschluss with Nazi Germany in 1938.

### Budapest and the Hungarian Path (1867–1914)

Hungarian Jewry experienced a different trajectory than their Austrian co-religionists. The 1867 emancipation came with a revolutionary project of assimilation: the Hungarian elite, including many Jewish industrialists and financiers, promoted the "Magyarization" of minorities, encouraging Jews to adopt Hungarian language and culture. This largely succeeded: Hungarian Jews became ardent Magyars, producing disproportionately high rates of conversion to Christianity (over 20 percent by 1910) and integration into Hungarian elite culture. The "Neolog" movement of progressive Judaism established the status quo in Hungarian Jewry; the Orthodox minority fought rearguard legal battles for communal autonomy. Budapest, with its Jewish population of perhaps 200,000 by 1910, was a major center of Jewish publishing, theater, and political activism (the first Zionist group in the world was founded in Budapest in 1881).

### Galicia, Bukovina, and the Eastern Provinces

The eastern crownlands of the empire — Galicia, Bukovina, Carpathian Ruthenia — housed the empire's Yiddish-speaking traditional communities. Galicia, annexed from Poland in 1772, was home to perhaps three-quarters of a million Jews, living in shtetls like Brody, Krakow (though officially Austrian, Krakow remained Polish in Jewish culture), Tarnow, and Rzeszow. Galicia produced both the Hasidic movement (the first Hasidic court was established in Medzhibozh, though this was in the Russian Empire; the Galician courts followed) and the Jewish Enlightenment (the Haskalah flourished in Lemberg/Lvov and czernowitz). Bukovina, with Czernowitz as its capital, hosted a uniquely pluralistic Jewish culture with German, Yiddish, and Hebrew literary all competing. The novelist Mendele Mocher Sforim and the poet Itzik Manger both worked here.

### Hasidic Dynasties and Yeshivot

The Austro-Hungarian Empire, particularly its eastern territories, housed major centers of traditional Jewish learning and piety. The yeshiva of Pressburg (Bratislava), led by the Chatam Sofer (1762–1839), was the citadel of Hungarian Orthodox opposition to Reform and to all forms of modern accommodation. The Hasidic courts of Galicia and Hungary — Belz, Bobov, Klausenberg, Sanz, Satmar — defined religious life for hundreds of thousands and produced distinctive Hasidic cultures that survived in diaspora after the Holocaust.

### Interwar Period and Holocaust (1918–1945)

The empire's collapse after World War I fragmented Austro-Hungarian Jewry into new national states: Austria (small community in Vienna), Hungary (large community in Budapest and provincial cities), Czechoslovakia (Prague and provincial communities), Poland (Galicia), Romania (Bukovina and Carpathian Ruthenia). The Anschluss of 1938 brought Austria under Nazi rule; Viennese Jewry was rapidly stripped of rights, property, and eventually life. Hungarian Jewry survived relatively intact until March 1944, when Germany occupied Hungary; the subsequent deportations between April and July 1944 sent over 400,000 Hungarian Jews to Auschwitz, including most of the Jews of Budapest (until Raoul Wallenberg and others rescued some in late 1944). Galician Jewry had already been largely destroyed between 1941 and 1943.

## Major Communities

**Vienna** — The imperial capital and cultural heartland, with a Jewish population that peaked at 201,000 in 1923. The Leopoldstadt district was predominantly Jewish. Freud's Berggasse 19, the cafés where Jewish intellectuals gathered, the Jewish Museum and University — all defined pre-1938 Viennese culture.

**Budapest** — The dual capital and perhaps the most integrated Jewish community in Europe. The Dohány Street Synagogue is the largest in Europe. The city produced Zionism's first leaders (Herzl, Nordau) and an extraordinary array of artists, scientists, and professionals.

**Prague** — The Bohemian capital that produced the Prague Circle (Kafka, Max Brod, Franz Werfel) and a unique bilingual Czech-German-Jewish culture that persisted until the Nazi occupation in 1939. The Prague Jewish community, dating from the tenth century, numbered over 50,000 in 1930.

**Krakow** — Though technically under Austrian rule after the first partition of Poland, Krakow's Jewish community remained culturally Polish, the center of Galician Jewry, and home to the Rema synagogue (Rabbi Moses Isserles, 1520–1572), the great Ashkenazic legal authority whose glosses to the Shulchan Aruch remain definitive.

**Czernowitz (Chernivtsi)** — The capital of Bukovina and home to an extraordinarily diverse Jewish culture: German-speaking acculturated Jews, Yiddish-speaking traditionalists, Hebraists, and Zionists all coexisted. The 1908 Czernowitz Conference declared Yiddish a national Jewish language.

**Lemberg (Lviv)** — The great Galician city whose Jewish community numbered over 100,000 before World War II, seat of the Lemberg yeshiva and a vibrant press and theater culture in Yiddish, Polish, and German.

**Pressburg (Bratislava)** — Home to the yeshiva of the Chatam Sofer, Rabbi Moses Sofer, the citadel of Hungarian Orthodoxy whose opposition to Reform and integration into modernity defined Orthodox Judaism for a century.

**Sighet and Carpathian Jewry** — The mountain shtetls of northern Transylvania and Carpathian Ruthenia were among the most traditional Jewish communities in Europe; Elie Wiesel, born in Sighet, described this world in his memoirs.

## Notable Figures

**The Chatam Sofer (Rabbi Moses Sofer, 1762–1839)** — The most influential Hungarian rabbi and the founder of modern Orthodoxy as a defensive ideological position, whose opposition to Reform and to changing any established Jewish custom became the battle cry of Orthodox resistance to modernity.

**Sigmund Freud (1856–1939)** — Born in Moravia and educated in Vienna, the founder of psychoanalysis whose theory of the unconscious revolutionized twentieth-century thought and whose Jewish identity was a subject of extensive analysis.

**Theodor Herzl (1860–1904)** — The Viennese journalist whose coverage of the Dreyfus Affair inspired him to write *Der Judenstaat* (1896) and convene the First Zionist Congress (1897), effectively launching political Zionism.

**Stefan Zweig (1881–1942)** — The Viennese writer whose memoir *The World of Yesterday* (1942) is the definitive portrait of Austro-Hungarian Jewish cultural life before its destruction.

**Arthur Schnitzler (1862–1931)** — The Viennese playwright and novelist whose psychological dramatizations of fin-de-siècle Vienna made him a central figure in European literature.

**Martin Buber (1878–1965)** — Born in Vienna, Buber's philosophy of dialogue (Ich und Du, 1923) and his Zionist and Hasidic scholarship made him one of the most influential Jewish philosophers of the twentieth century.

**Elie Wiesel (1928–2016)** — Born in Sighet, Transylvania (then Hungary), the survivor, author of *Night*, and Nobel laureate whose writing and activism made him the voice of the Holocaust to the world.

## Cultural Legacy

Viennese Jewish culture in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries stands among the most creative in Jewish history, producing foundational contributions to psychoanalysis, literature, music, journalism, and philosophy. Hungarian Jewish culture, while perhaps less celebrated internationally, produced the first Zionist organization, great modern literature, and an extraordinarily successful professional class. The literature of Galician Jewry — Sholem Aleichem's shtetl tales, the Hasidic stories of Martin Buber, the poetry of Itzik Manger — preserved a vanishing world in art. The Orthodox and Hasidic traditions of the empire survived in diaspora, particularly in Israel, the United States, and England, where Satmar, Belz, Bobov, and other dynastes reconstituted their communities after the Holocaust.

# Iberian Peninsula & Sephardic Diaspora

## Overview

No episode in Jewish history has stamped a Jewish community's identity more indelibly than the expulsion from Spain in 1492. The Sephardic world — named from "Sepharad," the Hebrew name for Spain — is defined by this catastrophe and by the extraordinary civilization that preceded it: the "Golden Age" of medieval Spanish Jewry, when Jewish poets, philosophers, physicians, and statesmen flourished under Muslim and Christian rule, producing works that remain central to Jewish intellectual heritage. The Iberian Peninsula, encompassing modern Spain and Portugal with the culturally adjacent southern French regions of Provence and Languedoc, was home to the largest, wealthiest, and arguably most creatively productive Jewish community of the medieval world.

Spanish Jewry traced its presence on the peninsula to antiquity — some traditions claimed roots going back to the Babylonian exile or even Solomon's time, while documentary evidence establishes Jewish communities in Roman Hispania by at least the third century CE. Under Visigothic Christian rule from the fifth to eighth centuries, Jews experienced increasingly severe restrictions, including forced conversions. The Islamic conquest of Iberia from 711 CE transformed their situation: under Umayyad and later taifa rulers, Jews rose to positions of extraordinary prominence as viziers, court physicians, poets, and financiers. Cordoba under Abderrahman III hosted Hasdai ibn Shaprut, whose patronage launched the Golden Age. Toledo, Seville, Granada, and Barcelona each produced generations of Jewish scholars and artists.

The Reconquista — the gradual Christian reconquest of the peninsula from the eleventh through the fifteenth centuries — brought increasing pressure on Jewish communities, culminating in the catastrophic pogroms of 1391 that killed thousands and forced tens of thousands into conversion, creating the New Christian or "Converso" class whose religious sincerity would become the obsession of the Spanish Inquisition. The final decree of expulsion, signed by Ferdinand and Isabella on March 31, 1492 — the same year Columbus sailed — gave Spanish Jews four months to convert or depart. Between 100,000 and 200,000 chose exile.

## Historical Arc

### Roman and Visigothic Period (c. 200–711 CE)

Roman tombstones, synagogue remains, and council records document Jewish communities in Hispania from the third century CE. The Council of Elvira (c. 306) enacted the earliest anti-Jewish legislation in Western Europe. Under the Visigoths, the situation deteriorated dramatically: the councils of Toledo enacted forced conversion decrees beginning in 613 under King Sisebut, driving many Jews to practice secretly — an early precedent for the Marrano experience. The Islamic conquest of 711 was therefore welcomed by many Jews as liberation from Christian persecution.

### The Islamic Golden Age (711–1085)

Under the Umayyad Caliphate of Cordoba and the subsequent taifa kingdoms, Iberian Jews entered what is justly called their Golden Age. The conditions that made it possible — a pluralistic Muslim court culture, Arabic as the lingua franca of philosophy and science, Jewish literacy and commercial networks — produced an explosion of Jewish cultural achievement. Hasdai ibn Shaprut (c. 915–970) served as court physician and foreign affairs adviser to Caliph Abderrahman III in Cordoba while patronizing Jewish poets and scholars and corresponding with the Khazar kingdom. Samuel HaNagid (993–1056) served as vizier and military commander of Granada while writing magnificent Hebrew poetry. His son Joseph succeeded him until assassinated in 1066. The twelfth century brought Moses ibn Ezra, Judah Halevi, and the young Maimonides, all writing in the culturally hybrid Andalusian environment.

### The Northern Courts and the Reconquista (1085–1391)

As the Reconquista advanced and Christian kingdoms expanded southward, Jewish communities adapted to new rulers. Toledo, captured by Alfonso VI of Castile in 1085, became a major center of Jewish learning and the great translation movement: Jewish scholars working between Arabic and Latin transmitted the Greek and Arabic philosophical and scientific heritage to Christian Europe. Nahmanides (Rabbi Moses ben Nachman, 1194–1270) flourished in Catalonia, while the kabbalistic school of Gerona and the mystical ferment that would produce the Zohar developed in the late thirteenth century. Rabbi Shlomo ibn Aderet (Rashba) led Barcelona's community. The catastrophic pogrom of 1391, instigated by the archdeacon Ferrant Martínez, swept through Jewish quarters across the peninsula, killing thousands and forcing mass conversions. The resulting Converso class became a permanent feature of Spanish Christian society.

### Expulsion and Diaspora (1391–1600)

The Spanish Inquisition, established in 1478 specifically to investigate the sincerity of Converso Christians, created a climate of terror and denunciation. The expulsion decree of March 31, 1492, signed by Ferdinand and Isabella, gave Jews the choice of conversion or exile. Estimates of those who left range from 100,000 to 200,000. They scattered across the Mediterranean: to the Ottoman Empire (especially Salonika and Istanbul), to North Africa, to the Netherlands (where Marranos who had converted but maintained secret Judaism could revert), to Italy, and eventually to the New World. Portugal initially accepted the exiles but expelled its own Jewish community in 1497, forcing mass conversion on those who remained — creating the Portuguese Converso community whose descendants secretly maintained Jewish practice for generations. In Amsterdam, beginning in the 1590s, Sephardic refugees established an openly Jewish community that became one of Europe's most prosperous and culturally significant.

### Sephardic Diaspora (1500–present)

The Sephardic diaspora created a global network of interconnected communities sharing language (Ladino, the Judeo-Spanish vernacular), liturgy (the Sephardic rite), and cultural memory of Spain. Salonika became the world's largest Sephardic city, with Jews making up the majority of the population by 1500. Istanbul's Sephardic community published the first Hebrew printing press in the Ottoman Empire in 1493. The Dutch Sephardic community in Amsterdam produced Spinoza, produced the first newspaper in a Judeo-language (Gazeta de Amsterdam), and established commercial networks linking Brazil, Curacao, and England. Sephardic merchant families established the first permanent Jewish settlement in North America (in New Amsterdam, 1654) and communities across the Caribbean.

## Major Communities

**Cordoba** — The Umayyad capital and center of the Golden Age. Hasdai ibn Shaprut's patronage made Cordoba a magnet for Jewish talent. Maimonides was born here in 1138. The city's Jewish quarter (Juderia) is today a UNESCO World Heritage site.

**Toledo** — The great city of the medieval translation movement and northern Jewish scholarship, Toledo hosted some of the most distinguished rabbinical courts of the medieval period and the translators who transmitted Greek and Arabic learning to Latin Europe.

**Barcelona** — The capital of Catalan Jewry, home to Nachmanides and the Rashba, and site of the famous Barcelona Disputation of 1263 in which Nachmanides was compelled to debate a converted Jew before King James I of Aragon.

**Granada** — The last Muslim kingdom of Spain and home to Samuel HaNagid's remarkable career as vizier and general. The Alhambra palace, built by the Nasrid dynasty, is the greatest monument of Andalusian civilization.

**Lisbon** — The Portuguese capital and site of the 1497 forced mass conversion of Portuguese Jewry, which created the Converso communities whose descendants secretly maintained Jewish practice and whose descendants eventually populated Amsterdam and the New World.

**Seville** — The starting point of the 1391 pogrom wave and later the main port of departure for New World expeditions in which Converso families would participate.

**Narbonne and Provence** — The southern French communities of Provence were closely linked to Iberian Jewry and produced important scholars including the Kimhi family (grammarians) and Levi ben Gershon (Gersonides), the astronomer-philosopher.

**Amsterdam** — Though in the Netherlands, Amsterdam became the most important post-expulsion Sephardic community: the "Dutch Jerusalem" where Marranos could openly return to Judaism, producing Spinoza, the Pinto and de Pinto banking families, and a vibrant publishing culture.

## Notable Figures

**Hasdai ibn Shaprut (c. 915–970)** — Court physician, foreign affairs adviser, and patron of learning whose position in the Cordoba court launched the Golden Age, supporting poets, scholars, and the reestablishment of a Palestinian-influenced academy on Spanish soil.

**Samuel HaNagid (993–1056)** — The only medieval Jew known to have commanded an army in battle, HaNagid (meaning "the Prince") served as vizier of Granada while writing sublime Hebrew poetry in biblical style and supporting scholars across the Jewish world.

**Judah Halevi (c. 1075–1141)** — The greatest Hebrew poet of the medieval period, whose secular poems of love and nature sit alongside the *Kuzari*, a philosophical dialogue defending Judaism against philosophy and religious rivals. He died, according to legend, at the gates of Jerusalem.

**Maimonides (Moses ben Maimon, Rambam, 1138–1204)** — Born in Cordoba, trained in Islamic and Greek philosophy, eventually settled in Egypt. His Mishneh Torah and Guide for the Perplexed are the twin peaks of medieval Jewish legal and philosophical achievement.

**Nachmanides (Moses ben Nachman, Ramban, 1194–1270)** — Catalonia's greatest rabbi, whose Torah commentary combines literal interpretation, kabbalistic insight, and halakhic analysis, and whose forced participation in the Barcelona Disputation ended with his exile.

**Rabbi Joseph Karo (1488–1575)** — Born in Toledo to a family that was expelled in 1492, Karo eventually settled in Safed and produced the Shulchan Aruch — completing, in exile, the legal codification that Spanish Jewry had been working toward for centuries.

**Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677)** — The Amsterdam-born philosopher, excommunicated from the Sephardic community at age 23, whose philosophical rationalism, biblical criticism, and political philosophy make him one of the founders of modernity.

## Cultural Legacy

The Sephardic legacy in Jewish civilization is immense and enduring. Ladino (Judeo-Spanish), the vernacular language carried into exile, survived as a living language into the twentieth century and produced a rich oral and literary tradition — romanceros (ballads), refranes (proverbs), poetry, and drama. The Sephardic liturgical tradition, with its distinctive prayers, melodies, and calendar of customs, represents one of the two major rites of Jewish practice worldwide. Medieval Iberian Jewish poetry — the secular and sacred poetry of Halevi, ibn Gabirol, ibn Ezra — transformed Hebrew literature and remains central to the literary canon. The philosophical tradition represented by Maimonides became the foundation of all subsequent Jewish philosophy. The Kabbalistic school that developed in thirteenth-century Catalonia produced the Zohar (attributed to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai but compiled by Moses de Leon in Castile), the foundational text of all subsequent Jewish mysticism.

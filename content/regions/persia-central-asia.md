# Persia, Central Asia & Arabian Peninsula

## Overview

Across the vast arc of territory stretching from the Iranian plateau through the mountain passes of Afghanistan to the oasis cities of Central Asia, and south across the Arabian Peninsula to the ancient ports of Yemen, Jewish communities have lived for two and three millennia. These are among the world's oldest and most isolated diaspora populations, communities that maintained Jewish identity in circumstances of extraordinary geographic and cultural remoteness, preserving liturgical traditions, legal customs, and languages that diverged from mainstream Jewish practice across the centuries of separation.

The Persian Jewish community is arguably the second oldest diaspora in the world, predating even the Babylonian by a generation. The Book of Esther is set in the Persian capital Shushan (Susa), and the Books of Ezra and Nehemiah center on Persian royal patronage of Jewish return and rebuilding. Persian Jews developed a rich Judeo-Persian literary tradition, writing Hebrew-script poetry and prose in Persian going back to the thirteenth century. The Bukharan Jews of Central Asia — Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and surrounding regions — maintained their own distinctive rite and spoke Judeo-Tajik (Bukhori), claiming descent from exiles going back to the Babylonian period. Yemenite Jews, perhaps the most studied of the isolated communities, preserved liturgical and biblical traditions of extraordinary antiquity, and their immigration to Israel in the dramatic airlifts of 1949–1950 captured the world's imagination.

These communities inhabited the intersection of multiple civilizations: the Persian-Zoroastrian world that gave Jewish theology its most significant external influence (the origins of angelology, dualism, and apocalypticism are often traced to Iranian contacts), the Silk Road trade network that connected China to Rome, and the Islamic world that provided the framework within which most of these communities lived for over a thousand years.

## Historical Arc

### Biblical Period and Persian Patronage (c. 600–330 BCE)

The Jewish presence in Persia begins with the Babylonian exile of 586 BCE, when communities were scattered eastward. The Persian empire under Cyrus the Great (559–530 BCE) became the great liberator of Jewish tradition: Cyrus's edict of 538 BCE permitting the return to Judea and the rebuilding of the Temple is quoted in full in the Book of Ezra. The Books of Esther and Nehemiah are set in the Persian court — Esther in Shushan (Susa), Nehemiah as cupbearer to Artaxerxes I. Many Jews who remained in Persia rather than returning to Judea became the foundation of a permanent diaspora community. Persian Jewish communities in Ecbatana (Hamadan, identified in tradition with the tomb of Esther and Mordecai) and Susa date from this period.

### Hellenistic and Parthian Periods (330 BCE–224 CE)

Alexander the Great's conquest of Persia in 330 BCE brought Greek culture to the eastern Jewish diaspora, though with less dramatic impact than in Alexandria. Under Parthian rule (247 BCE–224 CE), Jewish communities in Persia and Mesopotamia enjoyed considerable freedom, and the Parthian-Babylonian community formed the eastern anchor of global Jewry. Jewish settlement pushed eastward along the Silk Road into Central Asia during this period, with communities established in Margiana (modern Merv, in Turkmenistan) and Sogdiana (the Ferghana Valley, in Uzbekistan).

### Sasanian Persia (224–637 CE)

The Sasanian Empire brought Zoroastrian state religion and periodic hostility toward religious minorities. Jews faced cycles of tolerance and persecution, with some Sasanian monarchs actively supporting the academies while others imposed restrictions. The Christian-Zoroastrian wars that periodically devastated the empire affected Jewish communities as well. Nevertheless, the great Babylonian academies flourished under Sasanian rule, and Persian Jewish life developed its distinctive culture. The theological encounters between Judaism and Zoroastrianism during this period left traces in both traditions.

### Islamic Conquest and Medieval Flourishing (637–1500)

The Islamic conquest of Persia brought fundamental changes: the Persian Jewish community became a dhimmi community under Muslim rule, subject to protective but inferior status. Under the Abbasid Caliphate, Persian Jews participated in commercial networks linking Baghdad to Central Asia and beyond. The Silk Road's Jewish merchant networks, attested in the Radhanite traders of the ninth and tenth centuries, connected Jewish communities from France to China through Persian and Central Asian intermediaries. Bukhara, Samarkand, and other Sogdian oasis cities became significant Jewish centers. In Yemen, Jewish communities maintained their ancient settlement patterns under successive Islamic dynasties, producing distinctive religious scholarship including the works of Netanel al-Fayyumi (twelfth century).

### Mongol Disruption and Early Modern Period (1258–1800)

The Mongol conquests of the thirteenth century devastated the entire Islamic world from Persia to Iraq, disrupting Jewish communities along with all others. Under the Ilkhanid Mongols, some Jewish figures rose to prominent positions — the physician Sa'd al-Dawla served as Grand Vizier before his assassination in 1291. The Safavid dynasty (1501–1736), which made Shia Islam the state religion of Iran, brought renewed persecution of Jewish communities, including forced conversions, particularly in Isfahan and other major centers. Isfahan's Jewish community nonetheless remained significant through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

### Mashhad Conversions and Modern Period (1839–present)

In 1839, the entire Jewish community of Mashhad was forcibly converted to Islam following a pogrom. The "Jadid al-Islam" (new Muslims) of Mashhad practiced Judaism secretly for generations, one of the few large-scale Marrano communities outside the Iberian diaspora. Many eventually emigrated to Israel or elsewhere when the political climate permitted open Jewish practice. The twentieth century brought catastrophic losses: the 1979 Islamic Revolution in Iran prompted the emigration of most of Iran's 80,000 Jews, reducing the community to approximately 10,000 today. Yemenite Jewry was airlifted to Israel in Operation Magic Carpet (1949–1950) and Operation On Wings of Eagles (1992–2016), nearly completely emigrating. Central Asian Jews emigrated to Israel and the United States following Soviet collapse in 1991.

## Major Communities

**Isfahan** — The Safavid imperial capital, Isfahan housed one of Iran's largest and most cultured Jewish communities, despite periodic persecution under Shia Safavid rulers. Its mellah (Jewish quarter) produced scholars, merchants, and poets writing in Judeo-Persian.

**Hamadan (Ecbatana)** — The ancient Median capital, identified in tradition as the site of Esther and Mordecai's tombs, which remain a pilgrimage destination for Iranian and diaspora Jews. Hamadan's Jewish community traced its origins to the Persian period.

**Mashhad** — The holy city of Shia Islam and the site of the catastrophic 1839 forced conversion. The crypto-Jewish "Mashhadi" community that emerged from this event maintained a distinctive identity that persisted through the twentieth century.

**Bukhara** — The great Silk Road oasis city of Uzbekistan, Bukhara was the center of Central Asian Jewry, producing distinctive architecture (the synagogues and Jewish quarters survive), liturgy, embroidery, and music. The Bukharan Jews' unique Judeo-Tajik language (Bukhori) and their elaborately embroidered textiles are among the most distinctive products of any Jewish community.

**Samarkand** — The other great Silk Road city, Samarkand's Jewish community shared the Bukharan tradition and produced silk merchants who traded across Eurasia. Today a significant Bukharan Jewish community thrives in Queens, New York, and in Israel.

**Sana'a** — The capital of Yemen and center of Yemenite Jewry, Sana'a's Jewish community maintained a distinctive rite preserving ancient Hebrew pronunciation and liturgical practices that scholars consider the closest living approximation to pre-Masoretic tradition.

**Aden** — The great southern port of Yemen, strategically located at the mouth of the Red Sea, Aden's Jewish community dominated much of the medieval Indian Ocean trade. The Adenite Jewish merchants appear in the Cairo Geniza documents as major commercial figures.

**Shiraz** — An important Persian Jewish center, Shiraz produced significant Judeo-Persian poetry and scholarship, including the poet Shahin (fourteenth century), who wrote a Judeo-Persian epic retelling of the Pentateuch.

## Notable Figures

**Cyrus the Great (c. 600–530 BCE)** — The Persian king whose 538 BCE edict permitted the Jewish return to Judea is unique in ancient history: a conqueror who actively supported the restoration of a subject people's religion and homeland. Isaiah 45 calls him "God's anointed," the only non-Israelite to receive this title in the Hebrew Bible.

**Esther and Mordecai (5th century BCE, traditional)** — The protagonists of the Purim story set in the Persian court of Ahasuerus (Xerxes or Artaxerxes), they represent the archetype of diaspora Jewish survival through court influence and personal courage. Their tombs in Hamadan remain a pilgrimage site.

**Shahin of Shiraz (14th century)** — The greatest poet of the Judeo-Persian tradition, Shahin composed epic poems in Persian using Hebrew script, including a Moshe-nama (Book of Moses) and Ardeshir-nama, representing the synthesis of Persian literary culture with Jewish content.

**Rabbi Shalom Shabazi (c. 1619–1686)** — The most celebrated poet of Yemenite Jewry, Shabazi composed hundreds of poems in Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic that are still sung at Yemenite Jewish celebrations worldwide. He is revered as a saint in Yemen by both Jews and Muslims.

**Mawlana Yusuf Yahudi (15th century)** — A Bukharan Jewish poet and scholar who wrote in Judeo-Persian, representing the high culture of Central Asian Jewish intellectual life.

**Joseph al-Basyir (10th–11th century)** — A Persian Jewish Karaite philosopher whose rationalist works influenced both Karaite and Rabbanite thinkers, representing the sophisticated intellectual culture of the eastern Jewish diaspora.

## Cultural Legacy

The Judeo-Persian literary tradition, spanning from the thirteenth through the twentieth century, represents one of the most extensive non-European Jewish literary cultures, comprising poetry, biblical paraphrase, chronicles, and philosophical prose. The Yemenite Jewish liturgical tradition — with its distinctive biblical cantillation, prayers, and musical modes — preserves elements that musicologists and scholars consider among the most ancient in Jewish practice. Bukharan Jewish textile art, particularly the embroidered suzani hangings and elaborate robes, represents a distinctive artistic tradition of high quality. The theological contributions of Persian culture to Judaism — through the medium of Babylonian-Persian contact during the exilic and post-exilic periods — may include angelology, demonology, and apocalyptic literature, representing the most significant documented external influence on biblical and post-biblical Judaism.

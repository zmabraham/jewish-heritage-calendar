# Egypt & North Africa

## Overview

The great arc of North Africa — from Egypt's Nile delta westward through Libya, Tunisia, Algeria, and Morocco to the Atlantic coast — has been home to Jewish communities for over two millennia, and in Egypt's case considerably longer. This region produced two of the most transformative documents in Jewish intellectual history: the Septuagint, the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible created in Alexandria in the third and second centuries BCE, and the Cairo Geniza, the extraordinary medieval archive discovered in a Cairo synagogue in the late nineteenth century, which revolutionized our understanding of medieval Mediterranean Jewish life. It also produced Maimonides, the greatest Jewish philosopher and legal codifier of the medieval world, who wrote his masterworks while serving as court physician in Cairo.

The North African Jewish world was shaped by a succession of civilizations: Phoenician colonists who established Carthage (in modern Tunisia) and the Jewish communities that followed in their wake; the Roman Empire that made Africa a breadbasket and its cities cosmopolitan centers; the Byzantine period; the dramatic Islamic conquest of the seventh and eighth centuries; the Fatimid, Almohad, and other Islamic dynasties; the Ottoman Empire; French and Spanish colonial rule; and the catastrophic dislocations of the twentieth century. Through all these transformations, Jewish communities adapted while maintaining their distinctive identity, developing the Sephardic-Maghrebi culture that produced distinctive liturgy, cuisine, architecture, and legal tradition.

North African Jewry numbered approximately 500,000 at its peak in the mid-twentieth century. The founding of the State of Israel in 1948 and the independence movements that swept North Africa in the 1950s and 1960s precipitated the near-total emigration of these communities, primarily to Israel, France, and Canada. Today, tiny remnants remain — most famously the community of Djerba, Tunisia — but the vibrant civilization of North African Jewry now lives primarily in its diaspora.

## Historical Arc

### Ancient Egypt and the Hellenistic Period (c. 620 BCE–70 CE)

Jewish presence in Egypt is documented from the seventh century BCE, when a Jewish military colony at Elephantine (modern Aswan) served the Persian imperial garrison. The Elephantine papyri, discovered in the late nineteenth century, provide extraordinary detail about this early diaspora community — their temple (which they maintained alongside the Jerusalem Temple), their legal practices, and their social relations. The Greek conquest of Egypt under Alexander the Great in 332 BCE transformed Egyptian Jewish life. Alexandria, founded by Alexander, quickly acquired a large and prosperous Jewish community that numbered perhaps a million souls by the first century CE — the largest concentration of Jews in the ancient diaspora. Alexandrian Jews developed a distinctive Hellenistic Jewish culture, writing in Greek, philosophizing in Platonic terms, and producing the Septuagint, the first translation of the Torah (and eventually all of Hebrew scripture) into another language. This translation made Jewish scripture accessible to the Greek-speaking world and became the Old Testament of the early Christian church.

### Philo and Jewish Hellenism (c. 25 BCE–50 CE)

The philosopher Philo of Alexandria represents the pinnacle of Alexandrian Jewish intellectual achievement: his extensive philosophical writings attempted to synthesize Platonic philosophy with Jewish scripture through allegorical interpretation, anticipating the method that would later dominate Christian theology. Philo personally led an embassy to the Emperor Caligula to protest anti-Jewish riots in Alexandria in 38 CE. The Alexandrian Jewish community experienced periodic violence during the Roman period — the 38 CE pogrom was one of the first documented in Jewish history — and was largely destroyed in the suppression of the Jewish revolt of 115–117 CE (the "Kitos War").

### Rabbinic and Islamic Periods (200–1200 CE)

Jewish communities persisted and eventually revived throughout North Africa under Roman, Byzantine, and then Islamic rule. The Arab conquest of North Africa (639–709 CE) brought the region under Islamic governance and transformed the status of Jewish communities to that of dhimmis — protected but subordinate minorities. The great academy of Kairouan in modern Tunisia became an important center of Jewish learning in the ninth through eleventh centuries, corresponding with the Geonic academies of Babylon and serving as a transmission point for Talmudic scholarship to the western Mediterranean. Rabbi Hananel ben Hushiel and Rabbi Nissim Gaon of Kairouan were among the most important figures of this era. Fatimid Cairo (from 969 CE) proved generally hospitable to Jewish life, and the Fustat (Old Cairo) community produced the documents later discovered in the Cairo Geniza.

### Maimonides and the Geniza World (1100–1300)

Moses Maimonides (1138–1204) was born in Cordoba, Spain, but fled Almohad persecution and eventually settled in Fustat (Old Cairo), where he served as personal physician to Saladin's vizier Al-Qadi al-Fadil. In Egypt he wrote his greatest works: the Mishneh Torah (1180), the first comprehensive code of Jewish law, and the Guide for the Perplexed (1190), the most important work of Jewish philosophy, which reconciled Aristotelian philosophy with Jewish theology. His leadership of the Egyptian Jewish community and his correspondence — preserved in part in the Cairo Geniza — reveal a figure of extraordinary breadth. The Cairo Geniza itself, the storeroom of the Ben Ezra Synagogue in Fustat where damaged documents containing God's name were deposited rather than destroyed, preserved 300,000 fragments of medieval Jewish life: legal documents, personal letters, business records, religious texts, and community records spanning the tenth through nineteenth centuries.

### Sephardic Influx and Ottoman Period (1492–1830)

The expulsion of Jews from Spain in 1492 sent waves of Sephardic refugees across the Mediterranean, significantly augmenting North African Jewish communities. Morocco in particular received large numbers of Spanish Jews, creating a social distinction between the "megorashim" (expellees) and "toshavim" (earlier settlers) that persisted for generations. The city of Fez became a major Sephardic center. Under Ottoman rule (which extended to much of North Africa from the sixteenth century), Jewish communities generally enjoyed stable conditions, organized under the millet system. Moroccan Jews, under the sultan's protection, served as commercial intermediaries between the Moroccan court and European powers, producing dynasties of court merchants and diplomats.

### Colonial Period and Emigration (1830–1970)

French colonization of Algeria (1830), Tunisia (1881), and Morocco (1912) brought complex changes to North African Jewish communities. The Cremieux Decree of 1870 granted French citizenship to Algerian Jews, creating a lasting divide between Jewish and Muslim Algerians that would have tragic consequences during the independence movement. Under French rule, many Jewish families entered French education, professions, and culture, while maintaining religious practice. The Vichy period (1940–1942) brought anti-Jewish legislation to French North Africa, though without the full machinery of the Final Solution. The postwar period brought decolonization and the founding of Israel, both of which accelerated Jewish emigration. By 1970, the ancient communities of Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya had essentially ceased to exist, with populations in Israel, France, and elsewhere.

## Major Communities

**Alexandria** — The great Hellenistic city and ancient diaspora capital. At its height, Alexandria's Jewish population of perhaps one million made it the largest Jewish community in the world. The city produced the Septuagint, Philo's philosophy, and a Hellenistic synagogue culture that influenced early Christianity.

**Cairo / Fustat** — The medieval and modern Egyptian Jewish center. Fustat (Old Cairo), the Fatimid capital, housed the Ben Ezra Synagogue and its famous Geniza. Cairo's Jewish community remained significant through the twentieth century, numbering some 80,000 before 1948 and reduced to a handful today.

**Kairouan** — The holy city of Tunisia and the seat of the medieval Kairouan Academy, which served as the intellectual bridge between the Babylonian Geonim and European Jewry. The great scholars Hananel and Nissim established its reputation.

**Djerba** — The Tunisian island whose Jewish community traces its origins to the destruction of the First Temple in 586 BCE, making it one of the oldest Jewish communities in the world. The El Ghriba synagogue on Djerba is the oldest in Africa. The community, still numbering around a thousand, maintains its ancient traditions and hosts an annual pilgrimage on Lag b'Omer.

**Fez** — The intellectual capital of Moroccan Jewry, especially after the 1492 influx of Spanish exiles. Fez's mellah (Jewish quarter, a term originating here) housed a community of scholars, merchants, and craftsmen. Rabbi Joseph ibn Migash taught here, and the city's rabbinical court was long authoritative for Moroccan Jewry.

**Marrakech** — Southern Morocco's great imperial city, Marrakech's Jewish community was particularly notable for its role in trans-Saharan trade and for its distinctive musical and liturgical traditions.

**Tripoli** — Libya's Jewish community, centered in Tripoli, was particularly affected by Italian colonialism (from 1911) and suffered greatly in World War II. The 1945 and 1948 pogroms killed hundreds, and virtually the entire community emigrated to Israel and Italy by the early 1970s.

**Oran and Algiers** — The major centers of Algerian Jewry, whose unusual status as French citizens (after 1870) created a distinctive Franco-Jewish-Algerian culture. Albert Camus grew up in Algiers in proximity to this community.

## Notable Figures

**Philo of Alexandria (c. 25 BCE–50 CE)** — The greatest Jewish philosopher of the ancient world, whose allegorical interpretation of scripture and synthesis of Platonism and Jewish theology anticipates the entire tradition of Jewish (and Christian) philosophical theology.

**Moses Maimonides (Rambam, 1138–1204)** — Born in Spain, flowering in Egypt, Maimonides wrote his transformative works — Mishneh Torah and Guide for the Perplexed — while serving as court physician in Cairo and leading the Egyptian Jewish community as Nagid. He is buried in Tiberias.

**Rabbenu Hananel ben Hushiel (c. 990–1055)** — The head of the Kairouan academy whose Talmud commentary was the first to synthesize Babylonian and Palestinian traditions and remains important today.

**David Maimonides (1222–1300)** — Moses Maimonides' grandson, who served as Nagid of Egyptian Jewry and continued the family's tradition of philosophical and halakhic leadership.

**Rabbi Haim ibn Attar (1696–1743)** — Born in Morocco, the "Or ha-Hayyim," author of the most beloved Moroccan commentary on the Torah, which became especially cherished in Hasidic circles. He emigrated to the Land of Israel, where he died in Jerusalem.

**Solomon ibn Gabirol (c. 1021–c. 1058)** — Though Andalusian by birth, Gabirol's philosophical work *Fons Vitae* (written in Arabic as *Meqor Hayyim*) circulated in Latin translation through North Africa and was a major influence on medieval Scholasticism.

## Cultural Legacy

North African Jewish civilization produced distinctive contributions to Jewish law, liturgy, music, and literature. The Sephardic-Maghrebi rite, with its unique melodies, liturgical poetry, and legal customs, represents one of the major streams of Jewish practice. The music of North African Jews — combining Andalusian, Arabic, Berber, and Jewish elements — is among the richest in Jewish musicology; the malhun and piyyutim of Moroccan Jews remain living traditions. The mellah architecture of Morocco, with its distinctive synagogues, community institutions, and domestic spaces, represents a unique chapter in Jewish material culture. The Cairo Geniza, now housed primarily in Cambridge and other world archives, continues to yield new discoveries about medieval Jewish life and remains the most important archive in Jewish historiography.

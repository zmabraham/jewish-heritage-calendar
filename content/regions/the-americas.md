# The Americas

## Overview

The Jewish experience in the Americas — from the earliest settlement of Recife by Portuguese Marranos in the 1630s, through the establishment of the first permanent Jewish community in North America in New Amsterdam in 1654, to the complex multicultural landscape of contemporary American Jewry — represents both the newest and in some ways the most successful chapter in diaspora Jewish history. The United States, home to the largest Jewish diaspora community in the world (numbering roughly 7 million, second only to Israel), achieved the Jewish emancipation that Europe only haltingly and incompletely approached: the First Amendment's free exercise clause and its ban on religious establishment made America the only nation in Jewish history whose foundational legal principles explicitly protected religious equality.

The American Jewish story is fundamentally an immigrant story: the Sephardic merchants who arrived in the colonial period; the German Jews who came in the mid-nineteenth century and established the Reform movement and the institutions of communal life; the massive wave of Eastern European Jews (1881–1924) fleeing pogroms and poverty who created the Yiddish-speaking urban culture of the Lower East Side, the Garment District, and the sweatshop economy; and the refugee migrations of the 1930s and 1940s (European Jews fleeing Nazism) and the postwar period (Holocaust survivors and refugees from Soviet communism, the Middle East, and elsewhere). Each wave added new layers to the complex palimpsest of American Jewish culture.

Latin America and the Caribbean Jewish communities, though smaller than their North American counterparts, reflect the diversity of the Sephardic diaspora: the Marrano refugees who settled in Dutch Brazil, Curacao, and Suriname; the German and Eastern European Jews who arrived in Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries; the Holocaust survivors who found haven in South America; and the complex history of Jewish communities under authoritarian regimes. Argentina's Jewish population (roughly 300,000 in 1945) was the largest in the Spanish-speaking world; the 1994 AMIA bombing remains the worst antisemitic attack in the diaspora since World War II.

## Historical Arc

### Colonial Period: New Amsterdam and the Caribbean (1630–1776)

Jewish settlement in the Americas began not with the Puritans but with the Dutch colonial empire. The Dutch West India Company's policies of relative religious tolerance attracted Sephardic merchants from Amsterdam, many of them former Marranos who had returned to Judaism. Recife (Dutch Brazil, 1630–1654) hosted the first formal Jewish congregation in the Americas, Kahal Zur Israel; when the Portuguese reconquered Recife in 1654, the Jews scattered — some returning to Amsterdam, some settling in Dutch colonies in the Caribbean (Curacao, Suriname), and 23 landing in the small Dutch colony of New Amsterdam (New York). Peter Stuyvesant, the anti-Semitic governor who had petitioned the Dutch West India Company to refuse Jewish settlement, was overruled by his Amsterdam superieurs; the 23 refugees founded what became Congregation Shearith Israel, the oldest congregation in what would become the United States. Newport, Rhode Island; Charleston, South Carolina; Savannah, Georgia; and Philadelphia also developed Sephardic colonial communities. The Touro Synagogue in Newport, built in 1763, remains the oldest synagogue building in the United States.

### Early Republic and German Immigration (1776–1880)

American independence brought Jewish emancipation as a constitutional guarantee (though individual states maintained religious tests for office into the nineteenth century). The early American Jewish community was tiny — perhaps 2,500 in 1790, concentrated in New York, Philadelphia, Charleston, Newport, and Richmond — but well integrated. Jewish figures participated in the founding era, including Haym Salomon, the Polish-born financier who helped finance the Revolution.

The German Jewish immigration wave (roughly 1820–1880) brought approximately 200,000 Central European Jews to the United States, fleeing economic dislocation and the failed liberal revolutions of 1848. These immigrants — often peddlers who became small-town merchants — founded the Reform movement, establishing Temples in Cincinnati, Chicago, St. Louis, and elsewhere, and building institutions like the Union of American Hebrew Congregations (1873) and Hebrew Union College (1875). The early German Jewish establishment produced remarkable figures: Judah P. Benjamin, the first Jewish senator (Louisiana, 1852) and Confederate Secretary of State; Emma Lazarus, whose poem "The New Colossus" adorns the Statue of Liberty; Rabbi Isaac Mayer Wise, the architect of American Reform Judaism; and the family dynasties that dominated the American banking and retail industries (Lehman, Goldman, Sachs, Guggenheim, Straus).

### The Great Wave and the Lower East Side (1881–1924)

The assassination of Tsar Alexander II in 1881 triggered the largest Jewish migration in history: approximately 2.5 million Eastern European Jews came to America between 1881 and 1924, plus an additional 250,000 to Argentina, Brazil, and other destinations. This was a mass movement of the dispossessed: overwhelmingly poor, Yiddish-speaking, often refugees from pogrom violence. They settled overwhelmingly in urban areas: New York's Lower East Side became the most densely populated place on earth; Chicago's West Side, Philadelphia's South Philadelphia, Boston's North End all became Jewish neighborhoods. The sweatshop economy of the garment industry, the unions (the International Ladies Garment Workers Union, the Amalgamated Clothing Workers), the Yiddish press and theater, the settlement house movement (the Henry Street Settlement, the Educational Alliance) — all defined the first generation of American Jewish life. This period produced an explosion of Yiddish culture: the Yiddish theater on Second Avenue; the novels of Sholem Asch, Anzia Yezierska, and I.J. Singer; the poetry of Moyshe-Leyb Halpern; the political organizations (the Workmen's Circle, the Bund, Poalei Zion). This was the world that Irving Berlin, George Gershwin, the Marx Brothers, and Sophie Tucker would emerge from into American popular culture.

### Interwar Period and the Crisis of 1933–1945

The immigration restriction acts of 1921 and 1924, which severely limited immigration from Eastern and Southern Europe, effectively ended the Great Wave. The American Jewish community, now well-established, faced its greatest challenge with the rise of Nazism. Despite knowledge of the Holocaust as early as 1942, and despite significant lobbying by Jewish organizations, the United States admitted only a handful of refugees during the Holocaust era; the most notable exception was the establishment of the War Refugee Board in 1944, which is credited with saving perhaps 200,000 Jews. The period also saw the emergence of American Jewish denominationalism: the Conservative movement (Jewish Theological Seminary, 1886) and the Zionist Organization of America (established 1918). The Biltmore Conference (1942), held in New York, issued the first public call for a Jewish commonwealth in Palestine, marking the shift in American Zionism from a minority position to a majority position.

### Postwar Boom, Suburbanization, and Israel (1945–1990)

The postwar period brought American Jewry unprecedented affluence and integration. GI Bill education, suburbanization, and the postwar economic boom transformed Jewish demographics from urban centers (the Lower East Side was emptied by the 1950s) to suburban communities. The Conservative movement became the largest denomination. The 1967 Six-Day War and the 1973 Yom Kippur War mobilized American Jewish support for Israel in unprecedented ways. Soviet Jewry activism — the campaign to free Soviet Jews beginning in the 1960s, the Jackson-Vanik Amendment (1974), the massive demonstrations, and eventually the emigration of over 1 million Soviet Jews to Israel and the United States — united American Jews across ideological lines.

### Contemporary American Jewish Life (1990–present)

The collapse of the Soviet Union and the emigration of Soviet Jews to America (roughly 200,000 came to the United States) reshaped American Jewish demography and culture. Intermarriage rates, approaching 50 percent by the 1990s, raised questions about Jewish continuity. The Orthodox resurgence (the "ba'al teshuvah" movement, the growth of day schools and yeshivot), the emergence of nondenominational Jewish institutions (Birthright Israel, the independent minyan movement), and the increasing diversity of American Jewry (LGBTQ Jews, Jews of color, interfaith families) characterize the current period. Anti-Zionist and anti-Israel activism on American campuses and in progressive organizations has created tensions around Jewish identity and free speech.

## Major Communities

**New York City** — The largest Jewish city in history outside the Land of Israel. The Lower East Side at its height was the most Jewish place on earth; today Brooklyn alone houses perhaps 600,000 Jews. The city hosts the full spectrum: Hasidic Williamsburg, the Russian community of Brighton Beach, the Upper West Side's liberal Jewry, the Syrian Jewish community of Midwood.

**Los Angeles** — The second-largest American Jewish community, particularly strong in entertainment and the professions. The Hollywood connection — Jews built the studio system and have been central to American film and television — defines much of American popular culture.

**Chicago** — A major center with particularly strong institutions: the Chicago Theological Seminary, the Spertus Institute, the Hyde Park community, and the famous Chicago Jewish deli tradition.

**Buenos Aires** — The largest Jewish community in Latin America (roughly 200,000 today), concentrated in the Once district, which suffered the AMIA bombing in 1994, killing 85 and wounding hundreds — the worst antisemitic attack in the diaspora since World War II.

**São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro** — Brazil's major Jewish centers, product of early twentieth-century immigration and later Holocaust survivors, with strong institutions and cultural life.

**Miami and South Florida** — A major retirement and seasonal center, with particularly large communities of Cuban Jews, Argentine Jews, and retirees from the northern United States.

**Mexico City** — The center of Mexican Jewry, with schools, synagogues, and a robust institutional life.

## Notable Figures

**Emma Lazarus (1849–1887)** — The New York poet whose "The New Colossus" ("Give me your tired, your poor, / Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free") became the defining statement of American immigration and American identity itself.

**Louis Brandeis (1856–1941)** — The first Jewish Supreme Court Justice (1916) and a major Zionist leader whose concept of "ethical monotheism" and American Jewish identity linked American and Zionist ideals.

**Henrietta Szold (1860–1945)** — The Baltimore-born founder of Hadassah, the Women's Zionist Organization of America, and Youth Aliyah, whose organizational genius made her one of the most important female Zionist leaders.

**Abraham Joshua Heschel (1907–1972)** — Polish-born, Heschel escaped Europe in 1940 and taught at Jewish Theological Seminary, writing works of religious philosophy that became foundational for modern Jewish theology and marching with Martin Luther King in Selma.

**Bob Dylan (born 1941)** — Born Robert Zimmerman in Duluth, Minnesota, the son of Jewish hardware store owners, Dylan's transformation from the voice of his generation to explicitly Jewish identity in the late 1970s and his eventual album "Slow Train Coming" reflect the complexities of Jewish identity in America.

**Ruth Bader Ginsburg (1933–2020)** — The Supreme Court Justice whose passionate advocacy of gender equality and whose connection to her Jewish heritage ("I am a judge, born, raised, and proud of being a Jew") made her an icon of American Jewish liberalism.

## Cultural Legacy

American Jewish culture has become world culture: the music of Irving Berlin, George Gershwin, Leonard Bernstein, and Bob Dylan; the comedy of the Marx Brothers, Lenny Bruce, and Sarah Silverman; the literature of Philip Roth, Saul Bellow, and Cynthia Ozick; the films of Steven Spielberg and Woody Allen. The institutions of American Jewish life — the synagogue-center model, the Jewish Community Center, the Federation/UJA system of philanthropy, the day school movement — have been exported globally. The American Jewish relationship with Israel — complex, affectionate, sometimes critical — is central to both American Jewish identity and Israeli security. American Jewish religious denominations — particularly Reform and Conservative — have planted branches worldwide. America remains, with its constitutional guarantees and its large Jewish population, the safest and most prosperous Diaspora community in history.

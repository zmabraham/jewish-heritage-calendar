# Brussels

## Overview

Brussels's Jewish community is a modern creation — unlike the ancient Rhineland or Sephardic communities, it was built almost entirely after Belgian independence in 1830. Yet within a century, Brussels had developed into the center of Belgian Jewish life, a cosmopolitan community that reflected the city's identity as an international capital and crossroads. The community encompassed Sephardic and Ashkenazic elements, refugee and established families, and eventually became a significant node in post-war European Jewish life.

## Historical Origins

The medieval Jewish presence in the Duchy of Brabant, of which Brussels was the capital, was limited and disrupted by repeated expulsions. In 1370, Jews in Brussels were accused of desecrating the Host — a typical medieval antisemitic accusation — and the entire Jewish community was expelled from Brabant. A chapel built on the site of the supposed miracle became a pilgrimage site; the stained glass windows commemorating the alleged desecration remained in the Cathédrale des Sts-Michel-et-Gudule until the twentieth century.

For centuries, Brussels had no Jewish community to speak of. Individual Jews might reside temporarily as foreign merchants, but no permanent community existed.

The modern community was founded after Belgian independence (1830) established full legal equality for all religious denominations. A Jewish community organization was established in Brussels in 1833; by mid-century, the community was growing steadily.

## Golden Age

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw Brussels's Jewish community reach its greatest vitality. Immigration from Eastern Europe — from Poland, Russia, Lithuania, and Romania — transformed what had been a small, largely established community into a much larger and more diverse one. The newcomers brought Yiddish culture, socialist politics, Zionist organizing, and a religious intensity that contrasted with the more assimilated Belgian-born Jews.

Brussels became a center for Jewish refugees from Russian pogroms after 1881 and again after 1903. Many Russian and Polish Jews used Brussels as a transit point for emigration to the Americas; some remained, founding new communities and institutions. The diamond industry, centered in nearby Antwerp, had a satellite community in Brussels; Jewish lawyers, doctors, merchants, and intellectuals became visible in Belgian civic and professional life.

## Notable Figures

Philippe Wolfers (1858–1929), the leading Belgian Art Nouveau jeweler, was of Jewish origin — his family, German-Jewish immigrants, had established themselves in Brussels's commercial life.

Paul Hymans (1865–1941), a prominent Belgian statesman and diplomat, was of Jewish descent; he served as Foreign Minister multiple times and was the first president of the League of Nations Assembly. His career demonstrated the degree to which Belgian Jews had been integrated into national civic life.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Grande Synagogue de Bruxelles on the Rue de la Régence (1878) is the most important synagogue in Belgium, a magnificent example of neo-Moorish architecture reflecting the aspirations of Belgian Jewry. The community also maintained the Synagogue Beth Hillel and numerous smaller congregations reflecting the diversity of its membership.

The Consistoire Central Israélite de Belgique, with headquarters in Brussels, coordinates Jewish religious life across the country. The community maintains Jewish schools, including the Athénée Maïmonide, old-age facilities, and community centers.

## Cultural Life

Brussels's Jewish culture reflected its cosmopolitan character. French was the dominant language of the established community; Yiddish the language of Eastern European immigrants; Dutch the language of Jewish settlement in Flemish Brussels. The community produced newspapers in French and Yiddish, theater groups, political organizations ranging from Bundists to Zionists, and charitable institutions of various kinds.

Between the wars, Brussels was home to a particularly active Zionist community, which organized youth movements, fundraising, and emigration to Mandatory Palestine. Several thousand Belgian Jews made aliyah in the 1930s, a development that ultimately saved many lives.

## Decline & Transformation

The German occupation of Belgium (May 1940 to September 1944) brought catastrophe to Brussels's Jews. The city had approximately 25,000–30,000 Jews in 1940; the Nazis and their Belgian collaborators organized deportations beginning in 1942. Brussels Jews were sent to the Dossin transit camp in Mechelen and from there to Auschwitz. Approximately 25,000 Belgian Jews were murdered — roughly 40 percent of the Jewish population, a lower percentage than in the Netherlands but a catastrophic loss nonetheless.

Belgian civil society, including the Church, the resistance, and many ordinary citizens, helped hide Jewish children and adults; the rescue of Jewish children by Belgian institutions and individuals was particularly notable.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Brussels today has a Jewish community of approximately 15,000–20,000, making it the second-largest Jewish community in Belgium after Antwerp. The community includes long-established Belgian Jewish families, post-war immigrants from North Africa and the Middle East, and recent arrivals from Israel and Eastern Europe. The city's role as the seat of European Union institutions has attracted many Israeli and Jewish professionals involved in European politics.

# Rotterdam

## Overview

Rotterdam's Jewish community, though less celebrated than Amsterdam's, was a vital part of Dutch Jewish life — shaped by the city's identity as the Netherlands' great port, its gateway to the sea and to global commerce. The community's history interweaves Sephardic and Ashkenazic strands, port commerce and textile trade, and a tradition of practical engagement with the broader Dutch commercial world. Rotterdam's tragic destruction in the Nazi bombing of May 1940 — which flattened the historic city center — and the subsequent deportations brought this community's long history to a catastrophic close.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Rotterdam in the early seventeenth century, following the pattern of Dutch Sephardic expansion from Amsterdam. Portuguese conversos and their descendants established a small congregation in the city, drawn by the growing commercial opportunities of the port. The Ashkenazic community, arriving somewhat later from Germany and Central Europe, grew to become the majority by the eighteenth century.

Rotterdam's Jews received the standard Dutch tolerance — limited rights, freedom of worship, and access to commerce, but exclusion from guilds and civic office. The community was never as large or prestigious as Amsterdam's, but its port location gave it distinct commercial characteristics: Jews were prominent in the shipping trade, in the import of goods from the Dutch colonies, and in the textile commerce with Germany and the German interior.

## Golden Age

The eighteenth century saw Rotterdam's Jewish community reach its peak prosperity. The Sephardic congregation maintained its distinctive Portuguese rite and identity, while the larger Ashkenazic community dominated the communal landscape. Jewish merchants participated in all aspects of the port trade: importing goods from the East and West Indies, exporting Dutch manufactures, brokering commercial transactions between ships and inland merchants.

The community was respected enough to attract visits from Jewish dignitaries traveling between Amsterdam, Hamburg, and London, and it contributed to the broader conversation of Dutch Jewish life. The Rotterdam Jewish community published some Hebrew texts and maintained a network of educational institutions.

## Notable Figures

Rotterdam produced fewer internationally recognized Jewish scholars than Amsterdam or Hamburg. The community was characterized by practical mercantile achievement rather than intellectual celebrity. However, several Rotterdam-born Jews achieved distinction in Dutch and European commercial life; the Sephardic community maintained connections to the broader network of Portuguese Jewish merchant families across Europe.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The main Ashkenazic synagogue, built in the nineteenth century, was a substantial building reflecting communal prosperity. The Sephardic synagogue, a smaller and more ancient institution, maintained the Portuguese rite. Both communities maintained cemeteries, schools, and welfare institutions.

The community's institutional life was sustained through the nineteenth century as emancipation brought legal equality and new opportunities. Rotterdam Jews participated in Dutch civic life, entering the liberal professions, the press, and municipal governance.

## Cultural Life

Rotterdam's Jewish culture was shaped by its port character: practical, commercially oriented, internationally connected. The community was less given to the intense intellectual and literary culture of Amsterdam Jewry, though it participated in the Haskalah and the modernizing movements of the nineteenth century.

Dutch was the primary language of daily life for many Rotterdam Jews by the nineteenth century, distinguishing them from the more recently arrived immigrants of Eastern European origin who maintained Yiddish into the twentieth century. The two languages — Dutch and Yiddish — coexisted in the community's organizations and press.

## Decline & Transformation

The Nazi bombing of Rotterdam on May 14, 1940 — which destroyed the historic city center and killed approximately 900 civilians — was followed immediately by the Dutch surrender. The occupation began the process of persecution that would devastate the Jewish community. Rotterdam's Jews were registered, identified, economically excluded, and eventually deported. The Dutch railway system transported Rotterdam Jews, like those of Amsterdam, to transit camps and thence to Auschwitz and Sobibor. The deportation rate in the Netherlands was among the highest in Western Europe: approximately 75 percent of Dutch Jews were murdered.

Rotterdam's Jewish community, numbering approximately 12,000–13,000 in 1940, lost the great majority of its members. The city itself had been rebuilt from rubble, its medieval character lost; the Jewish community rebuilt alongside it, but never reached pre-war size.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Rotterdam today has a Jewish community of approximately 1,500–2,000. The post-war community was rebuilt by survivors, returning exiles, and new immigrants — including, in recent decades, Israeli expatriates and former Soviet Union migrants. The city's Jewish community maintains a synagogue, community center, and educational programs. The memory of the wartime catastrophe is honored in memorials and historical education.

## Key Facts

- **Peak Population**: ~13,000 (1940)

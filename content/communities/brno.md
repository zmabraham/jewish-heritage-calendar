# Brno

## Overview

Brno, the capital of Moravia and the second city of the Czech Republic, carries a rich Jewish history stretching from medieval expulsion to modern intellectual flourishing. It is also, strikingly, the city most closely associated with Gregor Mendel — the Augustinian friar who laid the foundations of modern genetics — whose experiments with pea plants in the monastery garden were observed and discussed by Brno's Jewish intellectuals and whose work intersected with early Jewish engagement with evolutionary science.

## Historical Background

Moravia's Jews maintained distinct legal status under the Habsburgs: they were governed by their own Landesordnung (provincial regulations) that differed from those applying to Bohemian or Hungarian Jews. The Moravian Jewish communities, known as the Máhren communities, were concentrated in specific towns where Jews were permitted to reside, with Brno among the most important.

The medieval Jewish community of Brno was expelled in 1454 following the agitation of John of Capistrano, the same Franciscan preacher who instigated expulsions in Breslau and elsewhere. Jews were not permitted to reside permanently in Brno for over three centuries thereafter, though they traded there with special permits. The modern Jewish community reestablished itself only in the nineteenth century, following the Josephine reforms and eventual emancipation.

## Golden Age

The nineteenth century saw Brno become a center of Moravian Jewish intellectual life. As the Habsburg Empire modernized, Brno's Jews — who included merchants, textile manufacturers, and professionals — embraced German-language culture and the Haskalah. The city's Jewish community was associated with the moderate, Wissenschaft-oriented approach to Judaism characteristic of Central European Jewry, rather than with either Hasidism or radical Reform.

Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) conducted his famous experiments with pea plants in the garden of the Augustinian monastery of St. Thomas in Brno, publishing his findings in 1866. While Mendel himself was a Catholic friar, his work was conducted in a city with an intellectually active Jewish community keenly interested in questions of heredity, variation, and natural law. The rediscovery of Mendel's laws in 1900 would later be taken up by Jewish scientists in ways that shaped both genetics and Zionist thought about Jewish peoplehood.

## Notable Figures

Hugo Bergmann (1883–1975), the philosopher and close friend of Franz Kafka, was associated with the Prague-Brno intellectual corridor of Moravian Jewish thought. The literary critic and Zionist intellectual Felix Weltsch was born in Prague but the Moravian Jewish intellectual network included figures from Brno throughout.

Leoš Janáček (1854–1928), Moravia's greatest composer, lived in Brno and had complex, productive relationships with the Jewish musical community there. His late-life operas, infused with Moravian folk idiom, were performed in venues attended by and partially supported by Brno's Jewish cultural patrons.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The New Synagogue of Brno, built in 1855 and subsequently expanded, was the community's main house of worship. It served the German-speaking bourgeois Jewish community and reflected the moderate aesthetic of Habsburg Jewry. The Jewish community also maintained schools, a burial society (Chevra Kadisha), and various welfare organizations. A second synagogue served the smaller traditionalist segment of the community.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Brno's Jewish community was deeply engaged with Moravian civic life, contributing to local industry (particularly textiles), medicine, and law. The textile industry of Moravia was substantially developed by Jewish entrepreneurs who connected local manufacturing with international trade networks. Jewish physicians staffed Brno's hospitals, and Jewish lawyers participated in its legal culture.

The community's engagement with German-language culture — in a city where Czech and German competed for dominance — gave Brno Jews a particular sensitivity to questions of cultural identity and national belonging. Many identified as culturally German while supporting Jewish national causes; others gradually shifted to Czech identity as the Habsburg era waned.

## Decline & Dispersal

Brno's Jewish community numbered approximately 10,000 at the time of the Nazi occupation of Czechoslovakia in March 1939. The community was subjected to the full range of anti-Jewish measures imposed across the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia. Most Brno Jews were deported to Terezín (Theresienstadt) and from there to Auschwitz and other death camps. Fewer than 1,000 returned.

The postwar community never recovered its prewar vitality. Communist Czechoslovakia suppressed religious life across all communities, and Jewish emigration to Israel and the West further diminished the community's numbers.

## Legacy Today

Brno has a small Jewish community today, centered on the restored synagogue and community center. The city takes increasing interest in its Jewish heritage, with museums and educational programs exploring the community's history. The connection to Mendel's legacy — now celebrated at the Mendel Museum at the Augustinian monastery — intersects with Jewish scientific heritage in ways that scholars continue to explore.

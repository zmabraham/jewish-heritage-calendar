# Rabat

## Overview

Rabat, the capital of Morocco, may be less celebrated than Fez or Marrakech in Jewish historical memory, yet its Jewish community left an indelible mark on the Atlantic coast of North Africa. Nestled where the Bou Regreg River meets the Atlantic Ocean, Rabat's mellah — the Jewish quarter established in the seventeenth century — housed a community of merchants, artisans, and scholars who served as intermediaries between the Moroccan sultanate and the European powers. Across the river from its sister city Salé, Rabat developed a Jewish presence that combined the ancient Maghrebi Jewish traditions with the Sephardic culture brought by the 1492 expellees from Spain, creating a distinctive community of considerable learning and commercial acumen.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in the Rabat-Salé region predates the Arab conquest of the seventh century. Ancient Berber Jewish communities dotted the Atlantic coast, and when the Almohad persecutions of the twelfth century devastated established centers further inland, some refugees made their way to coastal towns. The modern Jewish community of Rabat, however, took its characteristic shape in the sixteenth and especially seventeenth centuries, when Sultan Moulay Zidan (r. 1608–1627) and his successors formalized the mellah system and encouraged Jewish settlement to serve the court's commercial and diplomatic needs.

The geographical position of Rabat gave its Jews a unique role. Facing the Atlantic, the city was Morocco's gateway for trade with Europe — with Dutch, English, and French merchants all seeking reliable brokers who spoke their languages and understood their commercial customs. Jewish merchants of Rabat became indispensable as agents, translators, and trade intermediaries. The court Jews (tujjar al-sultan, "merchants of the sultan") arranged imports of weapons, textiles, and luxury goods while handling the export of Moroccan agricultural products, leather, and crafts.

## The Mellah

Rabat's mellah was established in the seventeenth century as a walled quarter adjacent to the royal palace — a typical pattern in Moroccan Jewish urban geography that simultaneously offered royal protection and enforced separation. The mellah's streets were narrow and intimate, lined with three-story houses whose elaborate ornamental woodwork and tilework reflected the prosperity of the community's merchant class. The quarter contained several synagogues, a mikveh (ritual bath), a Jewish cemetery, and communal institutions for the welfare of the poor and the education of children.

Life in the mellah followed the rhythms of Jewish law and the Moroccan seasons. The weekly markets and the great festivals — particularly the elaborate Moroccan celebrations of Mimouna (the post-Passover outdoor festival) and the hillula (pilgrimage festivals to the tombs of holy men) — defined the communal calendar. The Mimouna, celebrated on the night after Passover ended, was a uniquely Moroccan Jewish tradition in which homes were open to all, tables laden with milk, honey, and green herbs as symbols of spring and prosperity, with neighbors — Jewish and Muslim — exchanging blessings.

## Scholarship and Religious Life

Rabat produced significant rabbinic scholars whose legal rulings contributed to the wider tradition of Moroccan Jewish learning. The rabbinical courts handled family law, commercial disputes, and matters of religious observance. The community maintained a tradition of Torah study that blended the Sephardic legal tradition (following the Shulchan Aruch of Joseph Karo) with local Moroccan customs.

The piyyutim (liturgical poems) sung in Rabat's synagogues drew on the rich Andalusian musical tradition brought from Spain in 1492, preserving melodies and poetic forms of great antiquity. The distinctive Judeo-Moroccan musical style — fusing Hebrew liturgy with the Arabic-Andalusian maqam system — produced music of extraordinary beauty that remains alive in Moroccan Jewish communities in Israel, France, and Canada today.

## Court Jews and Diplomacy

Throughout the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, Jewish families of Rabat served the Alawi sultans as court merchants and diplomatic intermediaries. The Macnin, Corcos, and Pinto families were among the most prominent, maintaining commercial relations with British, Dutch, and later French trading houses. When Morocco fell under French protectorate in 1912, Rabat became the administrative capital — a transformation that brought new institutions and opportunities. The Alliance Israélite Universelle established schools in Rabat that offered Jewish children a French secular education alongside Jewish studies, producing a generation fluent in French who would later become prominent in Israeli, French, and Canadian public life.

## The Twentieth Century

The early twentieth century brought both prosperity and anxiety. As Morocco's administrative capital, Rabat attracted educated and commercially successful Jews from other regions. By the 1940s, the community numbered approximately 5,000 people.

The Vichy period (1940–1942) brought anti-Jewish legislation to French Morocco, stripping Jews of their French citizenship and imposing professional restrictions. Sultan Mohammed V's symbolic solidarity with his Jewish subjects during this period became a cherished memory. The founding of the State of Israel in 1948 and Moroccan independence in 1956 together transformed the community's situation. Hundreds of thousands of Moroccan Jews emigrated to Israel in waves through the 1960s, transforming Israeli demographics and culture. The Jews of Rabat largely joined this exodus; by the 1970s, the community had dwindled to a few hundred.

## Legacy

The diaspora of Moroccan Jews from Rabat and across Morocco created one of the world's most vibrant émigré communities. In Israel, Moroccan Jews constitute the largest single ethnic group, and their cultural contributions — Mizrahi music, distinctive cuisine, the Mimouna celebration (now a national Israeli holiday), and the veneration of kabbalistic saints — have profoundly shaped Israeli popular culture. In France, Sephardic Jews from Morocco became the backbone of the French Jewish community after the 1960s. In Canada, particularly in Montreal and Toronto, Moroccan Jewish communities established French-speaking synagogues and cultural institutions preserving their distinctive heritage.

The old mellah of Rabat still stands, its synagogues carefully preserved by the Moroccan government as cultural heritage sites — a physical reminder of the centuries-long partnership between the Moroccan throne and its Jewish subjects that distinguished Moroccan Jewish history.

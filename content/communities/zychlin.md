# Zychlin

## Overview

Zychlin was a textile town in the Kujawy-Mazovia region of central Poland, located in Łęczyca County. Its Jewish community, built over two centuries of commercial and artisan life, was annihilated in the spring of 1942 when the German occupiers deported its approximately 3,000 Jewish residents to the Chełmno extermination camp — one of the first death camps to operate during the Holocaust.

## Historical Background

Jews arrived in Zychlin in the late eighteenth century, initially as traders drawn by the town's position on regional commerce routes. Zychlin developed as a textile manufacturing center during the nineteenth century — part of the broader industrialization of the Congress Kingdom of Poland under Russian rule — and Jews integrated into this economy as weavers, cloth merchants, and factory workers. By the late nineteenth century the community was several hundred families strong, with a rabbi, a study house, and the social institutions of a functioning kehilla: a burial society (*chevra kadisha*), charitable funds, and elementary education.

## Golden Age

Zychlin's Jewish community reached its numerical peak in the interwar period (1918–1939), when approximately 3,000 Jews lived in the town — constituting roughly forty percent of Zychlin's total population. The Jewish quarter centered on the market square, with textile workshops, small shops, and the synagogue forming the fabric of daily life. Zychlin had a vibrant network of Jewish cultural and political organizations during this period: Zionist groups of various factions (General Zionists, Mizrachi, Poalei Zion), a Bund chapter, a Jewish library, and youth movements. The Agudat Israel represented the Orthodox constituency. This political pluralism was characteristic of interwar Polish Jewish life.

## Notable Figures

Zychlin did not produce figures of world-historical religious or literary significance, but its rabbis guided the community with distinction across generations. Rabbi Shmuel Zanvil Unsdorfer served the community in the early twentieth century. Like most small Polish communities, Zychlin's most important figures were its teachers, merchants, and artisans — people whose names survive only in memorial books and the memories of their few descendants.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The main synagogue of Zychlin was a wooden structure typical of the Mazovian architectural tradition, rebuilt after fires and expansions over the generations. A *beit midrash* served daily worshippers and scholars. Separate prayer houses (*shtiblach*) served Hasidic factions. A Jewish school (*cheder*) provided traditional education; a secular Jewish school offered a Yiddish-language curriculum in the interwar years. The *mikveh* (ritual bath) and the cemetery anchored the community's ritual geography.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Zychlin's Jews participated in the broader cultural life of Polish Jewry through their political organizations, Yiddish press subscriptions, and connections to larger urban centers. The weaving and textile trades gave the community a working-class character — many Zychlin Jews were artisans and laborers, not merchants or intellectuals — which shaped a culture of solidarity and mutual aid. The Bund was particularly active, organizing workers and providing secular Yiddish education.

## Decline & Dispersal

The German occupation began in September 1939. Zychlin's Jews were subjected to forced labor, confiscation of property, and the humiliations routine in occupied Poland. In March 1942 — during the first spring of the "Final Solution" — the German authorities concentrated the Jewish population for deportation. Approximately 3,000 Jews from Zychlin and surrounding villages were transported to the Chełmno extermination camp (German: Kulmhof), located about 60 kilometers to the northwest. Chełmno was the first stationary death camp to use gas for mass murder, employing sealed vans into which exhaust was pumped. There were no selections, no labor assignments; deportees were killed upon arrival. Virtually the entire Jewish community of Zychlin was murdered within days of the March 1942 deportation.

## Legacy Today

A memorial book (*Sefer Zychlin*) was compiled by survivors and their descendants, preserving photographs, testimony, and biographical records. It stands as the primary document of the community's existence. No Jewish life remains in Zychlin today. The site of the synagogue is unmarked. Yad Vashem's databases contain testimonies submitted by Zychlin survivors and their families.

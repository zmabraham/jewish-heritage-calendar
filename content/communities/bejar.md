# Béjar

## Overview

Béjar, a small mountain town in the Sierra de Béjar on the border between the provinces of Salamanca and Ávila, maintained a Jewish community remarkable for the quality of its scholarship, the distinctiveness of its local traditions, and the richness of the documentary record it has left behind. Located in a strategically important mountain pass on the road between the Castilian meseta and the Portuguese border, Béjar served as a genuine center of Jewish learning — protected by the powerful Zúñiga family — whose scholars contributed to the broader Sephardic intellectual tradition. Today it is one of the few small Spanish towns with a dedicated Jewish museum, and its expelled community left a diaspora trail of surnames that continues to circle the Jewish world.

## Historical Origins

Béjar's Jewish community developed from the eleventh or twelfth century under the lordship of the Zúñiga family, who became dukes of Béjar and maintained relatively protective policies toward their Jewish subjects. The community was one of only two documented *aljamas* in the entire Duchy of Béjar (the other being Hervás in Cáceres province), with smaller Jewish households in nearby villages including Becedas, Gilbuena, Candelario, and Sorihuela.

The legal foundations of Jewish life in Béjar were laid down in the **Fuero of Béjar**, the town's municipal charter governing the coexistence of Christians, Muslims, and Jews. This remarkable document stipulated that Jews could swear on the Torah in legal proceedings against Christians, that the community would have its own internal judge (the *albaladi*) for matters among Jews, and that Jews had access to the public bathing houses on Fridays and Sundays. The original Fuero is today displayed at the David Melul Jewish Museum in the town.

The town's wool industry — which would eventually make Béjar one of the most important textile centers in Spain — involved Jewish merchants and craftsmen from an early date in both the production and commercial distribution of woolen goods, connecting the community to the broader Castilian and Mediterranean textile economy.

## Golden Age

Béjar's most notable period of Jewish intellectual life appears to have been the fourteenth century, when the protection afforded by the local lords created an environment in which scholars could work without some of the political pressures felt in larger royal cities. The community maintained a full array of institutions: a **synagogue**, a **Talmudic school (yeshiva)**, a **mikveh** (ritual bath), a kosher butcher shop, a communal oven, a hospital, and a cemetery.

Notably, there were no separating walls between the Jewish and Christian quarters — Jewish households were interspersed throughout the town, including near the Duke's palace and the churches of Santa María, San Gil, and San Juan. This unusual degree of integration reflected the particular character of small-town Castilian Jewish life under aristocratic patronage, distinct from the more segregated arrangements typical of royal cities.

The community's relative seclusion in its mountain setting may have fostered a distinctive local tradition in liturgical practice and communal customs. Later scholars occasionally noted Béjar's practices as representing a tradition of its own — a mark of real communal depth.

## Notable Figures

Béjar's most significant intellectual figure is **Hayyim ben Judah ibn Musa** (c. 1380–1460), born in Béjar and one of the notable Jewish scholars of fifteenth-century Castile. A physician, biblical commentator, theologian, and liturgical poet (*paytan*), ibn Musa served for approximately forty years in the courts of kings and nobles. His major work, *Magen va-Romah* ("The Shield and the Lance"), was written as a practical guide for religious disputations with Christians and conversos. The book established twelve logical rules for defending traditional Judaism against missionary arguments and argued methodically that the Messiah had not yet come. Abraham Zacuto's bibliographical work *Yuhasin* records ibn Musa as both a poet and a scholar — a figure of broad cultivation who embodied the best of the small community that produced him.

The documentary record preserves many other community members in vivid specificity. **Lady Faduena**, whose gravestone (discovered during municipal works in 1879) bore the Hebrew inscription "Miss Faduena, rest in peace, glorious princess," gives a named face to the community's social fabric. Other records mention **Simuel de Medina** (tax collector), **Rabi Ça** (physician), **Samuel de la Tetilla** (shoemaker), and **Isaque Albuer** (farmer) — a range of occupations illustrating the full social breadth of Béjar's Jewish life.

Also notable is **Frances de Zúñiga**, born in Béjar shortly before 1492, who served at the court of Charles V and authored a burlesque chronicle with oblique allusions to converso ancestry — a literary trace of Béjar's Jewish heritage in early modern Spanish letters.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The community's full institutional apparatus — synagogue, yeshiva, mikveh, butcher shop, communal oven, hospital, cemetery — functioned within the compact medieval town center, which still preserves much of its medieval spatial organization. Unlike many towns, Béjar had no formal ghetto wall; the Jewish quarter was embedded in the general urban fabric, with Jewish and Christian neighbors living in close proximity.

The **David Melul Jewish Museum**, opened in 2004 in a fifteenth-century manor house, is the town's living memorial to this community. It displays the Fuero of Béjar, ritual objects including a menorah, tallit, and tefillin, and documents relating to the community's history and the broader Sephardic heritage. It is one of the most significant small Jewish heritage museums in Spain.

## Cultural Life

Béjar's Jewish culture was that of a small, self-contained mountain community: closely knit, with strong family and social bonds, maintaining traditions with perhaps less of the cosmopolitan openness of larger centers but with extraordinary internal coherence. The wool industry created economic connections to wider commercial networks while the community maintained its spiritual distinctiveness. The mix of occupations — scholars and physicians alongside shoemakers and farmers — was typical of a community small enough that all roles were represented without specialization into a narrow mercantile caste.

The community's proximity to Portugal added a particular dimension: the border location meant that Béjar's Jews had connections westward as well as eastward, with implications for both commerce and the eventual flight from persecution.

## Decline & Transformation

The **1391 pogroms** reached even Béjar's relatively isolated mountain setting. Paradoxically, the violence elsewhere drove refugees toward the relative safety of Béjar: the Jewish population actually grew considerably in the aftermath of 1391, expanding into a new area called *el Barrio Nuevo* (the New Neighborhood), as displaced Jews from more exposed cities sought refuge under the Zúñiga family's protection.

The community reconstituted itself and continued for another century. But the **1492 Expulsion** ended Jewish life in Béjar definitively. The synagogue was dismantled; its timber and roof tiles were removed and repurposed. Silver Torah crowns and lamps were sold or seized. Families who had lived in the mountain town for generations gathered their possessions and began the long journey into exile.

The depth of Béjar's converso legacy is captured in a remark made by the archpriest Jerónimo González de Lucio in **1665** — more than 170 years after the expulsion — who noted that "it's hard to find old Christians in Béjar," a striking testimony to how pervasively Jewish ancestry had woven itself into the town's Christian population.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Béjar's expelled Jews spread the city's name across the diaspora in the form of family surnames: **Béjar, Behar, Bejarano, Becerano** — variants carried by Sephardic families across Morocco, Turkey, Greece, the Balkans, and the Americas. These names persist in Sephardic communities worldwide as living traces of a small mountain town's centuries of Jewish life.

The David Melul Jewish Museum continues to serve as the community's memorial, and Béjar participates in the network of Spanish towns with documented Jewish heritage through the Red de Juderías de España.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 11th–12th century under Zúñiga lordship
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 300–700 (14th century); grew with refugee influx after 1391
- **Languages**: Castilian, Hebrew
- **Notable Figure**: Hayyim ben Judah ibn Musa (c. 1380–1460), author of *Magen va-Romah*
- **Museum**: David Melul Jewish Museum (opened 2004)
- **Diaspora surnames**: Béjar, Behar, Bejarano, Becerano
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; medieval Judería partially preserved; active heritage museum

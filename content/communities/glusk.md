# Głusk

## Overview

Głusk was a village in the Lublin district of eastern Poland, situated a few kilometers south of Lublin city — effectively a satellite community absorbed into the gravitational field of one of the great centers of Polish Jewish civilization. Its small Jewish population lived within the orbit of Lublin's renowned institutions, sharing in the religious and cultural life of the region while maintaining the intimacy of a village community.

## Historical Background

Lublin's Jewish community, established by the sixteenth century, was among the most distinguished in the Jewish world. The city was home to the Council of Four Lands (*Vaad Arba Aratzot*), the central governing body of Polish Jewry, which met in Lublin for its great trade fairs. The Maharshal (Rabbi Shlomo Luria) and the Maharsha (Rabbi Shmuel Edels) — two of the most important Talmudic commentators in history — were associated with Lublin. The city's yeshiva was known throughout Europe.

Villages around Lublin, including Głusk, existed within this cultural field. Jewish families in Głusk were economically connected to Lublin's markets and religiously affiliated with Lublin's rabbinate and institutions. The village setting provided agricultural land and proximity to the city's commercial opportunities.

## Golden Age

In the interwar period, Głusk's Jewish community was small — a few dozen to perhaps a hundred families — dwarfed by the 37,000 Jews in Lublin city. Nevertheless, the community maintained its own prayer arrangements and local identity. The Lublin Yeshiva, under Rabbi Meir Shapiro's legendary leadership from 1930, was just a short distance away, and its intellectual influence permeated the entire region.

Rabbi Meir Shapiro (1887–1933), the Lubliner Rav, was one of the most creative figures in interwar Orthodox life: he founded the Daf Yomi program (one page of Talmud daily, enabling worldwide synchronized study), built the magnificent Chachmei Lublin Yeshiva building (opened 1930), and served in the Polish Sejm. His influence made the Lublin region a center of renewed Talmudic energy in the interwar period.

## Notable Figures

Głusk's own figures are unknown beyond local memory. The community is defined in Jewish history primarily through its relationship to Lublin. But that relationship was not passive: families who lived in Głusk chose to remain in a village, chose to send their children to study in Lublin, chose to pray in their local *beit knesset* while participating in the larger kehilla's life. These were active choices that constituted Jewish identity.

## Institutions & Synagogues

A prayer house or small synagogue served the village's Jewish residents. Religious authority derived from Lublin. The community maintained a cemetery; burials may alternatively have been conducted in Lublin's large cemetery. A local *cheder* educated children in religious basics.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Głusk's cultural contribution is inseparable from Lublin's. The students of the great Lublin yeshivot, the congregants of Lublin's many synagogues, the readers of Lublin's Hebrew press — all of these passed through or near villages like Głusk, and the cultural vitality of the city permeated its surroundings. The concentration of Torah scholarship in Lublin meant that the entire region breathed a particularly learned air.

## Decline & Dispersal

The German occupation of Lublin in September 1939 was catastrophic for the entire region. Lublin became a center of the Holocaust in Poland: it was headquarters of the SS and Police Leader Odilo Globocnik, who coordinated Operation Reinhard. The Lublin ghetto was established in 1941 and liquidated in March–April 1942, with deportees sent to Bełżec. The Jews of Głusk were absorbed into the Lublin ghetto and shared its fate.

## Legacy Today

Głusk's Jewish dead are part of Lublin's memorial literature. The Chachmei Lublin Yeshiva building in Lublin has been restored and functions as a hotel and cultural center; the main hall has been returned to use as a synagogue. Lublin has made significant efforts to memorialize its Jewish heritage, including the RI-TURN foundation's work on Jewish culture and memory.

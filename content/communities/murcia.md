# Murcia

## Overview

Murcia, the capital of the southeastern Spanish region of the same name, developed one of the notable Jewish communities of the medieval Crown of Castile following its reconquest from Muslim rule in the mid-thirteenth century. Its Jewish history spans from the era of Moorish Mursiyya through more than two centuries of Castilian rule, with Jews participating at the highest levels of administration, diplomacy, commerce, and scholarship. The community benefited from extraordinary early privileges — equal to those of Toledo and Seville — and produced Jewish officials who shaped the political history of the region. The greater Murcia province also contains, at **Lorca**, the best-preserved pre-Inquisition synagogue discovered anywhere in Iberia, uncovered in 2003 and now one of Spain's most significant archaeological Jewish sites.

## Historical Origins

Jews had lived in Muslim **Mursiyya** before the Castilian reconquest. When King **James I of Aragon** besieged the city in 1243, he negotiated through **Astrue (Astruc)**, his Jewish secretary and interpreter — a detail that illustrates the degree to which Jewish intermediaries facilitated the Reconquista itself. **Judah de la Cavallería** financed the naval outfitting for the war against the Muslims, making a major financial contribution to the conquest of the kingdom. The diplomat **Astruc Bonsenyor** (d. 1280) negotiated the Muslim capitulation directly, translated Arabic documents, and served as a key intermediary between Aragonese power and the Moorish population. His son **Judah Bonsenyor** continued in administrative roles after his father's death.

When **James I transferred Murcia to Castile in 1265**, the Jewish community transitioned to Castilian jurisdiction. **Alfonso X "the Wise"** granted Murcia's Jews the same privileges as those of Toledo and Seville — the highest-status Jewish communities in Castile. Jewish cases involving non-Jews were heard before municipal courts; cases between Jews were decided by their own judges. Two community elders attended meetings of the **municipal council** — formal civic participation of a kind rarely paralleled elsewhere.

Alfonso X designated a separate Jewish quarter **by the gate of Orihuela** (near the city's southeastern approach) and allocated a cemetery. Jews paid a 30-dinar annual tax per person plus tithes, a structured relationship that defined the community's legal and financial position within the city.

## Golden Age

Murcia's community reached its peak in the late thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. The aljama maintained its own courts, schools, charitable institutions, a mikveh, and the full apparatus of a well-organized Sephardic community. Jewish physicians served the local nobility, and Jewish tax-farmers collected revenues for the Castilian crown throughout the region and in distant towns across the kingdom.

By the fifteenth century, approximately **2,000 Jews** lived in Murcia — a substantial community. The community's connection to the broader Sephardic halakhic world is documented in the responsa of **Solomon ibn Adret (the Rashba) of Barcelona**: Murcia's community consulted him on whether it was permissible to deal in the skins of rabbits, hares, and bears (Responsa No. 489) — a question that reflects the specifics of the local trade economy and the community's engagement with major authorities.

In **1292**, Moses ibn Turiel of Castile held important administrative positions in Murcia, where Jewish officials of Aragon and Castile met. In **1307**, Isaac ibn Yaish was the last Jewish official with formal jurisdiction over Muslims. **Solomon ibn Lop** (active post-1378) was a prominent tax farmer who later relocated to Majorca. **Samuel Abulafia** (1488) received royal protection for military service during the Granada campaign. **Solomon ben Maimon Zalmati** operated as a Hebrew printer in **1490**, just two years before the expulsion — evidence of a community that continued to invest in cultural production to the very end.

## Notable Figures

Murcia's Jewish community produced a remarkable range of figures across several categories. The **Cavallería family** were major financiers of the Reconquista. **Astruc Bonsenyor** negotiated the Muslim capitulation of the city. The community's engagement with **Solomon ibn Adret (Rashba)**, the leading Sephardic halakhic authority of his generation, placed Murcia within the highest circles of Jewish legal discourse.

The community also produced capable physicians, administrators, and merchants whose names appear in municipal and royal records across two centuries of Castilian rule. Their collective achievement — maintaining a well-organized aljama with formal civic representation for 250 years in southeastern Spain — is itself a significant historical accomplishment.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Judería of Murcia** was a well-defined quarter near the gate of Orihuela in the city center. Archaeological work has identified locations of communal structures. The community maintained a mikveh, schools, and the full apparatus of organized Jewish life.

The most extraordinary physical legacy of Jewish Murcia, however, is found not in the city itself but in **Lorca**, 65 kilometers to the southwest. In **2003**, during hotel construction within the Alcalá fortress-castle, archaeologists discovered the **best-preserved pre-Inquisition synagogue in Iberia that was never converted into a church**. The synagogue is rectangular with two entrances onto a courtyard, a foyer with an ablution fountain, and a large meeting room. Archaeologists also uncovered **eleven adjacent houses** opening onto small squares with narrow streets, each with patios, raised alcoves, workbenches, laundries, pantries, kitchens, and bathrooms — an extraordinarily complete picture of Jewish domestic and communal life. Documents from Murcia's fifteenth-century municipal archive record Jews from Lorca on their way to Hellín refusing to eat non-kosher food in Cieza — direct evidence of kashrut observance in the region. Since **2012**, the Lorca synagogue hosts an **annual Hanukkah candle-lighting ceremony** — the first time in 520 years. An ongoing interdisciplinary research project at the **University of Murcia** continues to study the site.

## Cultural Life

Murcia's Jewish culture was Castilian in orientation, following the liturgical and legal traditions of the broader Castilian community. The silk industry — which would make Murcia famous throughout Spain and the Mediterranean world — created particular economic and cultural connections between Murcia's Jews and their coreligionists in North Africa and the Ottoman Empire. These networks, built through commerce, would later ease the path of exile for families who fled in 1492.

The printing activity of **Solomon ben Maimon Zalmati** in 1490 is a remarkable final flourish: a Jewish community establishing a Hebrew press in its final two years, asserting its cultural vitality even as the political noose tightened.

## Decline & Transformation

The **1391 pogroms** struck Murcia as part of the wider wave of violence. The Judería was attacked, mass forced conversions occurred, and the community was significantly reduced. An Inquisition tribunal was established in Murcia at an early date. In **1490**, the La Guardia trial (a famous Inquisition show trial) mentioned Murcia conversos who had returned to Jewish practice — evidence of ongoing crypto-Jewish life in the community.

As late as **1474**, the community remained substantial and paid "considerable taxes." The final **Expulsion of 1492** ended Jewish life in Murcia. Many families departed through the port of **Cartagena**, just southeast of the city, finding passage to North Africa, where the silk trade connections established over generations facilitated their reception into established communities.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Murcia's expelled families settled primarily in **North Africa** and the **Ottoman Empire**, where the silk and textile expertise that had marked their community in Spain eased their integration into new trades and communities.

The archaeological discoveries at **Lorca** have transformed our understanding of Jewish life in medieval Iberia. The synagogue — preserved because it was never converted, simply abandoned and buried — offers a direct physical connection to the community's religious life. The annual Hanukkah ceremony since 2012 has given the site new life as a place of memory and continuity.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Pre-reconquest Jewish presence; formal community from 1243
- **Privileges**: Equal to Toledo and Seville (highest Castilian Jewish status)
- **Peak Population**: Approximately 2,000 (15th century)
- **Languages**: Castilian, Hebrew, Arabic (earlier period)
- **Hebrew printer**: Solomon ben Maimon Zalmati (1490)
- **Archaeological crown jewel**: Lorca synagogue (discovered 2003) — best-preserved pre-Inquisition synagogue in Iberia
- **Annual ceremony**: Hanukkah candle-lighting at Lorca synagogue since 2012 (first in 520 years)
- **Port of departure 1492**: Cartagena (southeast of Murcia city)
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; Lorca synagogue actively excavated; city heritage documentation ongoing

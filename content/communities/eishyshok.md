# Eishyshok

## Overview

Eishyshok — Eisiskes in Lithuanian, Ejszyszki in Polish — was a shtetl in what is now southern Lithuania, near the Belarusian border, whose Jewish community stretched back nearly 900 years. On September 25–26, 1941, German Einsatzgruppen and Lithuanian collaborators murdered the entire Jewish community — approximately 3,500 people — in a single two-day killing action. The destruction of Eishyshok in a matter of hours became one of the most documented instances of the Holocaust's total annihilation of individual communities and was memorialized in one of the most powerful installations in Holocaust remembrance: the Tower of Faces at the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum in Washington, D.C.

## Historical Background

According to Jewish tradition, Jews first settled in Eishyshok around the year 1065, making it one of the oldest continuous Jewish communities in the region of Lithuania-Poland. Whether or not the precise date is accurate, the community was certainly ancient by European Jewish standards, and by the early modern period it had developed the full institutional apparatus of a self-governing kehilla: a synagogue, a beit midrash, charitable societies, rabbinical courts, and commercial networks.

Eishyshok lay within the broader cultural orbit of Lithuanian Jewry and the Vilna rabbinate. Unlike the Hasidic-dominated communities of Poland and Galicia, Lithuanian shtetlach like Eishyshok were shaped more by the tradition of rigorous Talmudic scholarship, the yeshiva world, and the mitnagedim (opponents of Hasidism). The influence of the Vilna Gaon's intellectual legacy — emphasizing precise Torah text analysis and systematic Talmudic methodology — shaped local religious culture.

## 900 Years of Community Life

The near-millennium of Jewish life in Eishyshok encompassed enormous historical changes. The community survived Crusader-era migrations, the catastrophes of 1648–1649, multiple wars between Poland, Russia, and Sweden, and the upheavals of World War I — which brought German occupation, violence, and refugee displacement to the region.

The shtetl of Eishyshok in the early twentieth century was a community of approximately 3,500 Jews, comprising nearly 90 percent of the town's population. Its economic life centered on commerce between the Jewish townspeople and the Lithuanian and Polish peasant farmers of the surrounding countryside. Jewish merchants, craftsmen, and shopkeepers served as the economic intermediaries of the region. The town's market days, held weekly, were the pulse of local economic life.

Interwar Eishyshok (in the new state of Lithuania after 1918, then transferred to Poland in 1922–1939) experienced the full range of early twentieth-century Jewish political and cultural movements. Hebrew schools, Zionist youth groups, a Jewish library, and dramatic clubs coexisted with the traditional yeshiva and Orthodox synagogue world.

## Yaffa Eliach and the Documentation of Memory

The person most responsible for preserving Eishyshok's memory is Dr. Yaffa Eliach (1935–2016), a historian and Holocaust survivor who was herself born in Eishyshok. As a child of six, she hid with her family during the September 1941 massacre and survived, one of only a handful of the town's Jews to do so. Her family reached the Polish-Soviet front and eventually reached the West.

Eliach spent decades collecting photographs, testimonies, and documents relating to Eishyshok. Her 1998 book There Once Was a World: A 900-Year Chronicle of the Shtetl of Eishyshok is one of the most comprehensive studies ever written about a single Jewish community — a 900-page monument to a town that no longer exists, reconstructing in extraordinary detail its families, institutions, customs, rabbis, merchants, and daily rhythms.

## The Tower of Faces

The most visible memorial to Eishyshok is the Tower of Faces, a centerpiece of the permanent exhibition at the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum in Washington, D.C. Designed by architect James Ingo Freed and opened in 1993, the Tower is a three-story, skylit tower whose walls are covered with over 1,000 photographs from the Eishyshok community — black-and-white images of families, schoolchildren, rabbis, merchants, couples, youth groups, and ordinary street scenes, all from the decade before the massacre. Visitors walk through a corridor of this tower, surrounded on all sides by the faces of people who would be murdered in September 1941. The effect is overwhelming.

The photographs were collected by Yaffa Eliach from survivors and diaspora descendants. They transform Eishyshok from an abstraction into an intimate, particular community of recognizable human beings.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Eishyshok's Great Synagogue, built in the seventeenth century and modified over the centuries, was a landmark of the shtetl. Multiple smaller batei midrash and shtiblekh served different religious groups. The community maintained a yeshiva that, while not of the prestige of the great Lithuanian academies, provided advanced Talmudic education for the town and surrounding area.

## Decline & Dispersal

Soviet occupation from 1939 to 1941 disrupted but did not destroy the community. Many Eishyshok Jews who had emigrated in previous decades to South Africa, the United States, Argentina, and Palestine survived the Holocaust; their descendants are the primary custodians of the community's memory today.

## September 25–26, 1941

On September 25–26, 1941, a Einsatzgruppe unit and Lithuanian auxiliary police rounded up the entire Jewish population of Eishyshok — men, women, and children. The Jews were held in the town's synagogues and in the surrounding area for two days without food or water. On September 26, they were marched to pits outside the town and shot. Approximately 3,500 people were murdered in two days. The 900-year community of Eishyshok ceased to exist.

## Legacy Today

The Tower of Faces at the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum ensures that Eishyshok's memory reaches hundreds of thousands of visitors annually. Yaffa Eliach's book provides an incomparable record. The Jewish cemetery of Eishyshok has been preserved through the efforts of diaspora descendants. The town (today Eisiskes, Lithuania) has a monument at the mass murder site.

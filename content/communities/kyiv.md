# Kyiv

## Overview

Kyiv — capital of Ukraine and one of the oldest cities of the Slavic world — had one of the most legally constrained and yet most dynamically growing Jewish communities in the Russian Empire. Jews were officially barred from permanent residence in Kyiv for most of the nineteenth century (the city was excluded from the Pale of Settlement), yet the community grew to hundreds of thousands by the early twentieth century. The city is the site of Babi Yar — the ravine on Kyiv's outskirts where on September 29–30, 1941, the German Einsatzgruppe C and local collaborators shot approximately 33,771 Jews in two days — the single largest mass murder of the Holocaust. Babi Yar became a symbol of the entire Holocaust and its suppression under Soviet rule became a cause célèbre of Soviet Jewish and dissident history.

## Historical Origins

Jews lived in Kyiv from the medieval period — the city was one of the major centers of Kievan Rus and attracted Jewish merchants who traded with the Byzantine Empire and the eastern steppes. A Jewish quarter (*Zhidivsky kut*) existed in medieval Kyiv. However, after the Mongol devastation of 1240 and the subsequent political changes, Jewish presence became intermittent. When Russia incorporated Kyiv in 1686, Jews were systematically excluded.

Throughout the nineteenth century, Jews were technically not permitted to live in Kyiv — the city was declared off-limits even within the broader Pale of Settlement. Nevertheless, Jews with special status — "first-guild" merchants, university graduates, artisans — were permitted and settled in substantial numbers. This produced a community of disproportionately prosperous and educated Jews: merchants, professionals, and industrialists who had qualified for exemptions.

## Golden Age

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw Kyiv's Jewish community develop rapidly despite legal restrictions. The sugar refining industry, centered in Ukraine, drew Jewish industrialists; the Brodsky family became among the wealthiest in the Russian Empire through their sugar monopoly. Banking, trade, and the professions attracted educated Jews. By 1900 over 30,000 Jews lived in Kyiv; by 1917 the number approached 90,000.

Kyiv was a center of early Zionism. The Hovevei Zion movement had active chapters; the Bilu pioneers who went to Palestine in 1882 included Kyiv students. The Hebrew press had representation. Zionist congresses were attended by Kyiv delegates. The city also had a significant Hasidic presence: the surrounding Ukrainian region was among the heartlands of Hasidism, and Hasidic courts in nearby towns drew Kyiv Jews for religious holidays.

The infamous Beilis Affair (1913) had its center in Kyiv: Menachem Mendel Beilis, a Jewish factory manager, was charged with ritual murder — the medieval blood libel — and tried in Kyiv in one of the most internationally scrutinized antisemitic trials in history. His acquittal by a Ukrainian jury, despite the prosecution's best efforts, was celebrated worldwide as a victory for justice.

## Notable Figures

**Menachem Mendel Beilis** (1874–1934) — the accused in the blood libel trial, acquitted 1913, subsequently emigrated to the United States. **Lazar Brodsky** (1848–1904) — sugar industry magnate and major Jewish philanthropist who built the Brodsky Synagogue and funded numerous Jewish charitable institutions. **Sholem Aleichem** (Sholem Rabinovich, 1859–1916) — the greatest Yiddish writer and humorist, lived in Kyiv for years and set much of his work in the Ukrainian milieu. **Golda Meir** (1898–1978) — born in Kyiv, grew up in Milwaukee, became Israel's fourth prime minister.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Brodsky Synagogue (built 1898–1909, financed by Lazar Brodsky) was a magnificent Moorish-Renaissance structure and the premier synagogue of Kyiv. It survived the Soviet era but functioned as a puppet theater until restored to the Jewish community after 1991. The community maintained numerous other synagogues, Jewish schools, hospitals, orphanages, and cultural institutions, concentrated primarily in the Podil neighborhood near the Dnieper.

## Cultural Life

Kyiv's Jewish cultural life was shaped by its anomalous status as a restricted city with a prosperous, educated Jewish population. The community was more acculturated to Russian language and culture than Pale of Settlement communities; it had a significant intellectual class engaged with Russian literature, art, and politics. Hebrew and Yiddish were studied and used in cultural contexts. Zionist organizations were active. The Bund had some following. By the early Soviet period Kyiv became a center of Soviet Yiddish culture, with the production of Yiddish newspapers, theater, and literature before Stalinist suppression in the late 1930s.

## Decline & Transformation

Kyiv experienced severe pogroms in 1919 during the Ukrainian civil war, when forces loyal to various commanders — Petliura's Ukrainian nationalist armies, White Russian armies — killed thousands of Jews across Ukraine. The interwar Soviet period brought official equality but suppression of religious and cultural institutions, arrests of Zionists and religious leaders, and the murder of Jewish intellectuals in the Stalin-era purges.

The German invasion of June 1941 reached Kyiv in September 1941. Within days, the Germans posted notices ordering all Jews to report with their belongings. On September 29–30, 1941, those who reported — approximately 33,771 people — were marched to the Babi Yar ravine on the edge of the city and shot. Over the following months and years, further Jews, Roma, Soviet POWs, and others were murdered at Babi Yar. The total number of victims approaches 100,000. When Soviet forces returned in 1943, they attempted to destroy evidence of the massacre by burning the bodies. Soviet authorities then suppressed public commemoration of the Jewish character of the victims at Babi Yar for decades.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Babi Yar became a focus of Soviet Jewish identity and dissident culture. Yevgeny Yevtushenko's 1961 poem *Babi Yar* broke Soviet silence about the massacre's Jewish character and caused a cultural sensation. Dmitri Shostakovich set it to music in his 13th Symphony. Anatoly Kuznetsov's documentary novel *Babi Yar* was published in censored form in the Soviet Union and uncensored form in the West. The Babi Yar Memorial Center, opened in Kyiv in 2021, provides comprehensive documentation and commemoration. Golda Meir's birthplace is marked in Kyiv. The Jewish community of Kyiv has revived substantially since 1991 and is today one of Ukraine's most dynamic.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Medieval presence; major growth late 19th century despite legal restrictions
- **Peak Population**: ~224,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Russian, Yiddish, Hebrew, Ukrainian
- **Status Today**: Active major community; Babi Yar Memorial; Brodsky Synagogue restored

# Shepetovka

## Overview

Shepetovka — today Shepetivka in the Khmelnytskyi Oblast of northwestern Ukraine — was a Volhynian railway town that grew in strategic importance as the Russian rail network expanded in the late nineteenth century. It is best known internationally as a connection point in the family history of Moshe Dayan (1915–1981), the Israeli military commander and politician, whose paternal family originated from the Jewish community of Shepetovka before emigrating to Palestine.

## Historical Background

Shepetovka was a small market town in the historically Jewish-dense region of Volhynia, with a Jewish community typical of the area — engaged in trade, crafts, and later in the service economy generated by the railway junction. The town's strategic position on rail lines made it militarily significant, and it changed hands repeatedly during World War I, the Russian Revolution, and the Polish-Soviet War of 1920 — periods of intense suffering for the local Jewish population.

## Jewish Life in a Railway Town

The arrival of the railway transformed many Ukrainian shtetlach in the late nineteenth century. Shepetovka's economic life shifted as it became a transit point for goods and people moving through Volhynia. Jews adapted to the new economy — some worked in railway-related services, others continued traditional trades, and the presence of a rail connection to larger cities opened new commercial opportunities while also making emigration easier for those who chose to leave.

## The Dayan Family Connection

The family of Shmuel (Samuel) Dayan emigrated from Shepetovka to Palestine in the Second Aliyah wave (around 1908). His son Moshe Dayan was born in Deganya Alef, the first kibbutz in Palestine, in 1915 — one of the first children born in the Labor Zionist settlement movement. Moshe Dayan went on to become one of the most recognizable figures of twentieth-century Israeli history: the architect of Israel's victory in the 1967 Six-Day War as Minister of Defense, Chief of Staff during the 1956 Sinai campaign, and a complex, controversial figure who combined military genius with deep personal flaws.

The Shepetovka-to-Deganya-to-Israeli-leadership trajectory embodied in the Dayan family is representative of thousands of similar trajectories — the children of eastern European Jewish towns who became the founders and leaders of the Jewish state.

## Notable Figures

Shepetovka was also associated, in a different way, with Soviet literature: Nikolai Ostrovsky's famous Soviet novel "How the Steel Was Tempered" (1932–1934), the story of a young Ukrainian Communist's life, was partly set in Shepetovka. The town's Jewish community provided supporting characters in the novel — a reflection, however distorted by Soviet ideology, of the town's Jewish presence.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Shepetovka's Jewish community maintained a synagogue and the standard communal infrastructure. The Volhynian Hasidic tradition — influenced by the courts of Karlin, Slavuta, and other nearby centers — shaped the community's religious life. The community was engaged with both traditional Jewish life and the modernizing forces of Zionism, Bundism, and Russian/Ukrainian culture in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Shepetovka's contribution to Jewish history is representative rather than exceptional. Its story — Jewish merchants and craftsmen in a Ukrainian market and railway town, navigating the dangerous politics of the early twentieth century, with some emigrating to Palestine and others remaining to face the Nazi onslaught — is the story of hundreds of similar communities across Volhynia and Podolia.

## Decline & Dispersal

The civil war period of 1918–1921 brought severe violence to Shepetovka, as the town changed hands between various forces. Soviet rule suppressed Jewish communal and Zionist life. The German occupation in 1941 resulted in the mass murder of the Jewish community by September 1942.

## Legacy Today

Shepetovka today is a small Ukrainian city. The memory of its Jewish community is preserved in family histories, yizkor books, and through the broader Israeli memory of the families who emigrated from there to build the Jewish state.

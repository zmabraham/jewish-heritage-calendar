# Dupnitsa

## Overview

Dupnitsa, a mid-sized city in southwestern Bulgaria at the foot of the Rila Mountains, was home to a modest but historically important Jewish community that was part of the broader Bulgarian Jewish experience of relative — though not complete — safety during the Holocaust. The community followed the standard Sephardic history pattern: post-1492 arrival in Ottoman Bulgaria, Ladino-speaking culture, integration into Bulgarian civic life, and eventual emigration to Israel. Dupnitsa is of particular interest because of the local population's role in protecting its Jewish neighbors during the threatened deportations of 1943.

## Historical Background

The Jewish community of Dupnitsa was established, like most Bulgarian Jewish communities, in the period following the 1492 expulsion from Spain. Sephardic refugees settled in Ottoman cities throughout the Balkans, establishing Ladino-speaking communities that maintained their distinctive religious and cultural identity for centuries.

Dupnitsa was a regional commercial center in Ottoman Rumelia, and its Jewish community participated actively in the town's economic life. The community numbered several hundred persons at its peak in the late nineteenth century — a small but solidly established minority within a predominantly Bulgarian, Turkish, and Roma population. The transition from Ottoman to Bulgarian rule following the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878 brought the community into the Bulgarian national state, where Jews were recognized as a religious minority with certain communal rights.

## Community Life and Institutions

The community maintained a synagogue — a relatively modest Sephardic structure — and the full apparatus of communal life: a mikveh, charitable foundations (hevrot), a talmud torah for children's education, and a Jewish cemetery. In the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, the Alliance Israélite Universelle opened schools in Bulgarian cities including Dupnitsa, bringing modern French-language education and Western cultural influences.

Ladino (Judeo-Spanish) was the vernacular of daily life, though many Jews also spoke Bulgarian and some Turkish. The community was religiously traditional — the Bulgarian Jewish rite is a variation of the broader Sephardic-Ottoman liturgical tradition — but was gradually influenced by the modernizing forces that swept through Balkan Jewry in the early twentieth century.

## Notable Figures

Unlike larger Bulgarian Jewish communities (Sofia, Plovdiv, Ruschuk), Dupnitsa produced no internationally prominent figures. The community's history is known primarily through municipal records, Jewish communal documents, and the testimony of survivors and descendants. What is documented is the ordinary life of a provincial Sephardic community: craftsmen, small merchants, and a few professional people living within the structures of traditional Jewish practice while gradually integrating into Bulgarian society.

## The Wartime Crisis and Rescue

Bulgaria joined the Axis in March 1941 and immediately enacted antisemitic laws: Jews were stripped of citizenship, subjected to economic expropriation, forced to wear the yellow star, and conscripted into forced labor battalions. The Bulgarian government, under pressure from Germany, began planning deportations in early 1943.

In March 1943, the first roundups began in Bulgarian-occupied Macedonia and Thrace, and Bulgarian Jews were also targeted. However, the planned deportations of Bulgarian Jews from the prewar kingdom met with extraordinary resistance. The Bulgarian Orthodox Church — led by Metropolitan Stefan and Metropolitan Kirill of Plovdiv — publicly protested. Parliamentary deputies, professional associations, and ordinary Bulgarians mobilized against the deportations.

In Dupnitsa, local witnesses reported that Christian townspeople actively intervened to protect their Jewish neighbors. When police came to arrest the town's Jews, the local population — including the mayor and members of the municipal council — refused to cooperate. The planned deportation from Dupnitsa was aborted, and the community survived. Their reprieve was part of the broader story of how Bulgarian Jews — uniquely among Jewish populations of Nazi-allied countries — were saved from the death camps through a combination of government reluctance, church protest, and civil society resistance.

## Emigration to Israel

After the war, Bulgarian Jewry emigrated en masse to Israel in the years 1948–1952. Dupnitsa's Jewish community, like those throughout Bulgaria, was virtually emptied. Fewer than a handful of Jews remained in the town, and the synagogue fell into disuse and eventual abandonment. The community's descendants live primarily in Israel, where they have assimilated into the broader Bulgarian-Israeli immigrant community.

## Legacy Today

Dupnitsa today has almost no Jewish presence. The former synagogue is either abandoned or repurposed; the Jewish cemetery is partially maintained but in deteriorating condition. The story of Dupnitsa's rescue is commemorated in Bulgarian Holocaust remembrance, which emphasizes the unusually high degree of Bulgarian civilian and official resistance to the Final Solution within Bulgaria proper (as opposed to Bulgarian-occupied territories, where Bulgarian authorities did deport Jews).

The Dupnitsa community represents a smaller but significant chapter in the broader Bulgarian Jewish story: a Sephardic community that survived five centuries of changing regimes and ultimately escaped the worst horrors of the Holocaust through the courage and decency of its non-Jewish neighbors.

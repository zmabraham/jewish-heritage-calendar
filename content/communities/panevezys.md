# Panevėžys (Ponevezh)

## Overview

Panevėžys — known in Jewish tradition as *Ponevezh* — was a significant Lithuanian Jewish community whose greatest legacy transcended the city's destruction. The Ponevezh Yeshiva, founded in this northern Lithuanian city in 1908, was evacuated to Palestine in 1941 and reestablished in Bnei Brak, Israel, where it became one of the most prestigious and influential yeshivot in the world. The yeshiva's survival meant that Ponevezh's scholarly tradition outlived the Holocaust — a rare and extraordinary continuity amid the catastrophe of Lithuanian Jewry. The original Ponevezh community was a typical Lithuanian Jewish town, combining Torah scholarship, commerce, and the tensions of modernity.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Panevėžys in the seventeenth century during the expansion of the Jewish population across the Grand Duchy of Lithuania. The town was a market center for the surrounding agricultural region, and Jews served as merchants, innkeepers, craftsmen, and factors. Its community was part of the extensive network of Lithuanian Jewish communities connected by shared rabbinic authority, mutual aid networks, and the Council of Lithuania. By the nineteenth century, under Russian rule, Panevėžys had grown into a substantial town with a Jewish population representing a significant proportion of total inhabitants — often half or more of the urban population.

## Golden Age

Ponevezh's intellectual reputation derived primarily from its yeshiva. The **Ponevezher Yeshiva**, formally established in 1908 under Rabbi Yosef Shlomo Kahaneman, became one of the great Lithuanian yeshivot. Kahaneman, known as the *Ponevezher Rov*, was an extraordinary Torah scholar, administrator, and personality who transformed a local institution into a center of international reputation. Students came from across Lithuania and beyond to study in Ponevezh.

The community itself was typically Lithuanian: a combination of Mitnagdim (rational Talmudic scholars) and, in the surrounding villages, a sprinkling of Hasidic influence. The city had Hebrew and Yiddish schools, Zionist youth movements, cultural organizations, and the full apparatus of interwar Lithuanian Jewish communal life. Panevėžys was the second-largest city in Lithuania after Kaunas and served as a regional center for a wide area of northern Lithuania.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Yosef Shlomo Kahaneman** (1886–1969), the Ponevezher Rov, was the transformative figure of the community. He survived the Holocaust while visiting Palestine and, rather than being paralyzed by grief, channeled his energies into rebuilding. He founded or co-founded over forty educational institutions in Israel and built the Ponevezh Yeshiva in Bnei Brak into the foremost Lithuanian yeshiva in Israel. His fundraising ability and visionary institutional leadership became legendary in the Orthodox world. **Rabbi Yosef Leib Bloch** of Telz was a neighboring figure who shared a similar institutional legacy. Many distinguished rabbis passed through Ponevezh in its Lithuanian period, including students who later led yeshivot across Europe and America.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Ponevezher Yeshiva stood at the center of the town's Jewish intellectual life. The community also maintained a *beis din* (rabbinic court), elementary schools, a *talmud torah* (free school for poor children), charitable societies, and a full range of communal institutions. The synagogue complex served various congregations. The Jewish cemetery dated to the early modern period. During the interwar period, the community maintained Hebrew newspapers, Zionist clubs, and cultural institutions reflecting the vibrant state of Lithuanian Jewish life.

## Cultural Life

Panevėžys exhibited the characteristic Lithuanian combination of Talmudic rationalism, Mussar ethical discipline, and secular Jewish nationalism. Students at the yeshiva engaged in rigorous Talmud study, while across town Zionist youth read Hebrew literature and trained for agricultural life in Palestine. The Bund had a following among workers and artisans. Hebrew *Tarbut* schools and Yiddish CYSHO schools competed for students. The proximity to Kaunas meant that Panevėžys Jews were well aware of and connected to the broader currents of Lithuanian Jewish and European cultural life.

## Decline & Transformation

Soviet occupation in 1940 brought the closure of the yeshiva and Jewish organizations. Rabbi Kahaneman was in Palestine when the Soviet occupation began and the German invasion followed, and he was unable to return. The German invasion in June 1941 brought catastrophe: within months, the Jews of Panevėžys and the surrounding region were concentrated and shot at mass killing sites. The main murder of Panevėžys Jews took place in late August 1941, when approximately 7,000–8,000 Jews were taken to pits near the town and shot. The systematic murder of Lithuanian Jewry was among the fastest and most complete in all of the Nazi-occupied territories.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The extraordinary legacy of Panevėžys is the Ponevezh Yeshiva in Bnei Brak, Israel. Built by Rabbi Kahaneman on a hill in Bnei Brak beginning in the 1940s, the yeshiva grew into a vast complex with a main study hall, kollel (married scholars' program), dormitories, and affiliated institutions. It became a flagship of Haredi (ultra-Orthodox) Jewish educational ideology and trained thousands of rabbis, scholars, and communal leaders. The Ponevezh Yeshiva in Bnei Brak today enrolls hundreds of students from across Israel and the diaspora. In Panevėžys, a small memorial marks the mass murder sites, and the city's pre-war Jewish life is documented in memorial books (*yizkor bikher*) and archives.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 17th century; yeshiva formally established 1908
- **Peak Population**: ~8,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Hebrew, Lithuanian
- **Status Today**: Community destroyed; Ponevezh Yeshiva in Bnei Brak, Israel, among world's premier yeshivot

# Berlin

## Overview

Berlin's Jewish community is the story of modernity itself — of emancipation, assimilation, creative explosion, and annihilation compressed into barely two centuries. From Moses Mendelssohn's salon to the Weimar Republic's dazzling cultural flowering, from Sigmund Freud's intellectual children to the smoke of the death camps, Berlin Jewry stood at the very center of the German-Jewish encounter that shaped modern Western culture — and whose destruction shattered something irreplaceable in human civilization.

## Historical Origins

Jews were expelled from Brandenburg repeatedly — in 1446 and again in 1510 — and the city had no legally resident Jewish community for much of the early modern period. The decisive turning point came in 1671 when Elector Frederick William of Brandenburg admitted fifty families of wealthy Viennese Jews, expelled by Emperor Leopold I, granting them letters of protection. These families — primarily wealthy merchants and court Jews — formed the nucleus of the Berlin community.

The community remained small and closely controlled throughout the seventeenth century, subject to a heavy protection tax (Schutzgeld) and numerous restrictions. Jews could not worship publicly, were barred from most occupations, and lived under constant threat of renewed expulsion. Yet the community grew steadily; by 1700, some one thousand Jews lived in Berlin.

## Golden Age

The eighteenth century transformed Berlin's Jewish community intellectually and socially. Moses Mendelssohn (1729–1786) arrived in Berlin in 1743 as a young boy, carrying nothing but his Talmud, and became the most celebrated Jewish intellectual in European history. His German translation of the Torah (published 1780–1783) opened a new world to Ashkenazic Jews who had lived in the Yiddish cultural universe; his philosophical works, particularly Jerusalem (1783), argued for religious tolerance and the compatibility of Judaism with Enlightenment rationalism. Mendelssohn's Berlin salon brought together Jews and Christians, philosophers and artists, inaugurating the era of Jewish cultural integration into German life.

The salons of educated Jewish women — Rahel Varnhagen (1771–1833), Henriette Herz (1764–1847), Dorothea Schlegel (1764–1839) — became the social centers of Berlin Romanticism, hosting figures including Goethe, Hegel, and the Schlegel brothers. These women navigated, not always comfortably, the tension between Jewish identity and German cultural life.

The emancipation of Prussian Jews in 1812, though incomplete and subsequently restricted, opened professional and civic life to Jews in new ways. By the mid-nineteenth century, Berlin had become the largest and most influential Jewish community in Germany.

## Notable Figures

Moses Mendelssohn towers above all others as the "father of the Haskalah" (Jewish Enlightenment). His grandson Felix Mendelssohn-Bartholdy (1809–1847), baptized as a child, became one of the nineteenth century's greatest composers — yet his Jewish ancestry remained a source of antisemitic attack, most viciously from Wagner.

Heinrich Graetz (1817–1891), though based in Breslau, was deeply connected to Berlin's intellectual world; his monumental History of the Jews remains the foundational work of modern Jewish historiography.

Else Lasker-Schüler (1869–1945), expressionist poet; Kurt Tucholsky (1890–1935), satirist; Alfred Döblin (1878–1957), author of Berlin Alexanderplatz — these figures represent the extraordinary flowering of Jewish creativity in Weimar Berlin. Walter Benjamin (1892–1940), whose unfinished Arcades Project remains a monument of critical thought, grew up in Berlin and wrote his haunting "Berlin Childhood around 1900" as an elegy to the vanished city.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The New Synagogue on Oranienburger Strasse, completed in 1866, was one of the most magnificent synagogue buildings in the world — its Moorish-style golden dome visible across Berlin, a statement of Jewish confidence and civic belonging. Partially destroyed on Kristallnacht and further damaged in WWII, it has been partially restored and now houses the Centrum Judaicum.

The Jewish community maintained dozens of synagogues, a network of schools, hospitals, orphanages, and the Hochschule für die Wissenschaft des Judentums — the Seminary for the Scientific Study of Judaism, which trained rabbis and scholars from across Germany and beyond.

## Cultural Life

Weimar Berlin (1919–1933) was arguably the most creative period in Jewish cultural history outside of antiquity. Jews were disproportionately represented among the city's publishers, editors, theater directors, film producers, scientists, and intellectuals. The feuilleton culture of the Berlin newspapers — written largely by Jewish journalists — defined the witty, skeptical, ironic tone of Weimar intellectual life. Jewish artists participated in Expressionism, Dadaism, and the New Objectivity. The Schocken Verlag publishing house made serious Jewish literature widely accessible.

The Reform and Liberal movements in Judaism had their German intellectual headquarters in Berlin. The Wissenschaft des Judentums — rigorous academic study of Jewish texts and history — was largely a Berlin creation.

## Decline & Transformation

Hitler's appointment as Chancellor in January 1933 initiated the systematic destruction of German Jewish life. Berlin's approximately 160,000 Jews — the largest Jewish community in Germany — faced progressive exclusion, humiliation, and violence. Kristallnacht (November 9–10, 1938) destroyed Berlin's synagogues and marked a decisive turn toward genocide. The mass deportations from Berlin began in October 1941; by 1943 Berlin was declared "judenfrei" (free of Jews). Some 7,000 Jews survived in Berlin, many through the extraordinary courage of non-Jewish partners and protectors — the "submarines" (U-Boote) who hid in plain sight.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Berlin's Jewish diaspora seeded Jewish communities across the world — New York, Tel Aviv, London, Buenos Aires, São Paulo. Many became central figures in the intellectual and cultural life of their adopted countries.

Post-reunification Berlin has seen a remarkable Jewish renaissance. The community today numbers approximately 25,000–30,000, the largest in Germany, made up largely of immigrants from the former Soviet Union alongside Israeli expatriates and descendants of pre-war communities. Daniel Libeskind's Jewish Museum Berlin (2001), with its zigzag architecture and void spaces, stands as one of the most powerful architectural meditations on Jewish history and loss ever created.

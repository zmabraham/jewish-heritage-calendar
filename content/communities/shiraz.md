# Jewish Community of Shiraz

## Overview

Shiraz, the city of roses, nightingales, and poets, sheltered one of the most culturally distinctive Jewish communities in all of Persia. Situated in the fertile Fars province in the southwest of the Iranian plateau, Shiraz gave the world two of its most celebrated poets — Hafez and Sa'di — and the Jewish community that thrived in its shadow absorbed this literary culture deeply, producing their own poets who wrote in classical Persian meters while weaving in biblical themes. The Shirazi Jews developed a particularly rich synthesis of Jewish tradition and Persian aesthetics, one that expressed itself in poetry, music, wine culture (the Shirazi wine trade had Jewish dimensions), and a distinctive religious practice that preserved ancient customs with unusual fidelity.

The community's history extends at least to the early Islamic period and possibly to the Achaemenid era, given the region's long association with Jewish settlement in Persia. Through the medieval Islamic caliphates, the Mongol devastation, the regional dynasties, and the Safavid period, Shiraz maintained a Jewish presence that fluctuated in size and security but never entirely disappeared. At its peak in the 19th and early 20th centuries, the Shirazi Jewish community numbered several thousand souls and was renowned throughout Persian Jewry for its scholars and poets.

What made Shiraz exceptional was the depth of its Persian-Jewish cultural fusion. More than in any other Iranian city, the Jews of Shiraz participated in the ambient poetic culture of their city, celebrating wine, love, and divine longing in terms that spoke equally to Jewish and Persian sensibilities.

## Historical Origins

The traditional account traces Shirazi Jewish origins to the Babylonian exile, when deportees from Judea were scattered across the Persian empire. Cyrus the Great's base in Fars province means that his Jewish subjects had deep connections to this region from the very beginning of the Persian Jewish story. Archaeological evidence for Jewish presence in pre-Islamic Shiraz is limited, but the community's antiquity is well-supported by its deeply rooted customs and the Talmudic-era linguistic traces in its Judeo-Persian dialect.

The Islamic conquest of Persia in the 7th century CE incorporated Shiraz into the expanding caliphate. Medieval Islamic sources mention a Jewish community in Shiraz from at least the 9th century. The city's position on trade routes connecting the Persian Gulf ports to the interior made it commercially important, and Jewish merchants played a role in the regional economy. The geographer al-Muqaddasi (10th century) noted Jewish presence in major Persian cities including Shiraz.

The Mongol invasions of the 13th century devastated much of Persia but Shiraz, by a combination of luck and shrewd negotiation, was largely spared. This relative continuity allowed Shiraz's Jewish community to develop cultural institutions that were disrupted elsewhere.

## Golden Age

The Safavid period (16th–18th centuries) was paradoxical for Shiraz's Jews, as for Iranian Jews generally: periods of relative tolerance alternated with forced conversions and persecution. The real flowering of Shirazi Jewish cultural life came in the late Safavid and early Qajar periods, when the community produced its most celebrated literary figures.

By the 19th century, the Shirazi Jewish community was recognized throughout Iranian Jewry as a center of both traditional learning and Judeo-Persian poetry. The community maintained several synagogues and rabbinic courts, and its leaders corresponded with Jewish authorities in Baghdad, Istanbul, and Jerusalem. The Alliance Israélite Universelle established a school in Shiraz in the late 19th century, bringing modern French-medium education to the community and producing a generation that navigated both traditional Jewish learning and modern professional life.

## Notable Figures

**Shahin of Shiraz** (14th century) stands as the greatest Judeo-Persian poet of the medieval period. His epic versifications of the Pentateuch, the Book of Esther, and Ezra-Nehemiah in the classical Persian mashnavi form are literary masterpieces that demonstrate the depth of Jewish engagement with Persian literary culture. His *Ardashir-nameh* (Book of Esther), in particular, is a jewel of medieval Jewish literature.

**Imrani** (15th–16th century), another Shirazi Jewish poet, composed the *Fath-nameh* (Book of Conquest), versifying the Book of Joshua in Persian epic style. His work continued the tradition Shahin established and influenced subsequent Judeo-Persian poetry.

**Simeon Hakham** (c. 19th century), a rabbi and scholar in Shiraz, was known for his mastery of both halacha and Persian literary tradition, embodying the synthesis that characterized Shirazi Jewish culture.

**Mullah Yuda** (19th century), a popular Shirazi Jewish musician and poet, composed Judeo-Persian songs that were sung at weddings and celebrations, preserving ancient melodic modes that blended Persian classical music with Jewish liturgical elements.

**Aziz Nouriel** (20th century), communal leader and educator, helped guide Shiraz's Jews through the turbulent 20th century and was instrumental in maintaining the community's religious and cultural institutions during the Pahlavi era.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Mullah Yaqub Synagogue** was the oldest and most venerated of Shiraz's several synagogues, its foundation traced to medieval times. The **Karim Khan Zand Synagogue**, built or enlarged during the brief Zand dynasty when Shiraz was the de facto capital of Persia, reflected the community's status during that period. Multiple smaller synagogues served different neighborhoods of the Jewish quarter.

The Jewish cemetery on the outskirts of Shiraz contains graves going back many centuries, with inscriptions in Hebrew, Judeo-Persian, and Persian. The community also maintained a traditional mikveh and a rabbinic court that adjudicated matters of Jewish law.

The Alliance Israélite Universelle school, established in the 1890s, became the institutional center of modern Jewish education in Shiraz, coexisting somewhat uneasily with the traditional heder system but ultimately producing a generation of literate, professionally capable young Jews.

## Cultural Life

The wine culture of Shiraz had a peculiar Jewish dimension. While Islamic law prohibits wine, Jews as dhimmis were permitted to produce and consume it, and the famous Shirazi wine — which may be ancestral to the Syrah/Shiraz grape variety — was associated with Jewish producers in popular memory, though the historical evidence is complex. The convergence of wine, poetry, and divine intoxication themes in both Persian Sufi poetry and Jewish mysticism created a space of surprising cultural overlap.

Shirazi Jews spoke a Judeo-Persian dialect somewhat distinct from Isfahani or Tehrani variants, preserving archaic Persian vocabulary and distinctive Hebrew loanwords. Their liturgical traditions followed the Iranian Jewish rite but with local variations in prayer melodies and holiday customs. Nowruz celebrations were observed with Persian customs, while the spring festival was integrated into the rhythm of Passover preparation.

Jewish craftsmen in Shiraz were known for metalwork, particularly coppersmithing, and the city's carpet weaving and textile trades included Jewish participants.

## Decline & Transformation

The late 19th and early 20th centuries brought repeated crises. Anti-Jewish violence occurred periodically, driven by religious tensions and economic resentments. The 1910 pogrom in Shiraz, in which a false blood libel accusation triggered an attack on the Jewish quarter, killed or injured numerous community members and destroyed property. Alliance Israélite Universelle officials lobbied foreign governments for protection, and the incident drew international attention to the precarious status of Persian Jews.

The Pahlavi era brought formal equality and modernization, but many Shirazi Jews chose to migrate to Tehran, where economic opportunities were greater. The Islamic Revolution of 1979 accelerated the departure of the remaining community, and emigration to Israel and the United States reduced Shiraz's Jewish population from several thousand to a few hundred by the early 21st century.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Shirazi Jews in diaspora are concentrated in Israel (particularly in the southern cities and Tel Aviv) and in Los Angeles. They maintain a strong cultural identity, and the poetry of Shahin continues to be studied and celebrated as a crowning achievement of Persian Jewish civilization. Academic study of Judeo-Persian literature has given Shirazi Jewish culture wider recognition in the scholarly world.

The synagogues of Shiraz still stand, maintained by the tiny remaining community and occasionally visited by heritage tourists and researchers. The cemetery, with its centuries of inscribed stones, remains one of the most significant sites of Persian Jewish heritage.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Medieval period or earlier; strong presence from Safavid era; traditions reaching to Achaemenid times
- **Peak Population**: Approximately 8,000–10,000 (early 20th century)
- **Languages**: Judeo-Persian (Shirazi dialect), Hebrew, Persian
- **Status Today**: Tiny remnant community; majority of descendants in Israel and United States

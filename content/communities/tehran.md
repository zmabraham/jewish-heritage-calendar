# Jewish Community of Tehran

## Overview

Tehran became the undisputed center of Iranian Jewish life in the twentieth century, concentrating within its sprawling metropolis what had once been dispersed across dozens of provincial communities. When the Qajar dynasty elevated the modest city to the status of imperial capital in 1786, only a small Jewish community existed there. But as Tehran grew over the following two centuries into a city of millions, its Jewish population swelled correspondingly, drawing migrants from Isfahan, Shiraz, Hamadan, Yazd, and every other town in Persia where Jews had lived for generations. By the mid-twentieth century, Tehran hosted the largest and most modern Jewish community in Iran, with dozens of synagogues, schools, hospitals, and cultural organizations that represented the full spectrum of Jewish modernity.

The community's story is inseparable from the broader transformation of Iran itself. The Constitutional Revolution, the two Pahlavi dynasties, and the Islamic Revolution of 1979 each reshaped the lives of Tehran's Jews in profound ways. Under Mohammad Reza Shah, Tehran Jews reached unprecedented heights of professional integration and economic participation, with prominent families contributing to banking, medicine, law, and the arts. The revolution that ended the monarchy also ended this golden era, triggering a mass exodus that reduced the community from roughly 80,000 to a fraction of that number within a decade.

Yet Tehran still holds the largest remaining Jewish community in the Middle East outside Israel, a fact that reflects both the depth of Persian Jewish roots and the complex relationships that have persisted between Iranian Jews and the Islamic Republic.

## Historical Origins

Archaeological and documentary evidence for Jewish presence in the Tehran region is sparse before the Islamic period, when the area was overshadowed by the nearby city of Rayy (ancient Rhages), a major Silk Road center mentioned in the Book of Tobit. Jewish merchants and craftsmen lived in Rayy's shadow, and when Rayy was devastated by the Mongols in 1220, some of its population — including Jews — shifted toward the village of Tehran, then a minor settlement.

The first substantial documentation of a Tehran Jewish community dates to the Safavid period. As the dynasty's capital shifted between Qazvin and Isfahan, Tehran remained secondary, and its Jewish community was correspondingly modest. The real transformation came with the Qajar decision to make Tehran the capital. The city's rapid growth created economic opportunities that drew Jewish migrants from across Persia, and by the mid-nineteenth century a recognizable Jewish quarter had formed near the royal bazaar.

Jewish merchants in Qajar Tehran were active in the cloth trade, money-changing, and jewelry, occupations that put them in regular contact with the court and the merchant classes. Several Jewish bankers served the Qajar treasury, and Jewish physicians treated royal patients, establishing patterns of elite connection that would persist into the Pahlavi era.

## Golden Age

The Pahlavi period (1925–1979) constituted the golden age of Tehran's Jews. Reza Shah's modernizing program formally abolished the discriminatory jizya tax and other dhimmi restrictions, granting Jews equal legal status as Iranian citizens. His son, Mohammad Reza Shah, continued and deepened this policy, and the alliance between Israel and pre-revolutionary Iran created a warm climate for Jewish life.

Tehran's Jewish population grew from perhaps 20,000 in the 1920s to approximately 80,000 by the late 1970s. The community built impressive institutions: the **Ettefaq Hospital**, founded and operated by the Jewish community, became one of Tehran's finest medical facilities. Jewish schools offered modern education alongside Hebrew and religious instruction. The community supported multiple daily Minyan synagogues, a range of Zionist youth movements, and cultural organizations that published Hebrew and Persian-language Jewish periodicals.

Jewish entrepreneurs participated in the oil boom economy, and families like the Elghanian dynasty achieved national prominence in industry and philanthropy. Habib Elghanian, the community's most prominent leader, built business empires in plastics, real estate, and construction, earning the title of president of the Tehran Jewish community.

## Notable Figures

**Habib Elghanian** (1912–1979), industrialist and philanthropist, was the most prominent Jewish leader of 20th-century Iran. His execution by the Islamic Republic in May 1979, just months after the revolution, on charges of "corruption" and espionage, signaled to Tehran's Jews that their future in the new Iran was gravely uncertain and accelerated the mass exodus.

**Shams al-Din Muhammad ibn Mahmud** (medieval), an early Jewish scholar associated with the Tehran-Rayy region, contributed to the Judeo-Persian literary tradition that linked Iran's provincial communities.

**Lotfallah Hay** (20th century), a prominent communal rabbi and educator, helped modernize Jewish education in Tehran while maintaining traditional scholarship.

**Haroun Yashayaei** (contemporary), longtime leader of the Tehran Jewish community under the Islamic Republic, has navigated the delicate politics of minority life in post-revolutionary Iran, maintaining community institutions while working within the constraints of the new political order.

**Amnon Netzer** (1934–2009), scholar of Judeo-Persian language and literature, grew up in Tehran and became the foremost academic authority on the Iranian Jewish literary tradition, teaching for decades at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Tehran's synagogues reflected the diversity of the community's origins. The **Abrishami Synagogue** (also known as the Tehran Grand Synagogue) served as the community's central house of worship. The **Yusef Abad Synagogue** in an affluent northern neighborhood became symbolic of the community's prosperity. Dozens of smaller synagogues served specific ethnic and geographic subgroups — Isfahanis, Shirazi, Hamadanis — each maintaining their own liturgical customs.

The **Jewish Hospital (Ettefaq Hospital)** stood as the community's most visible philanthropic institution. Jewish schools included the **Cyrus High School**, the **Alliance Israélite Universelle** schools established in the late 19th century, and the **ORT** vocational schools. The **Tehran Jewish Cemetery** in the southern part of the city contains the graves of generations of community leaders and remains active.

## Cultural Life

Tehran's Jewish community in its 20th-century prime was linguistically complex: Hebrew for prayer and Zionist education, Persian as the language of daily life and culture, and remnants of Judeo-Persian dialects among older migrants from provincial towns. The city's Jews participated fully in Persian literary and artistic culture while maintaining distinctly Jewish celebrations.

Nowruz, the Persian New Year, was observed by Tehran Jews alongside Passover, with the two spring celebrations sometimes overlapping in an affectionate cultural synthesis. Jewish weddings combined Persian music — classical dastgah music and popular song — with Hebrew liturgical elements. The community produced writers, poets, and artists who contributed to both Persian and Hebrew-language cultural life.

## Decline & Transformation

The Islamic Revolution of 1979 transformed everything. While the new constitution formally recognized Jews as a protected religious minority with the right to a parliamentary representative, the practical climate deteriorated sharply. Habib Elghanian's execution, nationalization of Jewish-owned businesses, closure of Zionist organizations, and the broader atmosphere of suspicion drove a mass emigration. Of roughly 80,000 Jews in Tehran on the eve of the revolution, perhaps 10,000–15,000 remained by the 1990s.

The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988) brought additional hardships, as Jewish young men were conscripted into the Iranian military alongside their compatriots. A trickle of emigration continued throughout the 1980s and 1990s, and by the early 21st century the Tehran community had stabilized at roughly 8,000–10,000 people, still the largest Jewish community between Turkey and India.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Tehran Jews dispersed primarily to Israel, Los Angeles, New York, and London. The Los Angeles community — sometimes called "Tehrangeles" — became one of the most culturally vibrant Iranian Jewish communities in the diaspora, with its own synagogues, restaurants, media, and cultural organizations. Israeli communities of Iranian Jews, concentrated in cities like Tel Aviv, Holon, and Netanya, similarly maintained strong communal identities.

The remaining community in Tehran continues to operate synagogues, schools, and a Jewish hospital. The Tehran Jewish community still has a representative in the Iranian parliament (Majlis), a constitutional provision that has existed since 1906. The tensions and accommodations of Jewish life in the Islamic Republic remain a subject of international attention.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Significant community from early Qajar period, c. late 18th century; earlier presence in nearby Rayy
- **Peak Population**: Approximately 80,000 (late 1970s)
- **Languages**: Persian, Hebrew, Judeo-Persian dialects
- **Status Today**: Active community of approximately 8,000–10,000; largest Jewish community in the Muslim Middle East outside Israel

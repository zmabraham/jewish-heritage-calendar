# Gdańsk (Danzig)

## Overview

Gdańsk — known as Danzig to its German-speaking majority — was the great Baltic port city of the Hanseatic League tradition, a proud free city with a complex layered identity: linguistically and culturally German, politically oscillating between Polish sovereignty, Prussian rule, and the anomalous status of the Free City of Danzig (1920–1939) under the League of Nations. Its Jewish community was correspondingly distinctive: small compared to Polish Jewish cities, culturally German-Jewish rather than Yiddish-Polish, but historically significant and caught in the vise of Nazi ideology precisely because the Free City of Danzig became one of Hitler's first territorial demands. Danzig's Jewish community was largely dispersed before the Final Solution was implemented; those who remained faced destruction.

## Historical Origins

Jews lived in Danzig intermittently from the medieval period, subject to repeated expulsions. The city's merchant guilds fiercely resisted Jewish commercial competition, and Jews were frequently banned from residence within the city walls. They settled instead in suburban areas — primarily in the nearby village of Altschottland — from which they could trade in the city without residing there. A formal Jewish community gained more stable footing in the early eighteenth century, and by the Prussian period a resident Jewish community established itself in the city proper.

The community was always small and culturally German. Unlike the Yiddish-speaking masses of the Russian Pale of Settlement, Danzig Jews spoke German, attended German schools, and participated in German civic life. Emancipation under Prussia in 1812 brought full civic rights, and the community gradually integrated into the broader social fabric of German-Jewish Bildung (cultivation) culture.

## Golden Age

The nineteenth century represented Danzig Jewry's golden age in terms of civic integration and cultural achievement. The community built a significant synagogue, maintained schools and welfare organizations, and contributed to the city's commercial and professional life as merchants, lawyers, doctors, and academics. Jewish families were among the city's substantial bourgeoisie. The community maintained connections to Hamburg, Berlin, and other German-Jewish centers rather than to Warsaw or Vilna.

When Danzig became the Free City under the League of Nations after World War I, its Jews — approximately 7,000 in 1923 — found themselves in an unusual political position: citizens of a German-majority autonomous entity under international supervision. The Free City had its own parliament, constitution, and laws, and Jews initially had full civic rights.

## Notable Figures

**Arthur Schopenhauer** (1788–1860), the philosopher, was born in Danzig (to a family of Dutch-German origin with some Jewish ancestry according to some accounts, though this is disputed). **Aron Levy** (later Sir Aron Levy), a seventeenth-century court purveyor and merchant, typified the early modern Jewish commercial role. The community produced merchants, professionals, and businesspeople who contributed to Danzig's trading economy. **Fritz Stern** (1926–2016), the distinguished German-American historian, was born in Breslau of families connected to the German-Jewish bourgeoisie of which Danzig was part.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Great Synagogue of Danzig (built 1887) was a magnificent Neo-Romanesque structure in the Hanseatic tradition — one of the architectural jewels of Baltic Jewish architecture. It was demolished by the Nazis on November 12–13, 1938, during the Kristallnacht pogrom, one day after the nationwide destruction of German Jewish synagogues. The community also maintained a smaller, older synagogue, Jewish schools, a hospital, and welfare organizations oriented toward the German-Jewish tradition.

## Cultural Life

Danzig's Jewish culture was shaped by its German character. The community participated in German cultural life — theater, music, literature — and identified with German-Jewish institutions rather than the Polish-Jewish world. Zionism had a presence, particularly among younger members. The Reform movement was influential. The community was small enough that Jewish cultural life was more intimate and less institutionally diverse than in larger centers. The city's position as a major port connected its Jews to international trade networks.

## Decline & Transformation

The rise of the Nazi movement in Germany had immediate repercussions in Danzig, where the Nazis won a majority in the city's Senate in 1933. Anti-Jewish restrictions were imposed progressively, though the League of Nations' oversight prevented the most extreme measures initially. Jewish businesses were boycotted, Jews were expelled from professional positions, and violence increased. Most Danzig Jews emigrated between 1933 and 1939: to Germany (briefly), to Palestine, to the United States, and to South America. By September 1939, when Germany incorporated Danzig into the Reich, only a few hundred Jews remained. They were mostly deported to concentration camps. Very few Danzig Jews perished in the death camps — most had escaped in time.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Danzig's Jewish emigrants scattered primarily to Israel, the United States, and South America. The Danzig Jewish community produced the Danzig Torah Scroll Collection — a set of Torah scrolls saved from the Great Synagogue and donated to Yad Vashem and other institutions before its destruction. The memory of Danzig's Jewish community is preserved in the Leo Baeck Institute archives (dedicated to German-Jewish history) and in the records of survivors' organizations. Today Gdańsk has a tiny Jewish community; the site of the Great Synagogue has a memorial; the city's complex history is explored in the European Solidarity Centre and city museums.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Intermittent medieval presence; stable community from 18th century
- **Peak Population**: ~7,000 (1923); ~2,500 (1939)
- **Languages**: German, later Polish
- **Status Today**: Tiny community; Great Synagogue demolished 1938; memorial at site; city now part of Poland

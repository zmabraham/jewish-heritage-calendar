# Łomża

## Overview

Łomża is a city in northeastern Poland whose Jewish community combined the commercial vitality of a regional market town with an unusual distinction in the world of Lithuanian-style Torah learning. The Łomża Yeshiva, established in the late nineteenth century, became one of the premier institutions of Talmudic education in Poland — an island of classic Lithuanian mussar-inflected scholarship in a region where Hasidism dominated — and its graduates spread across the Jewish world, carrying Łomża's intellectual tradition to America, Palestine, and beyond.

## Historical Background

Jews settled in Łomża by the seventeenth century at the latest, and the community grew steadily through the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Łomża served as the administrative capital of the Łomża Governorate under Russian rule, and its Jewish population reached approximately 10,000 by 1900 — about half the city's total population. The northeastern Polish region of which Łomża was a center — the area around the Narew and Biebrza rivers — was culturally more closely aligned with Lithuanian than Galician Jewry: rationalist Talmudic scholarship, the influence of the Vilna Gaon, and the mussar movement of Rabbi Israel Salanter were stronger here than the mystical ecstasy of Hasidism.

## The Łomża Yeshiva

The Łomża Yeshiva (Yeshivat Lomzhe) was founded in 1883 by Rabbi Eliezer Shulevitz. It was established on the model of the great Lithuanian yeshivot — Volozhin, Slobodka, Telz — that had transformed Talmudic education in the nineteenth century into highly organized institutions with formal curricula, residential students, and rigorous examination. The Łomża Yeshiva incorporated the mussar movement's emphasis on ethical self-improvement and character development alongside rigorous Talmudic analysis.

Under its successive roshei yeshiva (deans), the Łomża Yeshiva gained a reputation throughout the Jewish world. Rabbi Yechiel Mordechai Gordon (1885–1964) led the yeshiva through much of the early twentieth century and, critically, survived the Holocaust: he had traveled to the United States before the war and reestablished the Łomża Yeshiva in America. The Yeshivat Lomzhe in Petach Tikva, Israel, is also a direct continuation of the Łomża tradition, founded by students and alumni who reached Palestine.

The yeshiva drew students from across northeastern Poland, Lithuania, and even from the Russian interior. Young men would spend years in Łomża, living in the yeshiva dormitory, studying ten to twelve hours daily, and absorbing the institution's distinctive combination of intellectual rigor and ethical seriousness. Graduates went on to become rabbis, teachers, and community leaders across the Jewish world.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Yechiel Mordechai Gordon, the long-serving rosh yeshiva, is the central figure of Łomża's yeshiva legacy. His leadership spanned decades; his ability to transplant the yeshiva's culture to America after the Holocaust demonstrated the resilience of the Torah institution as a form.

The Łomża community also produced Rabbi Moshe Gordon and several other significant rabbinic scholars. The town's earlier history includes rabbinic authorities who participated in the responsa literature of the Polish Talmudic tradition.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Great Synagogue of Łomża served the kehilla alongside multiple smaller prayer houses. The synagogue was destroyed during the war. The Jewish community maintained the full range of educational and charitable institutions, with the yeshiva as its crown jewel. Zionist organizations competed with the yeshiva world for the loyalties of young men, a tension characteristic of northeastern Polish Jewish life in the twentieth century.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Łomża's cultural position at the intersection of Lithuanian and Polish Jewry gave it a distinctive character. The mussar movement — with its insistence on regular self-examination, study of ethical texts, and cultivation of specific character traits (humility, honesty, diligence, and awe of heaven) — shaped the yeshiva's ethos and, through it, the broader sensibility of the community's educated class.

The non-yeshiva population of Łomża was subject to the same political and cultural forces as the rest of Polish Jewry: Zionism, Bundist labor organizing, Orthodox politics, and the attractions of secular modernization. The presence of the yeshiva gave the town a measure of prestige in the Orthodox world that colored its general reputation.

## The Holocaust in Łomża

Łomża was occupied first by Soviet forces in September 1939, then by Germany following Operation Barbarossa in June 1941. Under Soviet occupation, Jewish institutions including the yeshiva were closed, and some activists were arrested. German forces arrived in June 1941 and immediately began murdering Jews. A ghetto was established in the autumn of 1941. In November 1942, approximately 8,000 Jews from Łomża and the surrounding region were deported to Zambrów and from there to Auschwitz-Birkenau. The ghetto was liquidated; the Jewish community was destroyed.

## Legacy Today

The Łomża Yeshiva continues in two successor institutions: in Petach Tikva, Israel, and in the United States. These institutions keep alive the intellectual tradition of Łomża through their study programs and the chain of disciples descending from the original yeshiva's teachers. The Jewish cemetery in Łomża has been partially preserved. Memorial organizations of Łomża survivors in Israel compiled a Yizkor book.

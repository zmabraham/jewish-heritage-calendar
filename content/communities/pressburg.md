# Pressburg (Bratislava)

## Overview

Pressburg — known in Hebrew as Prespork and today as Bratislava, capital of Slovakia — occupies a singular position in Jewish history as the great fortress of Ashkenazic Orthodoxy. For much of the nineteenth century, this compact city on the Danube defined the boundaries of legitimate Jewish practice for millions of traditionalist Jews worldwide, largely through the towering influence of one rabbi: the Chatam Sofer.

## Historical Background

Jews lived in Pressburg from at least the thirteenth century. The medieval community suffered expulsions and readmissions typical of Central European Jewry, with a significant expulsion in 1526 following the Ottoman Battle of Mohács. Jews gradually resettled, and by the early eighteenth century, Pressburg had become one of the most important Jewish communities in the Habsburg lands.

Pressburg's strategic importance — it served as the capital of Habsburg Hungary when Budapest was under Ottoman control — meant its Jewish community interacted with the highest levels of imperial administration. The city was a coronation city for Hungarian kings, and Jews played roles as merchants and court suppliers. The community developed a distinctive character: learned, commercially active, and deeply rooted in tradition.

## Golden Age

The golden age of Pressburg Jewry is inseparable from the life and work of Rabbi Moses Sofer (1762–1839), universally known by the title of his collected responsa: the Chatam Sofer (Chiddushei Torah). Born in Frankfurt am Main, Sofer came to Pressburg in 1806 and remained its rabbi until his death, transforming the city into the intellectual capital of traditional Judaism.

The Chatam Sofer's genius was not merely halakhic but strategic. In the face of the Reform movement's growing influence — and what he saw as its dangerous accommodation of modernity — he articulated a bold counter-principle, drawing on a phrase from the Talmud: "Chadash assur min haTorah" — "the new is forbidden by the Torah." This slogan, taken from laws about new grain, became a rallying cry for Orthodoxy: innovation in religious practice was not merely inadvisable but categorically prohibited. This stance shaped the emerging Orthodox movement for generations.

## Notable Figures

The Chatam Sofer established a yeshiva in Pressburg that became the most prestigious in the Austro-Hungarian Empire, drawing students from across Europe and eventually from further afield. At its peak, over 500 students studied there simultaneously — an extraordinary number for the era. The yeshiva's graduates carried their teacher's uncompromising Orthodoxy to communities across Hungary, Galicia, and eventually America and Palestine.

Sofer's son Rabbi Abraham Samuel Benjamin Wolf Sofer (Ktav Sofer, 1815–1871) and grandson Rabbi Simcha Bunim Sofer (Shevet Sofer, 1842–1906) continued the dynasty, sustaining Pressburg's prestige as the pre-eminent Orthodox center in Central Europe across three generations.

Rabbi Akiva Eger (1761–1837), the Chatam Sofer's father-in-law, served in nearby Posen (Poznań) but his close connection to Pressburg reinforced the network of traditionalist leadership. Together these figures formed the intellectual backbone of what would become the Orthodox movement.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Chatam Sofer Yeshiva building and the great synagogue of Pressburg were centers of learning and prayer for over a century. When the Communist government of Czechoslovakia built a highway bridge through Pressburg in the 1940s, it required the destruction of the old Jewish cemetery. In a remarkable act of preservation, the Chatam Sofer's tomb — along with those of other great rabbis of Pressburg — was sealed within an underground bunker beneath the bridge approach, where it remains accessible to visitors today. This tomb-bunker has become one of the most visited Jewish pilgrimage sites in Central Europe.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Pressburg's Orthodox community established institutions that served as models for traditionalist communities across the Empire. The Chatam Sofer's responsa — thousands of them, addressing questions from across the Jewish world — constitute one of the most important halakhic works of the modern era. His rulings on technology, modernity, and religious change set parameters that Orthodox poskim (legal decisors) continue to grapple with.

The community also produced significant figures in secular culture. The philosopher Edmund Husserl had family connections to Pressburg. More notably, Pressburg was part of a broader Slovak and Hungarian Jewish intellectual culture that contributed to European thought.

## Decline & Dispersal

With the founding of Czechoslovakia in 1918, Pressburg became Bratislava, capital of Slovakia. The Jewish community, numbering roughly 15,000 in 1940, faced catastrophe under the wartime Slovak puppet state aligned with Nazi Germany. Deportations began in March 1942, making Slovakia one of the first countries to deport its Jews. The Working Group (Pracovná skupina), a clandestine rescue committee organized around Rabbi Michoel Ber Weissmandl and Gisi Fleischmann, undertook extraordinary efforts — including negotiations with SS officials — to halt deportations, with limited but real success.

## Legacy Today

Today's Bratislava has a small Jewish community of approximately 3,000 people. The underground Chatam Sofer burial site attracts pilgrims year-round, particularly on the yahrtzeit (death anniversary) of the Chatam Sofer and on Erev Rosh Hashanah. The reconstructed Orthodox legacy lives on most prominently in Brooklyn, Bnei Brak, and Jerusalem, where communities founded by Pressburg's émigrés and their descendants maintain the traditions the Chatam Sofer championed.

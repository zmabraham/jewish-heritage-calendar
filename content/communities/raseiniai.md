# Raseiniai

## Overview

Raseiniai (Yiddish: *Rosyan* or *Raseyn*; Russian: *Rossiyeny*) was a market town in western Lithuania, the administrative capital of Raseiniai County. Its Jewish community was one of the older in the region, established during the period of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, and maintained a consistent presence for several centuries. Raseiniai was not distinguished by a famous yeshiva or a great rabbinical dynasty — it was, in many ways, a typical Lithuanian Jewish town: commercially active, traditionally observant, engaged with the modernizing currents of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, and ultimately destroyed with devastating completeness in the early weeks of the German occupation in 1941.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Raseiniai in the seventeenth century, drawn by the town's role as a regional market center. The community was granted certain rights of settlement and commerce by Lithuanian nobility and later by Russian imperial authorities. Raseiniai served as the county (*apskritis*) capital in the period of Lithuanian independence (1918–1940), which gave the town some administrative significance. Jews were prominent in the town's commercial life — running shops, dealing in grain and livestock, and providing the crafts and services needed by the surrounding agricultural population.

## Golden Age

Raseiniai's nineteenth-century Jewish community was substantial, representing a large proportion of the town's total population. Community institutions were well-developed: a Great Synagogue, several smaller prayer houses, a *talmud torah* for poor children, a *yeshiva ketana* for adolescent boys, charitable organizations, and a cemetery. The community maintained connections to the major Lithuanian Jewish centers at Kaunas and Vilna for rabbinic guidance and cultural resources.

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries brought modernizing changes. A *Tarbut* Hebrew school opened, offering secular subjects alongside Hebrew language and Jewish studies. Zionist youth movements attracted young people. The Bund had some following among workers. The interwar period of Lithuanian independence gave Jews civic rights and cultural autonomy: Lithuanian law guaranteed minority rights, and the Jewish community maintained Hebrew-language schools and cultural institutions.

## Notable Figures

Raseiniai did not produce figures of international renown but contributed to the broader fabric of Lithuanian Jewry. Local rabbis served the community with distinction. Jewish emigrants from Raseiniai settled in South Africa, the United States, and Palestine, where some achieved professional or communal prominence. The community's contribution was collective rather than individual — the tens of thousands of ordinary Jewish lives shaped by Lithuanian Jewish culture and contributed to the broader tapestry of Ashkenazic civilization.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Great Synagogue of Raseiniai, a wooden structure typical of Lithuanian Jewish architecture, was the community's main house of worship. Several smaller *shtiblakh* and *batei midrash* served Hasidic and Mitnagdic congregations. The *cheder* provided elementary religious education to boys. In the interwar period, a Hebrew school, a library, various clubs, and communal welfare organizations rounded out the institutional landscape.

## Cultural Life

Raseiniai's Jewish cultural life evolved from strictly traditional patterns in the early modern period to include modern elements by the twentieth century. Yiddish was the vernacular; Hebrew was studied seriously; Lithuanian was adopted as an official language of instruction in the national school system. Political life was lively in the interwar period: Zionist parties, Agudath Israel, and the Bund competed for members. The Hebrew Gymnasium in Kaunas drew some of the brightest Raseiniai students for secondary education. Jewish youth moved between the town's traditional world and the modernizing currents of Lithuanian national culture.

## Decline & Transformation

Soviet occupation in 1940 brought nationalization of Jewish businesses and suppression of Jewish organizations. Community leaders were arrested or deported. When Germany invaded Lithuania in June 1941, violence against Jews began immediately. Lithuanian nationalist militia participated in killings alongside German forces. The Jews of Raseiniai were concentrated in June and July 1941, and the majority were murdered at sites near the town within weeks of the invasion. The speed of the killing was typical of the German campaign in Lithuania, which achieved near-total destruction of the Jewish population by the end of 1941.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Raseiniai's Jews who had emigrated before the war — primarily to South Africa, America, and Palestine — formed *landsmanshaftn* that documented the community's history and mourned its destruction. A memorial book (*yizkor bukh*) was published by Raseiniai survivors and emigrants. The killing sites near the town have been identified and marked with memorials. In contemporary Raseiniai, a small commemorative marker acknowledges the Jewish community that lived and perished there.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 17th century
- **Peak Population**: ~2,500–3,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Hebrew, Lithuanian
- **Status Today**: Community destroyed in summer 1941; memorial at killing site; no Jewish community remains

# Palma de Mallorca

## Overview

Palma de Mallorca, the capital of the Balearic island of Mallorca, is home to one of the most unusual and poignant communities in Jewish history: the Chuetas (or Xuetes), descendants of conversos who converted during the 1391 pogrom and subsequent decades, but who maintained a distinct identity for six centuries neither fully Jewish nor fully accepted as Christian. Living under the shadow of the Inquisition, marked by surname, neighborhood, and social stigma, the Chuetas were excluded from Mallorcan Christian society for centuries even as they no longer practiced Judaism. Their story is a meditation on identity, memory, and the long shadow of persecution.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Mallorca following its conquest by James I of Aragon in 1229, invited by the king to help populate and develop the newly conquered island. The community grew rapidly, with Jewish merchants participating in the island's maritime trade connecting the western Mediterranean.

Mallorca's Jewish community was organized in the Call (quarter) of Palma, centered around the present-day Carrer de la Portella. The community flourished in the thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries, producing scholars and, most remarkably, the finest tradition of Jewish cartography in the medieval world.

## Golden Age and Cartographic Achievement

Mallorca's Jewish community produced the Cresques family, whose contribution to the history of cartography is unmatched. Abraham Cresques (c. 1325–1387) and his son Jehuda Cresques were the preeminent mapmakers of the fourteenth century. Abraham composed the Catalan Atlas of 1375, a magnificent six-panel world map presented to the French king that combined the latest information from Arab geographers, Venetian merchants, and Portuguese explorers with the artistic traditions of Catalan illumination. It remains one of the most beautiful and informative maps of the medieval world.

Jehuda Cresques, after the 1391 pogrom, converted to Christianity and migrated to Portugal under the name Jaime Ribes — where he became the chief cartographer for Prince Henry the Navigator and played a foundational role in the Portuguese Age of Exploration. The Jewish geographic tradition of Mallorca thus flowed directly into the great voyages of the fifteenth century.

## The 1391 Pogrom and Aftermath

The 1391 pogroms that began in Seville reached Mallorca in August of that year. The attack on Palma's Call was devastating: hundreds of Jews were killed, and the vast majority of the surviving community converted to Christianity under duress. Of a community that had numbered in the thousands, perhaps three hundred families remained Jewish; these were expelled or converted in subsequent years.

The mass conversions of 1391 created a large New Christian population in Palma. Unlike on the mainland, this community remained geographically concentrated in the old Call neighborhood and maintained strong endogamous social networks — marrying almost exclusively among themselves, preserving distinct surnames, and occupying specific trades (particularly silver-smithing, which gave rise to one theory of the term "Chueta" from the word for pork, though the etymology is disputed).

## The Chuetas

The Chuetas were not crypto-Jews in the way that Belmonte's community was — by the seventeenth century, whatever Jewish practice they had maintained had largely disappeared. But they were not accepted as Old Christians either. The Mallorcan Inquisition conducted major trials of crypto-Jewish Chueta practices in 1675–76, burning thirteen people in autos de fe and imprisoning many more. Further persecutions followed in 1691.

What makes the Chueta story remarkable is the social persistence of their separate identity long after any religious distinction had vanished. The Catholic Church did not discriminate against the Chuetas in principle, yet Mallorcan society excluded them from guilds, confraternities, religious orders, and social life until the late eighteenth and even nineteenth century. They were required to live in the same streets (roughly the old Call), maintain the same trades, and marry among themselves — effectively a caste system enforced not by law but by social practice.

The fifteen Chueta surnames (surnames derived from converso families of the 1391 generation) were known to every Mallorcan: Aguiló, Bonnin, Cortès, Forteza, Fuster, Martí, Miró, Morey, Oliver, Picó, Pinya, Pomar, Segura, Tarongí, Valleriola, Valls. To bear one of these names in Mallorca was to carry a six-hundred-year stigma.

## Notable Figures

Abraham Cresques (c. 1325–1387) stands as the community's greatest figure. Described in documents as the "Master of Maps and Compasses," his Catalan Atlas represents the peak of medieval cartographic achievement and a monument to the Jewish contribution to European geography.

Among Chueta figures, Agustí Chueta — a generic reference to the community's social marginalization — lacks a single dominant figure; the Chuetas' history is collective rather than individual.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Call of Palma de Mallorca is one of the best-preserved medieval Jewish quarters in Spain. The present-day Carrer de la Portella, Carrer dels Jueus, and surrounding streets preserve the spatial organization of the medieval quarter. A heritage center has been established to document the Call's Jewish history. The Carrer dels Chuetes marks the area where converted Jewish families continued to live for centuries.

## Decline & Transformation

The 1967 encyclical and Vatican II declarations on Jewish-Christian relations helped begin a process of formal acknowledgment of Chueta full Christian status and social equality. By the late twentieth century, the formal social stigma had largely dissipated, though cultural memory persists. Some Chueta descendants in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries have sought to reconnect with Jewish identity, exploring conversion or Israeli citizenship.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Chueta story has become an important case study in the persistence of ethnic and religious stigma, the social effects of inquisitorial persecution, and the strange survivals of identity without content. For scholars of converso history and of social exclusion, Mallorca offers an extraordinarily well-documented example.

The Call of Palma is now a thriving heritage site, and Chueta identity has been reconsidered in contemporary Mallorca with new appreciation for its historical complexity.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Post-1229 conquest; Chueta identity crystallized from 1391 conversions
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 3,000–4,000 (14th century); Chueta population approximately 15,000–18,000 today
- **Languages**: Catalan, Hebrew (pre-1391)
- **Status Today**: Chueta community exists as cultural identity; Call preserved as heritage site

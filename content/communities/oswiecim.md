# Auschwitz (Oświęcim)

## Overview

Oświęcim — Auschwitz in German — is a name that has become synonymous with the worst crime in human history. The Nazi extermination complex built on the outskirts of this small Polish town was the site of approximately 1.1 million murders, the majority of them Jews. Yet Oświęcim was not always merely a place of death. Before the camp was built, before the trains arrived, before the world changed forever — Oświęcim was a living Jewish town with a community of approximately 8,000 Jews, an ancient kehilla with synagogues, schools, rabbis, market days, and all the ordinary and extraordinary textures of Jewish life. Recovering that history — the Jewish life of Oświęcim before it became Auschwitz — is an act of both historical justice and collective memory.

## Historical Background

Jews had lived in Oświęcim since at least the sixteenth century. The town, situated at the confluence of the Soła and Vistula rivers in western Galicia, was an important commercial hub. Its Jewish population grew steadily from the early modern period through the nineteenth century. Under Habsburg Austrian rule following the Partition of 1772, Oświęcim's Jews benefited from the Josephinian reforms that granted Jews greater legal equality. The community flourished in the nineteenth century as the town industrialized and developed commercial connections to Vienna, Cracow, and the broader Habsburg economy.

By 1900, Jews numbered approximately 4,000 out of a total population of around 12,000 — one-third of the town. By 1939, approximately 8,000 Jews lived in Oświęcim, comprising roughly half the population. This was a substantial, prosperous community by any measure.

## Jewish Life in Oświęcim

The Jewish quarter of Oświęcim clustered around the town's central market and the river. The Great Synagogue, built in 1900, was an imposing structure serving the larger community; the Chevra Lomdei Mishnayot synagogue served a different congregation. Multiple Hasidic shtiblekh of various affiliations added to the religious landscape. The town had a tradition of strong ties to the Belz and Bobov Hasidic dynasties.

Jewish life in Oświęcim encompassed the full range of activities characteristic of a prosperous Galician community. Merchants sold textiles and hardware; craftsmen worked leather, wood, and metal; professionals offered legal, medical, and banking services. The Haberfeld family's schnapps distillery, established in the eighteenth century, was one of the most famous Jewish businesses in Galicia, producing vodka and liqueurs that were sold across the Habsburg Empire and exported internationally. The distillery survived into the 1930s.

Jewish educational life in Oświęcim was similarly rich: cheders and a traditional yeshiva served the Orthodox community; a Hebrew school with secular curriculum served Zionist families; and a Beit Yaakov school for girls provided Orthodox women's education. Libraries, cultural clubs, and Zionist youth organizations added to the community's intellectual life.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Shimon Huberband (1909–1942) was born in the Oświęcim region and served as a rabbi in the Warsaw ghetto before his death at Treblinka. He was a member of the Oneg Shabbat archives project under Emanuel Ringelblum, contributing important testimony to the clandestine documentation of Holocaust events.

The Haberfeld distillery family is the most commercially prominent dynasty associated with Oświęcim. Max Haberfeld, who ran the distillery into the 1930s, was among the last Jews to flee before the German occupation; he reached Palestine and survived. The distillery building survived and was eventually restored as part of the Jewish Museum.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Great Synagogue of Oświęcim (1900) was damaged by the Germans but not completely destroyed; it was subsequently demolished during the Communist era. The Chevra Lomdei Mishnayot synagogue — a smaller, eighteenth-century structure — survived and has been restored. It is today the home of the Jewish Museum Auschwitz-Oświęcim, one of the few functioning Jewish museum institutions in post-Holocaust Poland operating in an original synagogue building.

The Jewish cemetery of Oświęcim, established in the early modern period, survives and contains tombstones from the seventeenth through twentieth centuries. It represents the oldest continuous physical trace of Jewish life in the town.

## The Holocaust

German forces occupied Oświęcim on September 4, 1939. Within days, violence against Jews began: beatings, forced labor, looting of businesses. The Great Synagogue was destroyed on November 29, 1939. Most of Oświęcim's Jews fled eastward during the first weeks of the occupation, dispersing to Cracow, eastern Galicia (then under Soviet control), and further east. Some reached Palestine or the United States before emigration became impossible. Those who remained were subjected to ghetto conditions and eventually murdered.

In May 1940 the SS established the Auschwitz concentration camp in the former Polish army barracks on the outskirts of the town. The camp's expansion into the Auschwitz-Birkenau extermination complex — the largest killing installation in history — transformed the town's surroundings into the geographic center of the Holocaust. The camp operated from 1940 until January 1945, when Soviet forces liberated it on January 27 — now commemorated worldwide as International Holocaust Remembrance Day.

## Legacy Today

The Jewish Museum Auschwitz-Oświęcim operates in the restored Chevra Lomdei Mishnayot synagogue at 13 Berka Joselewicza Street. The museum is dedicated to recovering and presenting the Jewish life of Oświęcim before the Holocaust, explicitly reclaiming the name and the place from its association exclusively with the camp. The Haberfeld distillery building has been restored and incorporated into Jewish heritage tours. The Jewish cemetery remains an active site of commemoration. Together, these sites represent one of the most important and poignant efforts in Polish Jewish heritage preservation: the insistence that Oświęcim was a place of life before it became a name for death.

# Kleck

## Overview

Kleck — Kletzk in Yiddish, Klyetsk in Belarusian — was a small town in western Belarus whose Jewish community was significant primarily because of the presence of the Kletsk Yeshiva, one of the notable Lithuanian-tradition yeshivot that played a major role in the transmission of Talmudic scholarship in the twentieth century. The town itself was a modest commercial and agricultural community, but its yeshiva attracted students from across the Jewish world and gave Kleck a distinction in Orthodox Jewish education that far exceeded its size.

## Historical Background

Kleck lies in the Minsk region of Belarus, historically part of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania and the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth before passing to Russian control in the late eighteenth century. Jews settled in Kleck in the early modern period as the community developed in a landscape of forests, marshes, and small agricultural towns. Under Russian rule in the Pale of Settlement, Kleck's Jewish population engaged in the typical economic activities of small Belarusian-Jewish towns: commerce with the surrounding peasantry, crafts, and small-scale manufacturing.

By the early twentieth century, Kleck's Jewish population was substantial relative to the town's total size; Jews comprised a large majority of the town's population, as was common in small Belarusian and Lithuanian towns. The community maintained synagogues, charitable societies, and educational institutions.

## The Kletsk Yeshiva

The Kletsk Yeshiva was established in 1903 by Rabbi Isser Zalman Meltzer (1870–1953), one of the great Talmudic scholars of the early twentieth century. Meltzer had studied at the famous Volozhin Yeshiva under Rabbi Chaim Soloveitchik and represented the highest level of Lithuanian Talmudic learning. He brought this tradition to Kleck, establishing a yeshiva that quickly gained a reputation throughout the Jewish world.

Under Rabbi Meltzer's leadership, the Kletsk Yeshiva offered a rigorous curriculum centered on Talmud, Rishonim (medieval commentators), and practical halakha (Jewish law). The yeshiva's approach drew on the analytical methods of the Brisker tradition — the highly precise, conceptual analysis of Talmudic categories pioneered by Rabbi Chaim Soloveitchik of Brisk — combined with the ethical and spiritual emphasis of the mussar movement.

In 1925, Rabbi Meltzer emigrated to Palestine, settling in Jerusalem where he became the rosh yeshiva of Etz Chaim and one of the central figures of the Old Yishuv's Torah world. Before leaving, he arranged for his son-in-law, Rabbi Aharon Kotler (1891–1962), to take over the Kletsk Yeshiva.

## Rabbi Aharon Kotler and His Legacy

Rabbi Aharon Kotler's tenure at the Kletsk Yeshiva (1925–1941) was the institution's most influential period. A brilliant Talmudist and charismatic leader, he developed the yeshiva into a major center of Torah learning, attracting students from Lithuania, Poland, and beyond. His teaching style combined the Brisker analytical method with an impassioned emphasis on Torah study as the highest form of divine service — a conviction that would animate his later career in America.

When the Germans threatened to invade in 1941, Rabbi Aharon Kotler fled, eventually reaching the United States. In 1943 he founded Beth Medrash Govoha in Lakewood, New Jersey, which became the largest yeshiva in the Western world — the direct institutional heir of the Kletsk tradition, transplanted to the New World. Beth Medrash Govoha today has thousands of full-time students and its influence on American Orthodox Judaism has been incalculable.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Isser Zalman Meltzer's Even HaAzel, a commentary on Maimonides' Mishneh Torah, remains a standard reference work in advanced Talmudic study. Rabbi Aharon Kotler's Mishnas Rabbi Aharon, a collection of his Torah discourses, is widely studied in yeshivot. Meltzer's daughter Raizel married Aharon Kotler; their family connection linked the two founders of the Kletsk tradition.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Beyond the yeshiva, Kleck maintained a Great Synagogue, various smaller prayer houses, a cheder system, and charitable organizations. The yeshiva functioned as a residential institution: students lived, ate, and studied in its buildings, supported by donations from Jewish communities across Eastern Europe and America. The yeshiva's presence gave the small town an international character, with students arriving from distant cities and countries.

## The Holocaust in Kleck

German forces occupied Kleck in late June 1941. In July 1941 the Germans conducted their first mass murder of Kleck's Jews. In October 1942, the remaining Jewish population — approximately 4,500 people — was rounded up and shot in pits outside the town in a single Aktion. A small group of young Jews, led by Yehuda Atlas (who became a partisan commander), attempted to resist and escape; some joined Soviet partisan units in the forests. This early armed resistance in Kleck is noted in Holocaust historiography as one of the first instances of organized Jewish armed response to Nazi murder. Virtually the entire Kleck community was annihilated.

## Legacy Today

Beth Medrash Govoha in Lakewood, New Jersey — now one of the world's largest yeshivot — is the living institutional legacy of the Kletsk tradition. The chain from Volozhin to Kleck to Lakewood represents one of the most important transmissions of Torah learning in modern Jewish history. A monument exists at the mass murder site in Kleck. The Kletsk Yizkor book preserves community memory.

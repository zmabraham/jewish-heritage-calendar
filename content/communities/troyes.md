# Troyes

## Overview

Troyes, the capital of the Champagne region in northeastern France, holds a place of supreme importance in Jewish intellectual history as the birthplace and lifelong home of Rabbi Shlomo ben Yitzchak (1040–1105), universally known by his acronym Rashi. Rashi's commentaries on the Torah and the Babylonian Talmud are the foundational texts of Ashkenazic Jewish learning — studied by every student of traditional Jewish texts from the elementary level through advanced yeshiva scholarship. Beyond Rashi, Troyes was the center of a flourishing tradition of northern French Jewish scholarship, continued by his sons-in-law and grandsons who developed the tradition of Tosafot (critical additions to Rashi's Talmudic commentary).

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Troyes was well established by the tenth century, fitting into the broader pattern of early medieval Ashkenazic Jewry that stretched from the Loire to the Rhine. The Jews of Champagne occupied a distinct economic niche: they participated in the famous Champagne Fairs — the great commercial gatherings at Troyes, Provins, Lagny, and Bar-sur-Aube that made Champagne the commercial hub of twelfth-century Europe — and served as moneylenders and traders. The counts of Champagne, pragmatic rulers who valued Jewish commercial expertise, provided a degree of protection that made northern France relatively hospitable for Jewish life in the eleventh and twelfth centuries.

## Golden Age

Rashi's presence transformed Troyes into the intellectual center of Ashkenazic Jewry. Rashi established an academy (yeshiva) in Troyes, and students came from across France and Germany to study with him. His commentary on the Torah, clear, concise, and pedagogically brilliant, became the standard text taught to Jewish children learning to read the Pentateuch — a role it has not relinquished in 900 years. His Talmud commentary, covering almost the entire Babylonian Talmud, provided the essential explanatory framework through which later generations have studied the Talmud.

Rashi's commentaries preserve earlier traditions, clarify obscure texts, cite Old French glosses (*la'azim*) for technical terms — making them a source of medieval French linguistic data — and reflect a legal and spiritual sensibility that has shaped Ashkenazic Judaism more than any other single work except the Talmud itself.

## Notable Figures

Rashi (1040–1105) is the defining figure. He studied in Worms and Mainz before returning to Troyes, where he spent most of his life. He supported himself as a vintner, ran his academy, maintained an extensive correspondence with other scholars, and composed his commentaries in a style of lapidary precision. He died in Troyes and was buried there.

His son-in-law Rabbi Meir ben Samuel and grandsons Rabbi Shmuel ben Meir (Rashbam, c. 1085–c. 1158) and Rabbi Jacob ben Meir (Rabbeinu Tam, c. 1100–1171) were leaders of the Tosafist movement, which applied dialectical analysis to Rashi's Talmud commentary and to the Talmudic text itself. The Tosafot, now printed flanking the Talmudic text on the opposite side from Rashi, represent the high point of medieval Ashkenazic legal analysis.

Rabbeinu Tam was not only a great Talmudist but an authority in halakha and a communal leader of all northern French and German Jewry. He presided over the Synod of Troyes and later the Synod of Sens (1150), which issued ordinances on community governance, marriage, and social ethics that governed Ashkenazic communities for generations.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Troyes supported a synagogue, a Jewish quarter (the *Juiverie*), a cemetery, and Rashi's academy. Archaeological remains of the medieval Jewish quarter have been identified, and the street names of the old city still reflect Jewish habitation patterns. A synagogue stood on what is now the rue de Vauluisant.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The culture of Talmudic scholarship that Rashi established in Troyes created the model for Ashkenazic yeshiva education that persists to this day. The pattern of learning — close textual reading, the interplay of Rashi and Tosafot, the cultivation of sharp dialectical argument — was institutionalized and transmitted through hundreds of subsequent generations of scholars.

Rashi's Old French glosses (*la'azim*) — over 3,000 of them — are a unique source for medieval French vocabulary and constitute an important contribution to Romance linguistics independent of their Jewish context.

## Decline & Dispersal

The comfortable position of northern French Jews began to deteriorate in the thirteenth century under Louis IX (Saint Louis), who was hostile to Jewish moneylending and enthusiastic about converting Jews. The deterioration culminated in the expulsion of Jews from France in 1306 (reversed in 1315), 1322, and finally definitively in 1394. The communities of Champagne, including Troyes, were dispersed eastward into Germany and southward toward Provence. The intellectual tradition of the Tosafists, however, was carried to Germany, where it was absorbed and continued.

## Legacy Today

Troyes commemorates its Jewish heritage with a Rashi museum (Musée Rachi) opened in a medieval building near the site of the Jewish quarter. A square in the city is named for Rashi. The Rashi Synagogue in Prague bears his name. More profoundly, Rashi's legacy is present in every beit midrash in the world where the Torah and Talmud are studied — which is to say, everywhere traditional Jewish learning is practiced.

# Porto

## Overview

Porto, Portugal's second city on the mouth of the Douro River, carries a distinctive Jewish story marked by the survival of Crypto-Jewish (Marrano) traditions in the northern Portuguese interior and a remarkable twentieth-century revival of open Jewish life. While the medieval community was not among the largest of the Iberian Peninsula, Porto became important both as a center of the forced converso community after 1497 and as the gateway through which the rediscovery of crypto-Jewish communities in the surrounding region reached the wider world in the early twentieth century.

## Historical Origins

Jews were present in Porto (Portucale) from at least the early medieval period, growing in number under the county and then kingdom of Portugal. The community's location near the Atlantic coast made it commercially significant in the wine and cloth trades. The Douro Valley's wine trade — the foundation of what would become the port wine industry — involved Jewish merchants as participants in its early development.

By the fourteenth century, Porto had an organized aljama with its own institutions and a recognized Jewish quarter. The community was smaller than Lisbon's but well-established, serving as an important regional center for the Jewish population of northern Portugal.

## Golden Age

Porto's Jewish community reached its peak in the late fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries. The city's commercial activity, its position as a hub for Atlantic-coast trade, and its relatively stable political environment under the Portuguese crown created favorable conditions. Jewish merchants traded in wool, hides, and other commodities. Jewish financiers provided credit to local merchants and to the crown.

The community maintained synagogues and schools, operated under the governance of its own aljama, and contributed to the fiscal and commercial life of the region. Though Porto never became a center of Torah scholarship on the level of Lisbon or Évora, its community was socially coherent and economically significant.

## Notable Figures

Porto does not produce the kind of individual intellectual giants associated with Toledo or Córdoba, but its community contributed capable administrators and merchants to Portuguese Jewish life. After the forced conversion of 1497, the most significant figures from Porto's Jewish heritage are the crypto-Jewish families who maintained hidden Jewish identity in the Douro Valley for centuries.

Samuel Schwarz (1880–1953), a Polish-born Jewish mining engineer working in Portugal in the early twentieth century, is arguably the most consequential figure in Porto's modern Jewish story. Working in the Beiras region near Belmonte, he discovered communities of secret Jews who had maintained identifiable Jewish practices — a modified Hebrew prayer, dietary laws, Sabbath and festival observances — for four hundred years without contact with open Jewish life. His 1925 book Os Cristãos-Novos em Portugal no Século XX (The New Christians in Portugal in the 20th Century) brought the crypto-Jewish communities of northern Portugal to international attention, initiating a process of gradual reconnection with Jewish identity that culminates in the present century.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Porto's most significant Jewish institution is the Comunidade Israelita do Porto, established in 1923 through the efforts of Captain Artur de Barros Basto, a converso descendant who himself returned to open Judaism. Known as the "Captain of the Marranos," Barros Basto worked tirelessly to bring crypto-Jewish families in northern Portugal back to open Jewish life, organizing circumcisions and religious education. He built the Kadoorie Mekor Haim synagogue, inaugurated in 1938 — the largest synagogue on the Iberian Peninsula — as a permanent monument to the revival of Portuguese Jewish life. The synagogue was funded partly by the Iraqi-Jewish Kadoorie family and remains one of the finest examples of Art Deco synagogue architecture in Europe.

## Cultural Life

Porto's Jewish culture in the modern revival period has been shaped by the encounter between diaspora Jews and the descendants of crypto-Jewish families. The community is genuinely hybrid: some members are from established European Jewish families, others are descendants of Marranos returning to open Judaism, and others are immigrants from Israel, Brazil, and elsewhere.

The crypto-Jewish culture itself — preserved in isolated villages of the Trás-os-Montes and Beiras regions — represents a fascinating example of religious memory under extreme pressure. Women carried the tradition, maintaining modified prayers (often in Portuguese rather than Hebrew), food customs, and holiday observances that their families maintained were merely "old family traditions" for generations.

## Decline & Transformation

The forced conversion of 1497 and the Lisbon massacre of 1506 ended public Jewish life throughout Portugal, including Porto. The Inquisition, established in Portugal in 1536, pursued conversos suspected of Judaizing for the next two and a half centuries. Porto's converso families either fully assimilated, fled to Amsterdam or Hamburg or Brazil, or retreated into the crypto-Jewish existence documented by Schwarz in the twentieth century.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Porto's most enduring contribution to Jewish history may be the survival story it represents. The crypto-Jewish communities of northern Portugal, connected to Porto through Barros Basto's revival movement, demonstrate the extraordinary persistence of Jewish identity under conditions of forcible suppression. Thousands of descendants of these communities have sought Portuguese citizenship under the 2013 Sephardic law. Porto's Kadoorie synagogue continues to serve a small but active community and draws Jewish visitors from around the world.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Early medieval; established aljama by 14th century
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 1,500–2,500 (15th century); modern community of 100–200
- **Languages**: Portuguese, Hebrew; crypto-Jewish prayers in modified Portuguese
- **Status Today**: Small active community; Kadoorie Mekor Haim synagogue (1938) operational

# Kalisz

## Overview

Kalisz is one of the oldest cities in Poland and the site of one of the most consequential moments in medieval Jewish history: the granting of the **Statute of Kalisz** in 1264 by Prince Bolesław Pious of Greater Poland, the first legal charter guaranteeing the rights of Jews in Polish territory. This single document opened Poland to Jewish settlement on an unprecedented scale and set the conditions for the flourishing of Ashkenazic civilization over the next seven centuries. Kalisz thus stands at the very foundation of Polish Jewish history.

## Historical Background

Jews were present in Kalisz — one of the oldest Polish cities, mentioned in the Ptolemy's geography of the second century CE — from at least the thirteenth century. The city was a significant commercial center on the amber trade route connecting the Baltic to the Mediterranean. Jewish merchants participated in this trade and in the broader commerce of the region.

In 1264, **Prince Bolesław Pious** (Bolesław V of Greater Poland, c. 1224–1279) issued the *Statut Kaliski* (Statute of Kalisz). This charter granted Jews in his territory a series of protections unprecedented in medieval Europe: the right to engage in money-lending, freedom of movement, protection of synagogues and cemeteries, the right to be tried by Jewish courts in inter-Jewish disputes, protection from blood libel accusations, and severe penalties for attacks on Jews. The statute explicitly rejected the ritual murder accusation — a significant moral statement for a thirteenth-century Christian ruler.

The Statute of Kalisz was confirmed and extended by subsequent Polish kings — Casimir the Great in 1334, later Jagiellonian rulers — and became the foundational legal framework for Jewish life in Poland. It is credited as a primary reason why Poland became, over the following centuries, home to the world's largest Jewish community.

## Golden Age

Kalisz developed a substantial Jewish community through the medieval and early modern periods. By the late sixteenth century, Kalisz was a significant kehilla in the constellation of Greater Poland's Jewish communities, with representation in the Council of Four Lands, the governing body of Polish Jewry.

The city's Jewish community was involved in trade, finance, and crafts. Kalisz's position on trade routes connecting Silesia, the German lands, and the Polish interior made it a natural commercial center. Jewish merchants participated in the cloth trade, money-lending, and the commerce that accompanied the major fairs held in the region. The community maintained a synagogue, a yeshiva, and the standard institutions of a major kehilla.

In the nineteenth century Kalisz, as part of the Congress Kingdom under Russian rule, developed as a textile manufacturing center. The Jewish community adapted to this industrializing economy, with Jewish manufacturers and merchants playing significant roles in the textile trade. By the early twentieth century, Kalisz had approximately 15,000–18,000 Jews, representing about 30 percent of the total population.

## Notable Figures

- **Prince Bolesław Pious (c. 1224–1279)**: The Polish ruler whose 1264 charter created the legal foundation for Jewish life in Poland. Not himself Jewish, he is remembered in Jewish history as an extraordinary protector.
- **Rabbi Meir of Kalisz (Maharam of Kalisz)**: A medieval rabbinic authority associated with the city.
- **Amos Gilboa (Glücksmann)**: Contemporary Israeli figure with Kalisz roots.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Kalisz maintained a major synagogue — the Great Synagogue, rebuilt several times over the centuries — along with multiple prayer houses and a *beit midrash*. The city had a traditional yeshiva and, in the modern period, a network of Jewish schools encompassing traditional and modern educational approaches. A Jewish hospital, *chevra kadisha*, and welfare institutions served community needs.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Beyond the Statute of Kalisz, the city contributed to Polish Jewry through its role as a commercial and cultural hub of Greater Poland. Its Jewish community produced rabbis, merchants, and scholars who participated in the broader life of Polish Jewry. The city's Hebrew printing press, operating in the early modern period, contributed to the dissemination of Jewish texts.

The Statute itself remains Kalisz's most enduring contribution: it established that Jewish difference — in faith, practice, and communal organization — was compatible with civil protection and civic peace. This principle, extended and violated in turn across seven centuries of Polish Jewish history, ultimately defined the Polish Jewish experience.

## Decline & Dispersal

Kalisz suffered enormously in World War I, when German forces bombarded and largely destroyed the city in August 1914 in one of the war's first urban atrocities. The Jewish community, along with the rest of Kalisz's population, was displaced and had to rebuild.

In September 1939 German forces again entered Kalisz. The city — close to the German border and quickly incorporated into the Reich as part of the Reichsgau Wartheland — saw some of the earliest and most brutal anti-Jewish actions of the occupation. The Jewish community was expelled, dispersed, and ultimately murdered in the ghettos and death camps of occupied Poland.

## Legacy Today

The Statute of Kalisz is commemorated at Yad Vashem and in the history of Jewish-Polish relations. In Kalisz, a memorial marks the former Jewish quarter. A small Jewish cultural museum documents the community's history. The city has made efforts in recent decades to acknowledge and commemorate its Jewish heritage, including events marking the anniversary of the 1264 statute.

# Rehovot

## Overview

Rehovot — named for the city of Rehoboth in Genesis 26:22, where Isaac dug a well and "there was room enough" (rehovot means "wide spaces" or "room") — was founded in 1890 as one of the early Zionist agricultural colonies of the First Aliyah period. It became most famous as the home of the Weizmann Institute of Science, established in 1934 and named for Chaim Weizmann, Israel's first president and one of the great chemists of the twentieth century. The institute has become one of the foremost scientific research centers in the world, making Rehovot the intellectual scientific capital of the Jewish state.

The combination of agrarian origins and scientific achievement gives Rehovot a distinctive character in the Zionist narrative: the orange groves and vineyards of the early settlers making way for the particle accelerators and biochemistry laboratories of their successors. But the continuity is real — both the orange growers and the physicists were engaged in the project of demonstrating that Jewish productivity and creativity could flourish in the ancient homeland.

## Historical Origins

Rehovot was founded in 1890 by a group of Warsaw Jews — members of the Hovevei Zion movement — who purchased land from Arab landowners in the coastal plain south of Jaffa and established an agricultural colony. Unlike some other early settlements, Rehovot was founded by settlers who insisted on economic independence from the beginning — they specifically declined Baron Edmond de Rothschild's patronage (accepted by Rishon LeZion and others) in order to maintain their autonomy. This decision to "go it alone" was both principled and difficult.

The founders planted vineyards and orange groves on the sandy soil. The choice of Rehovot as a name — with its biblical resonance of "there was room" — reflected both the physical reality of the open coastal plain and the founders' sense that they had found space for Jewish life after the cramped poverty of the Russian Pale of Settlement.

## Golden Age

Rehovot's golden age as a scientific center begins with Chaim Weizmann's connection to the city. Weizmann, the Zionist statesman and brilliant organic chemist (his process for producing acetone by bacterial fermentation was crucial to the British war effort in World War I), had a deep personal attachment to the Land of Israel and chose to build his home and institute in Rehovot. The Daniel Sieff Research Institute, established in 1934 with British Jewish philanthropy, was the seed from which the Weizmann Institute grew after independence.

The Weizmann Institute expanded dramatically after 1948, attracting scientists from around the world — many of them Holocaust survivors with destroyed European university careers, or brilliant young Israelis seeking a world-class research environment at home. The Institute's eight faculties cover biology, biochemistry, chemistry, computer science, mathematics, and physics. Its alumni have made fundamental contributions in a remarkable range of fields.

## Notable Figures

**Chaim Weizmann (1874–1952)**, Israel's first president and the guiding spirit of the institute that bears his name, was born in Motol, Russia, and educated in Berlin and Freiburg. His scientific career centered on organic chemistry; his political career was devoted to Zionism, most consequentially in his relationship with the British government during World War I and the negotiations leading to the Balfour Declaration (1917). He made Rehovot his home and is buried in the garden of his home there.

**Vera Weizmann (née Chatzman, 1881–1966)**, Chaim's wife and partner, was deeply involved in the social and cultural life of Rehovot and the institute. Her memoir and the Weizmann House (now preserved as a heritage site) provide a vivid picture of life at the center of Zionist scientific ambition.

**Ernst David Bergmann (1903–1975)**, a German-Jewish chemist who escaped Nazi Germany and joined the Sieff Institute in 1934, became the founding director of Israel's nuclear research program and was one of the key figures in building Israel's scientific-military capability. His work at Rehovot straddled fundamental research and national security.

**Ada Yonath (born 1939)**, biochemist and 2009 Nobel Prize laureate in Chemistry (for studies of the structure and function of the ribosome), conducted her path-breaking crystallographic research at the Weizmann Institute. She is the first Israeli woman to win a Nobel Prize.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Weizmann Institute of Science** is the city's defining institution — a campus of laboratories, libraries, and lecture halls spread across a beautifully landscaped site in the center of Rehovot. The **Weizmann House**, where Chaim and Vera Weizmann lived, is preserved as a heritage museum, its modernist architecture by Erich Mendelsohn (1936) being one of the finest examples of International Style architecture in Israel.

The **grave of Chaim Weizmann** in the garden of his house on the institute campus is a site of national pilgrimage. The **Clore Garden of Science** (science museum for visitors) and the **Yeda Research and Development Company** (the institute's commercial arm, responsible for hundreds of patents) are integral parts of the Weizmann ecosystem.

The **Agricultural Research Organization** (Volcani Center), adjacent to Rehovot, is Israel's primary agricultural research institution, continuing the practical agricultural tradition that the founders of Rehovot began in their orange groves.

## Cultural Life

Rehovot's Jewish culture has always had a pronounced intellectual character shaped by its scientific institutions. The presence of world-class researchers from multiple countries gave the city a cosmopolitan atmosphere unusual for an Israeli city of its size. Scientific conferences, public lectures, and the annual Weizmann Institute science festival draw audiences from across Israel.

The older moshava neighborhood of Rehovot, with its orange-grove heritage, is now an attractive residential area with century-old buildings alongside more recent construction. The **Rehovot Wine Cellar** tradition, following the pattern of other early Zionist settlements, gave the city a wine-producing character in its early decades.

## Decline & Transformation

Rehovot has not declined but has continuously transformed. The orange groves of the founding generation are entirely gone, replaced by urban development. The city grew from a few hundred settlers to a city of 150,000. Its transformation from agricultural colony to science city to modern metropolitan suburb is one of the most complete in Israeli urban history.

The relationship between the Weizmann Institute and the city of Rehovot has always been somewhat asymmetrical — the institute operates on a global scale while the city has remained relatively modest in size. But the pride of being "the city of the Weizmann Institute" is deeply embedded in Rehovot's identity.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Rehovot's legacy is Weizmann Institute science — Nobel Prizes, fundamental discoveries in molecular biology and physics, the training of generations of Israeli scientists, and the demonstration that Jewish intellectual creativity, transplanted to the ancient homeland, could flourish at the highest level of world science. Chaim Weizmann himself is buried there, his grave a point of national memory. The city's founding as an independent colony — rejecting Rothschild patronage — also left a legacy of self-reliance that shaped the character of subsequent Zionist agricultural and economic development.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: 1890
- **Peak Population**: 150,000+ (contemporary)
- **Languages**: Hebrew (dominant), Yiddish, Russian, Polish, German (scientific community)
- **Status Today**: Major Israeli city; home of the Weizmann Institute; central scientific hub of Israel

# Antwerp

## Overview

Antwerp's Jewish community is inseparable from the global diamond trade — a relationship so deep that the city's diamond district remains today one of the most distinctively Jewish commercial spaces in Europe. But Antwerp's Jewish history is richer and more layered than diamonds alone: it encompassed Sephardic merchants who arrived in the sixteenth century, Ashkenazic refugees from Eastern Europe who transformed the community in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, and a vibrant Hasidic life that gave the community its distinctive Orthodox character.

## Historical Origins

The first Jews in Antwerp were conversos and crypto-Jews from Portugal and Spain who arrived in the early sixteenth century, when Antwerp was the commercial capital of northern Europe. These New Christians operated openly as merchants while some maintained Jewish practices in secret. The Antwerp Portuguese community served as a crucial node in the international spice, sugar, and diamond trade. Their presence was tolerated precisely because their commercial networks were indispensable to Antwerp's prosperity.

The Dutch revolt against Spanish rule (1568–1648) and the subsequent decline of Antwerp's commercial dominance sent many Jewish merchants northward to Amsterdam, which became the new center of Sephardic Jewish trade. Antwerp's Jewish community shrank dramatically in the seventeenth century.

The modern community was effectively refounded in the early nineteenth century, after Belgian independence (1830) established legal equality for all religious groups. Ashkenazic Jews from the Netherlands, Germany, and eventually from Eastern Europe began settling in Antwerp in significant numbers from the 1840s onward.

## Golden Age

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw Antwerp's Jewish community reach its peak vitality. The diamond industry was the central axis. Jews had been involved in the diamond trade since the sixteenth century, but Antwerp's emergence as the world's premier diamond center after the discovery of South African diamonds in the 1870s brought a massive influx of Jewish cutters, polishers, merchants, and brokers. By 1900, approximately 80 percent of the world's diamonds passed through Antwerp, and Jews dominated every stage of that industry.

The Hoveniersstraat and surrounding streets of the diamond quarter became a world unto themselves — a place where business was conducted in Yiddish, where a handshake sealed million-dollar deals, where Orthodox synagogues stood adjacent to diamond bourses, and where the rhythms of the Jewish calendar organized the commercial week. This culture survived two world wars and persists to a remarkable degree today.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Chaim Soloveitchik's influence was felt through the Lithuanian yeshiva world that shaped Antwerp's Orthodox rabbinate. The Machzikei Hadas community, strongly Hasidic in orientation, produced and attracted leading rabbinic figures throughout the twentieth century.

Antwerp was a city of merchants and craftsmen rather than literary or philosophical stars, but its diamond dealers became legends in their own world — figures like the Oppenheimer and Steinmetz families whose names became synonymous with the global gem trade. The city's Orthodox community, particularly the Belzer and Satmar Hasidic groups, had major presence in Antwerp.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Antwerp's Jewish quarter, centered on the streets near the Central Station, contains a remarkable concentration of Jewish institutions. The Shomre Hadas synagogue and the Great Synagogue on Bouwmeestersstraat represent the community's architectural confidence. The Machzikei Hadas, founded in 1892, became the leading Orthodox institution and is today one of the most active Orthodox communities in Western Europe.

The community maintained Jewish schools, a hospital, old-age homes, and a network of charitable organizations. The diamond industry's bourse — the Diamantclub van Antwerpen — was closely intertwined with Jewish communal life.

## Cultural Life

Antwerp's Jewish culture was shaped by its working-class and artisan character. The diamond trade created a large community of skilled workers — cutters and polishers — alongside the merchants and brokers. Yiddish theater and press flourished. The community was politically diverse, encompassing Orthodox traditionalists, Bundist labor organizers, and Zionists of various stripes.

Between the wars, Antwerp became an important transit point for Eastern European Jewish emigrants heading to the Americas. The city's Jewish community had to manage a constant flow of newcomers while maintaining its own institutional coherence.

## Decline & Transformation

The German occupation of Belgium in May 1940 brought the Holocaust to Antwerp. Unlike in the Netherlands, Belgium's administration showed more resistance to German demands, and significant numbers of Antwerp Jews were hidden by Belgian non-Jews. Nevertheless, approximately 65 percent of Belgian Jews — including a large proportion of Antwerp's community — were deported and murdered. The Dossin barracks in nearby Mechelen served as the transit camp from which Belgian Jews were sent to Auschwitz. Of approximately 55,000–65,000 Jews in Belgium in 1940, around 25,000 were murdered.

Antwerp's community was devastated but not destroyed. Unlike German or Polish communities, it recovered with relative speed, bolstered by survivors, by Jews who had escaped, and by new immigration.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Antwerp today has one of the most notable Jewish communities in Western Europe — numbering approximately 15,000–20,000, a large proportion of them ultra-Orthodox. The diamond district remains a center of Jewish commercial life, though competition from India and China has transformed the industry. The Hasidic community, including large Belzer and Satmar populations, gives the neighborhood a character unlike any other Western European city — one can walk through streets where Yiddish is the first language, where children in peyot play in courtyards, and where the smell of cholent drifts from apartment windows on Friday afternoon.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 16th century (converso presence); modern community from c. 1830
- **Peak Population**: ~55,000 (c. 1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Flemish/Dutch, French, Hebrew
- **Status Today**: Active community of ~15,000–20,000; globally significant diamond trade community

# Jericho

## Overview

Jericho holds the distinction of being one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on earth — a remarkable fact for a city that, in the biblical narrative, was destroyed by Joshua's army at the beginning of the Israelite conquest of Canaan. The great tell of ancient Jericho (Tell es-Sultan) shows occupation layers stretching back ten thousand years, and the natural conditions that made it so ancient — a copious spring (Ein es-Sultan), a warm winter climate, and a fertile oasis surrounded by desert — are the same conditions that attracted human settlement in every era.

For Jews, Jericho carries a complex significance. It is the first city conquered in the Land of Canaan, the site of one of the most famous miracles in the Hebrew Bible (the falling walls), and a city associated with Elijah's ascent to heaven (2 Kings 2 places the prophet's departure near Jericho and the Jordan). In the Second Temple period, Jericho was a royal winter resort — Hasmonean and Herodian palaces have been excavated nearby — and a significant Jewish city. In the rabbinic period, it appears in multiple Talmudic discussions as a place with distinctive practices and problems.

## Historical Origins

The fall of Jericho's walls as described in the book of Joshua (chapters 2–6) — the spies hiding with Rahab, the seven days of circling with the Ark, the shofar blast, and the miraculous collapse — is one of the Hebrew Bible's most vivid narratives. Whether understood literally or symbolically, it marks Jericho as the threshold of Jewish history in the Land: the first place where the Israelites exercised sovereignty, the first city they encountered on their long journey from Egypt.

The archaeological evidence for the biblical conquest of Jericho is complex and debated, but the city's Jewish character in the Second Temple period is abundantly documented. Hasmonean rulers built their winter palaces at Jericho, taking advantage of the warm climate. Herod the Great constructed an elaborate palace complex near the ancient city, complete with swimming pools, gardens, and a hippodrome. The Balsam groves of Jericho were among the most valuable agricultural assets in the ancient world, and the city's production of dates and balsam made it wealthy.

## Golden Age

Jericho's Second Temple period (roughly 2nd century BCE–1st century CE) was its Jewish golden age. Under Hasmonean and then Herodian rule, the oasis supported a substantial and prosperous Jewish population. The presence of royal palaces brought court officials, craftsmen, and merchants; the balsam trade brought commercial activity; and the location near the Jordan River — the ritual boundary of the Land of Israel — gave Jericho special symbolic importance.

The Jewish community at the nearby site of Qumran, identified by most scholars as the home of the community that produced the Dead Sea Scrolls, was part of Jericho's broader Jewish landscape. The Essenes (if that is who they were) withdrew to the desert near Jericho to live their austere communal life; their extraordinary library, hidden in cave jars near the Dead Sea, was preserved by the desert's dryness for two thousand years.

## Notable Figures

**Zacchaeus** (1st century CE), mentioned in the Gospel of Luke as a tax collector of Jericho who hosted Jesus, provides a glimpse of the city's prosperous Jewish society — the tax collectors of Jericho, collecting duties on the balsam trade, were wealthy and somewhat controversial figures.

**Rabbi Eliezer ben Jacob (Tannaic period, 1st–2nd century CE)**, a sage who is recorded as having specific knowledge of the Temple's arrangements, is associated in some traditions with the Jericho region.

**Rahab (biblical figure)**, the innkeeper whose family was spared when Jericho fell, is remembered in rabbinic tradition as an ancestor of prophets and priests — her conversion to Israelite faith making her an early model of the righteous gentile.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

**Elisha's Spring (Ein es-Sultan)** — the spring whose waters Elisha purified (2 Kings 2:19–22), the water source that has sustained Jericho since prehistoric times — is one of the most visited natural sites in the Jordan Valley. The spring's association with Elisha gives it a specifically Jewish sacred significance.

The **Hasmonean and Herodian palace complexes** at Tulul Abu el-Alayiq, excavated by Ehud Netzer, are spectacular Roman-period remains: swimming pools, columned halls, and gardens in the desert. The **synagogue of Jericho** (Na'aran Synagogue), discovered north of the ancient city, features a mosaic floor with a zodiac wheel and biblical inscriptions dating to the Byzantine period — evidence of a surviving Jewish community long after the Temple's destruction.

**Qumran**, a short distance to the south along the Dead Sea shore, is not Jericho itself but belongs to its Jewish landscape. The site, its caves, and the Dead Sea Scrolls found therein represent perhaps the most extraordinary literary discovery of the twentieth century, providing direct access to the texts of a Jewish community living at the time of the Second Temple's final decades.

## Cultural Life

Jericho's Jewish culture in its golden age was shaped by its oasis character and royal presence. The balsam industry was a luxury trade — the resin was used in perfumes, medicines, and embalming, and was exported to Rome and Egypt. The date palms of Jericho produced another luxury product. Jewish farmers and merchants in this prosperous oasis city lived in relative comfort by the standards of the ancient world.

Talmudic discussions mention Jericho in connection with specific practices: the town's synagogue is noted for a custom of reading the Torah differently than the standard practice; its residents had unusual agricultural customs; and the city's proximity to the Jordan River made it a site for ritual immersion and purification.

## Decline & Transformation

Jericho suffered in the Roman-Jewish wars — devastated in 68 CE during Vespasian's campaign before the siege of Jerusalem. The Jewish community recovered in diminished form under Roman and Byzantine rule. The Na'aran synagogue, dating from the fifth or sixth century CE, attests to a surviving community. The Arab conquest of the seventh century and the subsequent Umayyad development of a new palatial complex (Hisham's Palace) near Jericho introduced a new chapter in the city's history.

By the medieval period, Jericho had shrunk to a small village. Its present-day status as a Palestinian Authority city dates from the Oslo Accords (1994), when it became the first area transferred to Palestinian civilian control.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Jericho's legacy in Jewish memory is primarily biblical: the falling walls, the miraculous entry into the Land, the threshold moment of Israelite history. The Dead Sea Scrolls, found in the Jericho region, have revolutionized scholarship on Second Temple Judaism and the origins of the Hebrew Bible. They preserve the oldest known manuscripts of virtually every book of the Hebrew Bible, alongside sectarian texts of extraordinary richness, connecting Jericho's landscape to the most intimate documentation of ancient Jewish religious life.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: c. 9000 BCE (oldest habitation levels); Jewish city from Hasmonean period
- **Peak Population**: Several thousand (Second Temple period, including royal court population)
- **Languages**: Aramaic, Hebrew, Greek
- **Status Today**: Palestinian Authority city; no Jewish community; significant archaeological and religious tourism

# Uman

## Overview

Uman is a city in the Cherkasy Oblast of central Ukraine, situated on the Umanka River. It holds a double significance in Jewish memory: it was the site of one of the most catastrophic massacres in pre-modern Jewish history — the Haidamak destruction of 1768 — and it is the burial place of Rabbi Nachman of Breslov, whose grave draws more than 100,000 Jewish pilgrims every Rosh Hashana, making it one of the largest annual Jewish gatherings in the world. The city thus holds both the deepest grief and the most intense annual joy of the Breslov Hasidic tradition in a single location — a juxtaposition that is, for Breslov followers, entirely intentional.

## Historical Background

Uman was established as a Polish fortress town in the mid-seventeenth century and became a significant Jewish community in Podolia. Its Jews engaged in trade, artisanship, and the grain economy typical of the Ukrainian steppe region. The town's fortified position gave it strategic importance, which would prove fateful in the violent upheavals of the eighteenth century.

## The 1768 Massacre — "Uman Bonfires"

The event that first inscribed Uman into Jewish collective memory was the Haidamak uprising of 1768, known in Jewish sources as the "Uman Fire" (Umaner Fayer). The Haidamaks were Ukrainian peasant insurgents who launched a massive anti-Polish revolt — the Koliivshchyna — across right-bank Ukraine. Led by Maxim Zaliznyak and Ivan Gonta, the rebels swept through Jewish communities with devastating violence. In June 1768, tens of thousands of Jewish refugees from surrounding towns flooded into Uman, seeking protection behind its walls. The Polish commandant Ivan Gonta eventually betrayed the city, and the Haidamaks carried out a massacre of catastrophic proportions. Estimates of Jewish dead range from 18,000 to 20,000, though historical uncertainty clouds the precise figures. Entire communities perished in a matter of days. The event entered Jewish liturgy and poetry and became one of the central tragedies of Ukrainian Jewish memory.

## Rabbi Nachman's Choice of Uman

It was precisely because of this massacre that Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (1772–1810), as he lay dying of tuberculosis, chose to move to Uman in 1810. He stated that he wished to be buried among the martyrs of 1768, that he could elevate their souls, and that he invited his followers to come to him at Rosh Hashana for all generations. This act of solidarity with the martyred dead gave his burial site its unique character — a place of pilgrimage that is simultaneously a site of mourning and of fierce spiritual joy.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Nachman died in Uman on the fourth day of Sukkot, 1810, at the age of 38. The local Breslov community maintained his grave through the nineteenth century. Under Soviet rule, access to the grave was severely restricted, and a housing project was built over parts of the original Jewish cemetery. A small group of Breslov Hasidim kept the pilgrimage tradition alive even under Soviet persecution. After Ukrainian independence in 1991, the annual gathering exploded in size.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The central institution in Uman for Jewish purposes is the Tziyun — the tomb of Rabbi Nachman, set within a prayer hall and surrounded by ancillary structures for pilgrims. Over the past three decades, a significant infrastructure of temporary and permanent facilities has grown up around the grave site to accommodate the massive annual influx. The annual Rosh Hashana gathering transforms the city entirely: streets fill with Hasidim, Israeli families, newly observant Jews, curious visitors, and vendors. The prayer services on the eve of the New Year, with tens of thousands chanting together, are an overwhelming sensory experience.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Uman pilgrimage has become a phenomenon that extends beyond Breslov Hasidism into Israeli and diaspora Jewish consciousness. It is a moment of communal identity assertion, spiritual renewal, and connection to historical memory. The juxtaposition of the 1768 massacre and Rabbi Nachman's grave in a single location gives Uman a density of Jewish meaning perhaps unmatched by any other site outside Israel. For Breslov followers, Rosh Hashana in Uman is not merely a tradition but a fulfillment of their rebbe's direct instruction.

## Decline & Dispersal

Uman's pre-war Jewish community, numbering several thousand, was largely annihilated during the Nazi occupation of 1941–1943. Soviet-era Uman had virtually no Jewish communal life. The contemporary presence is pilgrim-based rather than resident-based.

## Legacy Today

The annual Rosh Hashana gathering continues to grow. The Ukrainian government and local authorities manage the influx each year, with varying degrees of cooperation and tension. Rabbi Nachman's grave has been restored and expanded. The 1768 memorial is maintained nearby. Uman stands as proof that Jewish memory is not merely preservation but active, living practice.

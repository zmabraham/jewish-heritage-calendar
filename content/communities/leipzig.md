# Leipzig

## Overview

Leipzig's Jewish community was defined above all by the city's famous trade fairs — the Leipzig Messe, among the oldest and most important in Europe — which brought Jewish merchants from across Germany, Poland, Russia, and the Ottoman world to the city three times a year for centuries before Jews were even permitted to live there permanently. When permanent settlement was finally allowed, the community developed rapidly into one of the most significant in Germany: a publishing and intellectual center, home to leading Reform and Orthodox figures, and eventually destroyed by the Nazis.

## Historical Origins

Jews were forbidden to live in Leipzig for most of its history. However, they were permitted to attend the Leipzig fairs — beginning in the fifteenth century and expanding greatly through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. These fairs, held three times annually, were among the most important commercial events in Europe, drawing merchants from across the continent and beyond.

Jewish merchants — particularly Ashkenazic Jews from Poland, Germany, and Bohemia, and later Sephardic merchants from the Ottoman Empire — played a significant role in the fair trade. They were permitted to stay in Leipzig for the duration of the fair, housed in specially designated inns, and subject to special taxes and restrictions, but they participated vigorously in the commercial activities.

Permanent Jewish settlement was only permitted from 1837, when Saxon law changed to allow Jewish residence. The community grew extremely rapidly thereafter.

## Golden Age

The second half of the nineteenth century was Leipzig's Jewish golden age. The community grew from a handful of families in 1837 to nearly 10,000 by 1900. Leipzig's Jews were disproportionately represented in publishing — the city was Germany's preeminent publishing center — and several major publishing houses were founded or led by Jews. The Brockhaus encyclopedia, one of the most important reference works in the German language, involved Jewish editors and publishers. Jewish booksellers, printers, and publishers were central to Leipzig's intellectual identity.

The community also played a significant role in the textile and fur trades — industries that had developed partly through the fair connections — and in finance and manufacturing.

## Notable Figures

Felix Mendelssohn (1809–1847), though not born in Leipzig, made the city central to his career. As director of the Gewandhaus Orchestra from 1835, he transformed it into one of the world's great orchestras and founded the Leipzig Conservatory in 1843. The Conservatory — the first in Germany — trained generations of musicians and put Leipzig at the center of European musical life. Mendelssohn's Jewish ancestry was a source of ongoing antisemitic hostility even during his lifetime, and Wagner's virulent attacks on "Jewish music" were aimed partly at him.

Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch (1808–1888), the founder of Neo-Orthodox Judaism, had connections to the Leipzig rabbinate and influenced the community's religious development. The community was religiously diverse, encompassing Reform, Conservative, and Orthodox congregations.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Great Synagogue of Leipzig (1855) was replaced by a larger and more magnificent building in 1855; the community eventually built several synagogues reflecting its diverse religious orientations. The main synagogue, completed in 1855 and later expanded, was a major landmark.

On Kristallnacht (November 9–10, 1938), Leipzig's synagogues were burned. The community had already lost thousands of members to emigration; the violence accelerated the exodus.

## Cultural Life

Leipzig's Jewish culture was shaped by the city's identity as an intellectual and publishing center. Jewish intellectuals — editors, publishers, journalists, critics — participated actively in the city's literary and musical life. The community supported a Jewish press, learned societies, Zionist organizations, and the full range of Jewish cultural institutions.

The Leipzig Conservatory, founded partly through Mendelssohn's initiative, attracted students from across Europe and contributed to Leipzig's reputation as the musical capital of Germany.

## Decline & Transformation

Leipzig's Jewish community numbered approximately 13,000 in 1933. Nazi persecution drove emigration; Kristallnacht destroyed the remaining synagogues. Deportations to Riga, Chełmno, Auschwitz, and Theresienstadt decimated the community. Under East German Communist rule, the small remaining community was again marginalized.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Leipzig today has a Jewish community of approximately 1,000–1,200, rebuilt primarily by immigrants from the former Soviet Union after German reunification. The memory of Felix Mendelssohn is honored in the city, where the Mendelssohn Foundation maintains the composer's house. The Leipzig Conservatory continues as one of the great music schools of Europe. A new synagogue serves the contemporary community.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 1837 (permanent settlement); fair attendance from 15th century
- **Peak Population**: ~13,000 (1933)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, German, Hebrew, Polish (fair merchants)
- **Status Today**: Active community of ~1,000–1,200; Mendelssohn heritage preserved

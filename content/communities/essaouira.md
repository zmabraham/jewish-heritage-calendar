# Essaouira (Mogador)

## Overview

Essaouira, known historically as Mogador, was a purpose-built port city on Morocco's Atlantic coast whose Jewish community formed an integral and often dominant element of its commercial life from the city's founding in 1765. As the principal port through which Morocco conducted its European trade for over a century, Mogador's Jewish merchants occupied a unique position — licensed agents of the sultan, cosmopolitan traders fluent in European languages, and diplomatic intermediaries whose international connections made them indispensable to the Alawi throne.

## Historical Background

The city of Essaouira was constructed almost from scratch by Sultan Mohammed III beginning around 1765. The sultan's architect, the French prisoner-of-war Théodore Cornut, designed the city on European bastioned fortification principles, giving it the distinctive appearance — white ramparts overlooking the Atlantic — that makes it one of Morocco's most picturesque cities today.

From the beginning, Mohammed III invited prominent Jewish merchant families to settle in Mogador and conduct his European trade. These favored merchants received the title "tujjar al-sultan" (merchants of the sultan), a formal designation granting them special privileges: the right to reside in the European quarter (kasbah), access to the sultan's protection, diplomatic quasi-immunity, and the right to maintain commercial agents in European ports.

## Golden Age

The late 18th and early 19th centuries were Mogador's commercial apex. The Jewish community at this time comprised roughly half the city's population — an extraordinary proportion unique among Moroccan cities. Jewish merchants exported Morocco's primary products: gum arabic, almonds, argan oil, ostrich feathers, ivory (transshipped from West Africa), leather goods, and copper. They imported European manufactured goods, arms, and luxury items in exchange.

The Corcos family stands as the preeminent Mogador dynasty. David Corcos and his descendants served for generations as diplomatic intermediaries, consular agents for European powers, and personal merchants to the sultan. The Corcos family had origins in the Spanish exile community and maintained connections to Jewish merchant networks stretching from Amsterdam and London to Gibraltar and Livorno (Leghorn). Joseph Corcos served as vice-consul for several European nations simultaneously in the early 19th century — a remarkable example of the diplomatic leverage exercised by Mogador's Jewish elite.

Other important merchant families included the Afriat, Benhaim, Macnin, and Ohana families, each with distinct specializations and European connections. The Macnin family (also spelled Maknin) served as consular representatives of Britain and other powers.

## Notable Figures

Mordecai Corcos (18th century) was among the founding figures of the Mogador Jewish community, establishing the commercial dynasty that would dominate the city for generations.

Rabbi Mas'ud ben Mosheh Corcos and other family members combined commercial activity with rabbinic learning, a combination typical of the Mogador elite. Their correspondence, preserved in European archives (particularly British Foreign Office records), reveals the texture of their diplomatic and commercial operations.

Abraham Corcos (early 19th century) served simultaneously as an agent for multiple European consulates, navigating the complex politics of the Napoleonic era and its aftermath with considerable skill.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Mogador's Jewish quarter (mellah) was integrated into the city's fortified layout, though somewhat segregated from the Muslim quarters. The community maintained several synagogues, the most important being the Slat Lkbira (Great Synagogue) and the Synagogue Simon Attias, which has been partially restored as a heritage site.

The community supported a beth din, charitable societies, and an elaborate system of voluntary associations. Its commercial wealth funded religious and educational institutions of a quality that rivaled Moroccan cities many times its size.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Mogador's Jewish community was unusually cosmopolitan for Morocco. Its members spoke Arabic, Judeo-Berber, Spanish/Haketia (the Judeo-Spanish dialect of Moroccan Sephardim), French, English, and Italian — polyglottism that reflected their commercial role as intermediaries. Their letter-writing and contract conventions blended Hebrew, Arabic, and European commercial formats.

The community participated in the general religious life of Moroccan Jewry, celebrating the same saint pilgrimages and following the Moroccan rite. However, exposure to European merchants and travelers gave Mogador Jews early access to European ideas. The Alliance Israélite Universelle opened one of its earliest Moroccan schools in Mogador in 1868, producing graduates who could function in both Moroccan and European commercial contexts.

The writer and intellectual Elias Canetti, winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature (1981), spent part of his childhood in Manchester but was deeply shaped by his Judeo-Spanish (Ladino-speaking) family culture, which had roots in the Sephardic commercial networks of which Mogador formed a part.

## Decline & Dispersal

The opening of Casablanca as Morocco's dominant commercial port in the early 20th century gradually diminished Essaouira's importance. The Jewish merchant elite followed economic opportunity northward, and by the French Protectorate period (1912 onward), Mogador was a backwater. The 1940s–1960s saw the community's emigration accelerate; most went to Casablanca initially, then to France, Canada, or Israel.

## Legacy Today

Essaouira today is a UNESCO World Heritage city and a major tourist destination. The Espace Othello and the Simon Attias Synagogue have been partially restored. Annual music festivals and heritage events celebrate the city's multicultural history. Essaouira's diaspora communities maintain strong attachment to the city, and several documentary films have traced the Corcos and other family histories. The Moroccan-Jewish photographer and filmmaker Raymond Depardon captured aspects of Mogador's Jewish heritage in his work.

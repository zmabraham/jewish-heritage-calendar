# Altona

## Overview

Altona, today a district of Hamburg, was for centuries an independent city under Danish rule and home to one of the most complex and intellectually vibrant Jewish communities in northern Europe. Situated just across the border from the Free Imperial City of Hamburg, Altona served as a haven where Jews could settle under comparatively tolerant conditions when Hamburg itself restricted Jewish residence. Its twin-community character — encompassing both Sephardic and Ashkenazic congregations — made it a crossroads of Jewish legal, commercial, and intellectual life, and the stage for one of the most dramatic rabbinical controversies of the eighteenth century.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Altona began in earnest in the early seventeenth century. The Portuguese (Sephardic) Jews, expelled from the Iberian Peninsula, were among the first to arrive, establishing commercial networks and a congregation by the 1610s. Ashkenazic Jews followed, drawn by the relative openness of Danish rule compared to the restrictive ordinances of Hamburg. The two communities coexisted — sometimes uneasily — within the same city, maintaining separate synagogues, cemeteries, and communal organizations while sharing a geographic space.

The Danish crown issued charters of protection (Schutzbriefe) that allowed Jewish residents to engage in trade, practice crafts, and worship relatively freely. Altona accordingly grew into a commercial hub connecting Hamburg's international port trade with the broader Jewish mercantile diaspora stretching from Amsterdam to Istanbul.

## Golden Age

The eighteenth century represented Altona's peak of Jewish intellectual activity. The city supported a flourishing network of scholars, printers, and merchants. Hebrew printing presses operated here, producing liturgical and legal texts that circulated throughout European Jewry. The rabbinate of Altona was held by figures of continental renown, and the community maintained close ties with the Hamburg and Wandsbek communities — the three together sometimes referred to as the "AHW" community (Altona-Hamburg-Wandsbek).

Commerce remained central to community life. Sephardic merchants dealt in colonial goods, sugar, and textiles, while Ashkenazic Jews traded in a wider range of commodities. Wealth patronized scholarship, and the community produced a rich culture of learned discourse.

## Notable Figures

The most consequential intellectual drama of Altona's history was the bitter dispute between Rabbi Jonathan Eybeschuetz (1690–1764) and Rabbi Jacob Emden (1697–1776). Eybeschuetz served as rabbi of the AHW communities from 1750. Emden, himself a distinguished scholar and son of the great Chacham Tzvi Ashkenazi, accused Eybeschuetz of secretly being a follower of the false messiah Shabbetai Tzvi, pointing to allegedly Sabbatian amulets (kameot) that Eybeschuetz had distributed. The controversy engulfed European Jewry, drawing in leading rabbis from Frankfurt to Poland, the Danish crown, and even Christian authorities. Eybeschuetz denied the charges vigorously; the dispute was never definitively resolved. Jacob Emden himself lived in Altona for much of his life, running his own printing press and producing a vast corpus of responsa, commentary, and autobiography.

Chacham Tzvi Ashkenazi (1660–1718), father of Jacob Emden, also served as rabbi of the Ashkenazic community and was a towering halakhic authority of his generation.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Altona supported multiple synagogues serving its distinct communities. The Great Synagogue of the Ashkenazic community was a focal point of communal life. The Sephardic community maintained its own house of worship reflecting Portuguese liturgical traditions. A beth din (rabbinical court) operated continuously, adjudicating disputes not only for local Jews but for communities throughout northern Germany and Scandinavia. The community maintained a hekdesh (poorhouse), a mikveh, and educational institutions including a talmud torah for children.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Altona's Hebrew presses were among the most productive in northern Europe. Works of halakha, kabbalah, and homiletics flowed from these presses to communities across the continent. The printing activity of Jacob Emden's private press was especially notable; Emden used it to publish his own works as well as classic texts, ensuring that his editorial voice — sharp, independent, and deeply learned — reached wide audiences.

The Sephardic-Ashkenazic encounter in Altona also generated interesting cultural cross-pollination, with each community aware of and occasionally responding to the traditions of the other.

## Decline & Dispersal

The incorporation of Altona into Prussia in 1867 and then into the German Empire ended its special status as a Danish city. As Hamburg relaxed restrictions on Jewish settlement and emancipation opened new horizons, many Altona Jews relocated to Hamburg or further afield. The community that remained was eventually subjected to the full force of National Socialist persecution after 1933. The synagogues were destroyed during Kristallnacht in November 1938, and the Jewish population was deported during the war years. The cemeteries, however, survived and remain among the most significant Jewish historic sites in northern Germany.

## Legacy Today

The Altona Jewish cemetery (Königstraße) is one of the oldest and most important Jewish burial grounds in northern Europe, containing graves from the seventeenth century through the twentieth, including those of Chacham Tzvi and Jacob Emden. It is a protected historical monument and a destination for scholars of Jewish genealogy and history. The memory of the Eybeschuetz-Emden controversy continues to fascinate historians of Jewish intellectual life and the dynamics of rabbinical authority.

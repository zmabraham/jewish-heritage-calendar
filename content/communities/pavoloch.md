# Pavoloch

## Overview

Pavoloch — today Pavlovychi in the Zhytomyr Oblast of north-central Ukraine — was a small Ukrainian shtetl whose place in Jewish historical memory is marked by one of the most violent episodes of the eighteenth century: the Haidamak attack of 1768. Part of the broader Koliivshchyna uprising that devastated Jewish communities across right-bank Ukraine, the massacre at Pavoloch stands as one of the traumatic punctuation marks in the long history of Ukrainian-Jewish coexistence.

## Historical Background

Pavoloch was a modest Ukrainian market town in the Volhynia-Podolia borderland, with a Jewish community typical of the region — engaged in trade, crafts, and serving as economic intermediaries in the agrarian economy. The town's position in the complex ethnic landscape of right-bank Ukraine — between Polish nobility, Ukrainian Cossacks, and Jewish merchants and leaseholders — placed it in the zone of maximum tension during the cycles of Ukrainian peasant uprising that marked the eighteenth century.

## The 1768 Haidamak Massacre

The Koliivshchyna uprising of 1768 was a Ukrainian peasant revolt against Polish noble rule, directed with particular fury against the two groups most visible as representatives of the ruling order: Catholic Poles and Jewish traders, leaseholders, and innkeepers. Led by Maxim Zaliznyak and Ivan Gonta, the Haidamak bands swept through the right-bank Ukraine, burning towns and massacring their Jewish and Polish inhabitants.

Pavoloch was among the communities struck during this wave of violence. The Jewish community — men, women, and children — was attacked and many were killed. The survivors who escaped fled to larger, better-defended towns or simply into the forests. The Pavoloch massacre was part of a broader pattern that included the even larger catastrophe at Uman, where an estimated 18,000–20,000 Jewish refugees were killed in a single event.

The Haidamak massacres of 1768 entered Jewish consciousness alongside the Khmelnytsky massacres of 1648 as defining moments of victimhood and vulnerability. The laments written about these events — in Hebrew, Yiddish, and Ladino — became part of the liturgical memory of suffering that shaped Jewish self-understanding for generations.

## The Traumatic Landscape

What makes the Ukrainian-Jewish history of places like Pavoloch so complex is the multi-layered nature of the violence. The 1648 Khmelnytsky uprising, the 1768 Haidamak attacks, the civil war pogroms of 1918–1921, and the Nazi genocides of 1941–1942 form a continuous thread of catastrophe with relative peace in between. Jewish communities in this region knew they lived on historically dangerous ground — and came back to it anyway, generation after generation, because it was home.

## Communal Life

Between the episodes of violence, Pavoloch had a functioning Jewish community life: Shabbat prayers, market days, weddings and funerals, children in cheder, women running households, men studying in the evening after work. This ordinary life — the infrastructure of daily Jewish existence that sustained the community between catastrophes — is as important to remember as the violence that periodically destroyed it.

## Notable Figures

Pavoloch was not associated with famous rabbinic figures. Its significance in Jewish history is collective rather than individual — the story of a community that rebuilt, maintained its traditions, and was ultimately destroyed not by its own failings but by external violence.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Jewish community of Pavoloch maintained a synagogue and standard communal institutions. After each wave of violence — 1648, 1768, 1919 — the community was rebuilt by those who returned from flight or survived in hiding. This resilience, repeated across hundreds of similar Ukrainian shtetlach, is one of the most remarkable features of Jewish social history.

## Decline & Dispersal

Soviet collectivization and the destruction of traditional economic structures disrupted Pavoloch's Jewish community in the 1920s and 1930s. The German occupation of the region in 1941 brought mass murder: the Jewish community was shot by German Einsatzgruppen and their auxiliaries in late 1941.

## Legacy Today

Pavoloch today is a small Ukrainian village. The memory of its Jewish community is preserved in yizkor books and in the family memories of descendants who emigrated to America, Palestine, and elsewhere before the final catastrophe.

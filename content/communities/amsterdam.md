# Amsterdam

## Overview

Amsterdam earned the epithet "Jerusalem of the North" not merely as flattery but as recognition of a genuine phenomenon: a city where Jewish intellectual, spiritual, and commercial life flourished with unusual freedom in an age of persecution elsewhere. From the Portuguese conversos who transformed Dutch commercial life to the Ashkenazic refugees who created Europe's largest Jewish printing industry, Amsterdam became a beacon of relative tolerance — a place where the philosopher Spinoza could be excommunicated by his own community while simultaneously reshaping the intellectual foundations of Western thought.

## Historical Origins

The first Jews to settle in Amsterdam were Portuguese and Spanish conversos who arrived in the 1590s, drawn by Dutch commercial opportunity and relative religious latitude. Amsterdam's merchant oligarchy, itself frequently at odds with rigid religious orthodoxy, proved willing to tolerate Jewish residents in exchange for their commercial expertise and international trading networks. The converso community gradually shed its Christian veneer and reconstituted itself as an openly Jewish congregation, establishing the "Beth Jacob" congregation in 1598.

The formal recognition of Jewish settlement came incrementally, consolidated by a ruling of the Amsterdam city council in 1616. The first purpose-built synagogue opened in 1639. By mid-century, the community had organized into three congregations — Beth Jacob, Neve Shalom, and Beth Israel — which merged in 1639 into a single Sephardic community, the Talmud Torah congregation.

Ashkenazic Jews, fleeing persecution in Germany, Poland, and Ukraine, began arriving in significant numbers in the 1630s and especially after the Chmielnicki massacres of 1648–1649. The Ashkenazic community grew rapidly, eventually outnumbering the Sephardim and establishing their own institutions.

## Golden Age

The seventeenth century was Amsterdam Jewry's golden age. The Portuguese Jewish community achieved remarkable prosperity and cultural sophistication. Jewish merchants played central roles in the Dutch East and West India Companies, trading with Brazil, the Caribbean, and the Levant. The community attracted scholars from across the Sephardic diaspora, including Rabbi Menasseh ben Israel (1604–1657), who established the first Hebrew printing press in Amsterdam and engaged in fruitful dialogue with Christian Hebraists and millenarians — including Oliver Cromwell, whom he petitioned to readmit Jews to England.

The Portuguese Synagogue, completed in 1675 and still standing, is one of the architectural masterpieces of the Dutch Golden Age. Modeled after Solomon's Temple as described in the Bible, it was among the largest synagogues in the world at its completion, a breathtaking statement of Sephardic wealth and confidence. Built by master architect Elias Bouman, it features a vast sand-covered floor, massive brass chandeliers, and a library containing one of the world's finest collections of Hebrew manuscripts.

## Notable Figures

Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) is Amsterdam's most provocative Jewish legacy — and its most ambiguous. Born into the Sephardic community, Spinoza was excommunicated (cherem) in 1656 at age twenty-three in a document of unusual severity, the specific reasons still debated by scholars. He went on to write the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus and the Ethics, works that laid foundational stones for secular philosophy, biblical criticism, and liberal political thought. The community that expelled him could not have imagined how profoundly his thinking would reshape Western civilization.

Rabbi Jacob Sasportas (1610–1698) was among the most vigorous opponents of the Sabbatean movement, his correspondence a crucial primary source for understanding that messianic crisis. Rabbi Saul Levi Morteira (c.1596–1660) was a leading rabbinic authority whose sermons survive as masterworks of Jewish homiletics.

Daniel Levi de Barrios (1635–1701) was the leading poet of Amsterdam's Sephardic community, his Hebrew and Spanish verse preserving the cultural world of the converso diaspora.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Portuguese Synagogue (Esnoga) remains Amsterdam's most iconic Jewish monument and one of the best-preserved seventeenth-century synagogues in the world. The Ets Haim library attached to it contains manuscripts of incalculable scholarly value.

The Great Ashkenazic Synagogue (1671) and the subsequent complex of three Ashkenazic synagogues on Jonas Daniel Meijerplein now house the Jewish Historical Museum, one of Europe's finest institutions for Jewish material culture. The Dutch Jewish community established the first Hebrew printing presses in Amsterdam, which by the eighteenth century produced the majority of Hebrew books circulated in Europe and the Ottoman world.

## Cultural Life

Amsterdam's Jewish community created a distinctive culture at the intersection of Iberian Sephardic tradition, Dutch commercial republicanism, and Renaissance humanism. The community supported painters (including Rembrandt, who lived in the Jewish quarter and depicted Jewish subjects), poets, philosophers, physicians, and scholars. The printing houses of Amsterdam — Menasseh ben Israel's press, later the Proops family firm — produced not only liturgical texts but also kabbalistic works, secular literature, and vernacular books in Ladino, Yiddish, and Portuguese.

The Ashkenazic community developed a Yiddish-language press and theater culture that would later influence similar enterprises across Europe and in America. By the eighteenth century, Amsterdam had become the intellectual hub for both Sephardic and Ashkenazic Jewish scholarship.

## Decline & Transformation

Amsterdam's Jewish community entered a long decline in the eighteenth century as Dutch commercial power waned. Many Sephardic merchants emigrated to London, Bordeaux, or the Americas. The community remained significant but increasingly impoverished.

The Nazi occupation of the Netherlands (1940–1945) brought catastrophe. Amsterdam was home to approximately 80,000 Jews in 1940. The deportations, carried out with notorious efficiency by German authorities and Dutch collaborators, sent the overwhelming majority to Auschwitz-Birkenau and Sobibor. Fewer than 5,000 Amsterdam Jews survived the war. The Frank family — Anne Frank, whose diary written in hiding became the most widely read Holocaust memoir — represents the human face of this devastation.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Portuguese Synagogue still holds services, maintained by a small but devoted Sephardic community. The city hosts a Jewish population of approximately 15,000–20,000 today, including Ashkenazic and Sephardic communities and a substantial Israeli-born population. The Anne Frank House draws over a million visitors annually, ensuring that Amsterdam's Jewish story — glorious and tragic — remains before the world.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: c. 1598 (first congregation)
- **Peak Population**: ~80,000 (1940)
- **Languages**: Portuguese, Ladino (Sephardic); Yiddish, Dutch (Ashkenazic)
- **Status Today**: Active community of ~15,000–20,000; Portuguese Synagogue a UNESCO-listed monument

# Hippos (Sussita)

## Overview

Hippos — known in Hebrew as Sussita, "the horse" (both names mean "horse" in their respective languages) — was a Hellenistic-Roman city perched dramatically on a flat-topped basalt mesa overlooking the eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee in the Golan Heights. One of the cities of the Decapolis (the league of ten Hellenistic cities east of the Jordan River), Hippos maintained a small but identifiable Jewish minority community amid a predominantly Greco-Roman population. Archaeological excavations since 2000 have revealed a sophisticated urban center, and remarkable discoveries including a unique bronze mask of Dionysus and, most recently, a Byzantine-era church complex. Hippos illustrates the complex reality of Jewish life in the Hellenistic cities of ancient Palestine — minority communities navigating pagan urban culture while maintaining distinct identity.

## Historical Background

Hippos was founded as a Hellenistic polis (city-state) during the Seleucid period, likely in the 3rd–2nd century BCE. Like other Decapolis cities, it was organized along Greek civic lines with an agora, temples, colonnaded streets (a decumanus and cardo), theaters, and public baths. The city was briefly conquered by Alexander Jannaeus (late 2nd–early 1st century BCE) during the Hasmonean expansion eastward, and Jewish sources suggest a Jewish population may have settled during this period.

After Roman general Pompey reorganized the East in 63 BCE, he freed Hippos (along with other Decapolis cities) from Hasmonean control and restored its autonomy within the Roman provincial system. The city minted its own coins bearing pagan imagery — a horse, the city's symbol — and maintained the civic institutions of a typical Roman provincial city.

Josephus mentions Hippos in the context of the great revolt: Jewish residents and nearby Jewish communities were caught in violent inter-communal clashes at the revolt's outbreak in 66 CE, with Josephus recording massacres of Jews in Hippos and surrounding areas. The Jewish minority in Hippos existed in a potentially dangerous relationship with its pagan majority.

## Golden Age

The Byzantine period (4th–7th centuries CE) represents Hippos's architectural and cultural peak. The city expanded with churches, a rebuilt forum, and impressive civic buildings. The Jewish community of the Byzantine period would have existed alongside a now-Christian majority, maintaining their synagogue and communal life within a city increasingly defined by Christian institutions.

Excavations since 2000 by the University of Haifa (directed by Arthur Segal and later Michael Eisenberg) have uncovered extensive Byzantine remains and, crucially for Jewish history, evidence of a Jewish presence through inscriptions and artifacts.

## Notable Figures

The Jewish community of Hippos is largely anonymous in the historical record. Josephus mentions the city in the context of the revolt, and his accounts imply a small but identifiable Jewish population in a hostile pagan environment. The community's story is one of minority survival and accommodation rather than rabbinic leadership or institutional prominence.

The city's pagan intellectual life was more robustly documented: Hippos produced poets and philosophers in the Greek tradition, as was common among Decapolis cities. The juxtaposition of this sophisticated Hellenic culture with a small Jewish minority captures the complexity of Jewish existence in Roman Palestine beyond the Galilean Jewish heartland.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Archaeological evidence for a Jewish presence at Hippos includes:
- A **menorah carved into a stone lintel** found in excavations, suggesting a Jewish building — possibly a synagogue or private house — in the Byzantine period
- **Jewish names** in Greek inscriptions from the site
- Proximity to heavily Jewish settlements on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee, suggesting trade and family connections

No synagogue building has been definitively identified at Hippos thus far, though excavations continue. The small size of the Jewish community may mean their institutions were modest.

The city's civic institutions — **the Decumanus Maximus** (main colonnaded street), **the forum**, **temples to Zeus and Tyche**, and multiple **churches** — create the backdrop against which the Jewish minority lived. Jewish residents would have navigated daily life in a city whose public spaces were organized around pagan and later Christian religious practice.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Hippos's importance for Jewish history lies primarily in what it illustrates about Jewish minority existence in Hellenistic cities. The questions this community faced — how to live as Jews in a city organized around pagan civic religion, how to participate in commerce and public life, how to maintain Jewish identity across generations — were faced by Jewish minorities throughout the Roman Empire, from Alexandria to Rome to Antioch.

The Talmud contains scattered references to Jewish residents in Decapolis cities and their legal questions: may a Jew buy from a pagan butcher in a city with mixed population? What are the rules for Shabbat observance when one's neighbors are non-Jews? These legal discussions, though not specific to Hippos, reflect the real situations faced by communities like Hippos's Jews.

Recent excavations at Hippos-Sussita have also yielded a remarkable collection of Byzantine-era bronze objects including, most dramatically, a large bronze mask of the pagan god Pan/Dionysus, illustrating the persistence of pagan iconography even into the Christian Byzantine era — and the complex cultural environment in which the Jewish minority navigated daily life.

## Decline & Dispersal

Hippos was severely damaged by the earthquake of 749 CE — one of the most destructive seismic events in the history of the Levant — which destroyed numerous Byzantine-era cities in the Jordan Valley and Sea of Galilee region. The city was largely abandoned thereafter. The Jewish community, already small, disappeared along with the broader urban population.

## Legacy Today

The ruins of Hippos-Sussita (Qalat al-Husn in Arabic) sit atop the mesa overlooking the Sea of Galilee and Kibbutz Ein Gev. The site is a national park managed by the Israel Nature and Parks Authority. The University of Haifa's ongoing excavations continue to reveal new aspects of the city's multi-layered history. The view from the summit — the Sea of Galilee below, the Galilee hills to the west, the Golan Heights stretching north — remains breathtaking, unchanged since the ancient Jewish minority looked out from this pagan city toward the Jewish heartland across the water.

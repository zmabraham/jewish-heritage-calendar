# Ruschuk (Ruse)

## Overview

Ruschuk — today Ruse, on the Danube in northern Bulgaria — was a major Ottoman Danubian city and home to one of the most significant Sephardic Jewish communities of Bulgaria. The birthplace of the Nobel Prize-winning author Elias Canetti, Ruschuk was the kind of cosmopolitan Ottoman city where Jews, Turks, Bulgarians, Greeks, Armenians, and merchants from across Europe lived in close if not always harmonious proximity. The Bulgarian Jewish community was among the most fortunate in occupied Europe: through a combination of Bulgarian government resistance, church intervention, and civil society pressure, the vast majority of Bulgarian Jews were not deported to Nazi death camps during the Second World War.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Ruschuk followed the standard Ottoman Sephardic pattern: refugees from the 1492 expulsion arrived in the Ottoman Balkans and settled in cities along the major commercial routes. Ruschuk's position on the Danube — the great artery connecting the Ottoman interior to Central Europe — made it an important trading city, and a Jewish community was established by the sixteenth century.

The community grew substantially in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries as Ruschuk became an increasingly important Danubian port. By the nineteenth century, as the Ottoman Empire modernized and trade with Europe intensified, Ruschuk's Jewish community was deeply involved in the Danubian transit trade, acting as agents, merchants, and bankers in the commerce flowing between the Black Sea region and Central Europe.

## The Canetti World

The most famous product of Ruschuk's Jewish community is **Elias Canetti** (1905–1994), winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature (1981). Canetti's memoir, *Die gerettete Zunge* (The Tongue Set Free, 1977), opens with an extraordinary portrait of his childhood in Ruschuk — a city of "seven or eight languages" heard daily, where the Jewish community lived in a distinct but permeable world adjacent to Bulgarians, Turks, Greeks, and others.

Canetti's description of Ruschuk captures the essence of Ottoman Sephardic life in its final generation. His family spoke Ladino at home; his mother was cultured and demanding, passionately attached to German literature; his father's early death sent the family to Vienna, Manchester, and Zurich. The Ruschuk of Canetti's childhood was already undergoing rapid transformation — from Ottoman cosmopolitan city to Bulgarian national one — and his memoir catches it at this threshold moment. In his later masterwork *Masse und Macht* (Crowds and Power, 1960), some scholars have traced the formation of his extraordinary sensitivity to collective behavior to his early observations of the intensely communal life of Ruschuk's Jewish quarter.

## Notable Figures

**Elias Canetti (1905–1994)** — Nobel laureate, author of *Auto-da-Fé*, *Crowds and Power*, and the three-volume autobiography beginning with *The Tongue Set Free*. His work is one of the most significant literary achievements of twentieth-century German-language literature.

**Rabbi Abraham Aryeh Leib Ruschukli (18th century)** — A Talmudic authority of the Ruschuk community whose legal opinions circulated in Bulgarian and wider Ottoman Jewish circles.

**The Canetti family** — The extended Canetti clan, as described in Elias Canetti's memoirs, exemplifies the prosperous Ladino-speaking Sephardic merchant class of late Ottoman Bulgaria: multilingual, commercially active, engaged with European culture while maintaining strong Jewish communal identity.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The major synagogue of Ruschuk was a substantial Sephardic structure rebuilt in the nineteenth century as the community's prosperity grew. The community maintained a talmud torah, charitable societies, and in the modern period, an Alliance Israélite Universelle school. The Jewish press in Bulgaria published newspapers in Ladino from the late nineteenth century, and Ruschuk's community was connected to these Ladino cultural networks.

## The Bulgarian Jewish Community and the Holocaust

Bulgaria joined the Axis in March 1941, accepting German alliance in exchange for territorial gains in Macedonia and Thrace. The Bulgarian government under Tsar Boris III passed antisemitic legislation modeled on the Nuremberg Laws — Jews were stripped of citizenship, subjected to economic restrictions, forced to wear the yellow star, and subjected to forced labor. However, when Germany demanded the deportation of Bulgarian Jews in 1943, a remarkable coalition of resistance emerged.

The Bulgarian Orthodox Church, led by Metropolitan Stefan of Sofia, issued formal protests and threatened to lie down on the railroad tracks before Jewish deportees. Parliamentary deputies, intellectuals, and professional associations signed petitions. The Bulgarian population in many cities showed considerable reluctance to participate in persecution of their Jewish neighbors. Tsar Boris III, calculating both moral and political risk, ultimately refused to deport Bulgarian Jews to the death camps — though he did comply with the deportation of Jews from Bulgarian-occupied Macedonia and Thrace, who were not considered Bulgarian citizens.

The approximately 50,000 Jews of Bulgaria proper survived the war. It was one of the most significant acts of rescue in occupied Europe.

## Emigration to Israel

Following the war, Bulgarian Jewry underwent rapid mass emigration to Israel. Between 1948 and 1951, approximately 44,000 of Bulgaria's 50,000 Jews emigrated to the newly established State of Israel in one of the most complete transfers of a Diaspora community to Israel in the early state period. Ruschuk's Jewish community virtually ceased to exist as such, its members dispersing to Israeli cities, particularly Jaffa-Tel Aviv, Haifa, and Bat Yam.

## Legacy Today

Ruse today has a tiny Jewish community, primarily elderly. The former synagogue has been converted to other uses. The memory of the city's Jewish past is preserved in local history, in Elias Canetti's widely read memoirs, and in the work of Bulgarian Jewish historical organizations. Canetti's birthplace in Ruse is marked with a plaque, and his work has made Ruschuk a destination for literary pilgrims interested in the vanished world of Ottoman Sephardic culture.

# Monastir (Bitola)

## Overview

Monastir — known today as Bitola in North Macedonia — was for four centuries one of the great Sephardic Jewish communities of the Ottoman Balkans. Situated in the Pelagonia plain at the crossroads of routes connecting the Aegean coast to the Danube lands, Monastir was an Ottoman administrative and commercial center of the first order. Its Ladino-speaking Jewish community, established largely by refugees from the 1492 expulsion, grew to be one of the largest in the region, producing scholars, merchants, and a distinctive communal culture that survived until the Holocaust, when the great majority of the community was murdered at Treblinka.

## Historical Background

A small Jewish community may have existed in the region before the Ottoman conquest, but the defining moment in Monastir's Jewish history was the arrival of Sephardic exiles from Spain and Portugal in the years following 1492. The Ottoman Empire actively welcomed these refugees, Sultan Bayezid II famously remarking that Ferdinand of Spain had impoverished himself while enriching the Ottoman state. The exiles brought commercial skills, artisan knowledge, and medical expertise that the Ottomans valued.

Monastir (in Turkish, Manastir; in Jewish sources, Bitola or Monastir) was an important Ottoman provincial capital — the seat of the vilayet (province) of Monastir for much of the Ottoman period. The city's administrative importance, combined with its commercial position, made it an attractive destination for Jewish settlement. By the late sixteenth century, a substantial Ladino-speaking community was established.

## Golden Age

The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries represented the height of Monastir's Jewish community. The community numbered 5,000–7,000 persons at its peak — a substantial proportion of the city's total population. Jewish merchants dominated certain sectors of the Monastir economy, particularly the cloth trade, the leather industry, and commerce with the Danube provinces.

The community's intellectual life was vigorous. Multiple synagogues served different congregational groupings — as was common in Ottoman Sephardic communities, where immigrants from different Iberian cities initially maintained separate congregations named after their cities of origin (Castile, Aragon, Portugal, Catalonia). Over time these congregations merged, but their names and some distinctive customs were preserved. The community maintained a yeshiva, a talmud torah, and in the modern period, an Alliance Israélite Universelle school that enrolled hundreds of students.

Ladino — the Judeo-Spanish language — was the vernacular of daily life, along with some Turkish and, in the Albanian border region, Albanian. The community's Ladino had the distinctive characteristics of Ottoman Sephardic speech: archaic Castilian vocabulary, Hebrew liturgical insertions, and Turkish loanwords for administrative and material culture concepts. Ladino poetry, songs, and stories circulated in the community, connecting it to the broader Sephardic cultural world.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Joseph ibn Lev (Maharival, c. 1505–1580)** — Born in Monastir, studied in Salonika under the great Talmudist Joseph Caro and others, and became one of the leading responsa authors of sixteenth-century Ottoman Jewry. His *Responsa* addressed complex commercial, family, and religious questions arising from the dynamic world of Ottoman Jewish commerce.

**Rabbi Samuel de Medina (Maharashdam, 1506–1589)** — Though primarily associated with Salonika, de Medina had significant connections to the Monastir community, and his legal decisions shaped practice throughout the Ottoman Balkans.

**Mustafa Kemal Atatürk** — The founder of modern Turkey was born in Salonika but spent significant time in Monastir. Several of his formative teachers and contacts in the city were Jewish, and his exposure to the cosmopolitan world of Ottoman Jewish culture in Monastir influenced his secular nationalist vision — a connection noted by several historians of modern Turkey.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The community maintained numerous synagogues, of which the most important was the **Kal Grandi** (Great Synagogue). The building survived until the Second World War and was then destroyed. Other synagogues were named after the Iberian cities from which early settlers had emigrated.

The Alliance Israélite Universelle school, established in the nineteenth century, provided French-language modern education and was a vehicle for the Westernization and civic integration of Monastir's Jews in the late Ottoman and early Yugoslav periods.

## Modern Period

When Monastir became part of the Kingdom of Serbia following the Balkan Wars (1912–1913), the community transitioned from Ottoman to Balkan nationalist governance. The community maintained its Ladino language and culture under Yugoslav rule (the city became Bitola in the Yugoslav state). The interwar period saw continued communal life alongside increasing engagement with Yugoslav society.

Monastir's Jewish population stood at approximately 3,500 at the outbreak of the Second World War.

## Deportation and Destruction

Yugoslavia was occupied by the Axis powers in April 1941. Macedonia was annexed by Bulgaria, which was nominally allied with Germany. The Bulgarian occupation was initially somewhat less harsh than direct German administration, but in March 1943, under German pressure, the Bulgarian authorities collaborated in the roundup and deportation of all Jews in Bulgarian-occupied Macedonia.

On March 11, 1943, the Jewish communities of Monastir, Skopje, and Shtip were arrested. The Jews of Monastir were assembled, held for several days in brutal conditions in the Monopol tobacco warehouse, and then transported by train through Bulgaria to the German-administered territory of Treblinka. Virtually the entire community — approximately 3,276 persons — was murdered at Treblinka extermination camp. Fewer than 200 Monastir Jews survived the Holocaust.

## Legacy Today

A Jewish memorial and small community center exists in Bitola today. A handful of Jewish families remained in or returned to the city after the war. The former Great Synagogue was destroyed; a memorial site marks its location. The Monastir Jewish cemetery, one of the largest in the Balkans, has been partially restored through the efforts of descendants and heritage organizations.

The memory of the Monastir community lives primarily in the diaspora — in Israel, the United States, and Latin America, where descendants of Monastir Jews settled before and after the war. Ladino-language memory projects have worked to preserve the distinctive culture of Monastir's Sephardic world.

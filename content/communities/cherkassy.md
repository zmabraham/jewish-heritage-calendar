# Cherkas (Cherkassy)

## Overview

Cherkassy — today the capital of the Cherkasy Oblast of central Ukraine on the Dnieper River — was a city defined by its contradictions: it sat in the Cossack heartland of Ukraine, a region where Cossack identity was bound up with anti-Jewish violence, yet its Jewish community persisted, traded, and maintained communal life for centuries in this hostile environment. The city's Jewish merchants and artisans navigated the dangerous waters of Ukrainian peasant hostility and Cossack resentment to build a community that survived repeated persecution before succumbing to twentieth-century catastrophe.

## Historical Background

Cherkassy was one of the most ancient Cossack centers in Ukraine — the Cherkasy Cossacks took their name from the city. Jews had been present in Cherkassy since at least the sixteenth century, serving as merchants, tax farmers, and artisans in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth economy. After the Khmelnytsky uprising of 1648, the region became bitterly contested territory, and Jewish settlement was precarious. Under Russian imperial rule from the late eighteenth century, Cherkassy became a provincial center in the Kiev Governorate, and the Jewish community grew steadily.

## The Cossack Heartland

Living in the Cossack heartland meant that Cherkassy's Jews inhabited a landscape charged with deep anti-Jewish symbolism. Cossack collective identity — expressed in folk songs, folk tales, and popular memory — often cast Jews in the role of oppressor, tax farmer, and usurer. The structural reality of Jewish economic activity (as leaseholders and traders for Polish magnates) created social friction that could explode into violence at moments of political crisis. The 1768 Haidamak uprising, which devastated communities across Podolia and Right-Bank Ukraine, affected the Cherkassy region severely.

## Repeated Pogroms

Cherkassy experienced anti-Jewish violence during the 1881–1884 pogrom wave that swept southern Russia and Ukraine after Alexander II's assassination, and again during the civil war period of 1918–1921, when the city changed hands multiple times between various Ukrainian, Bolshevik, and White Army forces. The civil war pogroms in Ukraine were among the most devastating in Jewish history: estimates suggest that over 100,000 Jews were killed across Ukraine between 1918 and 1921, with Cherkassy and its region heavily affected.

## Notable Figures

Cherkassy was not a center of famous Hasidic masters or great scholars — its significance was as a regional market center whose Jewish community exemplified the ordinary life of the Ukrainian Jewish majority: traders, craftsmen, cobblers, tanners, tailors, and dealers in grain and timber who maintained Jewish observance under difficult conditions. The ordinary dignity of these communities — their Shabbat tables, their children's education, their mutual aid societies — is the story that must be told alongside the history of famous personalities.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Cherkassy maintained a full kehilla structure: a main synagogue, Hasidic shtieblach (influenced by the Berdichev and Chernobyl Hasidic traditions active in the region), a chevra kadisha, schools, and charitable organizations. The Jewish community was organized and self-governing within the limits of imperial and later Soviet law.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Jewish community of Cherkassy contributed to the regional economy and to the dense fabric of Jewish cultural life across the Ukrainian Pale. The community's persistence in a territory marked by organized anti-Jewish sentiment represents a form of cultural contribution often overlooked: the maintenance of Jewish life under chronic threat, generation after generation.

## Decline & Dispersal

Soviet rule suppressed Jewish communal institutions in the 1920s and 1930s. The German occupation of Cherkassy during World War II (September 1941 to December 1943) brought systematic murder: approximately 4,500 Jews who had not evacuated were killed. The famous "Cherkassy Pocket" battle of early 1944 was fought in the region as Soviet forces encircled German Army Group South.

## Legacy Today

Cherkassy today has a small Jewish community. The city's Jewish history is preserved in yizkor books (community memorial volumes) compiled by former residents in Israel and America.

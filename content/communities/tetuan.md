# Tetuán

## Overview

Tetuán, nestled in the Rif Mountains near Morocco's northern Mediterranean coast, earned the epithet "Jerusalem of Morocco" from its own Jewish inhabitants — a tribute to the extraordinary density of religious learning, the preservation of ancient Sephardic customs, and the community's intense identification with its Spanish exile heritage. Founded in 1492 largely by Jewish and Muslim refugees expelled from Granada, Tetuán maintained Castilian customs, accents, and liturgical traditions for over five centuries with a fidelity unmatched anywhere else in the Moroccan Jewish world.

## Historical Background

The modern city of Tetuán was founded — or refounded — in 1492 by Andalusian refugees, both Muslim and Jewish, following the fall of Granada to the Catholic monarchs. The Jewish founders came primarily from Granada, Castile, and other regions of al-Andalus, bringing with them the culture, customs, and institutions of medieval Castilian Jewry.

These founders established a community deeply conscious of its Spanish origins. They spoke Haketia — Judeo-Spanish (a variety of Castilian with Hebrew and Arabic elements) — as their primary vernacular, and maintained it with remarkable persistence against the encroachment of Moroccan Arabic. They preserved Spanish liturgical melodies, Spanish surnames, and Spanish legal customs. In letters and deeds, they referred to their distant ancestors' originating cities — Granada, Córdoba, Toledo — as family identifying markers generations after the expulsion.

## Golden Age

The 16th through 19th centuries represent Tetuán Jewry's sustained golden age. The community's position near the Spanish garrison town of Ceuta (a Spanish enclave) gave it unusual exposure to European commercial currents. Tetuán Jews served as interpreters and commercial intermediaries between Moroccan sultans and Spanish authorities, a role that generated wealth and influence.

The early 19th century brought Tetuán into dramatic contact with European modernity. When Spain launched a military campaign against Morocco in 1859–1860, Tetuán was captured and occupied by Spanish forces. The Jewish community's multilingual abilities made them indispensable to the Spanish occupiers, and several Tetuáni Jews served as interpreters and intermediaries. Following the Spanish withdrawal, Tetuán became a center of Spanish cultural influence, and the Jewish community was among the first in Morocco to adopt French and Spanish education under Alliance Israélite and Spanish Jewish school systems.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Yitzchak ben Walid (1780–1870) was among Tetuán's greatest rabbinic scholars, author of the responsa collection *Vayomer Yitzhak* and numerous other works. His long career spanned the most dramatic period of Tetuán's encounter with modernity, and his legal rulings navigated the tensions between tradition and change with considerable sophistication.

Rabbi Avraham Israeli and Rabbi Moshe Berdugo were among other noted Tetuán scholars whose works entered the broader canon of Moroccan Sephardic halakha.

The Nahon family produced merchants and philanthropists who funded communal institutions; the family's international connections spanned Gibraltar, London, and Amsterdam, exemplifying the commercial networks that sustained Tetuán's prosperity.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Tetuán mellah contained dozens of synagogues. Among the most famous is the Synagogue of the Megorashim, preserving the rite of the Spanish exiles. The community's beth din maintained Spanish Sephardic legal traditions, applying the rulings of Rabbi Joseph Karo's *Shulchan Aruch* through the lens of Andalusian custom.

The Alliance Israélite Universelle opened its Tetuán school in 1862 — one of the Alliance's first schools in Morocco — transforming the educational landscape and producing a generation of French-educated professionals.

The community's educational network was eventually supplemented by Spanish Jewish schools under Spanish Protectorate auspices (northern Morocco was Spain's protectorate, while the south and center were French).

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Tetuán's most distinctive cultural contribution is its preservation of Castilian Jewish heritage. The Haketia language spoken by Tetuáni Jews preserved medieval Castilian vocabulary, idioms, and grammatical structures lost in modern Spanish — making it a linguistic fossil of exceptional value. Haketia literature — proverbs, songs, stories, and correspondence — documents a living tradition maintained five centuries after the expulsion.

The community's liturgical music preserved distinctive Castilian-Jewish melodies for prayers and Shabbat songs (zmirot) that were transmitted orally and have only recently been recorded by ethnomusicologists. Their Passover Haggadah included Spanish-language songs alongside the Hebrew text.

Tetuáni Jewish women were famous for elaborate embroidery and textile work that combined Andalusian, Moroccan, and Jewish symbolic vocabularies, producing decorated household items — curtains, Torah mantles, wedding dresses — of outstanding artistic quality.

## Decline & Dispersal

The Spanish Protectorate era (1912–1956) brought modernization but also destabilization. Zionist activity and the events of 1948 prompted early emigration. After Moroccan independence in 1956 ended the Spanish Protectorate, Tetuán's Jews emigrated rapidly — most to Venezuela (Caracas), Spain (Madrid and Ceuta), France, and Israel. By the 1970s, the community had virtually disappeared.

## Legacy Today

Tetuáni Jews in Caracas, Venezuela, established one of the most cohesive diaspora communities, maintaining their Haketia language and Sephardic customs for generations in the New World. Ethnomusicological recordings of Tetuáni music and Haketia literature are preserved in Spanish and Israeli archives. The Tetuán mellah is a UNESCO-recognized heritage zone, and the Jewish Museum of Tetuán (Museo de la Historia y Cultura Judeo-Marroquí) documents the community's history.

# Larissa

## Overview

Larissa, the major city of the Thessalian plain in central Greece, was home to one of the most complex Jewish communities of the Ottoman Balkans — a community that combined ancient Romaniote roots with a substantial Sephardic overlay, producing a Jewish culture that incorporated elements of both traditions. The Jews of Larissa were significant participants in the commercial and intellectual life of Ottoman Thessaly. Though the community suffered major losses during the Holocaust, enough survivors returned after the war to reconstitute a small Jewish presence that continues in Larissa to this day.

## Historical Background

Jewish presence in Thessaly, and in Larissa specifically, is attested from Byzantine times. The ancient Romaniote communities of Greece — Greek-speaking Jews whose presence dated to antiquity — were established in Larissa and other Thessalian cities long before the Ottoman conquest. The Ottoman conquest of Thessaly in the late fourteenth century brought the region into the Turkish-Islamic world, but the Jewish community continued to exist under dhimmi status.

The transformative moment for Larissa's Jewish community, as for most Greek Jewish communities, was the arrival of Sephardic refugees after 1492. Large numbers of Iberian Jews settled in Ottoman Thessaly, and Larissa absorbed a substantial Sephardic population. The new settlers' Ladino language and Sephardic religious customs gradually became dominant, but traces of the older Romaniote tradition persisted in local customs and possibly in some family lineages. The community thus became a hybrid — predominantly Sephardic but with a Romaniote substratum.

## Community Life and Golden Age

The seventeenth through nineteenth centuries were the period of greatest Jewish presence in Larissa. The community numbered 1,500–2,500 persons at its peak, a significant minority in a city with a mixed population of Turks, Greeks, and smaller communities of Armenians and others. Larissa was a major commercial center of Thessaly, and Jewish merchants were involved in the grain trade (the Thessalian plain was one of the Ottoman Empire's breadbaskets), textiles, and regional commerce.

The community maintained multiple synagogues: a Romaniote synagogue (the Kahal Kadosh Romaniote) preserving the ancient Greek-Jewish rite, and several Sephardic congregations reflecting the different Iberian origins of settlers. The existence of both Romaniote and Sephardic institutions for centuries is a testament to the complexity of Larissa's Jewish identity.

Educational life was vigorous. The community maintained talmud torah schools and later a school of the Alliance Israélite Universelle. Larissa's Jews participated in the broader intellectual currents of Ottoman Jewry, maintaining correspondence and personal connections with the major centers of Salonika, Ioannina, and beyond.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Shlomo Ha-Kohen of Larissa (18th century)** — A Talmudic scholar whose legal responsa addressed questions arising from the mixed Romaniote-Sephardic character of the community.

**Members of the ha-Kohen family** — A family of kohanim (priestly caste) who served Larissa's Jewish community for generations. The name Cohen and variants (Kohén, Cohn) were common among Larissa's Jewish families, reflecting descent from the ancient priestly families of the Jewish people.

**Rabbi Nissim Yeoshua (late 19th–early 20th century)** — A community leader who navigated the transition from Ottoman to Greek rule and worked to integrate the Jewish community into the modern Greek state.

## The Ottoman-Greek Transition

Thessaly was annexed by Greece in 1881, decades before the Balkan Wars that brought Macedonia and Crete into the Greek state. The Jewish community of Larissa thus entered the Greek national framework earlier than most Greek Jewish communities, facing the challenges of modernization, state citizenship, and the sometimes tense relationship between Greek national identity and Jewish communal autonomy. The community was recognized as a religious minority under Greek law and maintained its institutions.

## The Holocaust

When Germany invaded Greece in April 1941, most of Thessaly came under Italian occupation, which was less immediately lethal for Jews than German occupation. The Italian surrender in September 1943 brought German forces to Thessaly, and the danger to Larissa's Jews increased dramatically.

However, the deportations from Thessaly were less thorough than those from Macedonia or Thrace. Some Larissa Jews received warnings from the resistance or sympathetic officials and were able to hide or flee to the mountains. Others were deported to Auschwitz in 1944. Exact figures are contested, but approximately 200–300 of Larissa's 800–900 Jews perished in the Holocaust — a devastating loss but significantly less than the near-total destruction suffered by communities like Salonika, Monastir, or Serres.

## Legacy Today

Unlike many Greek Jewish communities, Larissa's was not destroyed in the Holocaust. Enough survivors returned after the war to reconstitute the community. The Jewish community of Larissa today numbers approximately 200–300 persons and remains active, with a synagogue, community center, and active Jewish organizations.

The community represents one of the few remaining Romaniote-influenced communities in Greece. Though Ladino has largely disappeared as a spoken language among younger Greek Jews, traces of the Romaniote-Sephardic synthesis survive in Larissa's liturgical practices and communal memory.

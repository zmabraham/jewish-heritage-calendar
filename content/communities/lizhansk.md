# Lizhansk (Leżajsk)

## Overview

Lizhansk — Leżajsk in Polish — is a small town in southeastern Poland (historical Galicia) that occupies a place of singular importance in the history of Polish Hasidism. It was here that Rabbi Elimelech of Lizhansk (1717–1787) lived and taught, transforming Hasidism from a Ukrainian and Belarusian phenomenon into the dominant spiritual force of Polish Jewry. Lizhansk is one of the most important Hasidic pilgrimage sites in the world; on the anniversary of Rabbi Elimelech's death (21 Adar), tens of thousands of Hasidim travel to his grave from Israel, the United States, and across the Jewish world.

## Historical Background

Jews settled in Lizhansk in the seventeenth century as the town developed as a commercial center in the Rzeszów region of Galicia. The community grew modestly under Polish noble patronage, engaging in trade, crafts, and innkeeping in the surrounding countryside. Lizhansk's transformation into a spiritual capital came entirely through the figure of Rabbi Elimelech, who arrived as a young Hasidic teacher and remained for the rest of his life.

## Rabbi Elimelech of Lizhansk

Rabbi Elimelech Weisblum (1717–1787) was born in Łapczyca near Bochnia. He and his brother Zusha of Hanipol traveled together in a practice of galut (voluntary exile), wandering from community to community in a practice of spiritual self-abnegation common among early Hasidic leaders. Both brothers became disciples of Rabbi Dovber of Mezeritch, the Great Maggid, who was the heir to the Baal Shem Tov's movement. Elimelech eventually settled in Lizhansk and began attracting disciples from Polish Galicia.

His contribution to Hasidism was nothing less than a new social theology of the tzaddik — the righteous leader. For the Baal Shem Tov and the early Hasidic masters, the tzaddik had been primarily a spiritual guide who elevated the prayers of simple Jews by his mystical devotion. Rabbi Elimelech developed this into a comprehensive system in which the tzaddik is the channel connecting the Jewish people to God in every dimension of life — spiritual, material, and communal. The tzaddik prays for his followers' livelihoods, health, children, and prosperity, not merely their spiritual elevation. This "practical tzaddikism" made Hasidism accessible and urgently relevant to the masses of Polish Jews who faced grinding poverty, illness, and political insecurity.

## The Noam Elimelech

Rabbi Elimelech's major work, Noam Elimelech (The Pleasantness of Elimelech), published posthumously in Lwów in 1788, became one of the most widely read and reprinted books in the entire Hasidic library. The Noam Elimelech consists of homilies arranged according to the weekly Torah portion, each weaving together Kabbalistic interpretation, ethical instruction, and pastoral theology. The work elaborated the doctrine of the tzaddik in detail, describing the spiritual mechanics by which the holy leader intercedes with divine judgment to secure blessing for his people.

The Noam Elimelech has never gone out of print. It has been published in dozens of editions and remains a standard text in Hasidic study worldwide.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Elimelech trained a generation of disciples who became the founding figures of Galician and Polish Hasidism. His students included Rabbi Abraham Joshua Heschel of Apt (Apter Rebbe), Rabbi Menachem Mendel of Rimanov, Rabbi Avraham of Stretyn, Rabbi Moshe Leib of Sassov, Rabbi Naphtali of Ropshitz — in effect, the founding fathers of virtually every major Polish and Galician Hasidic dynasty. This gives Lizhansk a unique position as the seedbed from which the entire forest of Polish Hasidism grew.

His son Rabbi Eleazar of Lizhansk succeeded him and maintained the court, but the Lizhansk tradition was perpetuated more through the disciples than through the dynastic succession.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The synagogue of Lizhansk, associated with Rabbi Elimelech's court, served as a gathering place for the growing number of Hasidic pilgrims during his lifetime. After his death, the ohel (tomb structure) over his grave became the primary focal point of pilgrimage. The Jewish community of Lizhansk also maintained a yeshiva, beit midrash, charitable organizations, and the full institutional apparatus of a Galician kehilla.

## The Pilgrimage Tradition

The pilgrimage to Rabbi Elimelech's grave on 21 Adar (the anniversary of his death, which typically falls in February or March) became, over the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, one of the great institutions of Hasidic religious life. Hasidim believed — and believe — that praying at the grave of a tzaddik carries particular spiritual efficacy, that the soul of the righteous remains connected to its earthly burial site, and that petitions offered there rise more readily to heaven. Tens of thousands of Hasidim descend on Lizhansk annually for this date, transforming the small Polish town into a scene of intense, tearful, joyful prayer.

## Decline & Dispersal

The Jewish community of Lizhansk was murdered in the Holocaust. The Germans established a ghetto in 1941; deportations to the Bełżec extermination camp in 1942 killed the vast majority of the community. The synagogue was destroyed. Only the grave of Rabbi Elimelech survived, having been protected — in a remarkable act — by local Poles who maintained the fence and marker even during the occupation.

## Legacy Today

The ohel of Rabbi Elimelech in Lizhansk is today a functioning pilgrimage site drawing visitors year-round and enormous crowds on 21 Adar. The grave has been enclosed in a permanent structure with facilities for prayer and Torah study. Polish authorities have cooperated with Jewish organizations to maintain the site, which has become one of the most significant expressions of Jewish religious tourism in Poland.

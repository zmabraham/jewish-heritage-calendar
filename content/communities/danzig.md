# Danzig

## Overview

Danzig — now Gdańsk, Poland — was one of the great Baltic port cities of Europe and home to a Jewish community that lived at the intersection of Polish, German, and Prussian sovereignties across the centuries. As a Free City under different forms of international protection, Danzig's Jews navigated complex political arrangements with characteristic adaptability. The city's fate in September 1939 — when Nazi Germany's demand to annex Danzig became the pretext for the invasion of Poland — made it the geographic flashpoint of the Second World War, and with it the beginning of the Holocaust.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Danzig was long restricted. The powerful German merchant guilds of this Hanseatic city resisted Jewish competition fiercely. Jews obtained permission to visit Danzig's famous fairs but were excluded from permanent residence for most of the medieval and early modern periods.

The formal establishment of a permanent Jewish community came only in 1773, when Frederick the Great of Prussia, who had recently acquired Danzig, permitted ten Jewish families to settle. The community grew slowly at first; by 1810, it numbered approximately 1,500, and the pace of growth accelerated after emancipation.

## The Free City Period

The most distinctive period in Danzig's Jewish history was the interwar era, when Danzig became a Free City under the League of Nations (1920–1939). This anomalous political status — neither German nor Polish but an international protectorate — gave the city's Jews a peculiar legal position: they were citizens of the Free City, not of Germany, yet dominated culturally by German language and institutions.

The Free City period saw the Jewish community face growing Nazi pressure from within the German population long before Hitler took power in Germany. The Danzig Senate was dominated by Nazis from 1933; by 1935, Danzig's Jews were subjected to Nuremberg-style laws even before they formally applied throughout Germany. The Free City was a laboratory of Nazi antisemitism.

## Notable Figures

David Samuel Sassoon (1792–1864) and the Sassoon family, though primarily associated with Baghdad and India, had commercial connections to the Baltic trade through Danzig.

Albert Forster, the Nazi Gauleiter of Danzig from 1930, was among the most vicious persecutors of Jews in the early Nazi period — a reminder that Danzig's Jewish community faced antisemitic violence earlier and more intensely than most German communities.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Great Synagogue of Danzig, completed in 1887, was a magnificent Moorish-Renaissance building, among the finest synagogue architecture in the Baltic region. It was sold by the Jewish community in 1938 under Nazi pressure — an act of desperation as the community sought to raise funds for emigration — and subsequently demolished by the Nazis.

The community maintained a smaller synagogue (which did survive and was eventually restored), a mikveh, a cemetery, and educational institutions appropriate to a community of its size.

## Cultural Life

Danzig's Jewish culture was German in language and cultural orientation, reflecting the city's character as a German Baltic port. The community participated in the cultural and intellectual life of a major trading city, with Jewish merchants, lawyers, physicians, and professionals visible in civic life.

The interwar period saw intense political activity: Zionist organizations, Bundist labor movements, youth groups, and emigration campaigns all competed for the community's attention. The accelerating Nazi pressure made Jewish cultural life increasingly precarious from 1933 onward.

## Decline & Transformation

The community that numbered approximately 12,000 in 1920 had been reduced to approximately 7,000 by 1939 through emigration and persecution. When German troops entered Danzig on September 1, 1939 — the same day they invaded Poland — the remaining Jewish community faced immediate danger. Some had already fled. Those who remained were subjected to brutal treatment; men were immediately arrested and sent to concentration camps. By the time systematic deportations from the city began, relatively few Jews remained.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Danzig's Jewish community was essentially destroyed; its descendants are scattered across Israel, the United States, South America, and other parts of the world. The surviving small Danzig Synagogue (the Kleine Synagoge on Langgasse) was restored in the post-war period and serves as a reminder of the Jewish presence in what is now Gdańsk.

Danzig's position as the geographic starting point of World War II gives it a particular significance in the Holocaust narrative. The city's transformation from a German-Jewish cultural center to a city entirely emptied of Jews represents in concentrated form the broader history of the destruction of Central European Jewry.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 1773 (permanent community)
- **Peak Population**: ~12,000 (1920)
- **Languages**: German, Yiddish, Polish, Hebrew
- **Status Today**: Tiny Jewish presence in Gdańsk; Kleine Synagoge preserved

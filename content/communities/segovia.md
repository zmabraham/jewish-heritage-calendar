# Segovia

## Overview

Segovia, the magnificent walled city of Old Castile crowned by its Roman aqueduct and fairytale alcázar, was home to one of the most distinguished Jewish communities of late medieval Spain. The city is particularly associated with Abraham Seneor, the most powerful Jewish figure in late-fifteenth-century Castile — a man who served as royal treasurer, chief judge of the Jewish communities, and close advisor to Ferdinand and Isabella — until his dramatic conversion to Christianity just two years before the general Expulsion. Segovia also witnessed extraordinary converso history and produced some of Castile's most notable New Christian families.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Segovia is documented from the early centuries of Christian rule in Castile, probably the eleventh or twelfth century. The city's importance as an administrative and commercial center in the Castilian heartland attracted Jewish merchants and financiers. By the thirteenth century, the community was well-established with a recognized aljama.

The community occupied a quarter known as the Judería, located in the city's walled area near the present-day Church of Corpus Christi — itself originally a synagogue. Medieval Segovia was a city of wool merchants, and Jewish financiers and traders were integral to this economy.

## Golden Age

Segovia reached its peak of Jewish prominence in the late fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, when the community produced Abraham Seneor and his circle. Seneor's remarkable career connected Segovia directly to the highest levels of Castilian royal power. He served as chief tax-farmer for the crown, arranged the marriage negotiations between Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile (an act of Jewish political service of immense historical consequence), and was appointed chief rabbi (rab de la corte) of Castilian Jewry in 1488 — a royal appointment rather than an elected communal position, reflecting the degree to which he functioned as a royal agent within the Jewish community.

Segovian Jews were prominent in the wool trade, tax-farming, and royal finance. The community at its height probably numbered two to four thousand.

## Notable Figures

Abraham Seneor (c. 1412–1493) dominates Segovia's Jewish history. Born here, he rose to become the most politically powerful Jew in Spain since Samuel HaNagid. He arranged the 1469 marriage of Ferdinand and Isabella that united the crowns of Aragon and Castile, arguably the most consequential political act of the fifteenth century. He served both monarchs as chief royal tax contractor, accumulating enormous wealth and influence. In 1492, when the Expulsion decree was announced, Seneor did not go into exile: at the age of approximately eighty, he converted to Christianity under the sponsorship of Ferdinand and Isabella themselves, taking the baptismal name Fernán Núñez Coronel. His conversion — witnessed by the king and queen who had been his patrons — was experienced as a devastating blow by the Jewish community he had ostensibly led.

Isaac Abravanel, the great Talmudist, philosopher, and biblical commentator who served as royal treasurer alongside Seneor and famously refused conversion in 1492, choosing exile instead, was Seneor's colleague and in some ways his foil — representing the alternative response to the impossible choice of that year.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Segovia's most remarkable Jewish monument is the former main synagogue, converted to the Church of Corpus Christi. This large Gothic structure preserves the spatial organization of its synagogue predecessor and retains some architectural details from the Jewish period. A fire in the nineteenth century and subsequent rebuilding altered the structure significantly, but the building's Jewish origins are well-documented.

The Church of Corpus Christi is associated with an anti-Jewish legend common to many Castilian towns — the supposed desecration of the Host by Jews — though historians regard these accusations as pure fabrication used to justify persecution.

## Cultural Life

Segovia's Jewish culture was sophisticated and deeply embedded in Castilian commercial life. The community maintained Talmudic scholarship alongside its commercial orientation. Hebrew manuscripts were produced and copied in Segovia, and the community followed the Castilian liturgical rite.

Converso culture in Segovia was particularly complex: the descendants of converted Jews maintained crypto-Jewish practices for generations, creating a hidden religious life that the Inquisition worked systematically to uncover and punish.

## Decline & Transformation

The 1391 pogroms affected Segovia, producing a significant converso population. The converso bishop Alfonso de la Torre — a bishop of Segovia born to a converted Jewish family — illustrates how converted families could rise to the highest ecclesiastical ranks while their religious sincerity remained suspect in the eyes of Old Christians.

The Inquisition operated actively in Segovia from the 1480s, investigating converso families and conducting autos de fe. The 1492 Expulsion removed the remaining Jewish population. Abraham Seneor's dramatic conversion meant that even Segovia's most prominent Jewish leader chose baptism over exile.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Seneor's descendants under their Christian name Coronel became prominent Castilian nobles, ironically maintaining the family's social prominence through conversion rather than exile. Many Segovian Jewish families joined the broader Sephardic diaspora.

The Church of Corpus Christi continues to function as a Carmelite convent, preserving the space of Segovia's main medieval synagogue. Heritage tours of Segovia now include the former Judería and its Jewish monuments as a standard part of the city's historical presentation.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 11th–12th century; established aljama from early Castilian period
- **Peak Population**: 2,000–4,000 (15th century)
- **Languages**: Castilian, Hebrew
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; former synagogue (Corpus Christi) preserved; heritage trail developed

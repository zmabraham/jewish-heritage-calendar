# Radin

## Overview

Radin was a tiny shtetl in the Minsk region with a single extraordinary claim on Jewish historical memory: it was the home of Rabbi Yisrael Meir Kagan (1838–1933), universally known as the *Chofetz Chaim* — one of the most beloved and influential rabbinic figures in modern Jewish history. The Chofetz Chaim spent the entire ninety-five years of his life in or near Radin, refusing the prestigious rabbinic positions offered to him from across the world, and transforming this obscure village into a place of pilgrimage. His yeshiva in Radin, which attracted students from across Eastern Europe and the world, ensured that his approach to Jewish law, ethics, and piety became formative for Orthodox Judaism worldwide.

## Historical Origins

Radin (Polish: Radoń; Yiddish: Radin) was a village in the Grodno Governorate of the Russian Empire, with a modest Jewish population engaged in typical small-town commerce and crafts. It had no special distinction before Rabbi Yisrael Meir Kagan settled there in the 1860s. He had married into a family in Radin and set up a small grocery store run primarily by his wife, freeing him to spend his days in Torah study. His determination to remain in this modest setting even as his fame grew became a defining element of his persona: the greatest sage of his generation living in a tiny village, untouched by the ambitions and pretensions of the rabbinical marketplace.

## Golden Age

The Chofetz Chaim's influence reached its height in the last third of the nineteenth century and the first third of the twentieth. His first and most famous work, published anonymously in 1873 under the title *Chofetz Chaim* (the name derives from Psalm 34: "Who is the man who desires life"), was a comprehensive treatment of the laws of *lashon hara* (forbidden speech, gossip, and slander). This work — addressing a ubiquitous human failing with halachic precision and moral depth — struck a chord across the Jewish world. It became one of the bestselling Jewish books of the nineteenth century, and its anonymous author was identified and celebrated.

Subsequent works established him as a towering halachic authority: the *Mishnah Berurah*, a comprehensive commentary on the Orach Chaim section of the *Shulchan Aruch* (the laws of daily life and prayer), became the standard legal guide for Ashkenazic Jewry and remains so today. His *Ahavat Chesed* treated the laws of charitable giving. His *Machaneh Yisrael* addressed the religious challenges of Jewish soldiers in the Tsar's army.

The Radin Yeshiva (*Yeshivat Chofetz Chaim*), formally organized in the 1870s under Rabbi Naftali Tzvi Leibowitz, attracted students from across Eastern Europe who wanted to study in the town of the great sage. The Chofetz Chaim himself taught in the yeshiva, and his personal example — extraordinary piety, ethical sensitivity, accessibility to common people, and genuine humility — shaped the culture of the institution.

## Notable Figures

The Chofetz Chaim dominated Radin absolutely. No other figure from the town approaches his significance. Rabbi **Naftali Tzvi Leibowitz** ran the yeshiva's day-to-day operations. Among the yeshiva's distinguished alumni were numerous rabbis who led communities across Eastern Europe. The Chofetz Chaim's personal legend was so powerful that stories about him proliferated widely, many of them illustrating his extraordinary *tzidkut* (saintliness): his refusal of honor, his care for poor students, his refusal to speak ill of anyone even indirectly, his practice of returning small amounts of overcharged money from his grocery store.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Chofetz Chaim's home, his grocery store, the beit midrash where he studied, and the yeshiva building were all within a few steps of each other in tiny Radin. The yeshiva had a study hall, dormitories, and dining facilities funded by donations from communities across the world who considered supporting the Chofetz Chaim's yeshiva a supreme religious privilege. The community synagogue and the Jewish cemetery, where the Chofetz Chaim is buried, completed the village's Jewish landscape.

## Cultural Life

Radin's Jewish culture was defined entirely by the personality and teaching of the Chofetz Chaim. Unlike urban communities with competing political parties and cultural movements, Radin was a small traditional village where Torah study, prayer, and the practice of *mitzvot* constituted daily life. Students at the yeshiva came from varied backgrounds — some from Hasidic families, others from Mitnagdic homes, some from more acculturated environments — but all were drawn by the extraordinary spiritual atmosphere the Chofetz Chaim created. Visiting rabbis and scholars came from across Eastern Europe to seek his counsel and blessing.

## Decline & Transformation

The Chofetz Chaim died in 1933 at the reported age of 95 — still lucid, still writing, still teaching. He had witnessed the Great War, the Russian Revolution, the emergence of the Soviet Union, and the rise of Hitler, and had responded to each crisis with writings and public statements calling for Jewish unity, Torah study, and ethical renewal. The yeshiva continued under his successor for several years. World War II brought catastrophe: Radin was occupied by the Soviet Union in 1939 and by Germany in 1941. The Jewish community of Radin — several hundred people — was murdered in 1942. The Chofetz Chaim's grave was desecrated but survived; it was later restored and became a site of pilgrimage.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Chofetz Chaim's legacy is immeasurable. The *Mishnah Berurah* remains the standard Ashkenazic legal code for daily life and is studied continuously in homes and study halls worldwide. The *Chofetz Chaim* book on speech ethics has spawned a worldwide movement — the Chofetz Chaim Heritage Foundation — dedicated to promoting ethical speech in Orthodox communities globally. Yeshivot named for him operate in New York and Israel. His grave in Radin is visited by thousands of pilgrims annually, maintained by an organization dedicated to its preservation. In the broader Orthodox world, the name "Chofetz Chaim" evokes not merely a scholar but an entire ideal of ethical and spiritual greatness.

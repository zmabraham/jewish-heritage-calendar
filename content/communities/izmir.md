# İzmir

## Overview

İzmir, the great Aegean port city known to its Jewish community as Esmirna and to the wider world as Smyrna, was one of the most important centers of Sephardic Jewish life in the Ottoman Empire. The city is particularly famous as the birthplace of Sabbatai Zevi (1626–1676), the self-proclaimed Messiah whose movement attracted the largest mass messianic following in Jewish history before convulsing and then shattering when Sabbatai converted to Islam under Ottoman pressure. İzmir's Jewish community also distinguished itself as a center of Ladino printing, commercial enterprise, and intellectual life that bridged the Ottoman and European worlds.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Smyrna dates to antiquity — the city is mentioned in early Christian sources as having a significant Jewish community. A Romaniote community maintained presence through the Byzantine period. The Sephardic immigration after 1492 transformed the community, giving it the Ladino character it would maintain for centuries.

İzmir's commercial importance as a major Aegean port made it attractive for Jewish merchants. The city served as a gateway between the Ottoman interior and European maritime trade, and Jewish merchants — with their connections to Sephardic networks in Amsterdam, Livorno, and Salonika — were uniquely positioned to exploit this intermediary role.

## Golden Age

İzmir reached its peak as a Jewish center in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, when the community may have numbered twenty to thirty thousand. The city's commercial vitality, its relative distance from Ottoman political centers, and its European commercial connections gave the community particular dynamism.

The involvement of İzmir's Jews in the Levant trade — connecting European merchants with Ottoman products and vice versa — created a class of wealthy merchants who funded communal institutions and engaged with both Western and Eastern intellectual currents.

## Notable Figures

Sabbatai Zevi (1626–1676) is İzmir's most consequential and controversial figure. Born into a prosperous İzmir Jewish family, he showed signs of unusual religious intensity from youth. In 1648, during the period of the Chmielnicki massacres in Poland, he proclaimed himself the Messiah. His movement lay dormant for years before exploding into a pan-Jewish phenomenon in 1665–1666 under the influence of his prophet Nathan of Gaza. By late 1665, virtually the entire Jewish world — from Amsterdam to Yemen — was caught up in messianic anticipation. In September 1666, brought before the Ottoman Sultan, Sabbatai converted to Islam. The apostasy shattered his movement, though a group of followers — the Dönmeh — converted with him and maintained a crypto-Sabbatean existence in Salonika for centuries.

Rabbi Joseph ibn Ezra and other İzmir scholars maintained the community's halakhic and intellectual life alongside the messianic controversies.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

İzmir's Jewish quarter was centered in the Kemeraltı area of the city. The community maintained numerous synagogues, of which several survived into the twentieth century. The Shaar Hashamayim synagogue and other historic structures reflected centuries of Jewish communal life.

The 1922 fire that destroyed much of İzmir — in the aftermath of the Greek-Turkish War — consumed large portions of the Jewish quarter, though some structures survived. The community maintained a school network and cultural institutions.

## Cultural Life

İzmir's Jewish culture was Ladino-speaking and commercially oriented, with strong connections to the broader Sephardic world through trade and family networks. The city's European commercial relationships gave its Jewish community particular exposure to Enlightenment ideas, and İzmir was relatively early in the adoption of secular education and European cultural styles among its Jewish population.

The Sabbatean movement left a lasting mark on İzmir's religious culture: the tension between normative rabbinic Judaism and the messianic and antinomian currents that Sabbatai Zevi had unleashed continued to influence Jewish religious life in the city for generations.

## Decline & Transformation

The 1922 fire and the Greek-Turkish population exchange transformed İzmir dramatically. Many Jewish families, alarmed by the violence and instability, emigrated. The twentieth century brought progressive decline: emigration to Israel, the Americas, and Europe reduced the community from tens of thousands to a few thousand.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Sabbatai Zevi's messianic movement, despite ending in apostasy, was one of the most significant events in early modern Jewish history. It revealed the messianic tensions within post-expulsion Sephardic society, generated an enormous literature, and has been extensively analyzed by scholars of Jewish religion and history, most notably Gershom Scholem.

İzmir today has a small but active Jewish community of perhaps 1,500–2,000 people, maintaining synagogues and cultural life. The city's Jewish heritage is commemorated through the Sephardic community's institutions.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Ancient; Sephardic community established post-1492; peak 17th–18th centuries
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 25,000–35,000 (17th–18th centuries)
- **Languages**: Ladino, Hebrew, Ottoman Turkish, Greek
- **Status Today**: Small community (~1,500–2,000); several synagogues maintained

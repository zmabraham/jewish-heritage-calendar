# Montpellier

## Overview

Montpellier, the university city of Languedoc on the Mediterranean coast of southern France, was home to one of the most intellectually significant Jewish communities of the medieval period. The city's famous medical school — one of the earliest in Europe — was founded with substantial Jewish input, and Jewish physicians trained in Montpellier's tradition served courts and communities across Europe. The city is also remembered as the epicenter of the great Maimonidean controversy that divided Sephardic Jewry in the early fourteenth century, a debate about the limits of philosophical inquiry that reveals the tensions inherent in the golden age's synthesis of Judaism and Aristotelian philosophy.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Montpellier dates to at least the tenth century. The city's lords — initially the Guilhem dynasty and later the kings of Aragon, who acquired it in the twelfth century — maintained broadly tolerant policies toward the Jewish community, reflecting the general stance of Aragonese-Catalan power in the region.

The community grew significantly in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, benefiting from Montpellier's emergence as a center of learning and commerce. The city's location on trade routes between the Mediterranean coast and northern France, and its growing reputation as a center of medicine, attracted Jewish settlers of particular intellectual caliber.

## Golden Age

Montpellier's golden age as a Jewish center extended from roughly 1150 to 1300. The city's medical school, which may have grown partly from the practice of Jewish and Arabic physicians, became the leading medical institution of Western Europe, and Jewish physicians trained there or in institutions that shared its approach were among the most sought-after medical practitioners of the age.

The translation movement centered in Montpellier was of enormous importance: Jewish scholars translated Arabic medical, philosophical, and scientific works into Hebrew and from Hebrew or Arabic into Latin, making the learning of al-Andalus available to the Latin-reading scholarly world. The Ibn Tibbon family — Judah ibn Tibbon, Samuel ibn Tibbon, and Moses ibn Tibbon — did much of their work in Montpellier and Lunel, producing Hebrew translations that shaped the entire course of Sephardic intellectual life.

## Notable Figures

Samuel ibn Tibbon (c. 1150–1230) translated Maimonides' Guide for the Perplexed from Arabic into Hebrew in Montpellier, a project he undertook in correspondence with Maimonides himself. This translation — completed in 1204 — made the Guide accessible to the Hebrew-reading world and ignited the controversies over its philosophical content that would convulse Sephardic Jewry for the following century. Samuel's Hebrew translation style, though technical and sometimes opaque, established a philosophical vocabulary in Hebrew that influenced Jewish intellectual discourse for centuries.

Jacob Anatoli (c. 1194–1256), a Jewish physician in Montpellier, translated Averroes' commentaries on Aristotle from Arabic into Hebrew, making the major works of Islamic philosophy accessible to Jewish readers. Anatoli was an enthusiastic Maimonidean rationalist whose public lectures interpreting biblical texts philosophically caused considerable scandal.

Gersonides (Rabbi Levi ben Gershom, Ralbag, 1288–1344), though based in Orange and other Provençal towns, was deeply connected to the Montpellier scholarly network. His Wars of the Lord presented a systematic philosophical theology that pushed Aristotelian rationalism further than any previous Jewish thinker.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Montpellier's Jewish quarter was substantial, and the community maintained a large synagogue, schools, and the typical institutions of a major aljama. The city's connection to the medical school meant that Jewish physicians were a particularly prominent element of the community.

The yeshiva attracted scholars from across the Sephardic world, and the community's library was reportedly excellent.

## Cultural Life

Montpellier's intellectual culture was characterized by philosophical adventurousness and scientific engagement. The community embodied the Andalusian synthesis at its most ambitious: Torah study, Aristotelian philosophy, Arabic medicine, and astronomical calculation coexisted and interpenetrated. The Ibn Tibbon family's translation project effectively established Montpellier as the primary conduit through which Islamic-Jewish intellectual achievement passed into Hebrew-language culture.

The Maimonidean controversy, which erupted in Montpellier in the early fourteenth century, reflected the genuine dangers of this synthesis: when an anti-rationalist faction under Rabbi Solomon ibn Adret of Barcelona sought to restrict the study of philosophy for young students, Montpellier's community became a center of resistance, defending the Maimonidean tradition of philosophical inquiry as integral to Jewish intellectual life.

## Decline & Transformation

The transfer of Montpellier to the French crown (1349) brought harsher conditions. The expulsions of Jews from French royal territories — 1306 (under Philip IV), 1322, and finally 1394 — successively eliminated and attempted to restore the Jewish community. After 1394, Jewish life in Montpellier was effectively ended by royal decree.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The translation legacy of Montpellier's Jewish community is one of the most important in medieval intellectual history. Samuel ibn Tibbon's translation of the Guide for the Perplexed, produced in Montpellier, made one of the most consequential books in Jewish history accessible to the Hebrew-reading world. The city's medical tradition flowed into European medicine through its physicians and their students. Gersonides' philosophical works continue to be studied by Jewish philosophers and historians of medieval thought.

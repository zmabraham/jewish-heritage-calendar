# Rzeszów

## Overview

Rzeszów (Yiddish: *Reyshe*; German: *Reichshof*) was the principal city of eastern Galicia's central region, a community that served as a regional hub for the dense network of Jewish towns and villages of Galician Poland. Rzeszów and its surrounding region were among the most heavily Jewish areas of Europe — the Galician heartland where Hasidism had its origins in the eighteenth century, where the Baal Shem Tov had walked, and where Hasidic courts proliferated with exceptional density. The city itself was a significant Jewish center with a distinguished rabbinic tradition, important Hasidic courts, and the full apparatus of Galician Jewish civilization. Its destruction in the Holocaust came through the German occupation of 1939–1944, which killed the vast majority of the region's Jews.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Rzeszów in the sixteenth century under the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. The city was a royal town (*miasto królewskie*), which generally gave Jews somewhat stronger legal protection than in towns controlled by private nobility. The community grew steadily through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, supported by the city's role as a regional commercial center and market. Jews were prominent in the cloth trade, crafts, and money transactions.

The Galician partition of 1772 brought Rzeszów under Austrian rule. Austrian administration brought significant changes to Jewish life: Emperor Joseph II's Edict of Toleration (1782) and subsequent reforms pushed Jews toward social integration, secular education, and acculturation. Galicia under Austria was the setting for one of the most complex confrontations between traditional Jewish life and modernity in Jewish history.

## Golden Age

Rzeszów's Hasidic tradition gave the community much of its distinctive character. The region around Rzeszów was one of the birthplaces of Hasidism — the Baal Shem Tov (Israel ben Eliezer, c. 1700–1760) had lived and taught in Podolia and Galicia, and his disciples established courts throughout the region. Rzeszów itself had several significant Hasidic courts. The Reisher Rav (Rabbi of Rzeszów) was a position of regional significance.

One of the most important nineteenth-century Hasidic figures associated with the broader Rzeszów region was **Rabbi Elimelech of Lizhensk** (1717–1787), whose court in Leżajsk (a nearby town) was one of the most influential in Polish Hasidism. Rabbi Elimelech's *Noam Elimelech* is one of the most widely studied works of Galician Hasidism, and his grave at Leżajsk remains one of the most visited Hasidic pilgrimage sites in Poland, attracting tens of thousands of visitors on his yahrzeit (Adar 21).

The community's institutions were extensive: multiple synagogues including the magnificent Rzeszów Old Synagogue (built in the seventeenth century and surviving to this day as a museum), a significant rabbinic court, Hebrew schools, and an active Zionist movement in the interwar period.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Uri of Strelisk** — known as the *Seraph*, a major early Galician Hasidic figure associated with the region. **Rabbi Elimelech of Lizhensk** — preeminent Galician Hasidic rebbe; though based in Leżajsk, his influence defined the regional Hasidic character. **Rabbi Tzaddok HaKohen of Lublin** — another great Galician figure whose tradition extended to Rzeszów's orbit. **Rabbi Horowitz** (various), rabbis of the city in different generations. The interwar Jewish community produced Zionists who settled in Palestine. Many Rzeszów Jews emigrated to Argentina and the United States before the war.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Old Synagogue of Rzeszów (built c. 1613, fortified in 1624 with defensive towers) is one of the most impressive surviving Renaissance synagogues in Poland. Its fortified design — towers at the corners allowing defenders to cover all approaches — reflects the insecurity of Jewish life in early modern Poland, when Jewish communities sometimes served as military assets to noble patrons in exchange for protection. The New Town Synagogue (built in the nineteenth century) was another major structure. The city also had numerous Hasidic shtiblakh, a yeshiva, elementary schools, a Jewish hospital, and an extensive communal infrastructure.

## Cultural Life

Rzeszów's Jewish culture was predominantly Hasidic and traditional but engaged with modernizing currents through the Austrian educational system and the Galician Haskalah. The city had Hebrew and Yiddish schools, Zionist youth movements, and cultural clubs. The Polish language was more important here than Yiddish in the daily lives of more acculturated Jews — the Austrian educational system promoted German and later Polish. Galician Jewish culture had its own distinctive flavor: a blend of Hasidic devotion, rabbinic learning, Austro-Hungarian civic culture, and Zionist aspiration.

## Decline & Transformation

German occupation began in September 1939. The Rzeszów Ghetto was established in January 1942, and mass deportations to the Bełżec extermination camp began in July 1942. A second major deportation followed in November 1942. By the end of 1942, the great majority of Rzeszów Jews had been murdered. Several hundred Jews were kept in a labor camp until 1944; most of these were eventually murdered as well. Of approximately 14,000 Rzeszów Jews, fewer than 1,000 survived.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Old Synagogue of Rzeszów has been restored and functions as an art gallery and exhibition space — one of the better-preserved Renaissance synagogues in Poland. The Galician Jewish Museum in Kraków (a nearby city) has exhibits relevant to the Rzeszów region. The grave of Rabbi Elimelech of Lizhensk at Leżajsk draws thousands of Hasidic pilgrims annually. Rzeszów survivors and their descendants have published memorial books and maintained organizations in Israel, the United States, and Argentina.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 16th century; under Austrian rule from 1772
- **Peak Population**: ~14,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Polish, German, Hebrew
- **Status Today**: Old Synagogue preserved as museum; community destroyed; Hasidic pilgrimage site (Lizhensk) nearby

# Poznań

## Overview

Poznań — known in German as Posen — was the capital of the Wielkopolska (Greater Poland) region and one of the most important Jewish communities in Poland and later in Prussia. Situated at the crossroads between German and Polish Jewish worlds, the Poznań community experienced the full complexity of that in-between existence: Polish kings and then Prussian kings, Yiddish and German, Orthodox tradition and Enlightenment modernization. The community produced major scholars and communal leaders, and its complex history reflects the broader drama of Central European Jewish identity.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Poznań dates to the thirteenth century, when Jews were invited to settle in the nascent Polish state by princes who valued their commercial skills. The community grew rapidly in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries under Polish royal protection, and Poznań became the center of Jewish life in Wielkopolska.

A major fire in 1447 destroyed much of the Jewish quarter; the community rebuilt. The Jewish quarter in Poznań — centered on Żydowska (Jewish) Street — developed into a substantial neighborhood with a synagogue, yeshiva, and all the institutions of a major community.

## Golden Age

The sixteenth century was the community's golden age. Poznań's Jewish community was one of the largest and most influential in Poland, and its scholarly reputation was considerable. The community participated in the Council of Four Lands — the Polish-Jewish parliamentary institution that governed Jewish communal affairs across Poland — and Poznań's representatives were prominent voices in that forum.

Rabbi Akiva Gombin (Hayim ben Bezalel's brother-in-law) and several other prominent scholars were associated with the Poznań yeshiva. The community's connections to both the Polish Jewish world and the German Jewish world gave it a distinctive character.

The transition from Polish to Prussian rule came in 1793, when Poznań was annexed by Prussia in the Second Partition of Poland. This transition fundamentally altered the community's cultural and political context: suddenly, the community was Prussian, subject to Prussian Jewish policies, and exposed to German cultural and intellectual influences.

## Notable Figures

Akiva Eger (1761–1837), one of the greatest Talmudic authorities of the nineteenth century, served as rabbi of Poznań (Posen) from 1815 until his death. His responsa — thousands of legal decisions — are among the most important in post-medieval halakhic literature, and his Talmud commentary (Gilyon HaShas) is printed in virtually every standard edition of the Talmud. Eger was also notable for his practical leadership during a cholera epidemic in 1831, when he organized community response and earned widespread admiration.

His son-in-law Moses Sofer (the Chatam Sofer) of Pressburg was among the most important Orthodox leaders of the century; the family connection between Poznań and Pressburg linked two of the most important Orthodox communities in Central Europe.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Old Synagogue (Stara Synagoga) in Poznań, rebuilt multiple times, was the heart of the community's religious life. In the nineteenth century, the community built a larger and more impressive synagogue reflecting the prosperity of the Prussian period. The Jewish cemetery, established in the sixteenth century, contains the graves of Akiva Eger and other community leaders.

## Cultural Life

Poznań's Jewish culture navigated the tension between Polish and German influences with characteristic resourcefulness. In the Polish period, Yiddish was the primary language of daily life; in the Prussian period, German increasingly replaced it among the educated class. The community maintained a Hebrew press and produced scholars who contributed to both Ashkenazic halakhic literature and the emerging Wissenschaft des Judentums.

The community was divided between traditionalists who resisted Prussian modernization and reformers who embraced it. The presence of a rabbi of Akiva Eger's stature tipped the balance toward tradition throughout the first half of the nineteenth century.

## Decline & Transformation

Following German unification in 1871, Poznań was part of the Prussian Province of Posen. German nationalism intensified pressure on the Jewish community, caught between German and Polish national identities. After 1918, Poznań was included in the new Polish state; many German-identified Jews emigrated westward, while the community took on a more Polish character.

The German occupation in September 1939 immediately subjected Poznań's Jews to severe persecution. Most Jews were expelled to the General Government (occupied Poland); those who remained in the city were killed or deported to death camps. The pre-war community of approximately 2,000 (much reduced from its nineteenth-century peak) was almost entirely destroyed.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Today, Wrocław/Breslau's more prominent place in Polish Jewish history has somewhat overshadowed Poznań. The city has a very small Jewish community. The grave of Akiva Eger in the old Jewish cemetery is maintained and visited by Orthodox Jews from around the world, a pilgrimage site honoring one of the greatest halakhic authorities of the modern era.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 13th century (documented)
- **Peak Population**: ~8,000 (mid-19th century)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Polish, German, Hebrew
- **Status Today**: Tiny community; Akiva Eger's grave a pilgrimage site

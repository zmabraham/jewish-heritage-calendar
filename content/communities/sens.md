# Sens

## Overview

Sens, an ancient archiepiscopal city in Burgundy on the Yonne River, figures in Jewish history primarily as the site of the Synod of Sens in 1150 — a landmark gathering of northern French and German Jewish communities presided over by Rabbeinu Tam (Rabbi Jacob ben Meir, c. 1100–1171), the greatest Talmudic authority of his generation. The synod issued ordinances that governed Ashkenazic communal life for centuries and demonstrated the capacity of medieval Jewish communities to organize transregional governance structures without state authority.

## Historical Background

Jews had lived in Sens and the surrounding Burgundian region since at least the Carolingian period (eighth–ninth centuries). The city's importance as a major archbishopric and commercial center made it a natural location for Jewish settlement and trade. Sens Jews engaged in moneylending, commerce in agricultural products, and trade along the Seine-Yonne river system connecting Paris and Burgundy.

The medieval Jewish community occupied a defined quarter near the cathedral, as was typical in northern French episcopal cities. Relations with the archbishopric were complex: the church claimed both theological authority over Jews (as stubborn nonbelievers who must be corrected but not killed) and practical interest in restricting Jewish moneylending that competed with Christian creditors.

## The Synod of Sens (1150)

The Synod of Sens convened in 1150 under the leadership of Rabbeinu Tam, who was based nearby in Ramerupt (Aube) but whose authority extended throughout Ashkenazic Jewry. The synod brought together rabbis and communal leaders from northern France and the Rhineland — the two main regions of Ashkenazic Jewish life — to address pressing issues of communal governance and halakhic practice.

The ordinances (takkanot) issued at the Synod of Sens addressed several critical matters. They regulated marital practices, restricting polygamy (which was theoretically permitted by biblical law but had been circumscribed by the famous ordinance of Rabbeinu Gershom of Mainz, c. 1000 CE). They established procedures for excommunication (cherem), which was the main enforcement tool available to Jewish communities without state policing powers. They also regulated commercial practices, particularly those involving contracts with Christians that might be perceived as violations of rabbinic prohibitions.

The synod's significance lay not only in its specific rulings but in its demonstration that Ashkenazic Jewry could function as a self-governing entity across political borders — that the authority of halakhic leadership could create binding obligations for communities scattered across France and Germany, without any secular enforcement mechanism.

## Notable Figures

Rabbeinu Tam (c. 1100–1171), Rashi's grandson through his daughter, was the dominant intellectual and halakhic authority of twelfth-century Ashkenazic Jewry. He survived a near-fatal attack during the Second Crusade pogrom of 1146, when crusaders burst into his home and wounded him severely before he was rescued by a local nobleman. He continued his scholarly activity for decades thereafter, corresponding with scholars across the Jewish world and producing the Tosafot (critical additions) to the Talmud that, collected and edited by his disciples, became a permanent feature of Talmudic study.

Rabbeinu Tam's rulings on many matters of Jewish law — often differing from his grandfather Rashi — became standard positions in the Ashkenazic tradition. His stringent interpretation regarding the order of Torah portions in tefillin (phylacteries) led to the custom of "Rabbenu Tam tefillin," still worn by many pious Jews today.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Jewish community of Sens maintained the standard institutions of a medieval northern French Jewish settlement: a synagogue, a cemetery (some medieval Jewish gravestones from Sens and the region survive), and presumably the community administrative structures documented in the synodal ordinances.

The Sens cathedral treasury contains several medieval Hebrew manuscripts that were apparently repurposed from Jewish use — a reminder of the intimate physical proximity of Jewish and Christian sacred culture in medieval urban space.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Sens's primary contribution to Jewish culture is through the Synod of 1150 and the broader Tosafist tradition that Rabbeinu Tam led. The Tosafot composed in northern France — at Sens, Troyes, Paris, and related communities — represent one of the greatest achievements of medieval Jewish jurisprudence: a systematic critical engagement with the Talmud that combined rigorous logic with deep textual knowledge and practical wisdom.

The Tosafist method — identifying contradictions between Talmudic passages and proposing harmonizations — became the model for yeshiva learning across centuries and continents.

## Decline & Dispersal

The northern French Jewish communities suffered severely in the thirteenth century under Louis IX's anti-Jewish policies and were definitively expelled from France in 1306 and 1394. The Sens community, like its counterparts across Champagne and Burgundy, was dispersed eastward. Many families moved to Germany, where the Tosafist tradition was absorbed into the developing Ashkenazic scholarly culture.

## Legacy Today

Sens today has no Jewish community, but its place in Jewish intellectual history is secure. Discussions of the Synod of Sens appear in standard works on medieval Jewish history and law. The medieval Jewish cemetery has left some archaeological traces. Scholars of medieval French Jewry consider Sens, along with Troyes, Paris, and Ramerupt, as one of the central sites of northern French Jewish intellectual life.

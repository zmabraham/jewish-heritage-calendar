# Savannah

## Overview

Savannah, Georgia, founded in 1733 as the last of Britain's American colonies, was unusual from its inception in including Jews among its earliest settlers. General James Oglethorpe, the colony's founder, welcomed Jewish colonists from the very beginning, and the resulting community — Mickve Israel — became one of the oldest continuously functioning Jewish congregations in the United States and a symbol of early American religious tolerance.

## Historical Background

The Georgia colony was established with a utopian mission: Oglethorpe envisioned it as a refuge for England's poor, particularly those imprisoned for debt, and as a buffer protecting the prosperous colonies of the Carolinas from Spanish Florida. The colony was chartered by the Trustees of Georgia, who initially prohibited slavery and tried to create a society of small farmers rather than plantation aristocrats.

This reformist vision included religious tolerance. In July 1733, only five months after the colony's founding, a ship arrived from London carrying 42 Jewish refugees — primarily Sephardic Jews of Portuguese origin who had been living in London. Oglethorpe welcomed them despite the protests of some colonists who resented the Jews' presence. The Jews were granted land and permission to practice their religion openly. This reception was exceptional; most other colonies excluded Jews or tolerated them only grudgingly.

The early Jewish settlers included Abraham de Lyon, a vintner who attempted to cultivate grapes for wine, and Dr. Samuel Nunez Ribiero, a physician who helped save the colony from an epidemic. The Jewish community of Savannah was thus present from the very founding of Georgia.

## Golden Age

The Savannah community grew slowly but established itself as a stable presence. In 1735, the congregation received a Torah scroll — a crucial act of communal self-legitimation. The congregation's early years were marked by the challenge of maintaining Jewish life in a small frontier settlement; many Jews drifted away to Charleston or other larger cities.

In 1790, the congregation was formally reconstituted as Mickve Israel (Hope of Israel), adopting the Sephardic rite of its founders. The community constructed its first synagogue building in 1820. The magnificent Moorish Revival synagogue, standing today on Monterey Square, was consecrated in 1878.

Benjamin Sheftall (1694–1769), one of the earliest Jewish settlers, kept meticulous records of Savannah Jewish life in his diary — a crucial primary source for understanding early American Jewish life. Sheftall served as a commander of a militia company during the Revolutionary War, exemplifying Jewish participation in the patriot cause.

## Notable Figures

Mordecai Sheftall (1735–1797), son of Benjamin, became the highest-ranking Jewish officer in the American Revolutionary forces, serving as a colonel and deputy quartermaster general for Georgia. Captured by the British and imprisoned, he continued to serve the patriot cause after his exchange.

The Sheftall family — Benjamin, Mordecai, and their descendants — represent the story of a Jewish family deeply integrated into the civic and military life of the American South, their story told through the extensive family papers that survive in archives.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Congregation Mickve Israel's 1878 synagogue building, designed by the New York architect Henry G. Harrison in a Gothic-Moorish hybrid style, is one of the most architecturally distinguished synagogue buildings in America. The congregation maintains a museum of Jewish history with one of the oldest Torah scrolls in America — brought to Savannah in 1735 — and artifacts from the congregation's long history.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Savannah's Jewish community illustrates the early American practice of religious tolerance — not absolute equality, but a pragmatic acceptance of Jewish presence and participation in civic life. Oglethorpe's welcome to the Jewish refugees of 1733 established a precedent for American hospitality that would be formalized in the religious liberty clauses of the First Amendment decades later.

Savannah also exemplifies the complexity of Southern Jewish life: Jews participated in the economy and civic institutions of a slave society. Some Jewish families owned slaves; others did not. The Savannah Jewish experience, like that of other Southern communities, is complicated by this history.

## Decline & Dispersal

Savannah's Jewish population has remained small but stable — never growing to the size of Charleston's or other Southern communities, but persisting across nearly three centuries. The Jewish community of Savannah today is approximately 1,000 people, but Mickve Israel continues as an active congregation, and Savannah's Jewish heritage sites attract visitors.

## Legacy Today

The Mickve Israel congregation and its museum preserve the story of one of the oldest Jewish communities in the United States. Savannah's history as a city that welcomed Jews from its founding is commemorated in historical markers and heritage tours. The Sheftall family papers, preserved at the Georgia Historical Society, provide one of the most complete records of an American Jewish family across multiple generations.

# Gamla

## Overview

Gamla — whose name means "camel" in Aramaic, describing the hump-shaped ridge on which it stands — was a Jewish city in the Golan Heights whose last stand against Roman forces in 67 CE produced one of the most harrowing stories of the Great Revolt. Josephus Flavius, who commanded the Jewish forces in the Galilee before surrendering and becoming the Romans' collaborator-historian, describes the fall of Gamla in his Jewish War with a vividness that makes the reader feel the wind on that narrow ridge and hear the cries of the defenders. When Roman forces finally breached the walls, the defenders — men, women, and children — chose to throw themselves from the cliff rather than be taken captive. "No fewer than five thousand perished by throwing themselves over the edge," Josephus writes, and the number may include his rhetorical excess, but the catastrophe was real.

Gamla has been identified since the 1960s as the ancient site at Khirbet es-Salam in the Golan Heights, and archaeological excavations there have produced extraordinary findings: a synagogue from the first century BCE–CE that is among the oldest known, coins minted during the revolt bearing the inscription "For the Redemption of Holy Jerusalem," and physical evidence of the Roman siege that confirms and illuminates Josephus's account. The site is now a national park and an emotional place of pilgrimage — Israel's "Masada of the north."

## Historical Origins

The Golan Heights (ancient Gaulanitis) had a significant Jewish population in the Second Temple period, settled during the Hasmonean expansion. Gamla itself was established as a Jewish city probably in the second or first century BCE. It was founded on a topographically dramatic location: a long, narrow ridge dropping steeply on three sides, with only the northern approach offering any viable access, making it naturally defensive.

Under Roman rule, Gamla paid taxes to Rome but maintained its Jewish character. The city's population of perhaps five to ten thousand included farmers, craftsmen, and scholars. The synagogue excavated at Gamla, with its stone benches arranged around a central floor, is a typical early synagogue — a public assembly building for prayer, reading, and community gathering — dating from before the Temple's destruction. It is one of the earliest synagogues yet discovered.

## Golden Age

Gamla's "golden age" was its final years — the period of the Great Revolt (66–73 CE), when it became a center of Jewish resistance in the Galilee and Golan. The city joined the revolt enthusiastically, minting its own coins, resisting the initial Roman approach, and holding out after surrounding cities had fallen. Josephus himself was briefly involved in Gamla's defense as Roman general Vespasian and his son Titus systematically reduced the Jewish strongholds of the north.

The earlier centuries of Gamla's existence were characterized by the prosperity typical of Galilean-Golan Jewish agricultural life: olive oil production (presses have been excavated), grain cultivation, and animal husbandry on the volcanic basalt plateau. The synagogue's early date suggests a community of some cultural ambition — a building for Torah reading and community life constructed before the Temple's destruction had made synagogues the default alternative to Temple worship.

## Notable Figures

**Judah of Gamla (1st century CE)** — known as Judah the Galilean or Judah of Galilee — was the founder of the Zealot or "Fourth Philosophy" movement that advocated Jewish sovereignty and refused to pay Roman taxes on the principle that God alone was Israel's ruler. He may have been born in or associated with Gamla, and his militant theology contributed to the intellectual foundation of the Great Revolt. His sons and descendants continued the zealot tradition through the revolt.

**The Defenders of Gamla (67 CE)** are not individually named in most sources, but Josephus describes their courage: "The people of Gamla refused to negotiate or surrender... and chose death over slavery." The five thousand who threw themselves from the ridge have become symbols of Jewish resistance — people who chose self-determination in death over Roman enslavement.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Gamla Synagogue**, excavated in the 1970s and 1980s, is one of the most important early synagogue discoveries in the world. Dating to the first century BCE or first century CE, it predates the Temple's destruction and shows that the synagogue institution was well established as a community gathering place even while the Temple stood. Stone benches line the walls; a ritual bath (mikveh) is adjacent; and the architecture reflects a public building of communal dignity.

The **defensive wall** of Gamla, sections of which remain clearly visible, speaks to the city's last desperate preparation. **Roman siege ramp** remains have been identified. **Catapult stones** (Roman ballista balls) litter the site, providing tangible evidence of the siege. **Arrowheads** from both Jewish defenders and Roman attackers have been found — a forensic record of the final battle.

## Cultural Life

Gamla's culture was that of a Jewish agricultural community in Roman Palestine — Aramaic-speaking, synagogue-centered, farming the volcanic basalt plateau's surprisingly fertile soil. The proximity to the Kinneret (Sea of Galilee) meant fish from the lake may have supplemented the agricultural diet. The presence of the early synagogue suggests a community with literacy, a tradition of public reading and discussion of Torah, and a sense of its own dignity as a Jewish community.

The Zealot ideology associated with Gamla gave the city's culture a political edge: the belief that Jewish sovereignty, not accommodation with Rome, was the proper form of Jewish existence. This theology would have shaped the community's self-understanding and its decision to fight to the end.

## Decline & Transformation

Gamla's fall in 67 CE was its end as a significant Jewish city. The site was not re-inhabited. The dramatic topography that made it defensible also made it impractical for peaceful agricultural life without the protection of a large population. The survivors of the fall (those not killed or sold into slavery) dispersed into the Galilee and beyond.

The Golan Heights as a whole was depopulated of Jews in the aftermath of the Roman-Jewish wars. It was resettled by Jewish communities periodically in later centuries, but Gamla itself remained abandoned — a ruin on its striking ridge, its story preserved only in Josephus until modern archaeology confirmed and enriched the account.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Gamla has become, in modern Israel, a site of national significance second only to Masada as a symbol of Jewish resistance. The coin inscription "For the Redemption of Holy Jerusalem" — found at Gamla and dating to 66 CE — is one of the most moving artifacts of the Jewish Revolt. The phrase "Gamla shall not fall again" is sometimes heard as a parallel to "Masada shall not fall again," expressing the same determination not to repeat catastrophic defeat. The national park, with its griffon vulture colony (one of the largest in the world), the spectacular views over the Golan and the Kinneret, and the moving archaeological remains of the ancient Jewish city, draws hundreds of thousands of visitors annually.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: 2nd–1st century BCE; destroyed 67 CE
- **Peak Population**: 5,000–10,000 (1st century CE)
- **Languages**: Aramaic, Hebrew
- **Status Today**: National park in the Golan Heights; archaeological site; no living community

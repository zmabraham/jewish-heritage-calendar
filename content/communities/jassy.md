# Jassy (Iași)

## Overview

Jassy — Romanian Iași — was once the largest and most vibrant Jewish community in Romania, a city where Yiddish theaters, Hebrew presses, and Zionist societies thrived amid the pervasive antisemitism of Romanian public life. Its name became synonymous with one of the most horrific massacres of the Holocaust: the Iași pogrom of June–July 1941.

## Historical Background

Jews settled in the Moldavian principality from at least the sixteenth century, attracted by its position on trade routes connecting Poland and the Ottoman Empire. Jassy served as the capital of the Principality of Moldavia and later of the United Principalities of Romania, giving it political and economic importance. By the nineteenth century, it had become a major center of Romanian Jewish life.

Romanian Jews occupied a uniquely precarious position in European Jewish history. Despite being a significant portion of the population — Jews constituted 30–35% of Jassy's total population in the late nineteenth century — they were denied citizenship by successive Romanian governments. A series of laws and regulations specifically excluded Jews from citizenship, land ownership, and the professions, making Romanian Jewry among the most legally disadvantaged in Europe even as Jews in Western Europe were gaining full emancipation.

## Golden Age

Despite — or perhaps because of — this legal precarity, Jassy's Jewish community developed an extraordinarily rich cultural life. The city hosted thriving Yiddish theater companies, literary circles, and political movements ranging from socialist Bundism to Labor Zionism. The Hebrew writer Mendele Moykher Sforim lived in Jassy for a period, and the city's intellectual ferment was characteristic of the eastern European Jewish world at its creative peak.

Jassy was also a center of early Zionist organizing. Romanian Jews, denied citizenship in their country of birth, found Zionism's promise of national self-determination particularly compelling. The city hosted important Zionist congresses and was home to figures who played major roles in early Palestinian settlement.

## Notable Figures

The poet and philosopher Mihai Eminescu (1850–1889), Romania's national poet, spent time in Jassy and expressed — like many Romanian nationalists of his era — both admiration for Jewish creativity and hostility to Jewish "foreign" influence. This contradiction characterized elite Romanian attitudes toward Jews generally.

Barbu Știrbei, the Wallachian ruler, and various figures of Romanian political life intersected with the Jewish community of Jassy in commercial and political ways, but the Jewish community also produced its own leadership: rabbis, merchants, and intellectuals who navigated the hostile legal environment with remarkable resilience.

The historian Elias Schwarzfeld (1855–1915) documented the history of Romanian Jewry's legal disabilities in works that served as indictments of Romanian antisemitism before international audiences.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Jassy's Great Synagogue (Sinagoga Mare), built in 1671, was one of the oldest in Romania. The community also maintained dozens of smaller synagogues and prayer houses, reflecting the diversity of its population — Hasidic shtiebels alongside modernizing congregations. Jewish schools, a Yiddish theater, and eventually Zionist youth movement headquarters made Jassy a hub of organized Jewish life.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Jassy's Jewish community contributed significantly to Romanian medicine, law, and commerce, despite legal restrictions. Jewish doctors practiced with special permits; Jewish merchants were often the economic intermediaries between Romanian landowners and international markets. Culturally, the community sustained a rich tradition of cantorial music, Yiddish literature, and Hebrew education.

## Decline & Dispersal

The catastrophe came in June–July 1941, when Romanian military and police, acting in coordination with German Einsatzgruppen, carried out a massive pogrom against Jassy's Jews. Approximately 13,000 Jews were murdered — either shot in the streets, killed in police headquarters, or transported in sealed "death trains" in which hundreds suffocated. It was one of the largest single massacres of the Holocaust in terms of numbers killed in such a short period, and it was carried out almost entirely by Romanians without direct German participation.

The surviving Jews of Jassy endured further persecution throughout the war. Romanian Jews were not uniformly deported to Nazi death camps (as were the Jews of Bessarabia and Bukovina who were sent to Transnistria), but they suffered forced labor, confiscation, and constant threat.

## Legacy Today

Jassy today (Iași) has a small Jewish community that maintains a museum and several synagogues. The Great Synagogue has been restored. Annual commemorations of the 1941 pogrom draw survivors, descendants, and Romanian officials in acts of memorial and, increasingly, acknowledgment of Romanian responsibility. Jassy's Jewish cultural legacy — its theater, its literature, its Zionist ferment — is preserved in archives and the memories of the diaspora communities formed by its émigrés.

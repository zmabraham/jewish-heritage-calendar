# Radom

## Overview

Radom was a major industrial city in central Poland with one of the most important Jewish communities in the Congress Kingdom. Famous for its leather and shoe manufacturing industry — in which Jewish entrepreneurs, workers, and craftsmen played a dominant role — Radom's Jews built a rich communal life over three centuries before the Holocaust consumed them. The city is also remembered for the poignant documentary record left by its Jews in the final months before deportation: letters, diaries, and last messages smuggled out of the ghetto that form one of the most intimate testimonies to what was lost.

## Historical Background

Jews settled in Radom in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, gradually overcoming the restrictions that had long kept them from the city's center. By the end of the eighteenth century, when Poland was partitioned and Radom came under Russian control, the Jewish community was well-established. Under Russian rule in the Pale of Settlement, the community grew rapidly through the nineteenth century, reaching approximately 25,000 by the eve of World War II — nearly one-third of Radom's total population.

The Congress Kingdom — the rump Polish state established by the Congress of Vienna in 1815 under Russian sovereignty — allowed Jews relatively more economic freedom than the Russian interior, and Radom's industrialization benefited from Jewish entrepreneurship. The 1860s–1880s brought railway connections that integrated Radom into the larger Russian imperial economy and expanded the leather and arms industries.

## Leather and Industry

Radom's Jewish community was closely identified with the leather trade and shoe manufacturing. Jewish tanneries, shoe factories, and leather goods workshops were central to the local economy from the eighteenth century onward. By the early twentieth century, major shoe manufacturing firms with hundreds of employees operated under Jewish ownership or management. The Bata company's Polish operations had a significant Radom component. Jewish cobblers, tanners, saddlers, and harness-makers were present in every dimension of the leather economy.

The arms industry — Radom housed a major state arms factory (Fabryka Broni) — brought a different population of workers and engineers, some of them Jewish. The city's economic diversity made it an important regional center, and its Jews served as merchants and professionals throughout the surrounding Kielce region.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Radom's Great Synagogue, built in the late nineteenth century, anchored a religious landscape that included numerous smaller batei midrash and Hasidic shtiblekh. The Radom community had strong affiliations with the Alexander Hasidic dynasty, one of the most popular Polish Hasidic courts of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. Alongside religious institutions, the community supported modern Hebrew schools (Tarbut), Yiddish schools (TSYSHO, associated with the Bund), and a network of Zionist and political organizations. Radom published its own Jewish newspaper and had an active Jewish theater scene.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Radom rabbinate produced respected scholars in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The Hassidic life of the community was vibrant: the Alexander shtibel drew large crowds to the rebbe's tisch (festive table), holiday prayers drew thousands, and the Hasidic movement shaped the emotional and devotional life of a large portion of the traditional community.

The Zionist movement was powerful in Radom. Various Zionist parties operated training farms (hachshara) in the Radom region preparing youth for agricultural work in Palestine; Hechalutz, Hashomer Hatzair, and Beitar each had substantial local organizations. The years between the wars were marked by intense political mobilization alongside growing economic depression and antisemitic boycotts.

## The Holocaust in Radom

German forces occupied Radom on September 8, 1939. Immediately, Jews were subjected to forced labor, beatings, economic confiscation, and humiliation. A Judenrat (Jewish council) was established to administer German demands. In April 1941 the Germans established two ghettos in Radom — a large ghetto and a small ghetto in different neighborhoods — separating and concentrating approximately 32,000 Jews.

The great deportation (Aktion) took place on August 5–12, 1942. German police and Ukrainian auxiliaries rounded up approximately 18,000 Jews from the large ghetto and transported them to the Treblinka extermination camp, where they were gassed. A second deportation in August 1942 killed the inhabitants of the small ghetto. Approximately 14,000 Jews remained in Radom as forced laborers for German enterprises. In November 1942, further selections reduced the survivor labor force. The remaining Jews were murdered in 1944 at the Szydłowiec forest and in various camps.

Among the most affecting documents of the Radom Holocaust are the letters written by Jews in the ghetto to relatives abroad — in Palestine, America, and elsewhere — knowing they might be their last communications. These "last letters," collected and published after the war, give intimate voice to the community's final months.

## Legacy Today

A memorial marks the main deportation site in Radom. The Jewish cemetery survives partially. The Radom Yizkor book and survivor memoirs preserve detailed accounts of community life and destruction. Descendants of Radom Jews in Israel and North America maintain memorial organizations. The "last letters" of Radom Jews have been published and studied as documents of Holocaust testimony.

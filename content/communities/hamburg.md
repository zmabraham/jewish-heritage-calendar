# Hamburg

## Overview

Hamburg's Jewish community is among the most historically layered in Germany, shaped by a remarkable dual founding: a Sephardic community of Portuguese conversos who arrived in the late sixteenth century, followed by an Ashkenazic community that grew to overshadow them. Together, these two distinct communities created a port city of Jewish intellectual and commercial energy — a place where Iberian exile met Ashkenazic scholarship, where international trade and early Reform Judaism both found early homes.

## Historical Origins

The story of Hamburg's Jews begins not with German migrants but with Portuguese conversos — Jews forcibly baptized in the Iberian Peninsula who maintained secret Jewish practice. In the 1580s and 1590s, following the union of Portugal and Spain under Philip II and intensified Inquisition pressure, many of these New Christians migrated northward to the thriving port of Hamburg. Initially presenting themselves as Portuguese Christian merchants, they gradually reconstituted an openly Jewish community, formally organized by 1612 under the name "Beit Jacob."

The city of Hamburg, eager for the commercial expertise and international connections these Sephardic traders offered, tolerated their presence despite strong resistance from Lutheran clergy and local guilds. The community formally received right of residence in 1612, making it one of the earliest formally recognized Jewish communities in German Protestant lands.

## Golden Age

The Sephardic community at its height in the seventeenth century formed a mercantile aristocracy — connected by family and business networks to Amsterdam, London, Bordeaux, Leghorn, and the Ottoman Empire. They traded in sugar, spices, diamonds, and textiles. Their synagogue, built in 1652, was a statement of confidence and wealth. The community supported scholars, poets, and physicians; Hebrew printing flourished, and several important halakhic works were published in Hamburg.

The Ashkenazic community, which had grown rapidly through the seventeenth century primarily from Central and Eastern European Jews, long existed in an uneasy parallel with the Sephardim. In 1671, the Ashkenazic community established the "Drei-Gemeinden" (Three Communities) together with nearby Altona and Wandsbek — a remarkable umbrella structure that allowed Jews living in three different political jurisdictions to maintain a united communal administration, known as the AHW community.

## Notable Figures

Glückel of Hameln (1646–1724) is among the most celebrated voices in early modern Jewish literature. Her Yiddish memoirs, written for her children after the death of her husband, offer an incomparable window into the daily life, piety, business practices, and emotional world of Ashkenazic Jewry in Hamburg. They constitute the earliest extant autobiography by a Jewish woman.

Rabbi Jonathan Eybeschütz (1690–1764) served as chief rabbi of the Three Communities and became embroiled in one of the most bitter controversies of eighteenth-century Jewish life, accused by Rabbi Jacob Emden of writing kabbalistic amulets containing Sabbatean elements. The dispute consumed European Jewry for years and exposed deep fault lines in post-Sabbatean Jewish society.

Heinrich Heine (1797–1856), though born in Düsseldorf, spent formative years in Hamburg and worked in his uncle Salomon Heine's banking house before turning to literature. Salomon Heine, one of Hamburg's leading Jewish philanthropists, became the city's most prominent Jewish civic figure.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Portuguese Synagogue (Sephardic community) and the Great Synagogue of the Ashkenazic community both stood as architectural monuments to Jewish life in Hamburg. The Talmud Torah school, founded by the Sephardic community in the seventeenth century, became one of the leading Jewish educational institutions in northern Europe.

Hamburg also hosted the Hamburg Temple, founded in 1818 — one of the first Reform synagogues in the world. The Temple's founders, including Israel Jacobson and Eduard Kley, introduced vernacular German prayers, organ music, and a shortened service, pioneering the Reform movement that would spread across Germany, Europe, and eventually North America.

## Cultural Life

Hamburg's Jewish culture was shaped by its port identity: cosmopolitan, multilingual, entrepreneurially bold. The Sephardic community maintained Ladino and Portuguese cultural traditions well into the eighteenth century, while the Ashkenazic community produced scholars and merchants in roughly equal measure. By the nineteenth century, Jewish Hamburg had become a center of the Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment) in northern Germany, with a vibrant Jewish press and literary culture.

The community maintained strong connections to the emerging Zionist movement in the late nineteenth century; Hamburg's Jewish youth organizations were among the most active in Germany.

## Decline & Transformation

By 1933, Hamburg's Jewish community numbered approximately 17,000. The Nazi period brought progressive exclusion, emigration, and ultimately deportation. Kristallnacht devastated Hamburg's Jewish institutions. Between 1941 and 1945, the great majority of remaining Jews were deported to Łódź, Minsk, Theresienstadt, and Auschwitz. The firebombing of Hamburg in July 1943 ("Operation Gomorrah") destroyed much of the city, including many Jewish sites.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Hamburg's Jewish diaspora settled heavily in New York, London, Tel Aviv, and South American cities. The Warburg banking family, originally from Hamburg, became one of the most distinguished Jewish dynasties in the world, with Max Warburg remaining in Hamburg until 1938.

Today's Hamburg Jewish community numbers around 3,500–4,000, rebuilt largely by post-Soviet immigrants. The Jewish community center, a synagogue, and the Institute for the History of German Jews maintain the memory and scholarly study of this extraordinary community.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: c. 1590s (Sephardic); c. 1612 (formally recognized)
- **Peak Population**: ~20,000 (early 20th century)
- **Languages**: Ladino, Portuguese (Sephardic); Yiddish, German (Ashkenazic)
- **Status Today**: Active community of ~3,500–4,000; Institute for the History of German Jews

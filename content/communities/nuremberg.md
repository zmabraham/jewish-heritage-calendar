# Nuremberg

## Overview

Nuremberg's Jewish community stands as one of medieval Germany's great Jewish centers — and one of the most tragically destroyed. The city gave the Jewish world a significant medieval community before driving it out in a brutal expulsion in 1499; it gave the world the Nuremberg Laws of 1935, the legislative foundation of the Holocaust; and it gave the world the Nuremberg Trials of 1945–1946, the first international prosecution of crimes against humanity. In Nuremberg, the arc of European Jewish persecution and justice is compressed into a single city.

## Historical Origins

Jews lived in Nuremberg from at least the eleventh century, drawn by the city's growing commercial importance as a center of Central European trade. The imperial city's Jewish community was directly under imperial protection — a status that provided some stability but also made the Jews politically dependent and financially useful to successive emperors who could tax them, transfer them, and confiscate their property.

The medieval community grew through the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. Jews were involved in money-lending, trade, and the provision of luxury goods; their financial services were essential to the commerce of an imperial city at the crossroads of major European trade routes. The community maintained a synagogue, a yeshiva, and a cemetery.

## The Rindfleisch and Black Death Massacres

Nuremberg was the site of some of the worst medieval anti-Jewish violence. In 1298, during the Rindfleisch massacres — led by a butcher named Rindfleisch who claimed divine mandate to destroy Jews — Nuremberg's Jewish community was attacked and many were killed or forced to flee. The community rebuilt, only to face the Black Death pogroms of 1349, when Jews across Germany were accused of well-poisoning and attacked by panicking populations. In Nuremberg, the Jewish community was virtually destroyed; Jewish property was seized and the community was driven out.

After the Black Death, Jews were readmitted and expelled repeatedly over the next century and a half. The final expulsion of Nuremberg's Jews came in 1499, when the city expelled its Jewish population permanently. The confiscated Jewish property, including the synagogue site, was given to the city.

## The Modern Community and the Nuremberg Laws

Jews were not permitted to settle in Nuremberg for centuries after 1499. It was only in the nineteenth century, following Bavaria's gradual emancipation of Jews, that a modern community could be established. The community grew through the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, reaching approximately 9,000 by 1933.

Nuremberg had a sinister significance in Nazi ideology. The city was the site of the annual Nazi Party rallies (Reichsparteitag) — massive spectacles of mass mobilization that were the great set pieces of the Third Reich. Albert Speer designed the colossal rally grounds; Leni Riefenstahl filmed them in Triumph of the Will (1935). In September 1935, at the Nazi Party rally, the infamous Nuremberg Laws were proclaimed: the Reich Citizenship Law stripped Jews of German citizenship, and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honour prohibited marriage or sexual relations between Jews and non-Jews. These laws laid the formal legal foundation for the Holocaust.

## Notable Figures

The medieval community produced scholars who participated in the intellectual life of the Rhineland-Bavarian Jewish world. Seligmann Bär Bamberger (1807–1878), though primarily associated with Würzburg, represents the broader Franconian rabbinic tradition.

The most celebrated figure of Nuremberg's modern community was Julius Streicher (1885–1946) — a tormentor of the Jews rather than a member of it. As editor of the virulently antisemitic newspaper Der Stürmer and Gauleiter of Franconia, Streicher was among the most vicious persecutors of German Jews. He was convicted at the Nuremberg Trials and hanged.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The medieval synagogue in the Weißgerbergasse was seized at the time of the 1499 expulsion and converted into a church. No medieval Jewish structures survive.

The nineteenth-century community built a magnificent new synagogue on the Hans-Sachs-Platz in 1874. This was destroyed on Kristallnacht (November 9–10, 1938). After the war, a new synagogue was built in Nuremberg and a modest Jewish community re-established.

## Cultural Life

Nuremberg's nineteenth-century Jewish community participated in the cultural and intellectual life of German-Jewish integration. Jewish merchants, professionals, and intellectuals were visible in Nuremberg's civic life. The community maintained schools, charitable organizations, and cultural societies.

## Decline & Transformation

Nuremberg's Jewish community was subjected to severe persecution even before 1938 — Julius Streicher's virulent local antisemitism made Nuremberg one of the most hostile cities in Germany for Jews throughout the 1930s. Deportations from Nuremberg to Łódź, Riga, and Auschwitz destroyed the community.

The Nuremberg Trials (1945–1946) brought a measure of justice for some of the perpetrators, though the vast majority of those who murdered Jews were never prosecuted. The trials established the legal concept of crimes against humanity and genocide, foundational to international law.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Nuremberg today has a Jewish community of approximately 1,500–2,000. The Documentation Center at the Nazi Party Rally Grounds is one of the most important sites for understanding the history of National Socialism. The Jewish Museum in Fürth, nearby, and the modest Jewish community in Nuremberg maintain the memory of the region's Jewish history.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 11th century (documented); medieval community expelled 1499; modern community from early 19th century
- **Peak Population**: ~9,000 (1933)
- **Languages**: Yiddish (medieval), German (modern)
- **Status Today**: Small community of ~1,500–2,000; Documentation Center at rally grounds

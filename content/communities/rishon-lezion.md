# Rishon LeZion

## Overview

Rishon LeZion — "First in Zion," taking its name from Isaiah 41:27 ("First to Zion, I will give a herald of good tidings") — was the second Zionist agricultural settlement established in the Land of Israel, founded in July 1882, and has the distinction of being the site where the Hebrew national anthem Hatikvah was first sung and where the first Hebrew kindergarten was established. These two facts capture Rishon LeZion's essential character: it was a founding cultural laboratory of Zionism, a place where the practical and symbolic experiments of Jewish national renaissance were conducted in real fields and real classrooms, with real consequences.

The founders of Rishon LeZion were young Eastern European Jews — mostly from Kharkov and other Russian cities — members of the Hovevei Zion (Lovers of Zion) movement who arrived in the sandy coastal plain south of Jaffa with idealism, inadequate funds, agricultural inexperience, and a fierce determination to demonstrate that Jewish farmers could cultivate the ancient homeland. Their first years were a struggle against sand, malaria, poverty, and the indifference or hostility of the Ottoman administration. Baron Edmond de Rothschild's intervention — beginning in 1883, when the struggling colony appealed to the Paris philanthropist — saved Rishon LeZion and transformed its character.

## Historical Origins

The site chosen for Rishon LeZion was purchased from Arab landowners in 1882 by a group of ten young Jews, led by Zalman David Levontin, who paid 45,000 francs for approximately ten thousand dunams of sandy, largely uncultivated land. The first settlers arrived on July 31, 1882 — this date is considered the founding date — and established themselves in a cluster of modest structures on the edge of the coastal plain.

The early settlers discovered almost immediately that their agricultural knowledge was inadequate to the local conditions. The soil required deep plowing, water was scarce, malaria was a constant threat, and the Ottoman bureaucratic system was hostile to Jewish land purchases. The settlement faced crisis within its first year. The appeal to Baron Rothschild in 1883 brought his administrators, his resources, and his direction — which proved both saving and constraining.

## Golden Age

Rishon LeZion's golden age as a pioneer community runs from approximately 1890 to 1920. Under Rothschild's patronage, the settlement developed vineyards and a wine cellar — the Carmel Winery (1886, Société Coopérative Vigneronne des Grandes Caves), which became one of the most significant Jewish agricultural enterprises of the pre-state era and still operates today as one of Israel's leading wineries. The wine production gave Rishon LeZion economic stability and a distinctive identity.

The cultural achievements of the first decades were remarkable. **Hatikvah** (The Hope), the poem written by Naftali Herz Imber and set to its familiar melody, was first sung publicly at a Rishon LeZion gathering in 1882 or 1886 (sources disagree on the exact date). The melody, based on a European folk tune, was arranged for this purpose. **The first Hebrew kindergarten** in the Land of Israel was established in Rishon LeZion in 1898 by Dvora Katz, a milestone in the revival of Hebrew as a spoken vernacular language for children.

## Notable Figures

**Zalman David Levontin (1856–1940)**, the leader of the founding group, was a banker and Hovevei Zion activist who organized the purchase of the land and led the first settlers. He later became a major figure in Jewish banking in Palestine and was involved in founding the Anglo-Palestine Bank (predecessor of Bank Leumi).

**Baron Edmond de Rothschild (1845–1934)** was not a settler but was effectively the father of Rishon LeZion as a viable enterprise. His financial support, administered through his agents, funded infrastructure, agricultural experimentation, the wine cellar, water systems, and schools. He visited Palestine several times and took personal interest in the settlements he supported. His relationship with the settlers was complex — he demanded obedience, and the settlers sometimes chafed under his administrators' control — but without him, Rishon LeZion and many other early colonies would have failed.

**Naftali Herz Imber (1856–1909)**, the Hebrew poet who wrote the poem Hatikvah (originally titled Tikvatenu — "Our Hope"), visited the early settlements and read his poem in Rishon LeZion, where it was first set to music. A somewhat dissolute figure who spent much of his life in poverty, Imber's poem outlasted him to become the national anthem of the Jewish state.

**Eliezer Ben-Yehuda (1858–1922)**, the pioneer of Hebrew language revival, had close connections with Rishon LeZion and the Hovevei Zion settlers. His project of making Hebrew a living spoken language found its practical testing ground precisely in these early settlements.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Carmel Winery** (founded 1886), the oldest and largest winery in Israel, is both an economic institution and a cultural landmark. Its wine cellar, built with Baron Rothschild's support, gave Rishon LeZion its signature product and made kosher wine exportable from the Land of Israel.

The **Founders' Well (Be'er HaMeyasdim)**, the first water source of the settlement, is preserved as a heritage site. The **Museum of the First Aliyah** (Beit HaRishon) in Rishon LeZion documents the story of the founding generation and the broader First Aliyah.

The **Rishon LeZion Cemetery**, where many of the founders are buried, and the **founders' quarter** of the city preserve the physical fabric of the original settlement. The old water tower (built 1882) is the city's iconic landmark.

## Cultural Life

Rishon LeZion's cultural life in the early decades was a hothouse of the Zionist cultural revival. Hebrew language education — pioneered in the local schools — made the settlement's children among the first generation to grow up speaking Hebrew as a native language. The music school (founded 1895) trained musicians who would go on to create Israeli classical music culture. The settlement had a library, a reading room, and active cultural societies.

The wine culture introduced by Rothschild's agricultural program gave Rishon LeZion a distinctive character. The vineyards covered the sandy plain, and the annual harvest became a community celebration. The Carmel wine brand, distributed to Jewish communities worldwide, made Rishon LeZion's name known in distant synagogues and Jewish homes.

## Decline & Transformation

Rishon LeZion never declined in the way of diaspora communities — it grew continuously. From a colony of a few dozen families in 1882, it grew to a city of 20,000 by 1948 and over 250,000 today, making it Israel's fourth-largest city. Its transformation from agricultural moshava to industrial suburb to major city followed the trajectory of Zionist success.

The orange groves that replaced much of the vineyards in the early twentieth century gave the coastal plain its characteristic scent and its export economy (Jaffa oranges). The Second and Third Aliyah brought new settlers with socialist rather than private-farming ideologies, but Rishon LeZion itself remained a more bourgeois settlement than the kibbutzim.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Rishon LeZion's legacy is woven into the Israeli national anthem, the Israeli wine industry, and the modern Hebrew language. The fact that Hatikvah was first sung here makes Rishon LeZion — in a very real sense — the birthplace of the song that defines Jewish national aspiration. The city's wine production, educational innovation, and cultural pioneering mark it as one of the most fertile ground for Jewish national renaissance in the crucial early decades.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: July 1882
- **Peak Population**: 250,000+ (contemporary)
- **Languages**: Hebrew (revived as vernacular), Yiddish, Russian
- **Status Today**: Israel's fourth-largest city; thriving modern metropolis; major cultural heritage site of early Zionism

# Kielce

## Overview

Kielce is a regional capital in south-central Poland whose Jewish community, while not among the largest in Poland, is remembered above all for a single devastating event that occurred after the Holocaust had ended: the Kielce pogrom of July 4, 1946. On that day, a Polish mob — incited by a false blood libel accusation — murdered 42 Jewish Holocaust survivors and wounded more than 40 others. The Kielce pogrom was the most lethal antisemitic massacre in postwar Europe and precipitated the mass emigration of the remaining Jewish survivors from Poland, most of them making their way to Palestine. It remains one of the defining events in the history of Polish-Jewish relations and in the story of Jewish postwar reconstruction.

## Historical Background

Jews had lived in Kielce since at least the seventeenth century, though they were long subject to restrictions that limited their numbers and economic activities. By the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries the community had grown substantially. Kielce was the capital of Kielce Governorate, a significant administrative and economic center for the surrounding region.

By 1939 Kielce's Jewish population numbered approximately 25,000, about one-third of the city's total population. The community was engaged in trade, crafts, and professions; it maintained synagogues, schools, and political organizations spanning the full spectrum of Jewish political life.

## The Holocaust in Kielce

German forces occupied Kielce in September 1939. The Kielce ghetto was established in April 1941, concentrating the city's Jews and thousands more from surrounding communities. On August 20–24, 1942, the Germans conducted a mass deportation of approximately 21,000 Jews from the Kielce ghetto to the Treblinka extermination camp, where they were murdered. Additional deportations and shootings followed. By the time Soviet forces liberated Kielce in January 1945, the pre-war Jewish community had been almost entirely annihilated.

## The Kielce Pogrom: July 4, 1946

By July 1946 approximately 200 Jewish survivors had returned to Kielce, living in a building at 7 Planty Street that served as the local office of the Jewish Committee. Many were trying to retrieve property, locate surviving family members, or arrange emigration. They were fragile survivors of the camps and hiding places, attempting to rebuild their lives.

On July 4, 1946, a Polish boy named Henryk Błaszczyk told police that he had been kidnapped by Jews and held in the building at 7 Planty Street. This was false. But the accusation immediately triggered the oldest and most lethal libel in European antisemitic tradition: the blood libel, the claim that Jews kidnap Christian children.

A crowd gathered. Polish police officers and soldiers — rather than protecting the Jews — participated in the violence. A mob attacked 7 Planty Street. Over the course of several hours, 42 Jewish men, women, and children were beaten, stabbed, and shot to death. A pregnant woman was killed. Survivors were thrown from windows. Polish workers at a nearby factory joined the mob; soldiers from a nearby unit participated. The violence spread to Jews encountered elsewhere in the city — on trains and in the streets — and several more were killed.

The Polish government of the time prosecuted nine perpetrators; three were executed. But the judicial response did nothing to stem the shock and terror felt by the approximately 250,000 Jewish survivors remaining in Poland.

## The Aftermath

The Kielce pogrom had an immediate and decisive impact on Jewish survivors' decisions about their future in Poland. Within weeks of the massacre, the pace of Jewish emigration from Poland — the Bricha (flight) movement — accelerated dramatically. By the end of 1946, approximately 150,000 Jewish survivors had left Poland, most traveling through Central Europe to displaced persons camps in the American zone of Germany and Austria, and from there eventually to Palestine or the United States.

The Kielce pogrom convinced most Jewish survivors that there was no future for Jews in Poland, regardless of the outcome of the ongoing Arab-Jewish conflict in Palestine. Zionist leaders used Kielce as the most powerful argument for the immediate establishment of a Jewish state, and news of the pogrom reached the United Nations Special Committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) just as it was conducting hearings in 1947 on the question of Palestine's future.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Kielce's prewar synagogue, built in 1903, was a significant structure serving the kehilla. It was damaged during the war and subsequently demolished. The Jewish cemetery survived partially. The building at 7 Planty Street, site of the 1946 pogrom, became a memorial site.

## Legacy Today

The 7 Planty Street building in Kielce is now a museum and memorial dedicated to the victims of the 1946 pogrom. Annual ceremonies mark the anniversary. Polish historians and public intellectuals have engaged in ongoing debate about the pogrom's causes — Communist provocation, traditional antisemitism, resentment over property restitution — and its place in Polish historical memory. In 2001, Polish President Aleksander Kwaśniewski apologized for Jedwabne; the Kielce pogrom has been part of the same reckoning with Polish complicity in wartime and postwar anti-Jewish violence.

# Chicago

## Overview

Chicago's Jewish community is one of the great American Jewish stories: a community built on immigrant shoulders in the stockyards and garment factories of the Midwest, which produced Nobel laureates, labor organizers, jazz musicians, and some of America's most important novelists. From the Maxwell Street market to the lakefront mansions of the Reform establishment, Chicago Jewry embodied the full range of American Jewish experience.

## Historical Background

German Jewish immigrants arrived in Chicago in the 1840s, founding the city's first Jewish congregation and establishing themselves in commerce and finance. They were followed by Eastern European Jews beginning in the 1880s, who settled in the West Side neighborhoods of Maxwell Street and the surrounding area. By 1900, Chicago had a Jewish population of approximately 75,000; by 1920, over 225,000.

The geography of Chicago Jewish life followed the classic American pattern: immigrant settlement in dense urban neighborhoods, followed by movement northward and westward as economic success enabled departure to better housing. The progression from Maxwell Street to Lawndale to Rogers Park to Skokie traces the arc of Chicago Jewish mobility through the twentieth century.

## Golden Age

The Maxwell Street market was Chicago's Lower East Side — a chaotic, vibrant outdoor marketplace where Jewish immigrant vendors sold every conceivable product from pushcarts and stalls. Maxwell Street was also the birthplace of the electric blues (Maxwell Street Jimmy, Muddy Waters and other Black musicians played there), representing the remarkable cultural confluence of Chicago's immigrant communities.

The garment industry was Chicago's Jewish economic anchor. Workers organized in the Amalgamated Clothing Workers of America, led for decades by Sidney Hillman (born in Lithuania, raised in the movement), which became one of the most powerful labor unions in America and a pillar of the New Deal coalition.

## Notable Figures

Julius Rosenwald (1862–1932), who built Sears, Roebuck and Company into America's largest retailer, was Chicago's greatest Jewish philanthropist. Rosenwald Fund money built approximately 5,300 schools for Black children in the rural South — an extraordinary act of interracial philanthropy motivated by Rosenwald's conviction that Black advancement was inseparable from American democracy. He was also a major funder of the Chicago Museum of Science and Industry and dozens of Jewish institutions.

Saul Bellow (1915–2005), Nobel Prize laureate in Literature (1976), grew up in Chicago and set many of his novels — including *The Adventures of Augie March*, *Herzog*, and *Humboldt's Gift* — in the city's Jewish neighborhoods. His voice, mixing intellectual ambition with immigrant earthiness, defined a certain mode of American Jewish literary sensibility.

Benny Goodman (1909–1986), the "King of Swing," grew up on Chicago's West Side, where his immigrant parents enrolled him in music lessons at a Hull House program. His synthesis of jazz improvisation with classical discipline, and his racially integrated orchestras, made him one of the most consequential figures in American popular music.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Kehilath Anshe Maariv (K.A.M.) was Chicago's oldest congregation, founded in 1847, eventually redesigned by Louis Sullivan and later by Adler and Sullivan. Temple Sholom and other lakefront congregations represented the Reform establishment. The Jewish Peoples Institute (later part of the Jewish Community Centers movement) served the immigrant neighborhoods. The Spertus Institute (now Spertus Institute of Jewish Learning and Leadership) is Chicago's major institution of Jewish higher education.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Chicago was a significant center for Conservative Judaism — Congregation Etz Chaim and other Conservative congregations thrived in the Lawndale and Rogers Park neighborhoods. The Conservative movement's Chicago congregations helped establish it as the largest American Jewish denomination of the mid-twentieth century.

The University of Chicago, with its strong program in social sciences, attracted Jewish faculty and students who shaped American intellectual life: the economist Milton Friedman, the philosopher Leo Strauss, and many others were associated with the university.

## Decline & Dispersal

The postwar suburbanization that reshaped American Jewry profoundly affected Chicago. By the 1950s and 60s, Jews were moving to Skokie, Evanston, and the northern suburbs, leaving the old West Side neighborhoods to become predominantly Black. Skokie itself became famous in 1977–78 when a neo-Nazi march planned for the heavily Holocaust-survivor community was fought through the courts in a case that became a landmark in First Amendment jurisprudence.

## Legacy Today

Chicago today has approximately 300,000 Jewish residents, concentrated in the northern suburbs and the lakefront neighborhoods. The Chicago Jewish community remains culturally active, with major institutions including the Illinois Holocaust Museum and Education Center in Skokie, the Spertus Institute, and numerous congregations across denominations.

# Jerusalem

## Overview

Jerusalem stands at the absolute center of Jewish civilization — the eternal capital, the holy city, the axis around which Jewish history, law, theology, and longing have revolved for three thousand years. No city in Jewish memory carries the weight that Jerusalem does. Three times daily, Jews around the world orient their prayers toward it; for two millennia of exile, "Next year in Jerusalem" was both a cry of grief and a declaration of faith. The city is simultaneously a geographic reality and a spiritual archetype: the earthly Jerusalem (Yerushalayim shel matah) mirrored by the heavenly Jerusalem (Yerushalayim shel malah) in rabbinic imagination.

The history of the Jewish community in Jerusalem spans three distinct monarchies, two Temple destructions, Persian restoration, Hellenistic conflict, Roman conquest, Byzantine suppression, Arab, Crusader, Mamluk, and Ottoman rule, and finally modern statehood. Through each era, Jews have maintained a presence in the city, sometimes thriving, often persecuted, occasionally expelled, but always returning. This continuity — broken yet unbroken — is one of the most remarkable phenomena in world history.

What makes Jerusalem uniquely formative for Jewish civilization is that it was both the political and religious capital of ancient Israel. When Solomon built the First Temple around 960 BCE, he merged divine worship with national governance in a single place. The destruction of that Temple in 586 BCE by Babylon, and the Second Temple's destruction in 70 CE by Rome, created the defining trauma of Jewish history — and Jerusalem itself became the symbol of everything that was lost and everything that must be regained.

## Historical Origins

Jewish presence in Jerusalem begins with the conquest attributed to King David around 1000 BCE, though the city (then called Jebus) was inhabited millennia before. David captured it from the Jebusites and made it his capital, deliberately choosing a city that belonged to no single tribe — a neutral ground that could unify the fractious Israelite confederation. He brought the Ark of the Covenant there, transforming a political capital into a sacred center.

Solomon's construction of the First Temple (c. 957 BCE) on Mount Moriah — the site where Abraham had bound Isaac, according to tradition — consecrated Jerusalem as the singular place of divine presence. The Temple became the focus of the three pilgrimage festivals (Passover, Shavuot, Sukkot), drawing Israelites from across the land three times yearly. Under Solomon, Jerusalem flourished as a commercial and diplomatic hub, with trade links to Phoenicia, Egypt, and Arabia.

The Babylonian destruction in 586 BCE by Nebuchadnezzar scattered the Jerusalemite community, but the return under Cyrus the Great of Persia in 538 BCE — itself fulfilling Isaiah's prophecies — brought exiles back to rebuild. The Second Temple, completed around 516 BCE, re-established Jerusalem as Jewish capital. Under the Hasmoneans (after 164 BCE), the city expanded dramatically; under Herod the Great (37–4 BCE), it became one of the most magnificent cities in the Roman world, with a rebuilt Temple complex of breathtaking grandeur.

## Golden Age

Jerusalem's golden age under the Second Temple period (roughly 200 BCE–70 CE) saw a city of perhaps 80,000–100,000 inhabitants, with pilgrimage populations swelling it several times over during the festivals. The Temple Mount complex covered thirty-five acres; the Antonia Fortress loomed to the north; royal palaces occupied the Upper City. The city hosted multiple synagogues, academies of learning, and the Great Sanhedrin — the supreme rabbinic court of seventy-one sages that met in the Chamber of Hewn Stone within the Temple precincts.

A second golden age emerged in the Ottoman period (1517–1917), particularly the sixteenth century, when Jerusalem's Jewish community was reinvigorated by Sephardic exiles from Spain (1492). The community grew from a few hundred to several thousand. Yeshivot reopened, charitable institutions (kollelim) were established, and wealthy patrons in the Diaspora funded the rebuilding of the city's Jewish Quarter. By the nineteenth century, Jerusalem's Jewish population was already the city's largest single community, and by 1880 Jews constituted a majority of the city's residents.

## Notable Figures

**King Solomon** (c. 970–931 BCE) built the First Temple and made Jerusalem the undisputed center of Israelite civilization. His reign represented the high-water mark of ancient Israelite power and cultural achievement, with Jerusalem as its crown jewel.

**Nehemiah** (5th century BCE) served as Persian governor and rebuilt Jerusalem's walls after the return from Babylon. His memoir, preserved in the biblical book bearing his name, is one of the most vivid personal accounts of civic leadership in the ancient world.

**Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai** (1st century CE) recognized that Jerusalem was about to fall to Rome and negotiated with Vespasian to save the academy at Yavneh — thereby ensuring the survival of rabbinic Judaism even after the Temple's destruction. His foresight transformed catastrophe into survival.

**Nachmanides (Rabbi Moses ben Nachman, "Ramban")** (1194–1270) arrived in Jerusalem in 1267 to find only two Jewish families. He revived the community, established a synagogue, and wrote movingly of the city's desolation — and of his determination to remain. His letters home describe the ruined city with aching precision.

**Rabbi Yosef Karo** (1488–1575), though based primarily in Safed, spent formative years in Jerusalem and was deeply embedded in the Jerusalem-Safed network of sixteenth-century scholars. His Shulchan Aruch, the definitive code of Jewish law, shaped Jewish practice worldwide.

**Sir Moses Montefiore** (1784–1885) made seven pilgrimages to Jerusalem and funded the construction of Mishkenot Sha'ananim (1860), the first Jewish neighborhood outside the Old City walls. His practical philanthropy transformed Jerusalem's physical landscape.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Temple Mount (Har HaBayit)** remains the holiest site in Judaism, the location of both Temples. The **Western Wall (Kotel HaMaaravi)** — the retaining wall of Herod's Temple complex — is today the primary site of Jewish prayer and pilgrimage, where millions come to pray each year.

The **Jewish Quarter of the Old City** contains synagogues dating back centuries, most famously the **Hurva Synagogue** ("the Ruin"), destroyed by the Jordanians in 1948, rebuilt gloriously in 2010. The **Ramban Synagogue**, founded by Nachmanides in the thirteenth century, is one of the oldest functioning synagogues in Jerusalem.

**Yeshivat Merkaz HaRav**, founded by Rabbi Abraham Isaac Kook (1921), became the intellectual center of Religious Zionism. The **Hebrew University of Jerusalem** (1925) on Mount Scopus united modern scholarship with the ancient holy city. The **Israel Museum** (1965), housing the Dead Sea Scrolls in its Shrine of the Book, preserves the oldest biblical manuscripts known.

## Cultural Life

Jerusalem has always been a multilingual city. In the Second Temple period, Hebrew remained the sacred language while Aramaic served as the vernacular; Greek was the language of commerce and administration. Under Ottoman rule, Ladino (Judeo-Spanish) was spoken by Sephardic Jews, Yiddish by Ashkenazim, and Judeo-Arabic by Mizrachim. The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw the remarkable revival of Hebrew as a spoken vernacular, with Jerusalem as one of its primary proving grounds.

The city's Jewish cuisine reflects its layered communities: the rich Sephardic table brought from Spain and North Africa; the Ashkenazic traditions of Eastern Europe; and ancient Mizrachi cooking rooted in the broader Levant. Religious life in Jerusalem has always been intense, with dozens of competing traditions and customs observed simultaneously — a living anthology of Jewish practice.

## Decline & Transformation

Jerusalem suffered devastating cycles of destruction and depopulation. The First Temple's destruction (586 BCE) emptied the city for decades. The Roman destruction of 70 CE killed tens of thousands and scattered the survivors. Emperor Hadrian's construction of the Roman city Aelia Capitolina on Jerusalem's ruins (135 CE), barring Jews from even entering, symbolized total dispossession. The Byzantine period brought renewed restrictions; the Crusader conquest (1099) massacred the Jewish community. The Mamluk period saw gradual recovery; the Ottoman centuries brought relative stability.

The modern period brought new rupture: the Arab-Israeli War of 1948 divided the city, with Jordan annexing the Old City and expelling its Jewish inhabitants. For nineteen years (1948–1967), Jews were barred from the Western Wall. The Six-Day War (June 1967) reunited the city under Israeli sovereignty, and the return to the Western Wall became one of the most emotionally charged moments of modern Jewish history.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Jerusalem is the one community whose legacy is universal Jewish civilization itself. The Hebrew Bible, the rabbinic tradition, the liturgy, the entire conceptual architecture of Judaism — all are stamped with Jerusalem's imprint. The city's destruction in 70 CE is commemorated every year on Tisha B'Av; its restoration is celebrated in the modern holiday of Yom Yerushalayim (Jerusalem Day). Today, with a Jewish population of over 550,000, Jerusalem is once again a living, thriving Jewish city — one that carries the weight of its entire history in every stone.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: c. 1000 BCE (Davidic conquest); continuous Jewish presence since
- **Peak Population**: ~100,000 (Second Temple period); 550,000+ Jewish residents today
- **Languages**: Hebrew, Aramaic, Greek, Arabic, Ladino, Yiddish, modern Hebrew
- **Status Today**: Capital of the State of Israel; thriving Jewish majority city

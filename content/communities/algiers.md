# Algiers

## Overview

Algiers, the Algerian capital perched on a hillside above a Mediterranean bay, was home to one of the most significant Jewish communities in the Maghreb — intellectually distinguished, shaped by medieval Iberian exile, and transformed by French colonialism in ways that changed Jewish history. Algiers produced Rabbi Simon ben Zemah Duran, one of the greatest medieval halakhic authorities; witnessed the dramatic impact of the Crémieux Decree of 1870, which made Algerian Jews French citizens; and nurtured a generation of French-educated Jewish intellectuals who contributed enormously to French cultural and philosophical life, including connections to the existentialist milieu of Albert Camus and, later, the deconstructionist thought of Jacques Derrida.

## Historical Background

Jewish presence in the Algiers region — known as Icosium to the Romans — extends back to late antiquity. Medieval Arab geographers describe a Jewish population in Algiers before the Ottoman conquest, and the community received a major influx of Spanish exiles after 1492, including prominent rabbinical scholars who transformed the intellectual character of the community.

The Ottoman conquest of Algiers in 1516 (under Khayr al-Din Barbarossa) brought the city into the Ottoman realm, and Jews participated in the commercial and political life of the Regency of Algiers as merchants, corsair-commerce intermediaries, and diplomatic agents. Jewish merchants in Algiers supplied the corsair fleets and managed the ransom of European captives — a lucrative if morally complicated enterprise. The most famous of the "ransoming" merchant families was the Busnach-Bacri network, which in the late 18th century became deeply entangled in the financial affairs of revolutionary France, generating debts that contributed to France's decision to invade Algeria in 1830.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Simon ben Zemah Duran (Rashbatz, 1361–1444) is Algiers's towering intellectual figure. Born in Majorca and educated in Barcelona and elsewhere in Catalonia, Duran fled the 1391 Iberian pogroms and settled in Algiers, where he served as rabbi and ultimately chief rabbi for the rest of his life. His responsa collection *Tashbetz* (Teshuvot Shimon ben Tzemah) is a monumental work of halakhic scholarship, addressing hundreds of questions in Jewish law with a sophistication that reflects his dual training in rabbinic learning and secular philosophy. Duran also wrote *Magen Avot*, a philosophical work defending rabbinic tradition against philosophical and sectarian challenges, and *Ohev Mishpat*, a work on jurisprudence. His descendants continued as rabbinic leaders of Algiers for generations.

Rabbi Simon ben Zemah's son Rabbi Solomon ben Simon Duran (Rashbash) and grandson Rabbi Simon ben Solomon Duran (Rashbash II) continued the family's rabbinic dynasty, producing responsa that shaped North African halakhic practice.

Jacques Derrida (1930–2004), born in El Biar, Algiers, into a Sephardic Jewish family, became one of the most important philosophers of the 20th century. His deconstruction theory, his explorations of language, identity, and the margins of Western thought, and his late writings on hospitality, the gift, and forgiveness all bear the marks of his Algerian Jewish formation — the experience of being simultaneously French and Jewish, Algerian and European, inside and outside. He was stripped of his French citizenship by the Vichy regime in 1942 and expelled from his lycée.

Albert Camus (1913–1960), though not Jewish, was Derrida's fellow Algerian and a friend of several members of the Algerian Jewish intellectual milieu. His writings on absurdity, revolt, and solidarity reflect the multicultural world of Algiers in which Jews were significant participants.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Grand Synagogue of Algiers, constructed in the 19th century, was among the largest in North Africa and a symbol of the community's integration into French colonial society. The synagogue's architecture reflected French neo-classical and neo-Moorish styles, embodying the community's dual cultural position.

The mellah (Jewish quarter) of Algiers was less strictly enclosed than those of Morocco and Tunisia, reflecting the more integrated character of Ottoman Algiers. The Alliance Israélite Universelle established schools in Algiers among its earliest operations, transforming the community's educational profile.

## The Crémieux Decree

The Crémieux Decree of 1870 — named for Adolphe Crémieux, the French Jewish statesman and founder of the Alliance Israélite Universelle — granted French citizenship to the Jews of Algeria (but not to the Muslim population). This decree had enormous consequences: it separated Algerian Jews legally and institutionally from their Muslim neighbors, accelerated assimilation into French culture, and created a community simultaneously deeply French and increasingly alienated from Algerian Muslim society. The decree was revoked by the Vichy regime in 1940–1942 and restored after the Liberation.

## Decline & Dispersal

The Algerian War of Independence (1954–1962) placed Algerian Jews in an agonizing position: French citizens by law and culture, yet Algerian by birth and history. Most ultimately sided with the French settler (pied-noir) community and, following independence in 1962, emigrated en masse to France. The Jewish community of Algeria — perhaps 130,000 at its height — relocated almost entirely to France within a few years, settling primarily in Paris, Lyon, and Marseille. This constituted one of the largest and most rapid Jewish migrations in modern history.

## Legacy Today

No significant Jewish community remains in Algiers today. The Grand Synagogue was converted to a mosque after 1962. Algerian Jewish descendants in France maintain cultural and memorial organizations, and the community has produced a rich memoir literature reflecting on the lost world of Algerian Jewry.

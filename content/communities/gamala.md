# Gamla (Gamala)

## Overview

Gamla is one of the most evocative and heartbreaking sites in all of Jewish history — a Zealot fortress city perched on a dramatic ridge in the Golan Heights whose name means "camel" in Aramaic, for the hump-like shape of its topography. In the summer of 67 CE, Gamla held out for seven months against the Roman army of Vespasian before falling in a catastrophic siege. When the Romans finally breached the walls, the Jewish defenders — rather than surrender — threw themselves from the cliffs into the ravine below. The story of Gamla became, alongside Masada, one of the defining narratives of Jewish resistance to Roman conquest, preserved primarily by Josephus Flavius who was himself present at the siege.

## Historical Background

Gamla is located on a narrow ridge in the southern Golan Heights, descending steeply on three sides into deep ravines. The site was settled in the Hellenistic period and came under Hasmonean rule in the late 2nd or early 1st century BCE when Alexander Jannaeus (reigned 103–76 BCE) conquered the Golan and incorporated it into his kingdom. The city became part of Philip the Tetrarch's domain after Herod the Great's death and was known for producing excellent olive oil.

The city's physical topography — a ridge accessible only from the northeast — made it naturally defensible and gave its inhabitants the confidence to resist Roman power. The Jewish population of the Golan, though living in proximity to the Hellenistic Decapolis cities, maintained a strongly Jewish identity reinforced by Hasmonean conquest and settlement.

When the great Jewish revolt broke out in 66 CE, Gamla's population enthusiastically joined. Josephus Flavius, appointed commander of the Galilee and Golan by the Jerusalem rebel government, reportedly fortified Gamla and organized its defense before his own surrender to the Romans at Jotapata. After Josephus's capture, Gamla was left to defend itself.

## Golden Age

Gamla's golden age was the Hasmonean and early Roman period (1st century BCE through 67 CE). Archaeological excavations led by Shmaryahu Gutman beginning in 1976 revealed a densely built urban settlement of approximately 9,000–10,000 inhabitants. The city had:

- A large **synagogue** — one of the earliest synagogues yet discovered in the Land of Israel, predating the destruction of 67 CE by at least a century. The structure had benches running along the walls, a central reading platform, and a mikveh (ritual bath) in an adjacent room.
- Residential quarters packed tightly along the ridge
- Olive oil presses indicating the community's primary industry
- Extensive cisterns for water storage

The synagogue at Gamla is particularly significant because its early date (1st century BCE to 1st century CE) provides crucial evidence that the synagogue as an institution predates the Temple's destruction by Titus in 70 CE — challenging older theories that synagogues only became important after the Temple fell.

## Notable Figures

**Judah of Gamala** (also known as Judah the Galilean, fl. 6 CE) was one of the founders of the Zealot movement in Jewish history. He led a tax revolt against the Roman census of Quirinius in 6 CE, proclaiming that Jews should have no master but God and that paying tribute to Rome was tantamount to accepting Roman sovereignty over the Land. The Acts of the Apostles (5:37) records his name and revolt. His sons James and Simon were later crucified by the Romans, and his grandson Eleazar ben Yair led the defenders of Masada. The family of Judah of Gamala represents the most committed wing of Jewish resistance to Rome across three generations.

**Chares and Joseph**, two Jewish commanders, led the final defense of Gamla in 67 CE according to Josephus. When the Romans finally broke through, the population attempted to escape but found all routes blocked; Josephus reports that some 4,000 died in battle and another 5,000 threw themselves or fell from the cliffs.

**Vespasian** (9–79 CE), the Roman general and future emperor, commanded the siege of Gamla. His successful capture of the fortress contributed to his military reputation, which within two years brought him to the imperial throne.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The **Gamla Synagogue** is among the most important archaeological discoveries in the study of ancient Judaism. Rectangular in plan with columns and surrounding benches, it follows the design pattern of what scholars call "Second Temple period synagogues" — pre-destruction institutions that served primarily as community assembly halls. Unlike later synagogues oriented toward Jerusalem, the Gamla synagogue has no clear directional orientation. A stone carved with a seven-branched menorah found at the site is among the earliest depictions of this symbol.

The synagogue served both religious and civic functions — it was the community's assembly hall, court, and place of study, as well as prayer. Its presence at Gamla confirms that synagogue institutions existed in Jewish communities throughout the Galilee and Golan well before 70 CE.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Gamla's contribution to Jewish history lies primarily in the political-theological realm: the Zealot ideology associated with Judah of Gamala — that submission to foreign rule was a form of idolatry and that Jews must resist at all costs — became one of the defining currents of late Second Temple Jewish thought. The rabbis at Yavneh ultimately chose a different path (accommodation and legal creativity), but the Zealot legacy shaped Jewish memory and interpretation of the revolt for generations.

The synagogue at Gamla has contributed enormously to scholarly understanding of early synagogue architecture and function. Its early date and clear layout have become reference points in the ongoing debate about when and why the synagogue emerged as an institution.

## Decline & Dispersal

Gamla was destroyed in 67 CE by Vespasian's forces and was never reoccupied as a Jewish settlement. The ridge lay abandoned, its ruins slowly covered by debris and vegetation. The site was entirely forgotten until modern times.

## Legacy Today

Gamla Nature Reserve in the southern Golan Heights was established in 1984 and is one of Israel's most spectacular nature sites — home to a large griffon vulture nesting colony in addition to the archaeological ruins. The excavated ruins of the city, synagogue, olive presses, and fortifications are accessible to visitors along hiking trails. The site was used before and after the Six-Day War (1967) as a symbol of Israeli steadfastness, and the Zionist slogan "Gamla shall not fall again" (Gamla lo tipol shuv) echoed the earlier "Masada shall not fall again," expressing the determination that Jews would not again be driven to mass suicide rather than surrender.

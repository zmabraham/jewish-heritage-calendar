# Graz

## Overview

Graz, the capital of the Styrian province and Austria's second city, presents a paradox in Jewish history: a city that expelled its Jews in 1496 and kept them out for nearly four centuries, yet became home to a distinguished modern Jewish community with deep connections to the founding of Zionism and to Austrian intellectual life.

## Historical Background

Jews lived in Graz during the medieval period, engaged in moneylending and commerce as elsewhere in the Holy Roman Empire. In 1496, Emperor Maximilian I — responding to the demands of the Styrian Estates — issued an expulsion order that drove Jews from all of Inner Austria, including Graz. This expulsion was unusually complete and long-lasting: Graz remained effectively closed to Jewish settlement for centuries, far longer than comparable Austrian cities.

The Josephine reforms of the 1780s opened the door to limited Jewish presence, but the formal reconstitution of a Jewish community in Graz occurred only in the mid-nineteenth century. Jewish merchants and professionals, attracted by Graz's status as an industrial and academic center, established a community that received full legal recognition in 1856.

## Golden Age

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw Graz's Jewish community develop as a typical example of Austrian Jewish bourgeois integration. The community, numbering approximately 2,500 by the early twentieth century, was disproportionately represented in medicine, law, commerce, and the university. Jews contributed to Graz's growing industrial economy and its civic cultural life.

The connection to Zionism is striking: Jakob Herzl (1835–1902), father of Theodor Herzl, is buried in the Graz Jewish cemetery. The elder Herzl had business connections to Graz and died and was buried there. The grave of Herzl's father in a Styrian city — in the very region that had expelled its Jews four centuries earlier — carries a poignant historical resonance.

## Notable Figures

The philosopher Martin Buber (1878–1965) studied briefly in Graz and Vienna before his long career in Germany, Jerusalem, and the broader Jewish world. Buber's philosophy of dialogue and his seminal work on Hasidism emerged from the encounter between his Galician Jewish roots and his Central European philosophical education.

Sigmund Freud visited Graz and had professional connections to the University of Graz's medical faculty. The Austrian physicist Victor Hess (1883–1964), a Catholic convert of Jewish ancestry, discovered cosmic rays while affiliated with Graz institutions and won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1936.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Graz Synagogue, consecrated in 1892, was a striking Moorish Revival structure that served the community until its destruction on Kristallnacht in 1938. The Jewish community maintained a school, a library, a burial society, and social welfare organizations. The Jewish cemetery, where Herzl's father rests, remains an important site.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Graz's Jewish community contributed to the distinctive culture of Styrian Austria — a mix of Alpine tradition, baroque Catholicism, and emerging industrial modernity. Jewish doctors served in Graz hospitals; Jewish professors taught at the Karl-Franzens University; Jewish merchants participated in the city's commercial life. The community was largely acculturated and German-speaking, identifying strongly with Austrian culture while maintaining Jewish religious practice.

## Decline & Dispersal

Graz earned a grim distinction on November 9–10, 1938: it was one of the first cities in Austria to be declared judenfrei (free of Jews) following Kristallnacht, as local Nazis had been particularly aggressive in driving out the Jewish community. The synagogue was destroyed; Jewish businesses were looted; and the community was effectively dispersed or deported before the broader wave of deportations that came in 1941–1942.

Styria had been a stronghold of Austrian Nazism even before the Anschluss of March 1938, and Graz's Jewish community experienced the consequences of living in one of the Reich's most zealously National Socialist provinces.

## Legacy Today

A small Jewish community has reestablished itself in Graz in the postwar period, numbering a few hundred members. A new synagogue and community center were built in 2000. The city has engaged in memorial work around the destroyed community, and the Graz Jewish cemetery — including the grave of Jakob Herzl — is maintained. Graz's uncomfortable history as one of Austria's most antisemitic cities has been increasingly confronted by local historians and civic leaders.

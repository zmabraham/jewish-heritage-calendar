# Poznań

## Overview

Poznań (German: *Posen*) was the capital of the Wielkopolska (Greater Poland) region and one of the historic centers of Polish Jewish life — and also one of its most challenged. The community of Posen/Poznań was among the oldest in Poland, tracing its origins to at least the thirteenth century, and produced distinguished rabbinical scholars and communal leaders. But Poznań's history was also one of repeated expulsion, restriction, and ultimately demographic marginalization: by the time of the Holocaust, the Jewish community of Poznań was a fraction of its former size, having shrunk dramatically under Prussian rule and anti-Jewish pressure. The city's story illuminates the contrasts between the eastern and western branches of Polish Jewry.

## Historical Origins

Jews are documented in Poznań from the late thirteenth century, making the community one of the oldest in Poland. The city, situated at the heart of Piast dynastic Poland, was an important commercial center, and Jews served in the trade networks connecting the Baltic region with Central Europe. In 1264, King Bolesław the Pious issued the Statute of Kalisz, guaranteeing rights to Jews throughout the Polish kingdom — a foundational document for Jewish life in Poland. Poznań's Jews lived in a ghetto near the old market square and engaged primarily in trade, moneylending, and crafts.

The sixteenth century brought a period of distinguished scholarship: Poznań was home to Rabbi Akiva ben Jacob Frankfurter, the first printer to publish Hebrew books in Poland, and a center of Jewish intellectual life in the western Polish tradition. However, the community also faced repeated violence: pogroms in 1399, 1447, and subsequent centuries reflected the vulnerability of the Jewish position in a city with significant Christian merchant competition.

## Golden Age

The medieval and early modern periods were Poznań's golden age of Jewish life. The community was home to the famous Poznań *Klaus* — a center of Torah study — and the rabbinic tradition was distinguished. Rabbi Moses Isserles (the *Rema*), the great Cracow scholar who wrote the Ashkenazic glosses to the *Shulchan Aruch*, was connected to the Poznań scholarly tradition. Rabbi Yom-Tov Lipmann Heller, author of the *Tosafot Yom Tov* on the Mishnah, served in nearby communities. The community maintained a rich intellectual tradition through the seventeenth century.

Prussian rule from 1793 brought both emancipation and assimilation. Jews gradually received civic rights but at the cost of Germanization: Yiddish gave way to German, traditional dress was abandoned, and the community moved toward a more German-Jewish identity. The Reform movement gained strong influence in Poznań, as it did across German Jewish communities. The city's Jewish population became highly acculturated, economically prosperous, and culturally German rather than Yiddish-Polish.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Akiva Frankfurter** — sixteenth-century printer who brought Hebrew printing to Poland. **Rabbi Shabtai Cohen (Shakh)** — seventeenth-century legal authority whose commentary on the *Shulchan Aruch* is among the most studied. **Theodor Lessing** (1872–1933), the philosopher and critic, was born in Hannover of Poznań Jewish family background. **Johann Jacoby** (1805–1877), the German-Jewish physician and democratic political figure, was born in Königsberg (Kaliningrad) but represented the western Jewish emancipation tradition that Poznań exemplified. The community produced bankers, lawyers, and professionals who contributed to German and Polish public life.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Poznań's main synagogue — the New Synagogue, a magnificent Moorish-Romanesque structure built in 1907 — was one of the architectural glories of Central European Jewish life. It was demolished by the Nazis in 1940. The community also maintained an Orthodox synagogue, a rabbinical seminary (briefly), Jewish schools, a hospital, and welfare organizations. By the early twentieth century, most Jewish communal institutions reflected the community's German-Jewish orientation rather than Eastern European traditions.

## Cultural Life

Poznań's Jewish culture was primarily German in character by the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The community read German newspapers, spoke German at home, and identified more with German-Jewish culture than with the Yiddish-Polish world of the eastern Pale of Settlement. Zionism was present but less dominant than in eastern Poland; bourgeois liberalism and German patriotism were more characteristic orientations. After World War I returned Poznań to Poland (1919), Polish-language acculturation began, and many German-speaking Jews emigrated to Germany.

## Decline & Transformation

Poznań's Jewish community was already dramatically reduced by the interwar period — from perhaps 5,000–6,000 at its late nineteenth-century peak to under 2,000 in the 1930s. When Germany incorporated Poznań into the Reich in September 1939, the remaining Jews were immediately subjected to violent expulsions: the *Volksdeutscher Selbstschutz* (ethnic German self-defense force) murdered hundreds of Jews in the first days of occupation. The remaining Jews were expelled to the General Government (occupied Poland) by the end of 1939. There was no Poznań Ghetto — the community was essentially eliminated through expulsion and murder before the systematic extermination program was fully developed.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Poznań Jewish community was spread across Poland, Germany, and other countries through emigration and expulsion before 1939. Descendants of Poznań Jews are found across the world, primarily in communities shaped by German-Jewish rather than Eastern European Jewish culture. The New Synagogue's site is memorialized. The old Jewish cemetery was vandalized by Nazis but partially survives. The community's long history — from the thirteenth to the twentieth century — is preserved in historical archives and scholarly literature.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Documented from late 13th century; among Poland's oldest communities
- **Peak Population**: ~6,000 (mid-19th century); ~2,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish (early), German (modern), Polish (late)
- **Status Today**: Tiny community; New Synagogue demolished 1940; old cemetery partially preserved

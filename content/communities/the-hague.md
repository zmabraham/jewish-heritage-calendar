# The Hague

## Overview

The Hague, seat of the Dutch government and diplomatic capital of the Netherlands, was home to a distinctively patrician Jewish community — Sephardic and Ashkenazic families whose proximity to power and diplomatic life gave them a particular character. The city's Jewish community included prominent merchants, court agents, and eventually intellectuals and professionals whose integration into Dutch civic life was among the most complete in Europe. The Hague's Jewish story is also one of the first important steps toward international recognition of Jewish rights, a process eventually connected to the city's role as a center of international law.

## Historical Origins

The Hague, unlike Amsterdam or Rotterdam, was not primarily a commercial port city but the seat of the States-General and the center of Dutch political life. Jewish settlement followed the same general pattern as in other Dutch cities — Sephardic Jews from the Portuguese-Spanish diaspora arrived first in the late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries, followed by Ashkenazic Jews from Germany and Central Europe.

The Sephardic community was particularly prominent because of The Hague's political character. Jews seeking access to the Dutch government, Jews involved in diplomatic activities, and wealthy merchants who had connections to the stadholders and the States-General found The Hague a natural home. The community was never as large as Amsterdam's, but its social position was unusually elevated.

## Golden Age

The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries saw The Hague's Jewish community at its most distinctive. Sephardic families maintained the Portuguese rite and a refined social culture that gave the community prestige in diplomatic circles. Jewish bankers and merchants served as agents for the Dutch court, supplying luxury goods, providing credit, and maintaining connections to international trading networks.

The philosopher Baruch Spinoza, after his excommunication from the Amsterdam Sephardic community in 1656, eventually settled in The Hague, where he lived from around 1670 until his death in 1677. He lived in the house of an artisan (a lens-grinder), worked as an optician, and wrote the Ethics and the Political Treatise in the city that governed the Dutch Republic. The house where he died is preserved as a museum.

## Notable Figures

Spinoza's The Hague connection is the most celebrated in the city's Jewish history. The philosopher's willingness to live frugally in the city of political power while refusing a professorship at Heidelberg and a pension from Louis XIV — citing his desire for philosophical independence — is one of the most celebrated stories of intellectual integrity in European cultural history.

The Mendes da Costa family, prominent Sephardic merchants, had significant presence in The Hague. Various Jewish diplomats and commercial agents connected to the Dutch court maintained residences there.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Sephardic synagogue in The Hague, built in 1726, was a graceful building reflecting the community's wealth and taste. The Ashkenazic community had its own synagogue. The community maintained a cemetery and the standard communal institutions.

The Great Synagogue of The Hague, built in 1844, served the combined community in the nineteenth century and was an important architectural statement of Jewish confidence in the emancipation era.

## Cultural Life

The Hague's Jewish culture was shaped by the city's diplomatic and governmental character. Jewish families in The Hague cultivated connections to political power, maintained refined social standards, and participated in the intellectual life of a city that housed courts, legal institutions, and the diplomatic corps.

The Dutch Jewish community's intellectual tradition — shaped by the Amsterdam experience of Spinoza, Menasseh ben Israel, and others — found a complementary expression in The Hague's more political and socially prestigious environment.

## Decline & Transformation

The German occupation of the Netherlands (1940–1945) brought catastrophe to The Hague's Jews as to those of every Dutch city. The deportation of Dutch Jews proceeded with devastating efficiency; approximately 75 percent of all Dutch Jews were murdered, and The Hague's community was not spared. Jewish families who had maintained distinguished positions in Dutch society for generations were seized, deported, and murdered.

The Hague's role in international law — including the establishment of the International Court of Justice and, after the Second World War, the International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia — gave the city a direct connection to the post-Holocaust development of international human rights and genocide law.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Hague today has a Jewish community of approximately 2,500–3,000. The Spinoza House is a major cultural attraction. The city's role in international law — encompassing the tribunals that prosecute crimes against humanity and the International Criminal Court — gives it a unique connection to the legal legacy of the Holocaust and Jewish advocacy for international human rights.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Early 17th century
- **Peak Population**: ~15,000 (1940)
- **Languages**: Ladino, Portuguese (Sephardic); Yiddish, Dutch (Ashkenazic)
- **Status Today**: Active community of ~2,500–3,000; Spinoza House; International Court connections

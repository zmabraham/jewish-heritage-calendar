# Novofastov

## Overview

Novofastov is a small town in the Kyiv Oblast of central Ukraine, situated near the larger town of Fastov (Fastiv) on the Fastivka River. Its Jewish community, though small, experienced the full range of violence that characterized Ukrainian Jewish history — from the Cossack massacres of 1648 to the catastrophic civil war pogroms of 1919 — and represents the hundreds of similar communities whose stories are recorded primarily in yizkor books and Yad Vashem testimony rather than in the mainstream historical record.

## Historical Background

The Fastov-Novofastov area had a Jewish community dating to the seventeenth century. Fastov itself was the site of a significant massacre during the Khmelnytsky uprising of 1648, when the Cossack forces swept through the region destroying Jewish communities that had not fled. The area was known as a point of danger throughout the era of Ukrainian Jewish settlement, lying in the zone between Polish and Cossack spheres of influence where Jewish intermediaries were most exposed to violence.

## The 1919 Pogrom

The civil war period of 1917–1921 was catastrophic for the Jews of the Fastov region. The most infamous event was the Fastov pogrom of September 1919, carried out by forces of the Ukrainian People's Army (UNR) under General Anton Denikin's White Army as well as by various independent units and bands. In September 1919, approximately 1,500 Jews were murdered in Fastov over several days. The surrounding communities, including Novofastov, were affected by the waves of violence that swept through the region.

The 1919 pogroms in Ukraine — which killed tens of thousands of Jews across the country — were in some respects more prolonged and systematically destructive than the better-known 1881 and 1903 pogrom waves. They accelerated emigration to America and Palestine and confirmed the Zionist argument that Jewish safety in eastern Europe was not sustainable.

## Communal Life

Novofastov maintained a Jewish community that reflected the dense agricultural and commercial life of central Ukraine. Jews traded in grain, operated mills, worked as craftsmen, and served as the economic intermediaries typical of the Ukrainian Pale. The community maintained a synagogue and the basic institutions of kehilla life. Its Hasidic character was influenced by the proximity to Berdichev and the Chernobyl dynasty's influence throughout the region.

## Notable Figures

Novofastov was not associated with famous rabbinic or literary figures but contributed its share of emigrants who built new lives in America and Palestine. The community's resilience — rebuilding after 1648, after the Haidamak violence of 1768, after the 1919 pogroms, and under Soviet suppression — represents the tenacious attachment to place that characterized Ukrainian Jewish communities.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Jewish community of Novofastov maintained a synagogue, a small study house, and the charitable and educational institutions appropriate to a small kehilla. The community was embedded in the broader social and economic fabric of the region, with connections to Fastov and to Kiev as centers of commerce and communal governance.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The primary contribution of small communities like Novofastov to Jewish cultural history is the record of Jewish life they preserved in memory. The yizkor book (memorial book) tradition — in which survivors of destroyed communities compiled detailed accounts of community life for publication in Israel and America — preserved the memory of thousands of communities like Novofastov that would otherwise be entirely forgotten.

## Decline & Dispersal

Soviet collectivization disrupted the traditional economic base of Jewish small-town life. The German occupation of the region in 1941 brought mass murder: the Jewish community of the Fastov area was shot in late 1941 by German Einsatzgruppen and their auxiliaries. The few who had evacuated eastward with the Soviet retreat survived.

## Legacy Today

Novofastov today is a small Ukrainian town. The memory of its Jewish community is preserved in yizkor books and Yad Vashem testimony provided by survivors and their descendants.

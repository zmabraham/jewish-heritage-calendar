# Mashhad

## Overview

Mashhad, Iran's holiest city for Shia Muslims and the site of the Imam Reza shrine, was also home to a Jewish community that experienced one of the most dramatic forced conversions in Jewish history. In 1839, during the pogrom known as the Allahdad (God's Justice), Mashhad's Jews were given a choice: convert to Islam or die. Most chose conversion, becoming outwardly Muslim while secretly maintaining Jewish practice for generations. These Anusim (forced converts), known as "Jedid al-Islam" (New Ones of Islam), preserved an extraordinary hidden Judaism for over a century before many openly returned to Judaism and emigrated to Israel in the 20th century.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Mashhad predates the city's rise to prominence as a Shia pilgrimage center. The city became important following the martyrdom of Imam Reza in 818 CE, and the subsequent construction of the Imam Reza shrine made Mashhad Iran's second holiest city after Qom. Jews lived in Mashhad as dhimmis under Persian rule, subject to restrictions and occasional persecution but able to maintain communal institutions.

The most traumatic event in the community's history occurred on March 27, 1839 (27 Ramadan 1254 AH). Following a dispute between a Jewish woman and a Muslim street vendor during the Ramadan fast, an anti-Jewish riot erupted. A mob attacked the Jewish quarter, killing approximately 30 Jews, destroying homes and businesses, and desecrating Torah scrolls. The city's Shia religious leaders issued an ultimatum: the surviving Jews must convert to Islam immediately or face death.

## The Allahdad and Its Aftermath

In the immediate aftermath of the Allahdad, approximately 300 Jewish adults and children converted to Islam under coercion. The conversion was entirely external: the Mashhad Jews adopted Islamic names, attended mosque on Fridays, and outwardly observed Islamic norms, while secretly maintaining Jewish practice in private homes.

The community developed elaborate strategies for double-life Judaism. Jewish rituals were performed under the cover of darkness or behind closed curtains; mezuzot were hidden in hollowed-out doorframes; prayer services were whispered in basements or in rooms equipped with lookouts; religious texts were concealed under floorboards or sewn into furniture. Matzah was baked in secret ovens during Passover; Yom Kippur was observed while publicly pretending to fast during Ramadan.

This secret Judaism continued for generations. Children were initiated into the double-life gradually as they reached an age of discretion. The community maintained separate marriage registers, recording unions according to Jewish law while simultaneously conducting civil Muslim ceremonies. Births and deaths were recorded in both religious traditions.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi HaCohen Mullah Joseph Mashhadi** led the community through the crisis of 1839 and helped develop the institutions of secret Judaism.

**Mullah Zechariah** and other rabbis of the Mashhad Anusim maintained Torah study in secret, copying manuscripts and training children in Hebrew reading and halakha under dangerous circumstances.

The **Aghajan family**, wealthy merchants who converted outwardly while secretly funding Jewish institutions, played a crucial role in communal preservation.

The community's historians — including members who documented their experiences in emigration — preserved the memory of the Allahdad and its aftermath for later generations.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Following the conversion, the Mashhad Jews lost public synagogues and institutional infrastructure. Judaism was entirely domestic and clandestine. Secret rooms in houses served as prayer spaces; communal collections financed the copying of Hebrew manuscripts and the training of children.

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, as the community's economic situation improved and some members relocated to other cities (notably Tehran and later Herat, Afghanistan), more public institutions became possible. But in Mashhad itself, Jewish practice remained secret into the Pahlavi period.

## Return to Judaism and Emigration

The coming to power of Reza Shah Pahlavi (1925–1941) brought greater religious freedom and secularization to Iran. In the 1920s–1940s, Mashhad Anusim began to openly return to Judaism. The community formally reverted en masse in the 1940s, rebuilding Jewish institutions after more than a century of secrecy.

Most Mashhad Jews emigrated to Israel between 1950 and 1960, establishing themselves in Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, and Beersheba. A significant community settled in New York, where Mashhadi Jews became prominent in the diamond industry. Smaller communities exist in London, Milan, and elsewhere.

## Legacy Today

Mashhadi Jews in Israel and the diaspora maintain distinct customs and traditions reflecting their traumatic history. Their liturgy incorporates Persian elements; their cuisine is uniquely Iranian-Jewish; their collective memory includes detailed narratives of the Allahdad and the century of secret Judaism. The community's resilience and commitment to preserving Judaism under extraordinary duress has made them a celebrated example of Jewish survival.

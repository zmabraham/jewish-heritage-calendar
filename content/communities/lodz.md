# Łódź

## Overview

Łódź — pronounced approximately "Woodge" — was the great industrial Jewish city of the Russian Empire and interwar Poland, the "Manchester of Poland" whose textile mills and factories drew Jewish workers, merchants, and industrialists from across the region to create the second-largest Jewish community in Poland after Warsaw. Unlike Warsaw with its centuries of Jewish history, Łódź was essentially a modern creation: a village transformed within a single generation into a manufacturing metropolis by industrial capitalism. Its Jewish community was correspondingly modern in character — urban, commercial, politically radical, culturally secular — and its destruction in the Holocaust, carried out through what became the longest-surviving major ghetto in occupied Poland, was one of the most prolonged and documented tragedies of the war.

## Historical Origins

Łódź had virtually no Jewish history before the nineteenth century. In 1793, when it was annexed by Prussia, it was a village of perhaps 200 inhabitants. Its explosive growth began in the 1820s when the Russian Empire encouraged German textile manufacturers to settle and develop industry in Congress Poland. Jews initially faced restrictions on settlement in Łódź, but the city's explosive growth made such restrictions increasingly untenable. By 1865, Jews constituted roughly 35% of the city's population of 40,000. By 1897, there were over 98,000 Jews in Łódź — a community that had grown from almost nothing within living memory.

## Golden Age

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw Łódź's Jewish community reach its golden age. Jewish industrialists — the Poznański, Silberstein, and Eisenberg families among them — built vast textile factories whose products supplied the entire Russian Empire. Israel Kalmanowicz Poznański constructed a palace-factory complex that still stands as one of the architectural wonders of industrial Poland. Jewish merchants and manufacturers controlled much of the city's economic life. Alongside this industrial bourgeoisie was a large Jewish proletariat: tens of thousands of Jewish workers in textile mills, workshops, and small manufacturing enterprises.

This working-class base made Łódź a stronghold of the Jewish labor movement. The Bund was extraordinarily powerful in Łódź and organized massive strikes in the revolutionary year of 1905. Jewish workers participated in the broader labor movement and in general strikes alongside Polish and German workers — moments of cross-ethnic solidarity that were exceptional in the history of the region.

Culturally, Łódź developed a rich Jewish life: Yiddish theaters (the city had several professional Yiddish theater companies), newspapers, literary circles, Zionist organizations, and secular cultural institutions. The Hebrew writer and Nobel laureate Shmuel Yosef Agnon had connections to Łódź.

## Notable Figures

**Israel Kalmanowicz Poznański** (1833–1900) — the most prominent Jewish industrialist, whose factory palace now houses a shopping center. **Arthur Rubinstein** (1887–1982), the celebrated pianist, was born in Łódź. **Julian Tuwim** (1894–1953), one of Poland's greatest twentieth-century poets and a Jewish-identified intellectual, was born and raised in Łódź. **Chaim Rumkowski** (1877–1944) — the controversial chairman of the Łódź Ghetto Judenrat, who collaborated extensively with German authorities in organizing the ghetto and its deportations, believing he could save some Jews by making others productive; he perished in Auschwitz in 1944.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Łódź's Jewish institutions reflected its modern, secular character. The community had an unusual synagogue landscape: the large Altshtot Synagogue served one segment, while Reform and traditionalist congregations served others. The community maintained hospitals, welfare organizations, an extensive school network, a Jewish orphanage, and Hebrew and Yiddish libraries. The political and labor organizations — Bund, Zionist parties, Agudath Israel — maintained their own educational and cultural institutions.

## Cultural Life

Łódź was a city of remarkable cultural vitality and ethnic complexity — Jews, Poles, and Germans lived and worked in proximity if not always in harmony. Jewish literary and intellectual life produced important figures in both Yiddish and Polish letters. The Yiddish theater scene was vibrant, with visiting stars from Warsaw and New York performing for Łódź audiences. Politically, Łódź was a battleground: the Bund competed with Zionists and Communists for the allegiance of Jewish workers, and the Orthodox Agudath Israel competed for the traditionalists.

## Decline & Transformation

Germany occupied Łódź on September 8, 1939 — one week after invading Poland. The city was immediately incorporated into the Reich as *Litzmannstadt*. Within months, the Germans established the Łódź Ghetto: a sealed area in the poorest part of the city into which approximately 163,000 Jews were packed. Additional Jews were deported into the ghetto from Germany, Austria, and Western Europe. The Łódź Ghetto became a forced-labor installation, producing goods for the German military. Chaim Rumkowski administered it with extraordinary organizational efficiency — and extraordinary moral compromise.

The ghetto survived longer than any other major ghetto: most of its population was not deported until 1942–1944. Mass deportations to the Chełmno extermination camp began in January 1942; further deportations went to Auschwitz in 1944. By August 1944, when the last ghetto residents were deported to Auschwitz, over 200,000 people had passed through the Łódź Ghetto, of whom approximately 45,000 survived the war as the Red Army liberated them in January 1945.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Łódź Ghetto Chronicle — kept by a team of Jewish writers employed by the Judenrat — provides one of the most detailed records of ghetto life. The photographer Mendel Grossman secretly documented ghetto conditions. Survivors' testimonies from the Łódź Ghetto are among the most extensive of any single Jewish community. After the war, the Jewish community of Łódź briefly revived as survivors gathered, but emigration to Israel (especially after pogroms in 1946) dispersed the postwar community. Today Łódź has a small Jewish community; the Poznański factory palace is a major tourist attraction; memorial markers dot the former ghetto; and the Łódź Ghetto Chronicle has been published in English translation.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Large community from 1820s–1870s onward
- **Peak Population**: ~233,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Polish, German
- **Status Today**: Small community; Litzmannstadt Ghetto memorial; Poznański factory complex now museum/shopping center

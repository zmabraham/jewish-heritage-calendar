# Fürth

## Overview

Fürth earned its proud nickname "Bavaria's Jerusalem" — or sometimes "the Franconian Jerusalem" — through the remarkable density and vitality of its Jewish community, which flourished under the protection of competing lordships for centuries while nearby Nuremberg maintained its exclusions. In Fürth, Jewish life achieved an extraordinary concentration: dozens of synagogues and prayer houses, a famous yeshiva, the first Hebrew printing press in Bavaria, a Jewish hospital, and a cemetery that was one of the largest and most important in Germany. Fürth demonstrated what was possible when Jewish community life was given space to develop over generations.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Fürth began in the late sixteenth century, drawing Jews who had been excluded from neighboring Nuremberg. The unique political situation of Fürth — the town was divided among three overlapping jurisdictions (the Margrave of Ansbach-Bayreuth, the Bishop of Bamberg, and the Imperial City of Nuremberg) — created a legal ambiguity that Jews could exploit. Each jurisdiction competed for the taxes Jews paid, and none could expel Jews without the others taking advantage.

This competitive tolerance allowed Fürth's Jewish community to develop with unusual continuity. By 1600, there were already several Jewish families; by 1700, the community numbered in the hundreds; by 1800, it was one of the largest in Bavaria; by 1900, it was a major center of German Jewish life.

## Golden Age

The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries were Fürth's golden age. The community developed a remarkable infrastructure: a yeshiva that attracted students from across Europe, a Hebrew printing press (established 1691) that became one of the most important in the German-speaking world, a hospital, charitable institutions, and eventually the famous "cloisters" or courtyards of Jewish life. The community maintained multiple synagogues — a remarkable density reflecting the community's size and the proliferation of minyanim (prayer quorums) for different groups.

The Fürth yeshiva produced major scholars who shaped Ashkenazic halakhic tradition. The Hebrew printing house of the Schnaitach family and later others produced not only liturgical texts but philosophical and kabbalistic works, scientific treatises, and vernacular literature — making Fürth a significant node in the dissemination of Hebrew print culture.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Pinchas Horowitz (1730–1805), author of the Haflaah (a commentary on the Talmudic tractate Ketubot) and the Hamakneh, served in Fürth before becoming chief rabbi of Frankfurt. He was one of the greatest halakhic minds of the eighteenth century.

Henry Kissinger (born 1923 in Fürth, died 2023) is the most famous person born in twentieth-century Fürth. The future American Secretary of State grew up in a traditional Jewish family in Fürth before his family fled Nazi Germany in 1938. His career — diplomat, statesman, Nobel Peace Prize laureate — traces the trajectory of the Fürth Jewish diaspora in the twentieth century.

Rabbi David Sinzheim (1745–1812), born in Trèves but educated partly in Fürth, became the first Chief Rabbi of France and president of Napoleon's reconstituted Sanhedrin.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Fürth's Jewish cemetery, established in 1607, was one of the most important in Bavaria and Germany. Its thousands of graves — including those of major scholars and community leaders — are a physical record of Jewish life over three centuries.

The Main Synagogue of Fürth, built in 1615 and rebuilt several times, was the center of the community's religious life. The multiple prayer houses and smaller synagogues that dotted the Jewish neighborhoods gave the community its unique character.

The Hebrew printing house produced hundreds of editions of Hebrew texts between 1691 and the twentieth century, making Fürth one of the most productive centers of Hebrew print culture outside Amsterdam and later Eastern Europe.

## Cultural Life

Fürth's Jewish culture was richly traditional yet open to the broader currents of German Jewish modernization. The yeshiva tradition and Hebrew printing gave the community a strongly bookish, learned character. At the same time, the community's proximity to Nuremberg and its integration into Bavarian commercial life ensured contact with the broader German world.

The community produced not only scholars but merchants, physicians, lawyers, and artists. In the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, Fürth Jews participated in the cultural and civic life of Bavaria's industrial heartland.

## Decline & Transformation

Fürth's Jewish community numbered approximately 2,700 in 1933. The proximity to Nuremberg — where Julius Streicher's virulent antisemitism was concentrated — made Fürth a particularly dangerous place for Jews in the Nazi years. Kristallnacht devastated the community's institutions; the synagogue was burned and the cemetery was desecrated. Deportations sent Fürth Jews to Łódź, Riga, and Auschwitz. Henry Kissinger's family was among those who managed to emigrate in time; others were less fortunate.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Today Fürth has a small Jewish community of approximately 500–600. The old Jewish cemetery is maintained and receives visitors from around the world — descendants of Fürth Jews, scholars, and pilgrims. The Jewish Museum of Franconia, with its main branch in Fürth, preserves and interprets the rich heritage of Franconian Jewish life. Henry Kissinger's birthplace on the Mathildenstrasse is a local landmark.

# Tarudant

## Overview

Tarudant, the ancient walled city in the Souss Valley east of Agadir, was for centuries one of the most important Jewish communities in southern Morocco. Often called the "grandmother of Marrakech" for its antiquity, Tarudant served as a commercial and administrative center for the pre-Saharan region, and its Jewish community — speaking Tachelhit Berber as their primary vernacular — played a central role in the city's craft economy and long-distance trade. Jewish silversmiths, traders, and scholars made Tarudant a notable node in the network of southern Moroccan Jewry.

## Historical Background

Tarudant's Jewish community is of great antiquity; oral traditions connect it to pre-Islamic settlement, and the city's position as a regional center made it an obvious locus for Jewish commercial activity. The Souss Valley was the heartland of Morocco's Berber-speaking Jewish population, and Tarudant served as the urban center around which smaller village communities oriented their commercial and religious lives.

The city played a significant role in Moroccan political history. The Saadian dynasty, which ruled Morocco from the 16th century, originated in the Souss region, and Tarudant served as an early Saadian stronghold. Jews in Tarudant participated in the regional economy that supported the Saadian rise to power and benefited from the relative stability of their reign.

The mellah of Tarudant was established in the characteristic Moroccan pattern, providing the community with a defined physical space under the protection of the local governor (qaid), who both safeguarded and taxed the Jewish residents.

## Golden Age

The 17th through early 19th centuries represent Tarudant's most vibrant period. The city served as a market for the agricultural wealth of the Souss plain — argan oil, almonds, saffron, and grain — and Jewish merchants managed the commercial flows between local producers and buyers from Marrakech, the coast, and, through intermediaries, Europe. The caravan routes connecting Tarudant to the southern Sahara brought trade goods including gold dust, slaves (in earlier periods), and manufactured goods from sub-Saharan Africa.

Jewish silversmiths in Tarudant supplied the entire Souss region with the elaborate Berber silver jewelry — fibulae (brooches), pectoral ornaments, ankle bracelets, and earrings — worn by Amazigh women as markers of tribal identity and family wealth. This craft tradition, which blended Jewish metalworking techniques with indigenous Berber aesthetic preferences, produced one of the most distinctive jewelry traditions in the Moroccan world.

## Notable Figures

The rabbis of Tarudant are less prominently documented in printed halakhic literature than their northern counterparts, reflecting the more oral and manuscript-based character of southern Moroccan Jewish learning. However, the community maintained rabbinical courts and produced responsa in manuscript form that await full scholarly study.

Local saints (tzaddikim) venerated in and around Tarudant include figures remembered in oral tradition rather than in printed hagiographic collections — their graves served as pilgrimage sites for the entire region and continued to attract visitors into the 21st century.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Tarudant mellah contained multiple synagogues, serving both the resident community and Jewish merchants who visited on market days from surrounding villages. The Talmud Torah provided basic religious education, though advanced study often required travel to Marrakech or Fez.

The community's beth din handled the legal affairs of southern Moroccan Jews across a wide geographic area, its jurisdiction extending to smaller communities in the Souss Valley that lacked their own courts.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Berber-speaking identity of Tarudant's Jews was central to their cultural distinctiveness. Unlike the Arabic-speaking Jews of northern Moroccan cities, Tarudant Jews spoke Tachelhit as their daily language, with Hebrew reserved for religious contexts and commercial Judeo-Arabic for trade. Their liturgical Hebrew pronunciation was inflected by Tachelhit phonology, producing a distinctive sound that ethnomusicologists have documented as a linguistic-musical fossil.

The community's oral literature — proverbs, songs, wedding poems, and Passover celebrations — was conducted in Tachelhit, creating a body of Judeo-Berber expression that represents a unique branch of the Jewish linguistic-cultural tree. Their distinctive wedding ceremonies blended biblical and kabbalistic symbolism with Berber customary practices.

## Decline & Dispersal

The 20th century brought rapid change to Tarudant. Improved transportation connected the Souss Valley to Agadir, Marrakech, and Casablanca, drawing young people away from the ancient city. The 1948 Arab-Israeli War, the Agadir earthquake of 1960, and Moroccan independence all accelerated emigration. By the early 1970s, the ancient Jewish community of Tarudant had essentially relocated — most to Israel, others to France and Canada.

## Legacy Today

Tarudant's old mellah, remarkably preserved within the city's ancient walls, is now a heritage site. The city's Jewish cemetery remains; saint shrines attract occasional pilgrim visitors. Several documentary projects have preserved oral testimonies from elderly Tarudant Jews in Israel. The silversmith traditions of the Souss Valley are documented in museum collections in Israel (the Israel Museum, Haifa Museum) and Morocco.

# Rakhmistrivka

## Overview

Rakhmistrivka — a small town in the Cherkasy Oblast of central Ukraine — is known in Hasidic history as the seat of the Rakhmistrivka (Trisker-Rakhmistrivka) Hasidic dynasty, a branch of the Chernobyl Hasidic dynasty that took root in Podolia and became one of the distinguished courts of Ukrainian Hasidism. The dynasty's origins connect to the broader Chernobyl network — descended from the Maggid of Mezeritch through Rabbi Menachem Nachum of Chernobyl — and its history illustrates the way Hasidism spread through the dense landscape of Ukrainian Jewish towns.

## Historical Background

Rakhmistrivka was a small Podolian market town in the economically rich agricultural region between the Dnieper and the Southern Bug rivers. Its Jewish community engaged in the standard trades of the region: grain commerce, crafts, trade at the weekly market. The town's transformation into a Hasidic center came when a member of the Chernobyl dynasty — the Trisker rebbe's lineage — established his court there.

## The Trisker-Rakhmistrivka Dynasty

The Rakhmistrivka dynasty descended from the Chernobyl Hasidic tradition through Rabbi Mordechai of Chernobyl (1770–1837) and his many sons, who spread out across Ukraine to found branch courts. The Trisker Maggid — Rabbi Avraham of Trisk (1806–1889) — was one of Mordechai of Chernobyl's sons who established a court in Trisk (Turiisk) in Volhynia. Branches of this line eventually settled in Rakhmistrivka, giving the community its Hasidic character.

The Chernobyl dynasty as a whole was remarkable for its warmth toward ordinary Jews. Rabbi Menachem Nachum of Chernobyl, the dynasty's founder, had been a disciple of both the Baal Shem Tov and the Maggid of Mezeritch, and he carried forward the Besht's spirit of inclusive, joyful Hasidism. His major work, "Me'or Einayim" (Light of the Eyes), is a commentary on the Torah that emphasizes finding divine light in every aspect of creation. The Chernobyl courts — and their Rakhmistrivka branch — were known for fervent prayer, devotional singing (niggunim), and the rebbe's personal connection to his Hasidim.

## Notable Figures

The rebbes of Rakhmistrivka served their community as spiritual guides, counselors in personal matters, and intercessors before God. The rebbe's court in a small town like Rakhmistrivka functioned as the community's social and spiritual center: people came with their troubles, their questions, and their celebrations. The rebbe blessed newborns, advised on marriages, prayed for the sick, and upheld the dignity of each individual who came before him.

The dynasty survived World War II in truncated form; members of the Rakhmistrivka dynasty who escaped established communities in Israel and the United States, where the dynasty continues today in Jerusalem.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Rakhmistrivka court included the rebbe's prayer hall, his study, and the gathering space where Hasidim assembled for Shabbat and holiday celebrations. The town's ordinary Jewish community maintained a main synagogue and standard kehilla institutions. The two coexisted and overlapped — the rebbe's court was not separate from but rather the spiritual heart of the broader community.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Chernobyl dynasty's "Me'or Einayim" and the broader corpus of Chernobyl Hasidic teaching represent significant contributions to the literature of contemplative Judaism. The emphasis on finding divine light hidden within the "shells" of everyday material existence — a teaching rooted in Lurianic Kabbalah but made accessible by the Chernobyl masters — gave ordinary Ukrainian Jews a framework for sanctifying their daily lives. The dynasty's musical tradition, particularly its characteristic niggunim (wordless melodies), has survived and continues to be sung in Chernobyl-lineage Hasidic communities worldwide.

## Decline & Dispersal

The German occupation of central Ukraine in 1941 brought the murder of most of Rakhmistrivka's Jewish population. The rebbe and some family members escaped to Israel, where the dynasty was reestablished.

## Legacy Today

The Rakhmistrivka dynasty has its main center in Jerusalem, where it continues to attract followers from Israel, America, and the broader Hasidic world. The dynasty is part of the living legacy of the destroyed Ukrainian Hasidic world.

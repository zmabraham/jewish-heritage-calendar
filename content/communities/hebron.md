# Hebron

## Overview

Hebron is perhaps the most primordially Jewish city in the world — the place where the Jewish story as family history begins. Abraham purchased the Cave of Machpelah here to bury Sarah, in what the Torah presents as the first formal Jewish acquisition of land in Canaan. That cave, now covered by a magnificent Herodian structure, holds the tombs of Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebekah, Jacob and Leah. For Jews, entering Hebron is entering the first chapter of their own ancestry. This is not metaphor: these are specific graves in a specific place that has been venerated for three and a half thousand years.

The city's Hebrew name, Chevron, derives from the root for "connection" or "joining" — fitting for a city that connects the living to the dead, the present to the patriarchal past. Hebron is the second holiest Jewish site after Jerusalem (some traditions rank it fourth, after Jerusalem, Safed, and Tiberias), and the continuous Jewish presence there — however attenuated at times — reflects an attachment rooted in the deepest layers of Jewish identity.

Hebron's history is also one of the most painful in Jewish memory. The 1929 massacre, in which Arab rioters killed sixty-seven Jews and ended the ancient continuous community, stands as one of the worst acts of anti-Jewish violence in the Holy Land during the British Mandate. Yet even after that catastrophe, and after nineteen years of Jordanian rule that excluded Jews entirely, a Jewish community returned to Hebron after the Six-Day War of 1967.

## Historical Origins

The founding moment of the Jewish relationship with Hebron is recorded in Genesis 23: Abraham's purchase of the Cave of Machpelah (Mearath HaMachpelah) from Ephron the Hittite for four hundred shekels of silver — a carefully legal transaction, recorded in the presence of witnesses, intended to establish incontestable ownership. This grave for Sarah became the family tomb of the patriarchs and matriarchs, and Hebron became the first city where a Jewish family had a permanent, purchased stake in the land.

David was anointed king at Hebron — it was his first capital, where he reigned for seven and a half years before capturing Jerusalem. The city thus carries enormous political as well as religious significance: it was the seat of Davidic monarchy before Jerusalem assumed that role. After David's move to Jerusalem, Hebron retained its importance as the home of the ancestral graves and as a major Levitical city.

The Israelite presence in Hebron was ancient and deep: archaeological excavations have uncovered Bronze Age and Iron Age remains testifying to continuous habitation. The city appears throughout the biblical narrative — Joshua's allocation, Caleb's inheritance, Absalom's revolt (which began in Hebron) — as a central reference point in Israelite history.

## Golden Age

Hebron's Jewish golden age is harder to date than those of Jerusalem or Safed, because the city's importance was never primarily about scholarship or commerce but about sacred presence. The community that maintained the ancestral graves was performing a holy function: guarding and venerating the resting place of the founders of the Jewish people.

In the medieval and early modern period, Hebron supported a small but distinguished community of scholars. The arrival of Sephardic exiles after 1492 invigorated the community. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, Hebron was home to a yeshiva closely connected to Chabad Hasidism — the Tzemach Tzedek (Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneersohn, third Chabad Rebbe) sent students to strengthen the Hebron community, and it maintained a Chabad flavor for generations. The Slabodka Yeshiva, one of Lithuania's greatest, relocated to Hebron in 1925, establishing Hebron as a major center of Torah learning in the British Mandate period.

## Notable Figures

**Caleb ben Yefunneh** received Hebron as his inheritance after the conquest of Canaan, as reward for his faithful report as one of the twelve spies. His family maintained the ancient Israelite connection to the city, and Caleb is considered a founding figure of Hebron's Israelite identity.

**King David (c. 1010–970 BCE)** was anointed in Hebron and ruled his first kingdom from there. The seven-and-a-half years in Hebron before Jerusalem represent the incubation of the Davidic monarchy.

**Rabbi Avraham Azulai (1570–1643)**, a distinguished Kabbalist who settled in Hebron and produced important works including Chesed LeAvraham, a mystical treatise on the spiritual significance of the Land of Israel. He helped establish Hebron's place within the Kabbalistic geography of the Holy Land.

**Rabbi Eliyahu de Vidas (c. 1518–c. 1592)**, though associated primarily with Safed, maintained connections with the Hebron community and wrote Reshit Chochma, an influential Kabbalistic ethical work that drew on the proximity to the patriarchal graves.

**Rabbi Moshe Shmuel Shapiro (1892–1929)**, head of the Slabodka Yeshiva in Hebron until the 1929 massacre ended his community. He survived but was injured; the yeshiva eventually relocated to Jerusalem. He represents the Lithuanian Torah world's deep investment in Hebron.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Cave of Machpelah (Mearath HaMachpelah)** is the defining sacred space. The massive Herodian enclosure built by Herod the Great in the first century BCE — made of enormous, beautifully cut ashlar stones in the characteristic Herodian style — surrounds and covers the cave itself. The structure has served as a synagogue, church, and mosque at various points in history; today it is divided, with Jewish and Muslim access to different sections, and full Jewish access on certain occasions.

The **Abraham Avinu Synagogue** in Hebron's Jewish Quarter, originally built in the sixteenth century, was destroyed in 1929 and rebuilt after 1967. It serves the contemporary Jewish community of Kiryat Arba and the Jewish Quarter of Hebron. The **Tomb of Jesse and Ruth** (traditional attribution) is another venerated site in the area.

## Cultural Life

Hebron's Jewish cultural life was shaped by its dual character as a sacred pilgrimage city and a commercial center. The region has been famous for its grapes since biblical times — the scouts sent by Moses reportedly brought back enormous clusters of grapes from Hebron's valley. Glass production was a Jewish specialty in Ottoman Hebron; the distinctive blue Hebron glass, still produced in the region, has roots in centuries of Jewish craftsmanship.

The community's religious culture was influenced by both Sephardic traditions (dominant in the Ottoman period) and later by Ashkenazic Lithuanian Torah study (with the Slabodka Yeshiva). This dual inheritance gave Hebron a distinctive quality: ancient mystical piety combined with rigorous analytic Talmud study.

## Decline & Transformation

The **Hebron massacre of August 1929** was the culminating blow to the ancient community. During the Arab riots that swept the country, a mob attacked the Jewish Quarter, killing sixty-seven men, women, and children — including many yeshiva students — and wounding many more. The survivors were evacuated by the British. Though some families briefly returned, the Jewish community effectively ended its ancient continuous presence.

For nineteen years (1948–1967), Hebron was under Jordanian rule, and Jews were barred entirely from the Cave of Machpelah. The Six-Day War returned Hebron to Israeli control, and shortly afterward — in a controversial act — a group of Jewish settlers led by Rabbi Moshe Levinger established a new Jewish presence, eventually developing into the community of Kiryat Arba adjacent to Hebron and a small Jewish Quarter within the city itself.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Hebron's legacy is carried by every Jew who recites the Amidah prayer with its reference to the "God of Abraham, God of Isaac, God of Jacob" — the patriarchs buried in Machpelah. The city is an indelible presence in the Jewish imagination. The ongoing reality of the community — small, controversial, besieged — continues to embody the ancient claim to the first purchased land. Heritage organizations work to document and preserve the memory of the pre-1929 community, and descendants of Hebron's Jewish families maintain active memorial societies.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: c. 1800 BCE (Abraham's purchase); continuous Jewish presence until 1929
- **Peak Population**: ~1,500–2,000 (early 20th century, pre-massacre)
- **Languages**: Ladino, Yiddish, Hebrew, Arabic (Judeo-Arabic)
- **Status Today**: Small Jewish community in Kiryat Arba and H2 (Israeli-controlled) Hebron; major pilgrimage site

# Marburg

## Overview

Marburg, a university town on the Lahn River in Hesse, was home to a medieval Jewish community of modest size but notable antiquity. The community was definitively expelled in 1524 when the Protestant Reformation's early influence gave local authorities ideological cover for the removal of Jews. Marburg's Jewish history reflects the dual pressures on medieval German Jewry: the financial exploitation by territorial rulers who protected Jews for profit, and the social and religious hostility from Christian townspeople and clergy that could, at any moment, tip into violence or expulsion.

## Historical Background

Jews are documented in Marburg from at least the thirteenth century. The city's status as the residence of the Landgraves of Hesse gave the Jewish community a direct relationship with this ruling family. Landgraves could and did provide letters of protection to Jewish families; they could also, when politically expedient, withdraw that protection or allow mob violence to occur.

The Marburg community was small — likely never more than a few dozen families — but occupied an important commercial niche. Jewish moneylenders served university students, burghers, and nobles. Jewish traders participated in local and regional markets. Periodic violence marked the community's history, including accusations of ritual murder and host desecration that could trigger riots and judicial murder.

## The Expulsion of 1524

The definitive expulsion of Marburg's Jews in 1524 came in the early years of the Protestant Reformation, when the young Landgrave Philip of Hesse (known to history as "Philip the Magnanimous") came to power. Philip, a supporter of Lutheran reform, authorized the expulsion of Jews from Hesse in 1524. While Luther himself, at this early stage in his career, was not yet the vehement antisemite he would become in his later writings (his notorious 1543 tract *On the Jews and Their Lies* came decades later), the general atmosphere of religious transformation and reassertion of Christian identity created conditions unfavorable to Jewish residence.

The Jews of Marburg, like those of other Hessian cities, were compelled to leave. Their property was subject to seizure or forced sale at disadvantageous prices. Where they went — to other German territories, to Poland, to the Ottoman Empire — is only partially traceable.

## The University and Jewish Learning

The University of Marburg, founded in 1527 (just after the expulsion) as the first Protestant university in Germany, had no Jewish students or faculty for centuries, as Jewish access to German universities was legally restricted. The university's later history includes distinguished Jewish faculty; in the twentieth century, among the notable figures associated with Marburg was the philosopher Hermann Cohen (1842–1918), founder of the Neo-Kantian Marburg school.

Hermann Cohen's Neo-Kantian philosophy had profound implications for modern Jewish thought. He argued for a rational basis for ethics derived from Kantian philosophy, and in his later work developed a sophisticated philosophy of Judaism that rejected mysticism and nationalism in favor of an ethical monotheism he believed represented Judaism's highest spiritual achievement. His work influenced the development of Liberal Judaism in Germany and shaped major Jewish thinkers of the twentieth century, including Franz Rosenzweig and Martin Buber.

## Notable Figures

Hermann Cohen (1842–1918) stands as the dominant Jewish intellectual figure associated with Marburg, though he came from Coswig and taught at the university for decades. His *Religion of Reason out of the Sources of Judaism* (1919, posthumously published) remains a major work of modern Jewish philosophy.

The medieval community left no named scholars of comparable fame, though its existence was part of the fabric of Hessian Jewry whose traditions fed into later German Jewish scholarship.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The medieval community maintained a synagogue and a cemetery. A mikveh likely existed as well. The community's communal life followed standard patterns of medieval German Jewry: Shabbat and holiday observance, Torah study, charitable giving, burial arrangements, and the observance of family law under rabbinical supervision.

In the modern period, Jews returned to Marburg in the nineteenth century and established a new community with its own synagogue, which was destroyed during the Kristallnacht pogrom.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Through Hermann Cohen, Marburg became associated with an influential strand of modern Jewish thought that sought to ground Jewish religious life in rational ethics rather than theological revelation or mystical experience. Cohen's reading of Jewish sources through a Kantian lens, while controversial, opened new possibilities for understanding the relationship between Jewish tradition and Western philosophy.

## Decline & Dispersal

The medieval community was expelled in 1524. The modern community that developed in the nineteenth century was destroyed under National Socialism. Jewish professors were removed from Marburg University after 1933. Members of the community who did not emigrate in time were deported and murdered.

## Legacy Today

Marburg's Jewish heritage is preserved in historical scholarship and memorial sites. The preserved Jewish cemetery and archaeological evidence of the medieval community are maintained. The connection to Hermann Cohen is honored in philosophical scholarship, and several editions of his works have been published. Marburg today has a small Jewish community and active historical memory projects.

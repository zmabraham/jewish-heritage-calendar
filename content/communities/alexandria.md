# Alexandria

## Overview

Alexandria was the largest and most intellectually vibrant Jewish community in the ancient Hellenistic diaspora. Founded by Alexander the Great in 331 BCE, the city became the capital of the Ptolemaic kingdom and home to a Jewish population that at its height may have numbered 100,000 to 200,000 — perhaps a third to two-fifths of the city's total inhabitants. Here, Judaism first engaged in sustained dialogue with Greek philosophy, and the Hebrew Bible was translated into Greek, reshaping the history of both Judaism and Christianity.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Alexandria began almost with the city's founding. Alexander himself reportedly granted Jews rights of residence, and the Ptolemaic rulers who succeeded him actively recruited Jews for administrative and military roles. Two of Alexandria's five districts were predominantly Jewish. Jews served as soldiers in the Ptolemaic army, as tax-farmers, as merchants, and in the highest levels of court administration. The Letter of Aristeas (2nd century BCE) recounts — in idealized form — how Ptolemy II Philadelphus invited seventy-two Jewish elders to Alexandria to translate the Torah into Greek, producing what became the Septuagint (LXX).

The Septuagint translation, completed in stages over the 3rd and 2nd centuries BCE, is Alexandria's most enduring contribution to world civilization. It made the Hebrew scriptures accessible to Greek-speaking Jews throughout the diaspora and, later, to early Christians, shaping theological vocabulary and hermeneutical method for two millennia. The Alexandrian Jewish community celebrated the translation's completion annually on the island of Pharos.

## Golden Age

Under the Ptolemies and the early Roman period (after 30 BCE), Alexandrian Jewry flourished intellectually and commercially. The community possessed its own council (gerousia), synagogues, courts, and civic institutions. Jews participated in the cultural life of one of the ancient world's greatest cities, home to the famous Library of Alexandria and the Mouseion (proto-university).

The peak of Alexandrian Jewish intellectual achievement came in the first century CE with Philo of Alexandria (c. 20 BCE – 50 CE). Philo wrote entirely in Greek and produced an enormous body of allegorical biblical commentary that sought to demonstrate the harmony between the Torah and Platonic philosophy. His concept of the Logos (divine word/reason) as intermediary between God and creation was enormously influential on Christian theology, though his direct impact on rabbinic Judaism was limited. Philo also led a diplomatic mission to the Emperor Caligula in 39–40 CE to petition on behalf of Alexandrian Jews who had been subjected to violent anti-Jewish rioting and pogrom under the governor Flaccus — an episode he described in *Against Flaccus* and *Embassy to Gaius*.

## Notable Figures

Philo Judaeus remains the dominant intellectual figure: philosopher, biblical exegete, communal leader, and author of some fifty extant works. His nephew Tiberius Julius Alexander apostatized and became a prominent Roman general who commanded a legion at the siege of Jerusalem in 70 CE — a painful instance of assimilation's outer limit.

Earlier, the poet Ezekiel the Tragedian (2nd century BCE) composed Greek dramatic verse on the Exodus story, fragments of which survive, representing a remarkable fusion of Hebrew narrative and Greek theatrical form. The philosopher Aristobulus (2nd century BCE) argued that Plato and Aristotle had drawn on Mosaic wisdom — the earliest known attempt at Jewish-Hellenistic philosophical synthesis.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Alexandria's main synagogue was described by the Talmud as an architectural marvel: so vast that flags were used to signal worshippers in the back when to respond "Amen." This Great Synagogue, possibly a basilica-form structure, seated thousands and was destroyed during the Jewish revolt of 115–117 CE. The city also had numerous smaller synagogues (proseuche, "places of prayer") in different neighborhoods.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Alexandria's Jewish community pioneered the genre of Jewish Hellenistic literature: historical epics (Artapanus, who portrayed Moses as the founder of Egyptian civilization), philosophical treatises, tragic drama, and wisdom literature (the Book of Wisdom, or Wisdom of Solomon, was likely composed in Alexandria). The Septuagint itself generated further textual creativity: Greek additions to Esther and Daniel originated in Alexandria.

The community's bilingualism produced a distinctive form of Jewish piety comfortable in Greek dress while maintaining halakhic observance — a tension that generated both creativity and controversy with Palestinian Jewish authorities.

## Decline & Dispersal

The catastrophic revolt of Alexandrian Jews in 115–117 CE (the "Kitos War," under Trajan) resulted in massive loss of life and the effective destruction of the large Alexandrian Jewish community. The revolt spread across Egypt, Cyprus, and Cyrenaica and was suppressed with enormous brutality. The Jewish population of Alexandria never fully recovered.

Under Byzantine Christian rule (4th–7th centuries), Alexandrian Jews faced increasing legal disabilities. The patriarch Cyril expelled the Jewish community from Alexandria in 415 CE following sectarian violence — one of the first forced expulsions of a Jewish community in the Christian era. Though Jews later returned, the community never regained its ancient grandeur.

The medieval and modern community was smaller but significant. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, Alexandria again became home to a cosmopolitan Jewish population — Sephardic, Ashkenazic, and Italian Jews — connected to Mediterranean trade. Jewish families played central roles in Alexandria's cotton trade and intellectual life. By 1945 the community numbered approximately 25,000; by 1970, following the 1956 and 1967 crises, almost all had left.

## Legacy Today

The ancient Alexandrian synagogue tradition survives through the Rite of Alexandria, which influenced North African liturgy. Scattered Alexandrian Jewish artifacts and manuscripts are preserved in European collections. The city's Jewish cemetery was vandalized in the late 20th century but has been partially restored. The modern Bibliotheca Alexandrina, opened in 2002 near the ancient library's presumed site, maintains an exhibit on the city's multicultural past including its Jewish heritage.

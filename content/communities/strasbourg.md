# Strasbourg

## Overview

Strasbourg's Jewish community embodies the unique Alsatian Jewish experience — caught perpetually between France and Germany, shaped by their rivalry and alternating sovereignties, and producing a distinctive Jewish culture that was neither purely German nor purely French. It is also the site of one of the most infamous massacres of the Black Death period: in February 1349, all Jews in Strasbourg were seized and burned alive, one of the largest single acts of anti-Jewish violence in medieval Europe.

## Historical Origins

Jewish presence in Strasbourg is documented from the twelfth century. The city's position as the most important city in Alsace, at the crossing of major trade routes between France and the Holy Roman Empire, made it a natural center for Jewish commerce. The community grew through the thirteenth century despite periodic harassment; Jews were involved in money-lending, trade, and the provision of goods to the city's growing population.

The relationship between the Jewish community and Strasbourg's civic authorities was always tense. The city's guild-based oligarchy resented Jewish commercial competition; the local clergy maintained the standard repertoire of anti-Jewish accusations. Yet Jews remained because they were economically necessary — the credit they provided was essential to commercial life at a time when Christian usury prohibitions limited lending.

## The Black Death Massacre, 1349

The Black Death catastrophe of 1348–1349 brought one of the worst instances of anti-Jewish violence in medieval Europe to Strasbourg. As the plague devastated Europe, accusations spread that Jews had caused it by poisoning wells — accusations that began in southern France and spread rapidly northward.

The Strasbourg city council, which had a relatively cautious attitude toward the Jewish community, was overthrown in an internal political struggle. The new, guild-dominated council moved quickly against the Jews. On February 14, 1349 — a Saturday, Shabbat — approximately 2,000 Jews of Strasbourg were seized and taken to the Jewish cemetery. There they were burned alive; some 1,000 accepted baptism to save their lives. The sources describe women throwing their wedding rings into the fire.

The community's wealth was confiscated and distributed — to the guilds, the city, and the cathedral — and their property was seized. Jews were barred from Strasbourg for over a century after this event.

## Revival and the Early Modern Period

Jews began returning to Alsace in small numbers in the late fifteenth century, settling primarily in the villages rather than the cities. Strasbourg itself remained closed to Jewish settlement for centuries — the city was dominated by Protestant guilds after the Reformation, and these guilds maintained the exclusion of Jews fiercely.

The French annexation of Alsace in 1681 did not immediately improve conditions. It was only in 1791, with the Revolution's emancipation of all French Jews, that Jews were legally entitled to settle in Strasbourg. Even then, local resistance was substantial; the Revolution's proclamation of Jewish equality was met with riots in some Alsatian villages.

Napoleon's emancipatory policies of the early nineteenth century, particularly the establishment of the Consistoire system, created the framework within which Alsatian Jewry could develop. Strasbourg's Jewish community grew rapidly through the nineteenth century.

## Notable Figures

The Alsatian Jewish community produced notable figures across many fields. Rabbi Samuel Levy Hacohen Ettlinger (1798–1871), a leading halakhic authority, served in Alsace and maintained the traditional Orthodox standard against the Reform movement.

Strasbourg-born Jews participated prominently in French political and intellectual life after emancipation. The Alsatian Jewish community as a whole, with its German cultural formation and French political loyalty, contributed a distinctive intellectual style to French Jewish culture.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Grand Synagogue of Strasbourg (1898), in the Moorish-Romanesque style fashionable among nineteenth-century synagogue architects, is one of the finest synagogue buildings in France. The community also maintained the New Synagogue and a network of smaller congregations serving the diverse Jewish population.

After the Nazi annexation of Alsace in 1940, the Great Synagogue was desecrated and used as a stable; it was restored after the war and remains in active use.

## Cultural Life

Strasbourg's Jewish culture was distinctively bilingual — German and French — reflecting the city's perpetual border position. German Jewish intellectual and Reform influence was strong; the community produced graduates of Frankfurt yeshivas and Berlin seminaries, as well as French-educated lawyers and physicians. The Alsatian Jewish dialect — a form of Alsatian German with Hebrew and Yiddish elements — was the domestic tongue of many families well into the twentieth century.

The community maintained a rich associational life: learned societies, charitable organizations, Zionist groups, and cultural clubs. The French patriotism of Alsatian Jews, heightened by the German annexation of 1871–1918, gave the community a distinctive political character.

## Decline & Transformation

The German occupation of Alsace (1940–1944) forced most Jews to flee westward; many Strasbourg Jews evacuated to the unoccupied zone. Those who remained or returned were subject to persecution, deportation, and murder. After liberation in 1944, the community rebuilt.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Strasbourg today has a Jewish community of approximately 12,000–15,000, one of the largest in provincial France. The city's position as the seat of the European Parliament and the Council of Europe has given it a cosmopolitan character that supports a diverse Jewish community. The memory of the 1349 massacre is maintained in historical education and community commemoration.

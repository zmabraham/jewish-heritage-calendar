# Telšiai (Telz)

## Overview

Telšiai — known throughout the Jewish world as *Telz* — was a small but extraordinarily influential Lithuanian city. Its fame rested entirely on the Telz Yeshiva, founded in 1875, which became one of the great institutions of Lithuanian Torah scholarship and whose intellectual legacy — transplanted to America after the Holocaust — continues to shape yeshiva education in Cleveland, Ohio and beyond. Telz represented the quintessential Lithuanian yeshiva town: modest in size, intense in scholarship, shaped by the Mussar movement, and utterly destroyed by the Nazis in a single act of mass murder in the summer of 1941.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Telšiai during the eighteenth century as the town developed as a regional market center in northwestern Lithuania. The community was typical of Lithuanian Jewry in its structure: merchants and craftsmen supporting a learned class of students and scholars. Telz was part of the Kaunas Governorate under Russian rule and subject to the Pale of Settlement regulations that confined Jews to specific areas of the Russian Empire. Despite geographic restrictions, Telz developed a prosperous enough community to support significant educational institutions.

## Golden Age

The Telz Yeshiva, formally established in 1875, was reorganized and achieved its greatest stature under Rabbi Eliezer Gordon (1841–1910) and his son-in-law Rabbi Shimon Shkop (1860–1939). The yeshiva became known for its rigorous analytical method — systematic categorization of legal concepts, emphasis on developing original thinking, and application of the Brisker method adapted in distinctive Telz fashion. Rabbi Shkop's works, especially *Sha'are Yosher*, became classic texts of yeshiva study. The yeshiva produced hundreds of rabbis and scholars who served communities across Eastern Europe and the world.

The Telz Yeshiva also became associated with the Mussar movement, incorporating ethical self-examination alongside intellectual rigor. Its students underwent structured introspection and character development alongside their Talmudic studies. This integration of intellectual and ethical training gave Telz graduates a distinctive profile: learned, disciplined, and morally serious.

Rabbi Yosef Leib Bloch (1860–1930) led the yeshiva in its most distinguished period. Under his leadership, the yeshiva's student body swelled to several hundred, and its reputation extended across the Jewish world. His son Rabbi Avraham Yitzchak Bloch continued his leadership until the Holocaust.

## Notable Figures

**Rabbi Eliezer Gordon** — founding rosh yeshiva whose energy and scholarship established Telz's reputation. **Rabbi Shimon Shkop** — produced the definitive analytical works of the Telz method. **Rabbi Yosef Leib Bloch** — presided over the yeshiva's golden age and authored the ethical-philosophical work *Sha'arei Da'as*. **Rabbi Avraham Yitzchak Bloch** — led the yeshiva in the 1930s until his murder in the Holocaust. **Rabbi Eliyahu Meir Bloch** and **Rabbi Chaim Mordechai Katz** — escaped to America and reestablished the Telz Yeshiva in Cleveland, Ohio in 1941, ensuring the tradition's survival.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Telz Yeshiva was the dominant institution in the town's Jewish life. The yeshiva complex included a large study hall (*beis midrash*), dormitories, a dining hall, and administrative buildings. The town also maintained a regular synagogue, prayer houses, elementary schools, a *mikveh*, and the standard apparatus of a Lithuanian Jewish community. The community cemetery dated to the eighteenth century.

## Cultural Life

Life in Telz was organized around the yeshiva. The town's economy depended significantly on the yeshiva — dormitory students needed food, clothing, and supplies, and their families visiting for holidays provided income. The town had a modest Jewish intellectual and cultural life beyond the yeshiva: small Zionist groups, a Hebrew school, and connections to the broader Lithuanian Jewish world. But the overwhelming cultural identity of Telz was defined by Torah scholarship. Young men came from across Lithuania, Poland, and beyond to study; local boys aspired to be admitted to the yeshiva itself.

## Decline & Transformation

Soviet occupation in 1940 closed the yeshiva and Jewish organizations. Rabbi Eliyahu Meir Bloch and Rabbi Chaim Mordechai Katz were abroad when the occupation began and did not return, traveling instead to America where they would rebuild the institution. The German invasion in June 1941 was immediately followed by mass murder. In late June and July 1941, German forces and Lithuanian collaborators concentrated the Jews of Telšiai and surrounding villages. The men — including the remaining yeshiva faculty and students — were shot at the Rainiai massacre on June 25–26, 1941. The women and children were kept in a labor camp briefly before being shot at Lake Mastis in August 1941. Virtually the entire Jewish community of Telšiai perished — estimated at 3,000–4,000 people.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Telz Yeshiva in Cleveland, Ohio, established in 1941 by Rabbis Bloch and Katz, became a major American yeshiva. It moved to Wickliffe, Ohio (a Cleveland suburb), where it operates today as one of America's foremost Torah institutions. A sister institution was established in Chicago. The Telz tradition in America produced generations of American-born rabbis, scholars, and Jewish leaders. The analytical and ethical methods of Telz are taught in yeshivot worldwide. In Telšiai today, the mass murder sites at Lake Mastis and Rainiai are memorialized, and the city maintains historical documentation of its destroyed Jewish community.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 18th century; Yeshiva established 1875
- **Peak Population**: ~4,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Hebrew, Lithuanian
- **Status Today**: Community destroyed; Telz Yeshiva continues in Cleveland/Wickliffe, Ohio, USA

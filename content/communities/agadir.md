# Agadir

## Overview

Agadir, on Morocco's southern Atlantic coast at the gateway to the Souss Valley and the Anti-Atlas Mountains, was the commercial nexus for a distinctive population of Berber-speaking (Tachelhit) Jews who inhabited not only the port city but a vast network of small communities throughout the Sous plain and mountain valleys. These Berber Jewish communities — among the most ancient in Morocco — maintained a way of life distinct from both the Sephardic urban communities of the north and the Arabic-speaking communities of the center, preserving indigenous North African Jewish traditions of extraordinary antiquity.

## Historical Background

Jewish presence in the Souss Valley region predates the Arab conquest of the 7th century CE and may reach back to Punic or Roman times. The indigenous Berber population of the region — the Imazighen (Amazigh people), speaking Tachelhit — included Jewish communities that had presumably converted local populations or been present since antiquity. These communities lived in mountain villages and valley towns alongside Berber Muslim neighbors, maintaining a symbiotic relationship of trade and mutual dependence.

Agadir itself (the name means "fortified granary" in Tachelhit) was developed as a Portuguese fort (Santa Cruz do Cabo de Gué) in the 16th century, then passed to Moroccan control. The city served as the outlet for the Souss region's agricultural and craft production, and Jewish merchants based in Agadir or traveling regularly to the city managed the trade between the Souss interior and the coastal and international markets.

## Golden Age

The 18th and 19th centuries saw Agadir's Jewish community at its most dynamic. Jewish itinerant merchants from the Souss — known in the literature as "schlichim" (emissaries) or more accurately as traveling merchants (the Souss was famous for producing traders who traveled throughout Morocco and even to West Africa) — ranged across vast territories carrying manufactured goods from the coast and returning with agricultural products, livestock, and local crafts.

This itinerant trading pattern created communities that were widely dispersed geographically but tightly connected by kinship, credit networks, and religious observance. A merchant might spend most of the year in distant markets while his family remained in the home village; synagogues, Jewish cemeteries, and communal institutions existed in tiny Souss villages that might contain only a few Jewish families.

## Notable Figures

The rabbis of the southern communities are less well documented in print than their northern counterparts, but local traditions preserve the memories of Souss scholars who circulated through village communities providing halakhic guidance, scribal services, and religious leadership.

Rabbi Shlomo Amar (a name common in the region) and similar figures served as itinerant rabbis serving clusters of villages. Their manuscript responsa, many still unpublished, await scholarly attention in Israeli and Moroccan archives.

The community also produced silversmiths of great skill; Souss Jewish artisans supplied the elaborate silver jewelry worn by Berber women throughout the region, and their work was prized far beyond the immediate area.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Agadir's mellah contained several synagogues serving the port city community. Throughout the Souss Valley and Anti-Atlas, virtually every Jewish village — however small — maintained a synagogue (often a modest room in a private home), a Torah scroll, and enough observant men to maintain communal prayer.

The Alliance Israélite Universelle established schools in the Souss region relatively late compared to the north, and many southern communities continued to rely on traditional hadarim (religious elementary schools) well into the 20th century.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Berber-speaking Jewish communities of the Souss preserved traditions that differed notably from those of Morocco's Arabic-speaking urban communities. Their Hebrew pronunciation was influenced by Tachelhit phonology; their folk practices blended Jewish religious observance with Berber cultural customs in ways that ethnographers have found fascinating.

The silversmith tradition was the community's most celebrated artistic achievement. Souss Jewish jewelry — featuring coral, silver, amber, and enamel in distinctive geometric patterns — was both a commercial product and an expression of cultural identity. Women's jewelry served simultaneously as financial reserve, social display, and protective amulet.

## Decline & Dispersal

The catastrophic earthquake that destroyed Agadir in February 1960 — one of the worst natural disasters in Moroccan history, killing approximately 15,000 people — devastated the port city's community, Jewish and Muslim alike. Much of the Jewish quarter was destroyed. Coming just four years after Moroccan independence, the earthquake accelerated emigration: many surviving Agadir Jews chose to go directly to Israel or France rather than rebuild.

The smaller Souss Valley communities had already been declining as economic development drew young people to Casablanca and other cities. The post-1960 period saw rapid abandonment of the mountain and valley communities. By 1970, the ancient network of Souss Berber Jewish villages was effectively empty.

## Legacy Today

Israeli cities with concentrations of Moroccan immigrants — particularly Ashdod, Netanya, and Beersheba — contain significant populations of Souss Jewish descent. Their distinctive musical traditions (including the use of Berber-language Jewish songs) and craft traditions have been partially preserved through ethnomusicological recording and museum collection. The Memory of the Jews of the Souss project in Israel has worked to document and preserve this heritage.

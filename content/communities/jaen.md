# Jaén

## Overview

Jaén, the Andalusian city known as the "gateway to Andalusia" for its strategic position beneath the Sierra Morena mountains, harbored Jewish communities across two distinct eras: a distinguished community during the Muslim period that produced one of the most celebrated Jewish statesmen of medieval Iberia, and a significant post-reconquest community under Castilian rule that flourished until the catastrophes of 1391 and 1492. Located where the olive-covered hills descend toward the Guadalquivir valley — a region that today produces more olive oil than any other province on earth — Jaén's Jewish history is one of remarkable early brilliance followed by centuries of frontier life, violence, and ultimate exile.

## Historical Origins

Jews lived in Muslim **Jayyān** before the Castilian reconquest, and it was from this community that one of medieval Jewry's greatest figures emerged. **Isaac ben Ezra** — father of the celebrated **Hisdai ibn Shaprut** (c. 915–970) — moved from Jaén to Córdoba, where his son would become physician to Caliph Abd al-Rahman III and one of the most powerful Jewish statesmen in world history. Hisdai ibn Shaprut used his position to support Jewish learning throughout the Mediterranean, corresponded with the Khazar kingdom, and patronized the scholars who made Córdoba the cultural capital of medieval Jewish civilization. That this lineage originated in Jaén gives the city an outsized importance in the early history of Sephardic civilization.

In the late eleventh century, a **Rabbi Isaac** headed Jaén's Jewish community and corresponded with the great legal authority **Isaac Alfasi (the Rif)** on halakhic questions. Jews in Muslim Jaén were known to specialize particularly in **tanning** and "all branches of commerce." The Almohad invasion of the twelfth century brought this Muslim-era community to an end, forcing its surviving members to flee northward.

The Castilian reconquest under **Fernando III** in **1246** brought new possibilities. As in other recently conquered Andalusian cities, Fernando encouraged Jewish settlement to help repopulate and administer new territories. A Jewish quarter was re-established, seemingly on its former site between the streets of Santa Cruz and San Andrés. Royal charters defined the community's legal standing.

## Golden Age

The community grew steadily in the second half of the thirteenth century, as the broader Castilian Jewish population expanded into the newly conquered south. Jaén's strategic importance as a **frontier fortress city** adjacent to the still-Muslim kingdom of Granada added a military-commercial dimension to Jewish life: Jewish tax-farmers helped finance the ongoing Reconquista campaigns, and Jewish artisans and merchants served the military economy of an active frontier.

By the **mid-fourteenth century**, Jaén's community had reached its peak of approximately **300 families** — a substantial aljama maintaining synagogues, schools, and courts. Jews cultivated vineyards, practiced crafts and commerce, and had business partnerships with Christian neighbors. The olive oil economy of the Jaén region involved Jewish merchants in the trade and processing of agricultural products that connected the city to broader Mediterranean markets.

The community's frontier character — on the border of the Muslim kingdom of Granada — added a distinctive dimension to daily economic life, with commercial contacts crossing the religious frontier that divided Christian Castile from Muslim Granada.

## Notable Figures

Jaén's greatest contribution to Jewish history predates the Christian era: the **ibn Shaprut family**, with its roots in Jayyān, gave the Sephardic world **Hisdai ibn Shaprut**, whose patronage shaped the first golden age of Jewish culture in Muslim Spain. In the Christian period, Jaén's rabbis and community leaders appear in local records and halakhic literature, though no figure of comparable renown emerged.

The community's most dramatic historical moment involved not a scholar but a political catastrophe: during the civil wars of the 1360s, King **Pedro I of Castile** permitted his Granadan Muslim allies to **take the Jews of Jaén captive and sell them into slavery** — a shocking episode that illustrates the extreme vulnerability of Jewish communities caught between competing powers in the late medieval Iberian world.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The **Judería of Jaén** occupied a recognized quarter of the city between the streets of Santa Cruz and San Andrés. The main synagogue stood on what is now **Santa Cruz Street**. After the violence of 1391, this synagogue was converted into the **Church of Santa Cruz** — a pattern repeated across Castile and Aragon, where the renaming of Jewish spaces as "Holy Cross" neighborhoods was a deliberate act of symbolic replacement.

The community maintained the full apparatus of Castilian Jewish communal life: courts, schools, charitable institutions, a mikveh, and a cemetery. The **1483 Inquisition tribunal** established in Jaén — the third on the Iberian Peninsula — targeted the converso community that had emerged from the forced conversions of 1391 and subsequent years.

## Cultural Life

Jaén's Jewish culture was thoroughly Castilian, following the liturgical rites and legal customs of the broader Castilian aljamas. The community's frontier character, on the border of the Muslim kingdom of Granada, added a dimension of commercial contact with Muslim southern neighbors that shaped daily economic life and created networks that would later ease, for some, the path into exile.

The community consulted the great Sephardic legal authorities of the period on questions of practice, maintaining its connection to the wider halakhic world even as its political situation became increasingly precarious.

## Decline & Transformation

The **1391 pogroms** struck Jaén as part of the wave of violence that originated in Seville and swept through Andalusia. Contemporary sources record that within days, violence spread to Alcalá de Guadaira, Carmona, Écija, Cazalla, Fregenal, Córdoba, Montoro, Andújar, **Jaén**, Úbeda, and Baeza. Many community members were killed or forced to convert. The former Jewish neighborhood was renamed *Santa Cruz* — as in Seville — and the synagogue converted to a church.

The proximity to Muslim Granada added a dimension unique to Jaén: some Jews in crisis in 1391 considered fleeing across the frontier to the Muslim kingdom, where Jews were still permitted to live. Whether any actually did so is uncertain, but the option reflects the desperate calculations of a community under attack.

After 1391, no organized *aljama* survived in the Kingdom of Jaén. A converso community remained, and in **March 1473**, it was massacred in events described as "even more terrible than that at Córdoba." The **Constable Miguel Lucas de Iranzo**, who tried to protect the conversos, was speared to death in a church by the infuriated populace, who then fell upon the converso community, plundering and killing them indiscriminately. The **1483 Inquisition tribunal** established in Jaén — merged with Córdoba's tribunal in 1526 — extended the persecution into the sixteenth century.

The final **Expulsion of 1492** completed the end of Jewish and openly converso life in Jaén.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Jaén's expelled Jews joined the Sephardic diaspora, with many heading to **North Africa** — Morocco in particular, which was geographically closest via the Strait of Gibraltar. Others followed established trade routes to the Ottoman Empire.

The city's most enduring contribution to Jewish history remains its early one: as the birthplace of the ibn Shaprut family and the origin of a lineage that shaped the first golden age of Sephardic civilization in Muslim Córdoba. Modern Jaén participates in the wider Spanish effort to document and acknowledge its Sephardic heritage, with the former Judería recognized as part of the city's medieval patrimony.

## Key Facts
- **Muslim-era community**: Pre-10th century; birthplace of the ibn Shaprut family
- **Christian-era founding**: After the 1246 reconquest by Fernando III
- **Peak Population**: Approximately 300 families (mid-14th century)
- **Languages**: Castilian, Arabic (Muslim era), Hebrew
- **Most notable connection**: Hisdai ibn Shaprut (c. 915–970), physician to the Caliph of Córdoba, patron of Jewish learning
- **1391**: Synagogue converted to Church of Santa Cruz; Judería renamed *Santa Cruz*
- **1483**: Third Inquisition tribunal on the Iberian Peninsula established in Jaén
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; former Judería documented; modest heritage commemoration

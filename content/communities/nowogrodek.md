# Nowogródek

## Overview

Nowogródek (Navahrudak in Belarusian; Nowogródek in Polish) was a historic city in the western Belarus region, associated with the Polish national poet Adam Mickiewicz, who was born in the surrounding area and set his great epic *Pan Tadeusz* in the Nowogródek landscape. The Jewish community of Nowogródek was one of the older in the region and had a significant cultural and commercial presence. The city is also notable for an extraordinary act of collective resistance: the Nowogródek Ghetto resistance, organized by the Bielski brothers and others, which enabled one of the largest partisan escapes of the Holocaust and saved hundreds of lives.

## Historical Origins

Jews settled in Nowogródek in the sixteenth century under the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, when the city was the capital of the Nowogródek Voivodeship and an important administrative center. The community grew under the standard patterns of Pale of Settlement life — commerce, crafts, inn-keeping, and the learned professions. By the nineteenth century, under Russian rule, Nowogródek was a provincial town of modest size but significant Jewish presence.

The city's associations with Polish national romanticism were strong: Adam Mickiewicz, though ethnically mixed-background and deeply influenced by Lithuanian landscape and culture, immortalized the Nowogródek region in his masterpiece *Pan Tadeusz* (1834). Jewish residents lived alongside Polish, Belarusian, and later Russian populations in the complex ethnic patchwork of the region.

## Golden Age

Nowogródek's Jewish community reached its peak in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The community was primarily engaged in commerce and crafts — typical of Pale of Settlement towns — with a learned class supported by communal institutions. Traditional Judaism dominated: the community had a rabbi, a rabbinical court, a yeshiva, and numerous synagogues and prayer houses. In the interwar period, under Polish rule, the community developed modern institutions: Hebrew and Yiddish schools, Zionist youth movements, labor organizations, and secular cultural clubs.

The city was known for its rabbis and scholars, several of whom achieved regional reputations. The Jewish community maintained a hospital, social welfare organizations, and the full array of institutional life characteristic of Polish-Jewish communities between the wars.

## Notable Figures

The most famous figures associated with Nowogródek's Jewish history are the **Bielski brothers** — Tuvia, Zus, Asael, and Aron Bielski — sons of a Jewish miller from the nearby village of Stankevich. When the Germans occupied the area and began murdering Jews, the Bielski brothers escaped to the forests and organized a partisan and rescue unit that eventually sheltered over 1,200 Jews — men, women, and children. Tuvia Bielski's insistence on accepting non-combatant refugees, including the elderly and children, distinguished his unit from most partisan groups. The Bielski partisans (*Bielski Otriad*) survived the war in the Naliboki forest, and Tuvia and Zus Bielski emigrated to Israel and later America. Their story was told in Nechama Tec's book *Defiance* and the subsequent film.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Nowogródek contained a Great Synagogue (a notable structure), several smaller synagogues, a rabbinical court, a yeshiva, Jewish schools, charitable organizations, and communal welfare institutions. The community cemetery dated to the early modern period. During the interwar Polish period, the community maintained modern educational and cultural institutions alongside traditional ones.

## Cultural Life

Nowogródek's Jewish culture was influenced by its position in the multilingual landscape of western Belarus. The community was exposed to Polish, Belarusian, and Russian cultural currents. Zionist movements were active, particularly among the youth. Hebrew and Yiddish education competed. The socialist Bund had followers. The community's location in former Polish territory meant that Polish language and culture were more present than in more eastern communities.

## Decline & Transformation

The Soviet occupation of 1939 brought arrests and deportations of community leaders. The German occupation in July 1941 brought immediate mass murder: the first major massacre took place on December 7–8, 1941, when approximately 4,500 Jews from Nowogródek and surrounding areas were shot at a nearby site. Further massacres followed in 1942. In September 1943, when the remaining ghetto inhabitants realized they faced imminent murder, they organized an extraordinary escape: they dug a tunnel approximately 250 meters long under the ghetto fence and through the German guard perimeter. On the night of September 26, 1943, over 250 Jews escaped through the tunnel into the forest, where many joined the Bielski partisans. Of the escapees, around 100 survived the war. Of approximately 6,000 Nowogródek Jews, fewer than 200 survived.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Bielski partisans' story has become one of the most celebrated narratives of Jewish resistance and rescue in Holocaust history. The film *Defiance* (2008) brought their story to a wide audience. Tuvia Bielski is buried in Israel after initially being interred in New York; his remains were reinterred on Mount Herzl in Jerusalem in 1988. Nowogródek survivors and their descendants maintain memorial organizations. The tunnel escape is commemorated annually by survivors and descendants. The city's Polish and Belarusian residents today preserve some memory of its Jewish past, and the mass murder sites have been marked.

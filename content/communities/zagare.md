# Žagarė

## Overview

Žagarė (Yiddish: *Zhager*; Russian: *Zagare*) was a small town in the Šiauliai district of northern Lithuania, situated on the Švėtė River near the Latvian border. Though modest in size, the Žagarė Jewish community was one of the older in Lithuania and had a surprisingly rich intellectual and cultural life. The town produced scholars, emigrants to America and Palestine, and ultimately suffered one of the most brutal and early massacres of the Holocaust — the Žagarė massacre of October 2, 1941, in which approximately 2,500 Jews were murdered in a single day, just months after the German invasion.

## Historical Origins

Jews lived in Žagarė from at least the seventeenth century, when the town was part of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania. A community charter granted rights of settlement and commerce. The town was divided into two sections — Old Žagarė and New Žagarė — separated by the river, and the Jewish community had presence in both parts. The community was typical of northern Lithuanian Jewish life: merchants, craftsmen, innkeepers, and a learned class supported by communal charity.

## Golden Age

Žagarė's golden age was the nineteenth century, when its population was substantially Jewish and the community maintained vibrant religious and cultural institutions. The town had a reputation for Torah scholarship: several distinguished rabbis served Žagarė, and its yeshiva attracted students from the surrounding region. The community maintained a beis midrash, multiple prayer houses, elementary schools, and a cemetery with centuries of gravestones.

Žagarė also became notable for emigration: in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, a significant proportion of the community emigrated to America, South Africa, and Palestine. Emigrants from Žagarė in New York and other American cities formed a prominent *landsmanshaft* that maintained connections to the hometown community and eventually documented its history.

## Notable Figures

Žagarė produced several notable emigrants and scholars. **Rabbi Shmaryahu Leib Hurwitz** was a nineteenth-century religious leader. The town's community included families who produced rabbis, businessmen, and professionals who achieved distinction after emigrating. The American Žagarė community (*landsmanshaft*) in New York included prosperous merchants and communal leaders who published a memorial book after the Holocaust. The local tradition of Torah study produced lesser-known scholars who served in rabbinical positions across Lithuania and the diaspora.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Žagarė had a Great Synagogue, several smaller synagogues and prayer houses, a *talmud torah*, a *mikveh*, a *gemilut chassadim* fund, and a cemetery dating to the early modern period. In the interwar period, the community added Hebrew schools, Zionist youth clubs, and a library. The Great Synagogue of Žagarė was a notable wooden structure, typical of the architectural tradition of small Lithuanian Jewish communities — an architecture of carved wooden synagogues that was one of the great, largely unappreciated artistic traditions of Eastern European Jewish culture.

## Cultural Life

Žagarė's Jewish culture was traditionally oriented, with Torah study and religious observance as central values, but the early twentieth century brought modernizing influences. Zionism was popular among the young, and Hebrew education was embraced. The community maintained connections to Šiauliai, the regional capital, and to Kaunas and Vilna for cultural and religious resources. Yiddish was the daily language; Hebrew was studied and revered; Lithuanian was increasingly known after Lithuanian independence in 1918.

## Decline & Transformation

Soviet occupation in 1940 brought the suppression of Jewish institutions. When Germany invaded Lithuania in June 1941, Lithuanian nationalist militia (*Šauliai* and others) participated alongside Germans in anti-Jewish violence. On October 2, 1941, the entire Jewish population of Žagarė — approximately 2,500 people — was marched to a prepared pit site outside the town and shot. The massacre was one of the early major killings in Lithuania and came just a few months after the invasion. The speed and totality of the killing reflected the murderous efficiency the Germans and their Lithuanian collaborators would apply across the entire country. Very few Jews from Žagarė survived.

## Legacy & Diaspora

American Žagarė emigrants, who had left before the Holocaust, documented the community's history and published a memorial book. Several former Žagareans who had emigrated to South Africa, America, and Palestine lived to see the State of Israel established. The mass murder site outside Žagarė has been marked as a memorial, and Lithuanian historians have documented the massacre. The community's disappearance — from a living town with centuries of history to complete extinction in a single day — represents in microcosm the totality of the destruction of Lithuanian Jewry.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: 17th century
- **Peak Population**: ~2,500 (1941)
- **Languages**: Yiddish, Hebrew, Lithuanian
- **Status Today**: Community completely destroyed October 2, 1941; memorial at murder site; no Jewish community remains

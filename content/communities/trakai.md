# Trakai

## Overview

Trakai — the ancient capital of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, set dramatically on islands and peninsulas amid a lake district southwest of Vilnius — is unique in Jewish history as the home of the Lithuanian Karaites, a non-Rabbanite Jewish sect brought to Lithuania in the late fourteenth century by Grand Duke Vytautas. The Karaites, who reject the Oral Torah and Rabbinic tradition and adhere only to the literal text of the Hebrew Bible, maintained their distinct community and culture in Trakai for over six hundred years, preserving a Turkic language (*Karaim*), distinctive religious practices, and a community identity that has survived — barely — to the present day.

## Historical Origins

The Karaite presence in Trakai traces to approximately 1397–1398, when Grand Duke Vytautas of Lithuania brought a group of Karaites from the Crimea following his military campaigns in that region. Crimean Karaites — Turkic in language and culture, with Central Asian origins predating their adoption of the Karaite interpretation of Judaism — were settled in Trakai to serve as elite guards for the Grand Duke and as craftsmen and merchants. This Turkic-Jewish community, speaking a language related to Crimean Tatar and Cuman, maintained its distinctiveness from both the surrounding Lithuanian-Christian population and from the Rabbanite Jews who also lived in the Grand Duchy.

The Karaites were granted specific privileges by Vytautas and subsequent rulers — rights of residence, freedom of worship, and exemption from certain restrictions applied to Rabbanite Jews. This privileged status helped preserve their community but also contributed to a certain distance from other Jewish populations.

## Golden Age

The Trakai Karaites flourished during the period when Trakai was the primary capital of the Grand Duchy (fourteenth-fifteenth centuries), serving as craftsmen, guards, and merchants in the ducal center. Even after political power shifted to Vilnius, the Trakai community maintained its character. The eighteenth and nineteenth centuries saw the Karaites of Lithuania produce scholars who wrote in the Karaim language and in Hebrew, produced liturgical poetry, and engaged in theological debates with Rabbanite scholars.

Key Karaite scholars of the Lithuanian tradition include **Simhah ben Samuel Lutski**, a copyist and scholar of the eighteenth century, and various liturgists and commentators who worked in the Karaim language. The Trakai Karaite community maintained its distinctive prayer house (*kenesa*), its communal cemetery, and its social institutions through the nineteenth century.

## Notable Figures

**Grand Duke Vytautas** of Lithuania, while not Jewish, is the historical figure responsible for the Karaites' presence in Trakai. Among Karaite scholars, **Abraham ben Josiah Yerushalmi** (1685–1740), born in Trakai, was one of the most important Karaite theological writers. **Abraham Firkovich** (1786–1874), a nineteenth-century Karaite scholar and collector, traveled across the Middle East and Eastern Europe collecting Karaite manuscripts and inscriptions; his collection, now in the Russian National Library in St. Petersburg, is the largest archive of Karaite manuscripts in the world. Firkovich controversially altered some documents and inscriptions to support claims that the Karaites had arrived in Eastern Europe before Jesus's time — a claim designed to exempt Karaites from anti-Jewish discrimination by proving they could not have been complicit in the death of Jesus.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Trakai *kenesa* (Karaite synagogue) is the oldest and most significant Karaite house of worship in Lithuania. The original building dates to the seventeenth century; the current structure was built in the nineteenth century. The *kenesa* differs from Rabbanite synagogues in design and ritual: it features a distinctive porch, separate seating arrangements, and liturgical forms that preserve ancient practices unmediated by Rabbinic development. The Karaite cemetery in Trakai contains inscriptions in Hebrew, Karaim, and occasionally Russian that document the community's history.

## Cultural Life

Karaite culture in Trakai was shaped by their unique hybrid identity — Turkic in language and many customs, Jewish in monotheistic faith and Biblical observance, Lithuanian in terms of civic identity. The Karaim language, a Turkic tongue with Hebrew and Slavic loanwords, was preserved in liturgy, poetry, and domestic life. Traditional foods, clothing styles, and social customs distinguished the Karaites from their neighbors. The community was small — never more than a few hundred in Trakai itself — but tightly knit.

## Decline & Transformation

The nineteenth century saw declining population and increasing assimilation. The Lithuanian Karaite community shrank as members moved to other cities or assimilated into Russian and Lithuanian societies. By 1939 there were perhaps 200–250 Karaites remaining in Trakai. During the Holocaust, the Nazis debated whether Karaites were "racially Jewish" and therefore subject to extermination; after consulting with racial pseudoscientists who concluded the Karaites were of Turkic rather than Semitic descent, the Nazis classified them as non-Jewish. This determination, whatever its intellectual validity, saved the lives of most Lithuanian Karaites. The Karaites themselves largely did not intervene to save Rabbanite Jews.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Trakai Karaite community today numbers approximately 50–100 individuals, making it one of the smallest religious communities in the world with a continuous institutional presence. The *kenesa* functions as both a place of worship and a tourist attraction; Trakai Castle, on its island in the lake, draws thousands of visitors who also visit the Karaite neighborhood. The Karaim language is critically endangered. International efforts to document and preserve Karaite culture include scholarship at universities in Lithuania, Poland, and Israel. The Karaite museum in Trakai preserves artifacts of the community's distinctive culture. Whether this remarkable community will survive another generation is genuinely uncertain.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Karaite community established c. 1397–1398 by Grand Duke Vytautas
- **Peak Population**: ~200–300 Karaites in Trakai (17th–18th century)
- **Languages**: Karaim (Turkic), Hebrew (liturgy), Lithuanian, Russian
- **Status Today**: ~50–100 Karaites remain; *kenesa* active as museum and occasional house of worship; community critically endangered

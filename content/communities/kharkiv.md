# Kharkiv

## Overview

Kharkiv (also Kharkov) was the second-largest city in Ukraine and its first capital after the Russian Revolution, a major industrial and academic center where Jewish life developed under conditions quite different from the Pale of Settlement shtetl world. As a city that was technically outside the original Pale for much of the nineteenth century, Kharkiv attracted primarily educated, professional, and commercially privileged Jews. The community was therefore modern and largely acculturated from its beginnings — more integrated into Russian intellectual and professional culture than most Eastern European Jewish communities. The Soviet era brought Kharkiv's Jews into positions of significant political and cultural influence before Stalinist terror decimated the Jewish intellectual class.

## Historical Origins

Kharkiv was founded as a Cossack settlement and military outpost in the 1650s. Jews were not a significant presence until the nineteenth century: Kharkiv was initially outside the Pale of Settlement, restricting Jewish residence to those with special status. As the city grew into a major industrial and educational center — the University of Kharkiv was founded in 1804, making it one of Russia's oldest universities — it attracted educated Jews who qualified for residence under the exemptions allowed for graduates, professionals, and first-guild merchants.

The community grew substantially after the 1881–1882 pogroms elsewhere in Ukraine, as Kharkiv's relative security attracted Jewish refugees. By 1897 approximately 11,000 Jews lived in Kharkiv; by 1917 the number had grown to over 50,000 as the city industrialized rapidly.

## Golden Age

The late imperial and early Soviet periods marked Kharkiv's Jewish golden age. The community was disproportionately educated and professional — engineers, doctors, lawyers, academics, and merchants. The Kharkiv Jewish community engaged extensively with Russian intellectual culture: participation in the university, in the professions, in literary and artistic circles was more natural here than in the isolated shtetl communities of the Pale. Jewish students at Kharkiv University participated in the revolutionary movement, and Kharkiv became a city of radical Jewish politics — Socialist Revolutionary, Menshevik, and Bolshevik.

After the Bolshevik revolution, Kharkiv became the capital of the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic (1919–1934), making it the political center of Soviet Ukraine. Jewish Communists who supported the Bolshevik cause gained positions in the Soviet apparatus. The 1920s in Kharkiv was a period of relative cultural openness — the "Ukrainization" policy allowed Ukrainian, Yiddish, and other national cultures brief periods of institutional development. Jewish cultural institutions, Yiddish publications, and Jewish academic institutions briefly flourished.

## Notable Figures

**Simon Petliura** had his political career in the Kharkiv-Ukrainian context (though as leader of forces that committed pogroms, not a community figure). **Ilya Erenburg** (1891–1967), the Soviet Russian journalist and writer, was born in Kyiv but spent formative years in Kharkiv's intellectual milieu. **Josif Sliozberg** was a Kharkiv-based Jewish legal expert. The Kharkiv Jewish community produced numerous Soviet-era scientists, engineers, and academics whose Jewish identity was subordinated to Soviet professional identity. Physicists, mathematicians, and engineers trained at Kharkiv's technical institutions contributed significantly to Soviet science.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Kharkiv's principal synagogue, the Choral Synagogue (built 1913), served the acculturated community. The community also maintained smaller prayer houses. In the Soviet period, religious institutions were systematically closed or converted. Jewish educational and cultural institutions operated briefly in the 1920s before being suppressed in the 1930s. The community maintained cemeteries that are still partially preserved.

## Cultural Life

Kharkiv's Jewish cultural life was shaped by its Russian-educated, professional character. Cultural engagement meant participation in Russian literary and intellectual culture alongside Jewish religious and communal life. In the early Soviet period, Yiddish cultural institutions briefly flourished: a Yiddish theater, Yiddish newspapers, and academic institutions studying Jewish culture. The Ukrainian avant-garde movement of the 1920s — Constructivist art, innovative theater, modernist literature — involved Jewish intellectuals and artists.

## Decline & Transformation

The Stalinist terror of the 1930s devastated Kharkiv's Jewish intellectual class. The "Ukrainization" policy was reversed; Jewish cultural institutions were closed; Jewish Communist officials were arrested in the purges. The Holodomor — the man-made famine of 1932–1933 — killed millions of Ukrainians but also affected urban Jewish populations. The German occupation of Kharkiv (1941–1943) brought mass murders: approximately 15,000–20,000 Jews were killed in Kharkiv, primarily at Drobitsky Yar on the city's outskirts in December 1941. The community was effectively destroyed. Soviet forces liberated Kharkiv in August 1943.

## Legacy & Diaspora

After the war, Jews gradually returned to Kharkiv as it was rebuilt, eventually forming a significant postwar community. In the Soviet system this was a community of assimilated Jews largely disconnected from Jewish religious and cultural practice. Ukrainian independence in 1991 allowed a revival: synagogues reopened, Jewish cultural organizations reestablished themselves, and Kharkiv became one of Ukraine's significant Jewish communities. The Drobitsky Yar memorial commemorates the victims of the 1941 massacre. The 2022 Russian invasion has dramatically affected Kharkiv as the city closest to the Russian border; significant portions of the Jewish community have been evacuated or displaced.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Jewish community grew through 19th century; major growth after 1880s
- **Peak Population**: ~130,000 (1939)
- **Languages**: Russian, Yiddish, Ukrainian
- **Status Today**: Active community prior to 2022; severely affected by Russian invasion; Drobitsky Yar memorial

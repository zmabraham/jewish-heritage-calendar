# Dresden

## Overview

Dresden's Jewish community flourished as a center of German-Jewish cultural achievement in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — a community of composers, artists, merchants, and intellectuals integrated into one of Germany's most aesthetically sophisticated cities. Like Dresden itself, the Jewish community was largely destroyed in the catastrophe of the 1940s — the Allied firebombing of February 1945 and the Holocaust together erasing much of what had been built. Yet Dresden also represents a remarkable story of rebuilding in the post-war era, under Communist rule and beyond.

## Historical Origins

Jews were effectively excluded from Saxon electoral territories for much of the early modern period. Dresden, the capital of Saxony, was among the cities from which Jews were formally barred. Individual Jews gained access as court agents and merchants in the eighteenth century — the Saxon court at Dresden was one of the most extravagant in Europe, and Jewish traders supplied luxuries, art objects, and credit to the Electors and Kings of Saxony.

The formal establishment of the community dates to the early nineteenth century, when Saxon laws gradually relaxed and Jews were permitted to settle. Full legal equality came with German unification in 1871. The community grew rapidly through the late nineteenth century, reaching approximately 12,000 by 1910.

## Golden Age

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries were Dresden's Jewish golden age. Jews were prominent in the city's cultural life at every level — as patrons of the arts, as participants in the famous Dresden Opera and Philharmonic, as publishers and book dealers, and as artists themselves. Dresden's Jewish community was deeply embedded in the city's identity as a center of German culture.

The architect Victor Klemperer — he later became a philologist — grew up in a Dresden rabbinical family; his cousin Otto Klemperer became one of the twentieth century's greatest conductors. Victor Klemperer himself survived the war hidden in Dresden and kept the remarkable diary I Will Bear Witness, a detailed account of daily life under Nazi terror.

## Notable Figures

Victor Klemperer (1881–1960) is the most important Jewish figure associated with Dresden. His diaries, published posthumously, document with extraordinary precision and courage the daily experience of a German Jew under Nazi rule — the progressive exclusion, humiliation, and terror, experienced by a man who had fought for Germany in World War I and was deeply integrated into German culture. His philological study The Language of the Third Reich (LTI) analyzed how Nazi terminology corrupted German public discourse.

Richard Wagner composed important works in Dresden and was the city's most celebrated musical figure — but also its most virulent Jewish antisemite. His essay "Das Judenthum in der Musik" (Judaism in Music, 1850) attacked Jewish composers like Mendelssohn and Meyerbeer. The tension between Wagner's antisemitism and the Jewish contribution to German musical life was a major theme of Dresden cultural politics.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Dresden Synagogue (Semper Synagogue), designed by the great architect Gottfried Semper and completed in 1840, was one of the architectural masterpieces of nineteenth-century Jewish Germany — a neoclassical building of austere beauty that reflected the confidence of the emancipation era. It was destroyed on Kristallnacht.

A new synagogue, designed by the architects Wandel, Hoefer, Lorch & Hirsch, was completed in 2001 on the site of the old. Its distinctive design — a cube of pale stone inscribed with Hebrew texts — is oriented at an angle to the surrounding street grid, aligned with Jerusalem. It stands as one of the most successful post-war synagogue buildings in Germany.

## Cultural Life

Dresden's Jewish community participated deeply in the city's cultural life. Jewish merchants supported the Dresden art galleries and opera; Jewish physicians and lawyers were prominent in professional life; Jewish publishers and editors shaped the city's intellectual culture. The community maintained schools, charitable organizations, and Zionist youth groups.

## Decline & Transformation

Nazi persecution drove most of Dresden's Jews to emigrate or flee in the 1930s. By 1939, only about 1,600 Jews remained — most in "mixed marriages" that provided some protection. The firebombing of Dresden on February 13–14, 1945, killed an estimated 22,000–25,000 people; paradoxically, Victor Klemperer and other Jews living in the city survived because they were temporarily released from their required labor on the day before the bombing and used the chaos to escape.

Under East German Communist rule, the small remaining Jewish community occupied an awkward position — officially respected as victims of fascism, practically marginalized in an atheist state.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Dresden today has a Jewish community of approximately 800–1,000, including post-war survivors, returnees, and immigrants from the former Soviet Union. The new synagogue is a major landmark of post-war German-Jewish architectural achievement. Victor Klemperer's diaries are required reading in German schools, a monument to the humanity that persisted through terror.

# Djerba

## Overview

The island of Djerba, off the southern coast of Tunisia, harbors what many consider the oldest continuously inhabited Jewish community in the world outside the Land of Israel. Its Jewish quarter, Hara Kebira ("large quarter") and Hara Sghira ("small quarter"), maintained a distinctive way of life for at least 2,500 years. The community's most revered institution — the El-Ghriba synagogue — is by tradition the oldest synagogue in Africa and possibly the world, and today draws thousands of Jewish pilgrims each year during the Lag B'Omer festival.

## Historical Background

Djerbani Jewish tradition holds that the island's Jewish settlement dates to 586 BCE, when priests fleeing the destruction of the First Temple in Jerusalem brought with them a stone from the Temple and a door from the sanctuary, enshrining these relics in the original El-Ghriba synagogue. While this claim cannot be archaeologically verified, the antiquity of the community is broadly accepted; ancient sources refer to Jewish communities in the region, and the Talmud mentions communities in proximity to the Libyan and Tunisian coast.

The name "El-Ghriba" (Arabic for "the strange" or "the miraculous") is associated with a legend of a mysterious holy woman — perhaps representing the community's spiritual origins — who appeared on the island and whose death led to the construction of the synagogue on the site where she fell. The community's kohanim (priestly families) trace their lineage directly to the Temple priests, and this priestly identity shapes communal identity and practice to an unusual degree.

## Golden Age

Djerba's isolation was paradoxically its greatest asset. Cut off from the rapid religious and cultural changes that swept through Kairouan, Fez, and the Levant, the Djerbani community preserved ancient practices, liturgical traditions, and social structures that disappeared elsewhere. The community followed its own legal customs (*minhagim*), maintained Kohen-intensive ritual practices, and resisted the influence of later Sephardic immigration that transformed most North African communities after 1492. This makes Djerba an invaluable laboratory for studying pre-expulsion North African Jewish practice.

The community supported its own yeshivot (the *Yeshivat HaGra* and others), and Djerbani rabbis were recognized scholars. The community's Torah scrolls were traditionally inscribed in the ancient Sefer Torah format, and the community maintained scribal and ritual traditions of notable antiquity.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Moshe Khalfon HaKohen (1874–1950) was the most prominent modern Djerbani scholar, a prolific halakhic authority whose responsa collection *Brit Kehuna* addresses hundreds of questions in Jewish law. He served as the community's chief rabbi for decades and his works are still studied in Tunisian diaspora communities.

Rabbi Fraji Shawat was among earlier significant figures. The community also produced notable payytanim (liturgical poets) who composed in Judeo-Arabic and Hebrew, many of whose compositions survive in communal liturgy.

The kohanim of Djerba exercised unusual communal authority, setting the island apart from most other communities where the kehuna (priestly class) had largely ceremonial functions. Their involvement in marriage disputes, ritual supervision, and community leadership reflects an archaic social structure.

## Institutions & Synagogues

El-Ghriba synagogue, located in Hara Sghira, is the island's sacred center. The current building dates to the late 19th and early 20th centuries, though built on far older foundations. Its interior is covered in hand-painted blue-and-white Tunisian tiles and features ancient Torah scrolls believed to be of great antiquity. The synagogue's inner sanctuary contains a sacred stone reputedly from the Jerusalem Temple.

The community also maintained the synagogue of Hara Kebira and numerous smaller houses of study. The island's two Jewish quarters each had their own institutions, a division that sometimes generated inter-communal tension but also resilience.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Djerbani Jews preserved distinctive liturgical practices: their Torah reading customs differed from both Sephardic and Ashkenazic norms, and their pronunciation of Hebrew retained features lost elsewhere. Their Passover Haggadah included unique piyyutim (liturgical poems). The community maintained a distinctive Judeo-Tunisian Arabic dialect distinct from that of mainland Tunis.

The annual Lag B'Omer pilgrimage to El-Ghriba, called the *Hillula*, became one of the largest Jewish pilgrimage events in North Africa, drawing Jews from across Tunisia and from Europe and Israel. Pilgrims would light candles, make vows, and pray for miraculous intervention — practices blending kabbalistic piety with folk religion.

## Decline & Dispersal

Djerba's Jewish population remained remarkably stable through the 20th century, longer than any other North African community. Even after Tunisian independence in 1956 and the departure of most Tunisian Jews, several thousand Jews remained on the island. The 1967 Six-Day War prompted further emigration, and by 1970 the community had shrunk dramatically. Today approximately 900–1,200 Jews remain on Djerba, making it the largest Jewish community still living in an Arab country outside Israel, though this figure continues to decline.

In April 2002, a truck bomb exploded outside El-Ghriba synagogue, killing 19 people, mostly German tourists — a devastating reminder of the community's vulnerability. Security at the synagogue has been heavily reinforced since.

## Legacy Today

Djerba remains a living community, not merely a heritage site. Shabbat is observed, kosher food is available, yeshivot continue to operate at a reduced level, and the Lag B'Omer pilgrimage draws thousands of Jews from the diaspora each spring. Djerbani Jews in France and Israel maintain tight communal networks. The community is studied by anthropologists, liturgists, and historians as a model of preserved ancient practice.

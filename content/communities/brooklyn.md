# Brooklyn (Williamsburg, Crown Heights, Borough Park)

## Overview

Brooklyn is today home to the largest concentration of observant Jews outside Israel — a remarkable reversal of expectations in a story of destruction and rebuilding. Three neighborhoods in particular define Brooklyn's Jewish character: Williamsburg, where the Satmar dynasty built its American fortress; Crown Heights, where Chabad-Lubavitch established its world headquarters; and Borough Park, where dozens of Hasidic and traditionally Orthodox communities created a dense Jewish landscape unique in the diaspora.

## Historical Background

Brooklyn's Jewish history predates its current Hasidic character by nearly a century. German Jewish immigrants arrived in the nineteenth century; Eastern European Jews followed in the great immigration wave of 1880–1924. By the 1920s and 30s, Brooklyn had large Jewish working-class communities in Brownsville, East New York, and Williamsburg — neighborhoods of labor organizing, Yiddish culture, and socialist politics.

The transformation began after World War II, when Holocaust survivors arrived in America carrying the remnants of European Hasidic dynasties. Williamsburg, with its affordable housing and existing Jewish infrastructure, became the preferred destination for groups of survivors who arrived together and sought to reconstitute their communities. The concentration of Hasidic groups in a single neighborhood created critical mass that attracted more survivors and their families.

## Golden Age

The postwar decades represent the founding period of Brooklyn's distinctive Orthodox culture. Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum of Satmar, arriving in 1947, immediately set about building the most comprehensive Hasidic community infrastructure in America — yeshivot, mikvaot, kosher establishments, housing, and welfare organizations. Satmar's success attracted other displaced Rebbes and their followers, and Williamsburg became a mosaic of different Hasidic courts sharing streets, businesses, and the commitment to rebuilding traditional Judaism in America.

Crown Heights developed along a different axis. The Lubavitcher Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson, arrived in Brooklyn in 1941 and assumed leadership of the Chabad movement in 1950. Under his direction, Crown Heights became not merely a Hasidic enclave but the headquarters of an outreach movement of unprecedented global ambition. Chabad emissaries — shluchim — were dispatched to hundreds of cities worldwide; Crown Heights was their base and spiritual center.

Borough Park became the home of dozens of smaller Hasidic courts and the largest concentration of non-Hasidic Orthodox Jews in America, with a Jewish population today estimated at over 150,000.

## Notable Figures

The Satmar Rebbe Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum (1887–1979) dominates Williamsburg's spiritual history (see also the Satmar entry). The Lubavitcher Rebbe Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson (1902–1994) transformed Crown Heights into the world center of Jewish outreach — sending thousands of young couples to establish Chabad houses in cities, university towns, and remote locations worldwide.

The Bobover Rebbe Rabbi Shlomo Halberstam (1908–2000) reestablished the Bobov dynasty in Borough Park after surviving the Holocaust as one of the few members of his family to escape the Nazis. From near-nothing, he rebuilt a dynasty of thousands.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The institutions of Brooklyn's Hasidic communities are comprehensive and self-contained. Williamsburg has the Satmar main synagogue (one of the largest in the world), dozens of smaller shuls, Satmar yeshivot serving thousands of students, and the United Talmudical Academy. Crown Heights has 770 Eastern Parkway — the Chabad world headquarters, replicated in dozens of Chabad houses globally — along with Lubavitch yeshivot and the Machon Chana women's seminary. Borough Park has the Bobov synagogue, numerous other Hasidic institutions, and Yeshiva Torah Vodaath (founded 1918, one of the oldest yeshivot in America).

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Brooklyn's Hasidic communities have made contributions to American Jewish culture that extend beyond their neighborhoods. The klezmer revival of the 1970s–80s drew heavily on Brooklyn's musical traditions. Hasidic music — particularly Chabad and Carlebach-influenced melodies — has influenced mainstream Jewish liturgy. The commitment of Brooklyn's Orthodox communities to Jewish day school education contributed to the normalization of intensive Jewish schooling across denominations.

Crown Heights was the site of a major racial conflict in August 1991, when three days of violence between Black and Jewish residents following the accidental death of a Black child in a Chabad motorcade resulted in the murder of Australian rabbinical student Yankel Rosenbaum. The Crown Heights riots exposed racial and economic tensions in urban Jewish-Black relations and prompted important soul-searching in both communities.

## Decline & Dispersal

Brooklyn's Hasidic communities have not declined — they have expanded dramatically. High birth rates, strong communal infrastructure, and continued immigration from Israel and Europe have made them among the fastest-growing Jewish communities in the world. The displacement has been of non-Orthodox Jews, priced out by gentrification, and of the secular Jewish working-class culture that characterized Brooklyn in the early twentieth century.

## Legacy Today

Brooklyn today has an estimated 500,000 Jewish residents — the largest single borough concentration of Jews in the United States. The visual presence of Hasidic Judaism on Brooklyn streets, the Shabbat stillness that descends over large sections of Williamsburg, Crown Heights, and Borough Park on Saturday, and the sounds of Hebrew and Yiddish represent a living reconstruction of Eastern European Jewish culture unlike anything that exists outside Israel.

# Palencia

## Overview

Palencia, a cathedral city on the Carrión River in the Castilian heartland, was home to a well-established Jewish community across several centuries of medieval Iberian life. The city's dual role as an episcopal center and a major way-station on the Camino de Santiago pilgrimage road created a distinctive and often complicated environment for its Jews: they were simultaneously valued commercial partners, royal tax agents, and convenient targets for ecclesiastical hostility. Their story is one of resilience and adaptation, punctuated by moments of civic violence, until the final catastrophe of 1492.

## Historical Origins

The earliest documented date for Jewish settlement in Palencia is **1175**, when King Alfonso VIII formally delivered forty Jewish families to the bishop of Palencia, placing them under episcopal jurisdiction — an arrangement reconfirmed in 1351. This early transfer to episcopal authority made Palencia's Jewish status legally distinctive: unlike many Castilian communities held directly under royal protection, Palencia's Jews were bound in a relationship of dependency with the bishop's court.

By 1192, Alfonso VIII had exempted the city's Jews and Moors from royal taxation, since they already contributed to the town's municipal revenues. The community at this point numbered roughly forty families — a modest but organized nucleus that would grow considerably over the following century. The city was also one of several locations where Alfonso VIII resettled Jews displaced northward by the Almohad invasions of al-Andalus, joining communities at Fromista and Carrión in absorbing refugees from the persecuted south.

Palencia's early thirteenth-century role as host to one of Spain's first universities — the studium generale founded circa 1212 — brought it into the orbit of medieval intellectual life. Jewish scholars, who had long served as transmitters of Arabic learning into Latin, were active in this broader culture of translation and commentary, though their specific contributions to Palencia's short-lived university are difficult to document precisely.

## Golden Age

Palencia's community reached its peak in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, when the aljama maintained synagogues, schools, and courts, and its members participated in the wool and cloth trades that defined Castilian commercial wealth. Jewish tax-farmers collected revenues for both the crown and ecclesiastical institutions across the region — a position that brought both financial benefit and political exposure.

The community's relationship with the ecclesiastical establishment was characteristically ambivalent. In **1295**, Palencia's Jews took part in a revolt against royal authority that included the destruction of the bishop's palace — a striking moment of civic engagement that illustrates how embedded the Jewish community was in the city's political life. Yet this closeness to power also created vulnerability: during the fourteenth-century civil wars between Pedro I ("the Cruel") and Henry of Trastamara, the community suffered severely. The scholar **Samuel Zarza**, whose major work *Mekor Ḥayyim* ("Spring of Life") is one of the key intellectual documents of the period, wrote that Henry of Trastamara extracted a punishing ransom from Palencia's Jews and left them "in great distress."

## Notable Figures

Palencia's rabbis and scholars appear regularly in the broader responsa literature of the period. **Rabbi Jacob ben Moses of Palencia** (recorded as *Debalincia*) was a local authority whose rulings on eruv arrangements were declared invalid by the great Toledan jurist **Asher ben Jehiel (the Rosh)**, who condemned him as a "rebellious scholar" — a dramatic exchange preserved in Asher's responsa (21:8) that illustrates both the community's legal seriousness and its integration into the wider Sephardic world.

**Samuel Zarza of Palencia** is the most significant intellectual figure the community produced. His *Mekor Ḥayyim* is a philosophical commentary on the Torah in the tradition of Jewish Neoplatonism, and his personal testimony about the community's suffering during the civil wars makes him an important historical witness as well as a thinker. His work bridges the philosophical traditions inherited from Maimonides with the experiential reality of a community caught in the political upheavals of fourteenth-century Castile.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The community maintained at least **two synagogues**. One was located on what is now San Marcos Street; the other stood between the streets today known as Manflorido and Regimiento Villarrobedo, near the Plaza de León. The Jewish quarter — the *Judería vieja* — occupied the neighborhood of La Pellejería, between San Marcos and Cardenal Almaraz streets, near the first settlement tradition associated with the church of San Julián on the right bank of the Carrión River.

In **1480**, following new royal legislation separating Jews and conversos into distinct quarters, a new Jewish area was allocated along María Gutiérrez Street (now Martín Calleja Street). By **1485**, Jews were required to wear a distinctive badge and Christians were forbidden to lodge in Jewish homes. That same year the community was assessed 501,183 maravedis toward the ransom of prisoners taken during the siege of Málaga — a measure of the community's continued financial significance even in its diminished late-medieval state.

## Cultural Life

Palencia's Jewish culture was thoroughly Castilian, participating in the liturgical rites and legal customs of the broader aljama network. The community's unusual position under episcopal authority created particular dynamics: Jewish physicians and scholars regularly engaged with the cathedral city's Christian intellectual life, while the proximity to church institutions kept certain legal and social pressures always present.

A messianic episode in nearby Herrera de Pisuerga around 1500 — where a prophet arose calling for repentance and announcing redemption — reflects the psychological intensity of Jewish life in the aftermath of expulsion and mass conversion, and illustrates the spiritual currents that ran through the communities of northern Castile in this period.

## Decline & Transformation

The **1391 pogroms** struck Palencia, producing forced conversions and deepening the already severe wounds inflicted by the civil war era. The community reconstituted itself partially in the early fifteenth century but never recovered its former strength. The years after 1391 saw the rise of a substantial converso community alongside the remaining Jewish population, creating the socially complex configuration that would define Palencia's religious life until 1492.

When the final Expulsion came, the synagogue on San Marcos Street was sold in **May 1492**, with the proceeds distributed among departing Jews to help finance their journey. A second synagogue was converted into a hospital in **November 1492**. Most of Palencia's expelled Jews crossed into Portugal, following the route north and westward. The street that had been the heart of Jewish life was renamed *Santa Fé* after their departure, and the authorities imposed fines on anyone who continued to call it by its old name, the *judería*.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Palencia's Jewish heritage is preserved in the documentary record of the Castilian archives and in the broader responsa and philosophical literature that Palencia's scholars contributed to. The physical quarter has not survived — nothing of the medieval Jewish neighborhood remains standing — but the historical record is relatively rich by the standards of smaller Castilian communities.

The community's displaced descendants joined the broader Sephardic exile, carrying the memories and traditions of a city that had been a place of Jewish learning and commerce for over three centuries.

## Key Facts
- **Earliest documented**: 1175 (forty families delivered to episcopal jurisdiction)
- **Peak Period**: 13th–14th centuries
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 800–1,500 (14th century)
- **Languages**: Castilian, Hebrew
- **Two synagogues** documented; both converted or sold at expulsion
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; former Judería documented in archives; no physical remains

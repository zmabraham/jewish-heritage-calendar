# Będzin

## Overview

Będzin — pronounced approximately "Bendzhin" and known in Yiddish as Bendin — was a significant Jewish community in the Upper Silesian industrial region of southern Poland. Unlike many Polish Jewish communities, which had centuries of history under the Polish Crown, Będzin's major Jewish community developed primarily in the nineteenth century under Prussian/German and then Russian rule, in close proximity to the coal mines and ironworks of Upper Silesia. By the interwar period it had become a prosperous, politically active community — and one of the last major Jewish communities in occupied Poland to be liquidated, its Jews deported to Auschwitz-Birkenau in August 1943.

## Historical Background

Będzin's position on the Czarna Przemsza River made it a natural commercial site, and Jews had been present from at least the early modern period. However, significant Jewish settlement expanded dramatically in the nineteenth century as industrial development attracted workers and entrepreneurs from across the Russian Pale of Settlement and from German Silesia. The city's growth as a coal-trade and textile center created commercial opportunities that Jewish merchants and industrialists were quick to seize.

Under Russian rule in the second half of the nineteenth century, Będzin attracted Jews from the Pale who were permitted to settle in the industrialized Congress Kingdom zone. By 1897 the Jewish population was approximately 10,000; it grew to approximately 28,000 by 1939 — comprising nearly 45 percent of the total population, one of the highest ratios of any Polish industrial city.

## Industrial and Commercial Life

Będzin's economic character was shaped by coal, iron, and textile industries. Jewish merchants served as middlemen in the coal trade, selling fuel mined by Polish workers to factories and households across the region. Jewish-owned textile mills and workshops produced cloth for regional markets. The commercial center of Będzin was heavily Jewish: dry goods stores, hardware dealers, food wholesalers, and professional services were dominated by Jewish families.

The community was also notable for its political sophistication. The density of Jewish population and the industrial character of the city meant that labor Zionism, Bundist socialism, and competing Zionist parties all had vigorous local organizations. The Będzin youth were among the most politically mobilized in Jewish Poland.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Great Synagogue of Będzin, completed in 1881, was one of the most impressive synagogue buildings in the region — a large, elaborately decorated structure that served the kehilla's main congregation. The Nazis burned it on September 9, 1939, within days of their arrival, together with hundreds of Jews who had taken shelter inside. This act of arson and murder, committed in the very first week of the occupation, inaugurated the community's destruction.

Multiple batei midrash, Hasidic shtiblekh, and prayer houses of various affiliations served the diverse religious community. The Ger, Alexander, and Radomsk Hasidic courts all had significant followings in Będzin.

## The Judenrat and Resistance

The story of Będzin's Holocaust is particularly associated with Merin Moshe Merin (1906–1943), the head of the Judenrat (Jewish Council) who governed the combined Jewish communities of eastern Upper Silesia from his headquarters in Będzin. Merin was a controversial and ultimately tragic figure who collaborated extensively with Nazi authorities — providing labor for German factories, compiling deportation lists, maintaining order during selections — in the belief that this compliance would preserve the community's remnant.

The Zionist youth movement in Będzin took a different view. Members of Hashomer Hatzair and other groups contacted the Warsaw ghetto underground and prepared for armed resistance. When the final deportations came in June–August 1943, a small uprising occurred, though it was quickly suppressed. Merin himself was summoned to Auschwitz and killed in June 1943 as the ghetto liquidation began.

The final deportation of Będzin Jewry to Auschwitz-Birkenau took place on August 1–3, 1943. Approximately 10,000 Jews were taken directly to the gas chambers; a small number were selected for slave labor.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Będzin's interwar Jewish cultural life was rich: Hebrew and Yiddish schools operated side by side; a Jewish library held thousands of volumes; dramatic societies performed theater; and the full range of Jewish political associations published newspapers and organized rallies. The competition between ideologies — Orthodox, Zionist, socialist, Bundist — gave Będzin Jewish public life an intensity typical of Polish industrial cities.

## Legacy Today

The site of the Great Synagogue in Będzin is marked by a small memorial. The Jewish cemetery survives partially. A growing interest in local Jewish history has produced exhibitions and educational programs at the Będzin Museum. Descendants of Będzin Jews have maintained memorial organizations primarily in Israel, where many survivors settled after the war. The story of the Będzin underground's resistance has been told in several survivor memoirs and historical studies.

# Zhitomir

## Overview

Zhitomir — today Zhytomyr, the capital of the Zhytomyr Oblast in north-central Ukraine — was one of the most important Jewish communities in the Volhynia-Podolia region. It served as the administrative capital of the province and, crucially for Jewish cultural history, as the home of one of the most significant Hebrew and Yiddish printing establishments in the Russian Empire. The printers of Zhitomir gave the Jewish world a stream of texts that reached communities from St. Petersburg to Odessa and across the Atlantic.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Zhitomir dates to the early modern period under the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. The city served as the capital of Volhynia Province and later of the Volhynia Governorate under Russian imperial administration. Its position as a regional administrative center gave the Jewish community a structural stability: Jews served as merchants, artisans, and intermediaries in a city that needed commercial activity to function as a provincial capital.

## The Zhitomir Printing House

The most distinctive contribution of Zhitomir to Jewish cultural history was its printing establishment. The Shapira printing house — operated by the brothers Chanina Lipa and Yehoshua Heshel Shapira — operated in Zhitomir from 1804 and became, particularly in the mid-nineteenth century, one of the most prolific producers of Hebrew books in the Russian Empire. The Zhitomir printers produced Talmudic tractates, Hasidic texts, prayer books, Bibles, and popular religious literature at a time when the Russian government was restricting the import of Hebrew books from abroad and limiting which cities could maintain printing establishments. The famous "Zhitomir Talmud" editions (1858–1864) were the standard editions of the Talmud for many Russian Jewish communities for generations.

This printing activity was not merely commercial — it was a form of Jewish cultural defense. At a time when the Tsarist government was attempting to centralize Hebrew printing in two approved cities (Vilna and Zhitomir, by a decree of 1836), the Zhitomir printers used their privileged position to ensure a steady supply of traditional Jewish texts across the empire, sustaining religious life in communities that might otherwise have been cut off from new books.

## Notable Figures

Zhitomir was associated with the broader world of Volhynian Hasidism, lying between the Hasidic centers of Berdichev, Polonnoye, and Mezeritch. Rabbi Levi Yitzchak of Berdichev's influence extended into the Zhitomir region. The city also had a significant Mitnagdic (anti-Hasidic) presence, typical of Lithuanian-influenced northeastern Ukraine.

The Hebrew poet Y.L. Gordon (Judah Leib Gordon, 1830–1892), one of the greatest voices of the Jewish Enlightenment, spent formative years in the region. The maskilim (Enlightenment intellectuals) of Zhitomir and its surroundings contributed to the broader Haskalah movement that challenged traditional Jewish life from the 1820s onward.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Zhitomir maintained a full communal structure with a main synagogue, numerous batei midrash, a kehilla administration, and charitable societies. The printing establishment was unusual in being both a commercial enterprise and a communal cultural institution. The Rabbinical Seminary established by the Tsarist government in Zhitomir in 1847 — one of two such officially mandated seminaries designed to produce modernized rabbis loyal to Russian state priorities — was a controversial institution, resisted by traditional Jews and enthusiastically supported by maskilim. It produced graduates who often became teachers, journalists, and writers rather than practicing rabbis.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The printing establishment's legacy is the clearest measure of Zhitomir's cultural contribution: hundreds of editions of foundational Jewish texts, reaching Jewish communities across the empire. The Rabbinical Seminary, despite its controversial character, produced a generation of Jewish intellectuals who contributed to Hebrew and Russian-Jewish literature. The city's position as a regional capital gave its Jewish community an unusual stability and connection to wider administrative structures.

## Decline & Dispersal

Zhitomir's Jews suffered during the pogroms of 1905 and the violence of the civil war period (1918–1921), when the city changed hands many times between Russian, Ukrainian, Polish, and Bolshevik forces, each transition accompanied by attacks on the Jewish community. World War II brought German occupation and the murder of most of Zhitomir's approximately 30,000 Jews in 1941–1942.

## Legacy Today

Zhytomyr today has a small Jewish community. The legacy of the Zhitomir printing house lives in the Hebrew books it produced, which survive in libraries and private collections worldwide. Several significant Talmudic editions still used today trace their text to the Zhitomir printers.

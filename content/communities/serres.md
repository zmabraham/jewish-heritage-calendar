# Serres

## Overview

Serres (Serrai, Serez), in east-central Greek Macedonia, was one of the major Jewish communities of Ottoman Macedonia. A Ladino-speaking Sephardic city of considerable commercial importance, Serres housed a Jewish community that, like most Macedonian Jewish communities, was destroyed in the Holocaust. The deportations from Macedonia in 1943 were particularly thorough, and Serres' Jews — numbering approximately 600 persons — were rounded up by Bulgarian authorities acting under German direction and deported to Treblinka, where virtually all perished.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Serres followed the general Ottoman Sephardic pattern: refugees from Spain after 1492 established communities in the major Macedonian cities, and Serres — a significant regional center with a substantial Muslim population, large Greek Orthodox community, and smaller Armenian population — absorbed a Jewish contingent that became part of the city's cosmopolitan fabric.

The community grew steadily through the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries as Serres developed as a commercial center connecting the Aegean port of Thessaloniki to the interior of Macedonia and Thrace. Jewish merchants participated in the textile trade, grain commerce, and local retail trade. The Jewish quarter was a defined neighborhood in the Ottoman urban layout, with its own synagogue, mikveh, school, and communal institutions.

## Community Life and Culture

The Jews of Serres spoke Ladino as their vernacular, used Turkish in dealing with Ottoman authorities, and gradually acquired Greek as Greek influence grew in Macedonia in the nineteenth century. The community was religiously traditional, following the Sephardic-Ottoman rite practiced throughout the region.

As was typical of Macedonian Sephardic communities, Serres' Jews were urban-dwelling merchants and artisans, concentrated in specific commercial sectors. They maintained active trade relationships with the much larger Jewish community of Thessaloniki, with which they shared religious rites, Ladino dialect features, and family connections. Rabbinical scholars from Thessaloniki served the Serres community at various periods, and the yeshiva of Serres trained students who sometimes went on to study in Thessaloniki.

## Institutions

The community maintained a major synagogue — the Kahal Kadosh — on a site that has been identified in modern urban archaeology. The building survived into the twentieth century but was destroyed during the German occupation. The community also operated a talmud torah, a mikveh, and charitable foundations. The Jewish cemetery of Serres was located outside the city walls, as mandated by both Jewish law and Ottoman urban practice.

## Notable Figures

No internationally prominent figures emerged from the Serres community, which was a modest provincial Sephardic center rather than a major intellectual hub. The community's history is documented in municipal archives, Ottoman tax records, and the memoir literature of the Macedonian Jewish world. Rabbinical figures from Serres appear in the broader Sephardic responsa literature, documenting the legal and religious questions arising from the community's life.

## The Macedonian Jewish Tragedy

The German occupation of Greece in 1941 divided the country: eastern Macedonia and Thrace were placed under Bulgarian occupation as a reward for Bulgaria's Axis alliance. Bulgaria administered these regions with considerable autonomy but followed German demands regarding the Jewish population.

In March 1943, Bulgarian authorities, under intense German pressure, conducted a systematic roundup of Jews in Bulgarian-occupied Macedonia and Thrace. The Jews of Serres were arrested, assembled, and transported via Bulgaria to the death camp at Treblinka. Virtually the entire community — approximately 600 persons — was murdered. The deportations from Macedonia were among the most thorough of the Holocaust; nearly all Macedonian Jewish communities were destroyed completely, with survival rates in the low single digits.

## Legacy Today

Serres today has essentially no Jewish community. The former Jewish quarter has been absorbed into the modern city. The synagogue site was built over after the war. The Jewish cemetery, though traces remain, is not actively maintained as a Jewish site. The memory of the community lives primarily in the testimonies of survivors who were not in Serres during the deportations — travelers, students in Athens or Thessaloniki, or those who managed to escape capture — and in the historical literature on Macedonian Jewry.

A memorial plaque was erected in Serres commemorating the deportation. The community is discussed in broader works on Macedonian Jewish history and the Holocaust in Greece. The tragic story of Serres is typical of Macedonian Jewry: centuries of continuous Sephardic life, integrated into Ottoman urban society, brought to an abrupt and total end in a single week of deportations in 1943.

# Fez

## Overview

Fez (Fas) was for centuries the intellectual and spiritual capital of Moroccan Jewry and one of the most important Jewish communities in the medieval and early modern world. It served as a refuge for the young Maimonides, a cradle of rabbinic scholarship, and the birthplace of the Moroccan mellah — the Jewish quarter system that would define North African Jewish urban life for five centuries. The community's scholars bridged Andalusian and North African traditions, producing works of lasting importance in halakha, grammar, and biblical commentary.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in Fez dates at least to the city's founding by Idris II in 808–809 CE, who reportedly invited Jewish traders and artisans to populate his new capital. A Jewish quarter was established in what became known as "Funduk al-Yahud." The community grew through subsequent waves of immigration, including refugees from persecutions elsewhere in the Islamic world.

The most transformative event in Fez's Jewish history was the arrival of refugees from Almohad persecution in al-Andalus and Morocco itself. The Almohad (al-Muwahhidun) dynasty, which seized power in the mid-12th century, demanded conversion or death from Jewish and Christian populations. The family of Maimonides fled Córdoba and spent time in Fez before moving onward — Maimonides' father, Rabbi Maimon ben Joseph, likely composed his famous "Letter of Consolation" (*Iggeret HaNechamah*) while in Fez, urging crypto-Jews to maintain secret loyalty to Judaism.

In 1492, following the Spanish expulsion, thousands of Spanish Jewish exiles settled in Fez, dramatically enriching the community and creating lasting tensions between the "megorashim" (expelled Sephardim) and the earlier "toshavim" (resident Moroccan Jews).

## Golden Age

Fez's 16th and 17th centuries represent its cultural peak. The influx of Spanish exiles brought Talmudic scholars of the highest rank, who established yeshivot, printing presses, and communal institutions. The Spanish-descended community preserved Castilian Jewish customs and produced scholarship deeply influenced by the Spanish rabbinic tradition, including the school of Rabbi Joseph Karo and the Kabbalah of Safed.

Rabbi Samuel Valenci, Rabbi Abraham ibn Usque, and many other Spanish scholars contributed to the flowering of Fezzi Jewish intellectual life. The community was also noted for its skilled artisans — Jewish metalworkers, weavers, and dyers were integral to Fez's famous crafts economy.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Vidal HaTzarfati (15th–16th century) was among the Spanish exiles who settled in Fez and became a leading rabbinic authority, his responsa addressing the complex legal situations created by the mass dislocation.

Rabbi Yehuda ibn Tibbon (1120–c. 1190) — though primarily associated with Provence — spent his formative years in the Fez region before emigrating north; his translation dynasty rendered Arabic philosophical works into Hebrew for European Jews.

Rabbi Ya'akov ibn Tsur (Yaavetz, c. 1673–1753) was one of Fez's greatest 18th-century figures, author of the responsa collection *Mishpat uTzedakah BeYaakov*, a massive work addressing Moroccan halakhic practice, and the important responsa collection *Eved HaMelekh*. His poetry and commentary on the Passover Haggadah remain in use.

Rabbi Chaim ibn Attar (Or HaChaim, 1696–1743) was born in Salé near Rabat but studied and taught in Fez before his journey to Italy and ultimately to Jerusalem. His Torah commentary *Or HaChaim* is one of the most celebrated in the Sephardic world, beloved also among Hasidim who regard him as a saint.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The mellah concept — a walled Jewish quarter — was reportedly born in Fez in 1438, when the Jewish community was moved to a new quarter east of the royal palace following anti-Jewish violence. The word "mellah" derives from the Arabic for salt, possibly because the quarter was near salt marshes or because Jews were required to salt the heads of executed criminals. The mellah system subsequently spread throughout Morocco and became the defining feature of Moroccan Jewish urban life.

Fez's mellah contained dozens of synagogues and a rich communal infrastructure including a beth din (religious court), yeshivot, mikveh (ritual bath), and communal bakery. The Ibn Danan Synagogue, constructed in the 17th century, is among the most beautifully preserved medieval synagogues in North Africa; it has been restored and is open to visitors.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Fez community produced the foundational document of Moroccan Jewish legal custom, the *Takkanot Fez* — a series of communal regulations enacted jointly by the exiled Spanish community and the indigenous Moroccan community, governing marriage, inheritance, and commercial law. These takkanot, debated and revised over generations, represent the legal constitution of Moroccan Jewry.

Fez was also a center of Jewish mysticism. Kabbalistic texts and practices from Safed reached Fez in the 16th century and were enthusiastically adopted; the city produced notable kabbalists and maintained a vibrant tradition of Zohar study and practical Kabbalah.

## Decline & Dispersal

The 18th and 19th centuries brought periodic violence, including the notorious pogrom of 1790 during a succession crisis. Fez's Jewish population declined gradually through the 20th century; many moved to Casablanca, and most emigrated to France or Israel in the 1950s–1970s. By the 1990s, only a handful of elderly Jews remained in the historic mellah.

## Legacy Today

The Ibn Danan Synagogue has been restored by the Moroccan government and UNESCO. The mellah of Fez, though emptied of Jews, retains its distinctive architectural character and is a UNESCO World Heritage site within the medina. A small Jewish museum operates in the mellah. Moroccan Jewish communities in France, Israel, and Canada maintain strong memory of Fez as a spiritual center.

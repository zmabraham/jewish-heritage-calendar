# Munich

## Overview

Munich occupies a singular position in modern Jewish history as the birthplace of National Socialism — the city where Hitler launched his political career, where the Beer Hall Putsch of 1923 failed but foreshadowed what was to come, and where the laws and institutions of Nazi antisemitism were first developed. Yet Munich also had a flourishing Jewish community — intellectually distinguished, economically successful, thoroughly integrated into Bavarian civic life — that was systematically destroyed by the movement that had been incubated in its midst.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Munich was long forbidden. The city's guilds and civic authorities kept Jews out for most of the medieval and early modern periods. Jews were permitted to attend the famous trade fairs (briefly and conditionally), but permanent residence required exceptional circumstances.

The modern Jewish community was established in the early nineteenth century, following Bavaria's gradual legal reforms. Jews were allowed to settle in Munich from 1813, when Bavarian Jews received limited civil rights. The community grew slowly but steadily through the mid-nineteenth century; full legal emancipation came in 1871 with the unification of Germany.

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw the Munich community flourish. By 1910, the community numbered approximately 11,000; by 1933, approximately 9,000 Jews lived in Munich.

## Golden Age

Munich's Jewish golden age occupied the decades before and after 1900. Jewish artists, writers, collectors, and intellectuals were deeply integrated into the Munich cultural scene — one of the most vibrant in Germany. The expressionist movement Blaue Reiter (Blue Rider), founded in Munich in 1911, included several artists of Jewish background.

The community maintained a magnificent synagogue, the Hauptsynagoge on the Herzog-Max-Strasse (1887), one of the grandest synagogue buildings in Germany, with a spectacular Moorish interior. Jewish merchants, bankers, and industrialists were visible in the city's commercial life. The Simson family, the Levy department store, and several major banking houses represented Jewish commercial achievement.

## Notable Figures

Kurt Eisner (1867–1919), the leader of the Bavarian Revolution of 1918–1919 and briefly Prime Minister of Bavaria, was a Berlin-born Jewish journalist and socialist politician. His assassination by right-wing nationalists in February 1919 — and the subsequent suppression of the Bavarian Soviet Republic — helped feed the poisonous political atmosphere in which Nazism would grow.

Rainer Maria Rilke, while not Jewish, was deeply connected to Munich's Jewish intellectual circles. The poet Else Lasker-Schüler, though primarily associated with Berlin, had Munich connections.

Lion Feuchtwanger (1884–1958), born in Munich, was one of Germany's most successful novelists. His historical novels — including Jud Süss (1925), which presented a more nuanced view of Jewish history than its later Nazi film adaptation — were best-sellers. He was forced into exile in 1933 and eventually settled in Los Angeles.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Hauptsynagoge (1887), destroyed by the Nazis in June 1938 — months before Kristallnacht, on direct orders from Hitler himself — had been one of the finest synagogue buildings in Bavaria. Hitler reportedly viewed the building with personal hatred and had it demolished while most German synagogues were still standing.

After the war, the Jewish community rebuilt with a new synagogue on the Reichenbachstrasse (1949) and eventually a major new complex — the Ohel Jakob synagogue (2006), the Jewish Community Center, and the Munich Jewish Museum — built at the St. Jakobs-Platz in the heart of the city as a statement of renewed Jewish presence.

## Cultural Life

Munich's Jewish culture before 1933 was deeply intertwined with the city's identity as a center of art, music, and theater. Jewish publishers, gallery owners, theater directors, and musicians shaped the Munich cultural scene. The city's Jewish community maintained an active intellectual life: a Jewish school, charitable organizations, Zionist youth groups, and learned societies.

The proximity of the Nazi movement to Munich's Jewish community created an atmosphere of growing terror from 1919 onward. Munich's Jews watched the rise of Nazism at close quarters, living in the city that was its epicenter.

## Decline & Transformation

Munich was one of the first German cities to experience the full impact of Nazi persecution. The Hauptsynagoge was demolished in June 1938 — the first major synagogue destruction under the Nazi regime. Kristallnacht (November 1938) saw Jewish businesses attacked and the remaining synagogues burned. Deportations from Munich began in November 1941 and continued through 1943; Jewish residents were sent to Łódź, Riga, Theresienstadt, and Auschwitz. Of approximately 9,000 Munich Jews in 1933, approximately 4,000 were murdered in the Holocaust; most others had emigrated before the deportations began.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Munich today has a Jewish community of approximately 9,000, one of the largest in Germany, rebuilt by post-war survivors, displaced persons, and a large wave of immigrants from the former Soviet Union after 1991. The new Jewish center at St.-Jakobs-Platz — with its stunning contemporary architecture — has become a celebrated public space and a statement that Jewish life in Germany has genuinely been renewed.

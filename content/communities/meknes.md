# Meknès

## Overview

Meknès, one of Morocco's four imperial cities, hosted an ancient and distinguished Jewish community that experienced both moments of great flourishing and devastating violence. The city's mellah, established in the 17th century during the reign of Sultan Moulay Ismail, became a center of Moroccan Jewish scholarship and commerce. The Meknès community is remembered not only for its contributions to Torah learning but also for the catastrophic pogrom of 1728, which tested and ultimately demonstrated the community's resilience.

## Historical Background

Jewish settlement in the Meknès region predates the Islamic period. The city itself was founded in the 11th century by the Almoravid dynasty, and Jews were resident from its early history. As with other Moroccan communities, the Meknès Jewish population received an influx of Spanish and Portuguese exile families after 1492, enriching its culture and scholarship.

The most significant transformation of the Meknès Jewish community came under Sultan Moulay Ismail (r. 1672–1727), who made Meknès his imperial capital and invested enormously in its construction, employing thousands of laborers including European captives. Jews in Meknès served as the sultan's financial agents, tax collectors, and commercial intermediaries, a role that brought wealth and influence but also vulnerability to political change.

## Golden Age

The 17th century, despite its challenges, was a period of notable intellectual achievement for Meknès Jewry. The community supported active yeshivot and produced halakhic scholars whose responsa illuminate Moroccan Jewish life in the imperial capital. The proximity of the royal court meant that Meknès Jewish leaders were often deeply involved in diplomatic affairs, serving as intermediaries between the Moroccan sultans and European powers — particularly in ransom negotiations for European captives held in Morocco.

The Sephardic community's influence is evident in the adoption of the Spanish exile legal customs and liturgy alongside the older "toshav" (indigenous) practices. The resolution of tensions between these two traditions generated substantial halakhic creativity, as rabbis addressed novel questions arising from the merger of communities with different legal traditions.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Raphael ibn Tsur (also ibn Zur, 18th century) was the most celebrated scholar of Meknès, a prolific halakhic authority whose responsa collection *Mishpetei Tzedek* remains a fundamental source for Moroccan Jewish law. He served as the community's chief rabbi and his rulings on questions of commercial law, family status, and communal governance were respected throughout Morocco.

Rabbi Yehuda ibn Attar (d. 1733), known as the "Rav" of Fez but with connections throughout the imperial cities, was among the leading scholars of his generation and represented the broader network of Moroccan rabbinic learning to which Meknès belonged.

The Ben Walid family and other prominent dynasties combined commercial activity with patronage of Torah scholarship, endowing synagogues and maintaining scholars in residence.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Meknès mellah, constructed in the 17th century adjacent to the royal palace complex, was at its height home to several thousand Jews. Its main synagogue, the Synagogue de la Bahia or Slat Ahl Fes, was among the community's principal institutions. The mellah also contained numerous smaller synagogues, a yeshiva, mikveh, and the community's beth din.

The cemetery at Meknès contains the graves of notable rabbis and communal leaders and remains a site of pilgrimage, particularly the grave of Rabbi Raphael ibn Tsur.

## The Meknès Pogrom of 1728

The event that most deeply marks Meknès's Jewish memory is the pogrom of Tammuz 1728 (summer 1728), known in Hebrew sources as the "Hurban Meknes" (Destruction of Meknès). Following the death of Sultan Moulay Ismail in 1727, the city was plunged into a succession crisis and civil conflict. In the ensuing chaos, the Jewish mellah was attacked by armed bands. Hundreds of Jews were killed; women were taken captive; the mellah was pillaged and burned.

The survivors fled to the countryside and to neighboring cities, particularly Fez and Rabat. The ransom of Jewish captives required enormous communal resources and the intervention of prominent community leaders. Rabbi Shlomo Maimaran and other figures played heroic roles in organizing relief, ransoming captives, and negotiating the community's restoration.

The community eventually returned and rebuilt, but the trauma of 1728 remained a central event in communal memory, commemorated as a fast day and recorded in Hebrew and Judeo-Arabic lamentation poetry.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Meknès was a significant center of liturgical poetry in the Moroccan tradition. Its scholars composed *piyyutim* in Hebrew and Judeo-Arabic that were sung at Shabbat meals, life-cycle events, and the annual *mimouna* celebration (the joyous party held on the night following Passover). The *mimouna* itself, a distinctly Moroccan Jewish tradition, was celebrated with great intensity in Meknès, with neighboring Muslim families bringing flour and symbolic gifts to Jewish homes.

The community also preserved distinctive culinary traditions, musical styles (including the Andalusian-derived *malhun* and *gharnati* forms), and artisanal crafts — Jewish goldsmiths and silversmiths of Meknès were famous throughout Morocco.

## Decline & Dispersal

The 20th century brought gradual decline. Many Meknès Jews moved to Casablanca during the Protectorate period in search of economic opportunity. The establishment of the State of Israel in 1948 and Moroccan independence in 1956 accelerated emigration. By the 1970s, the community was greatly diminished; by the 21st century, only a tiny remnant remained.

## Legacy Today

Several Meknès synagogues have been preserved as heritage sites. The mellah district retains its historic character. Annual pilgrimage events to the graves of local tzaddikim draw diaspora visitors. Meknès's Jewish descendants in France (particularly Paris), Israel, and Canada maintain strong communal organizations.

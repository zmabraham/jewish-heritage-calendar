# Mannheim

## Overview

Mannheim's Jewish community is a product of the Palatinate's peculiar tradition of religious tolerance — a tradition forged by the terrible experience of the Thirty Years' War and the subsequent efforts of the Electors Palatine to rebuild their devastated territories by welcoming settlers of various faiths. In this context, Jews were invited to Mannheim as useful commercial citizens, and the community that developed was among the most prosperous and culturally sophisticated in the German southwest. The tolerance era's legacy made Mannheim one of the more hospitable environments for Jewish life in seventeenth- and eighteenth-century Germany.

## Historical Origins

Mannheim itself is a relatively new city — founded as a planned city by the Elector Palatine Frederick IV in 1606 on a grid plan at the confluence of the Rhine and Neckar rivers. The city was destroyed in the Thirty Years' War (1622, 1689) and repeatedly rebuilt. In this process of reconstruction, Jews were among the groups invited to settle and contribute to the city's economic development.

Elector Karl Ludwig of the Palatinate, who began rebuilding Mannheim after the Thirty Years' War, issued invitations to Jews, Huguenots, Mennonites, and other religious minorities to settle in his territories. This policy of confessional tolerance — pragmatic rather than principled — created the framework for Mannheim's Jewish community.

The first Jews settled in Mannheim in the 1650s; by 1700, the community had grown significantly. The Electors' protection gave Mannheim's Jews unusual stability compared to communities in more politically rigid imperial cities.

## Golden Age

The eighteenth century was the community's golden age. Protected by the Palatinate court, Mannheim's Jews developed a prosperous commercial life, trading in textiles, tobacco, and various goods that moved through the Rhine valley. Several Jewish court agents maintained close connections to the electoral court at Mannheim and Heidelberg.

The community attracted refugees from less tolerant territories — Jews expelled from other German cities or fleeing periodic violence found in Mannheim a relatively secure haven. The community grew to several hundred families by mid-century. A synagogue was established, and the community developed the educational and charitable infrastructure typical of a well-organized Ashkenazic community.

## Notable Figures

Moses Haas (d. 1750) and subsequent community leaders managed the delicate diplomacy of maintaining good relations with the Palatinate court while representing the community's interests to a wider German world.

Aaron Guggenheimer was among the leading Jewish merchants of eighteenth-century Mannheim; the Guggenheimer family, like several other Mannheim Jewish families, maintained commercial networks extending across Germany and into France and the Netherlands.

The Mannheim community contributed to the German-Jewish Haskalah through its connections to Jewish intellectual life in Frankfurt, Berlin, and Amsterdam.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

The Mannheim synagogue, built in the eighteenth century, served as the community's spiritual center. In the nineteenth century, with emancipation opening new possibilities, the community built a larger and more architecturally impressive synagogue.

The Great Synagogue of Mannheim, completed in 1855 in a Moorish-Romanesque style, was one of the finest synagogue buildings in southwest Germany. It was burned on Kristallnacht in November 1938 and never rebuilt; a modest community synagogue serves today.

## Cultural Life

Mannheim's Jewish culture was shaped by its border position — between Catholic and Protestant Germany, between French and German cultural influences, between the worlds of commerce and the early German Enlightenment. The community participated in the cultural life of a city that supported one of the most important German theatrical institutions (the Nationaltheater Mannheim) and that prided itself on its Enlightened character.

Jewish intellectuals in Mannheim read Mendelssohn and engaged with the Haskalah; Reform tendencies were present in the community. At the same time, traditional Orthodoxy maintained a strong presence, and the community's diverse membership encompassed a range of religious orientations.

## Decline & Transformation

The Nazi accession to power in 1933 found Mannheim's Jewish community numbering approximately 6,500. Progressive persecution — professional exclusion, Aryanization of businesses, violence — drove emigration. Kristallnacht in 1938 destroyed the Great Synagogue. Deportations from Mannheim began in October 1940, when the entire Jewish community of Baden and the Saarpfalz region was deported to the French internment camp of Gurs; subsequent transports sent survivors to Auschwitz. The Mannheim deportation of October 22–23, 1940 was among the largest single deportations from any German city and preceded the systematic deportations that would begin throughout Germany in 1941.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Mannheim today has a Jewish community of approximately 600–800, maintained primarily by immigrants from the former Soviet Union. The memory of the deportation — organized and documented with disturbing thoroughness — is an important element of the city's historical consciousness.

# Valencia

## Overview

Valencia, conquered from Muslim rule by James I of Aragon in 1238, rapidly developed into one of the most important Jewish communities of the Crown of Aragon. Strategically located on the Mediterranean coast with an excellent harbor and a fertile hinterland, Valencia became a major commercial hub, and its Jewish community — bolstered by immigrants from across the peninsula and North Africa — participated fully in this prosperity. The city's Jewish life was marked by both intellectual achievement and vulnerability, culminating in the devastating 1391 pogrom that nearly destroyed the community.

## Historical Origins

Jews had lived in Muslim Balansiya since the early Islamic period, though the community was not among the largest of al-Andalus. James I's conquest in 1238 transformed the city's status: the new Christian king actively encouraged Jewish settlement, offering favorable terms and tax incentives to attract residents from other parts of the peninsula and from North Africa. This policy succeeded rapidly, and Valencia's Jewish community grew to become one of the major aljamas (Jewish communities) of Aragon by the late thirteenth century.

The community's legal status was protected by the Furs of Valencia — the city's charter — which guaranteed Jews the right to practice their religion and regulated their communal autonomy.

## Golden Age

Valencia's golden age extended from its conquest through the mid-fourteenth century. The community's economic position was strong: Jewish merchants participated in Mediterranean trade, Jews were prominent in the textile industry, and Jewish tax-farmers collected royal revenues for the Aragonese crown. The community's wealth supported substantial institutions and a class of learned scholars.

Intellectually, Valencia was noted more for its Talmudic scholarship than for philosophical or poetic production. The community maintained an important yeshiva and produced respected halakhic authorities whose responsa addressed questions from throughout the region. The Valencian rite — the local variation in synagogue practice — was well-known across Sephardic Jewry.

The Jewish quarter, the Judería, occupied a substantial area near the city center. At its height, the community probably numbered between two and three thousand people, making it one of the five largest Jewish communities in the Crown of Aragon.

## Notable Figures

Valencia produced no single figure of Nahmanides' or Maimonides' stature, but its scholarly community was respected. Rabbi Isaac bar Sheshet Perfet, known as the Rivash (1326–1408), though born and educated elsewhere, served as a major rabbinic authority in Valencia before fleeing the 1391 pogrom. His responsa, collected as She'elot uTeshuvot haRivash, are among the most important sources for Sephardic halakhic practice of the period.

Rabbi Hasdai Crescas (c. 1340–1412), the great philosopher and critic of Aristotelian rationalism, lived in Valencia and may have served as its chief rabbi before 1391. His Light of the Lord (Or Hashem) presented a systematic Jewish alternative to Aristotelian philosophy and exercised enormous influence on later Jewish thought.

The community also supported its own cadre of dayyanim (judges), teachers, scribes, and communal administrators, though their names are less widely known.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Valencia's Judería was centered around the present-day Plaça de Sant Llorenç, extending through the network of narrow streets between the cathedral and the river. The main synagogue, a substantial structure, was converted to a church after 1391 and today forms part of the convent of Sant Llorenç.

The community maintained a bathhouse (miqveh), a communal oven, schools, and charitable institutions. Archaeological work has identified the boundaries of the Jewish quarter and the locations of several synagogues.

## Cultural Life

Valencian Jewish culture was Catalan in orientation but with distinctive local elements. The community used Valencian Catalan in daily life and Hebrew for religious and scholarly purposes. Trade connections with North Africa gave the community particular contact with Maghrebi Jewish traditions.

Liturgical practice followed the Catalan rite, with local variations that distinguished Valencia from Barcelona and other Catalan communities. The city's Jews participated in Valencia's civic festivals and markets, while maintaining their separate communal identity through the aljama's internal governance.

## Decline & Transformation

The wave of violence that began in Seville in June 1391 reached Valencia on July 9. The Judería was stormed, the main synagogue destroyed, and hundreds of Jews were killed. Many more accepted forced conversion in the immediate aftermath. Valencia's community, already weakened by economic difficulties and plagues in the preceding decades, never recovered.

A small Jewish community reconstituted itself in the years after 1391, but it was a fraction of the former population. The community limped along through the fifteenth century, increasingly isolated and vulnerable, until the final expulsion of 1492 ended Jewish life in Valencia entirely.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The Rivash and Crescas, displaced by the 1391 violence, carried Valencia's intellectual tradition into exile. The Rivash ended his career in North Africa, while Crescas became a major figure in Aragonese Jewish life after the pogrom. Their writings — Crescas's philosophical works in particular — represent the most enduring legacy of medieval Valencian Jewry.

Today, Valencia acknowledges its Jewish past through historical markers, guided tours, and ongoing archaeological work. The boundaries of the Judería are well-documented, and visitors can follow routes through the medieval Jewish quarter. The city hosts a small contemporary Jewish community separate from the medieval lineage, serving international residents and tourists.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Established by James I after 1238 conquest; attracted immigrants from across Iberia and North Africa
- **Peak Population**: 2,000–3,000 (mid-14th century)
- **Languages**: Valencian Catalan, Hebrew, Judeo-Arabic
- **Status Today**: Small modern Jewish community; medieval Judería documented and commemorated

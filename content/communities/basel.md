# Basel

## Overview

Basel (Basle), the Rhine city at the meeting point of Switzerland, Germany, and France, occupies a pivotal moment in modern Jewish history: it was the site of the First Zionist Congress in August 1897, where Theodor Herzl convened delegates from across the world to establish the World Zionist Organization and formally launch the modern political movement for a Jewish state. Herzl famously wrote in his diary after the congress: "In Basel I founded the Jewish State." Basel thus became the symbolic birthplace of the movement that would lead, fifty-one years later, to the establishment of the State of Israel.

## Historical Background

Basel's Jewish history predates the Zionist Congress by many centuries. Jews were present in medieval Basel, forming part of the dense web of Jewish settlement in the upper Rhine valley. Like other Swiss and German Rhineland communities, Basel's Jews suffered in the Black Death persecutions of 1348–1349, when the entire Jewish community was burned to death by the city's Christian population — one of the most complete massacres of any medieval Rhineland community. The city subsequently banned Jewish residence for extended periods.

Jews began to return to Basel in small numbers in the seventeenth century. The Swiss cantons were, in general, slow to emancipate their Jewish populations: full legal equality for Swiss Jews came only in 1874, after pressure from foreign powers (including France and the United States, whose Jewish citizens faced discrimination when visiting Switzerland). Basel's Jewish community grew modestly in the nineteenth century, attaining civil equality alongside its Swiss counterparts.

## The First Zionist Congress (1897)

The congress was originally planned for Munich, but opposition from the German Jewish establishment — who feared that Zionist agitation would undermine their position as German citizens — forced Herzl to find an alternative venue. Basel, with its large civic concert hall (the Stadtcasino), agreed to host the event.

The congress convened August 29–31, 1897. Some 200 delegates attended, representing Jewish communities from Russia, Romania, Western Europe, and the Americas. The congress adopted the "Basel Program," declaring that "Zionism seeks to establish for the Jewish people a publicly recognized, legally secured home in Palestine." It founded the World Zionist Organization (WZO), established the shekel as a membership fee creating an international Zionist constituency, and launched the institutions — the Jewish Colonial Trust, the Jewish National Fund (Keren Kayemeth LeIsrael) — that would underpin the Zionist project over subsequent decades.

## Notable Figures

Theodor Herzl (1860–1904), Viennese journalist and playwright, was the visionary who transformed Jewish nationalist sentiment into a political movement. His pamphlet *Der Judenstaat* (The Jewish State, 1896) proposed a political solution to the "Jewish question" — the persistent antisemitism of European civilization — by creating a sovereign Jewish state. The Basel Congress was his greatest organizational achievement. He died seven years later, aged 44, having devoted his remaining years to diplomacy with European powers, without seeing his vision fulfilled.

David Wolffsohn (1856–1914), a Cologne timber merchant who succeeded Herzl as WZO president (1905–1911), played a crucial role in institutionalizing the Zionist movement. He reportedly designed the Zionist flag by combining the blue stripes of the tallit (prayer shawl) with the Star of David — a flag that became, with modifications, the flag of the State of Israel.

Max Nordau (1849–1923), Herzl's close associate and the congress's principal orator, electrified delegates with his vision of the "Muskeljude" (muscular Jew) who would replace the stooped ghetto Jew with a vigorous, physically proud Zionist pioneer.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Basel's Jewish community, though never large, maintained the institutional infrastructure of Swiss Jewish life: a synagogue, community organizations, and educational institutions. The Basel Synagogue (1868) stands as a center of local Jewish life. The Stadtcasino, where the congress was held, was renovated in the early twenty-first century; its main hall where the congress convened is maintained and marked as a historical site.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Basel's significance to Jewish culture is almost entirely located in the 1897 congress and its aftermath. The city became the annual meeting place for early Zionist congresses (the movement held many of its early assemblies there), establishing Basel as a symbol of the Zionist movement's origins. The congress proceedings and documents in the Basel archives are primary sources for the history of political Zionism.

## Decline & Dispersal

Switzerland's Jews were not subject to the same catastrophes as German or Eastern European Jewry during the World War II period, though Swiss border policy during the war — which turned back Jewish refugees from Nazi-occupied Europe — is a dark chapter in Swiss and Jewish history that has been the subject of significant post-war reckoning.

## Legacy Today

Basel's Jewish community today numbers a few thousand. The Stadtcasino congress hall is a pilgrimage destination for Zionist history enthusiasts. Israeli schoolchildren and diaspora visitors come to see the hall where Herzl's vision was formally launched. The World Zionist Organization, based today in Jerusalem, traces its institutional lineage to Basel 1897.

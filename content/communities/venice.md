# Venice

## Overview

Venice holds a unique and somber distinction in Jewish history: the Venetian Republic established the world's first ghetto in 1516, confining its Jewish residents to a small island in the Cannaregio district — the word "ghetto" itself derives from the Venetian *geto* (foundry), named for the iron foundry that once stood on that island. Yet Venice was simultaneously a center of extraordinary Jewish intellectual, commercial, and cultural vitality. Jews helped finance Venetian trade, print Hebrew books, practice medicine, and engage in philosophical debate in a city that was, by Renaissance standards, unusual in its willingness to harbor Jewish life at all.

## Historical Background

Jews had lived in Venice and its mainland territories (the *terraferma*) before the ghetto's establishment, though they were formally prohibited from residing in the city itself. They were tolerated as moneylenders — a function the Christian church officially forbade to its own members — and as traders, especially those with ties to the Levant. The Jewish presence intensified in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries as Sephardic refugees expelled from Spain (1492) and Portugal (1497) sought new homes across the Mediterranean.

On 29 March 1516, the Venetian Senate decreed that all Jews living in Venice must move to the island of the *geto nuovo* (new foundry), a small area locked at night and guarded by Christian watchmen paid for by the Jewish community itself. This institution — the ghetto — became a model copied across Italy and eventually across Europe.

## Golden Age

Despite its physical confinement, the ghetto was a site of remarkable vitality. Three distinct Jewish communities coexisted within its walls: the Ashkenazim (German and Italian Jews), the Levantine Sephardim, and the Ponentine (Western) Sephardim — each with their own synagogue, confraternity, and customs. The ghetto expanded twice (the *geto vecchio* in 1541 and the *geto nuovissimo* in 1633) as the Jewish population grew, and buildings rose six and seven stories high — the original "skyscrapers" of the Venetian skyline — as the only way to accommodate more residents within fixed walls.

Venice was also the center of Hebrew printing in the sixteenth century. The Christian printer Daniel Bomberg (a Flemish Catholic!) established a press in Venice that between 1516 and 1549 produced the first printed editions of both the Babylonian and Jerusalem Talmuds, setting the page layout with Rashi commentary and Tosafot flanking the central text — a format still used in Talmud editions worldwide. Bomberg also printed the first Rabbinic Bible (*Mikraot Gedolot*, 1516–1517).

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Leone Modena (Yehuda Aryeh mi-Modena, 1571–1648) was one of the most remarkable figures of Venetian Jewish life. A polymath and preacher of extraordinary gifts, he wrote in both Hebrew and Italian, corresponded with Christian scholars, composed music, translated texts, and authored a groundbreaking description of Jewish customs for Christian readers (*Historia de' riti Hebraici*, 1637). His autobiography, *Hayyei Yehuda* (The Life of Judah), is an early Jewish autobiographical text of unusual candor.

Rabbi Simone Luzzatto (c. 1583–1663) was another towering Venetian Jewish intellectual. His *Discorso circa il stato de gl'Hebrei* (1638) was a sophisticated political-economic argument addressed to the Venetian Senate defending Jewish residence as economically beneficial — one of the earliest systematic defenses of Jewish civil rights in European history.

Sara Copia Sullam (c. 1592–1641), a Venetian Jewish woman of letters, hosted a literary salon and engaged in published theological and poetic debate, remarkable for any woman of the period and extraordinary for a Jewish woman confined to the ghetto.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The five synagogues of the Venetian ghetto — the Scuola Grande Tedesca (1528), Scuola Canton (1531–32), Scuola Italiana (1575), Scuola Levantina (1541), and Scuola Spagnola (rebuilt c. 1584) — are among the finest examples of Renaissance and Baroque Jewish sacred architecture in the world. They survive intact and are still used for worship today, maintained by the small remaining Jewish community. The synagogues were built above ground level (to avoid the appearance of rivaling Christian churches) and feature opulent interiors with carved wood, gilded decorations, and elaborate Torah arks.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Venice was a center of Kabbalistic printing and scholarship. Works of kabbalah, musar (ethical literature), and philosophy poured from Venetian Hebrew presses. The Zohar was printed in Venice. The city's role as a Mediterranean commercial hub meant that Jewish merchants served as essential brokers between the Christian West and the Ottoman East, handling textiles, spices, and luxury goods while maintaining information networks that served both Venetian state interests and Jewish communal needs.

## Decline & Dispersal

The ghetto system persisted until Napoleon abolished it in 1797, when French troops tore down the ghetto gates to cheers from the Jewish community. Under Austrian rule the ghetto was temporarily restored (1798–1806), then finally abolished under Italian unification. The community contracted through the nineteenth and twentieth centuries as Jews left for other Italian cities. During the German occupation of Italy (1943–45), Venetian Jews were deported to Auschwitz; approximately 200 of Venice's roughly 2,000 Jews were murdered.

## Legacy Today

The Venice Ghetto is a UNESCO-recognized heritage site and a major destination for Jewish historical tourism. The Jewish Museum of Venice (Museo Ebraico) operates inside the ghetto and conducts tours of the historic synagogues. A small but active Jewish community still worships in the historic synagogues on Shabbat and holidays. The 500th anniversary of the ghetto's founding in 2016 was marked by international commemoration events.

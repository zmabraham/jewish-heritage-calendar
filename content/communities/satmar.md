# Satu-Mare (Satmar)

## Overview

Satu-Mare — known in Yiddish as Satmar — gave its name to what became the world's largest anti-Zionist Hasidic dynasty. The Satmar Rebbe, Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum, survived the Holocaust through one of its most controversial rescue operations, built an empire of traditional Judaism in Brooklyn, and created a theological legacy that continues to define the most uncompromising strand of Haredi opposition to the State of Israel.

## Historical Background

Satu-Mare lies in northwestern Romania, in the same Maramureș region as Sighet. Like many towns in this area, it was part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire until 1918, passed to Romania, was returned to Hungary by the Second Vienna Award of 1940, and became Romanian again after World War II. Its Jewish community had deep roots in the Hasidic traditions of Hungarian and Galician Jewry.

The town's Jewish life was shaped by the contests between traditional Orthodoxy and the modernizing tendencies of Hungarian Jewry. In the urban centers of Hungary, the Neolog movement had considerable influence; in the Carpathian northeast, traditionalism dominated. Satu-Mare was a stronghold of the latter.

## Golden Age

Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum (1887–1979) became the defining figure of the Satmar community when he assumed the rabbinate of Satu-Mare in 1934. A scion of a distinguished Hasidic family (his father was the Sigheter Rebbe), Rabbi Yoel brought his formidable personality and learning to the task of creating an unbreachable fortress of traditional Judaism against all modernizing forces.

Rabbi Yoel's great theological work is *Vayoel Moshe* (1959), a dense halakhic and kabbalistic argument against Zionism. Drawing on a mystical interpretation of three oaths in the Talmud — that Israel would not "ascend like a wall," that they would not rebel against the nations, and that the nations would not oppress Israel excessively — Rabbi Yoel argued that the Zionist project constituted a fundamental transgression of divine will, that the establishment of a secular Jewish state was a rebellion against God's decree, and that it was therefore not only politically mistaken but spiritually catastrophic. The Holocaust, in this reading, was related to Zionism's transgression — an argument that placed Satmar theology in direct, bitter conflict with the mainstream of post-Holocaust Jewish thought.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Yoel Teitelbaum himself towers over the story. His survival is inseparable from the most debated episode in the history of Jewish rescue: the Kastner Train. In June 1944, as the deportations of Hungarian Jews to Auschwitz were proceeding at unprecedented speed, Rudolf (Rezsö) Kastner, a Zionist leader in Budapest, negotiated with SS-Obersturmbannführer Adolf Eichmann for the release of approximately 1,700 privileged Jews in exchange for material goods. The Satmar Rebbe was among those included on the train, which made its way to Switzerland in December 1944.

The inclusion of the virulently anti-Zionist Rebbe on a Zionist-organized rescue transport is deeply ironic. Rabbi Yoel never acknowledged any debt to Zionism for his rescue; his followers maintain that divine providence alone saved the Rebbe.

## Institutions & Synagogues

In Brooklyn's Williamsburg neighborhood, where Rabbi Yoel settled after the war, Satmar built an extraordinary communal infrastructure: yeshivot, mikvaot, kosher food establishments, welfare organizations, and eventually an independent postal system, summer camps, and schools serving tens of thousands. Today Satmar is also the largest presence in Kiryas Joel, a Satmar village in Orange County, New York, with approximately 35,000 residents — the most densely populated municipality in New York State.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Satmar Hasidism is characterized by its comprehensive rejection of modernity in religious matters. Television, internet, and most secular media are forbidden; the Yiddish language is the language of instruction in schools; dress is strictly controlled; interaction with the secular world is minimized. This comprehensive separatism, combined with extraordinarily high birth rates, has made Satmar not merely a surviving community but one of the fastest-growing in the Jewish world.

Paradoxically, Satmar is also deeply engaged with Israeli Jewish welfare through Hatzalah emergency services, medical charity organizations (Satmar runs one of the world's largest Jewish ambulance corps), and support for non-Zionist Israeli Orthodox communities.

## Decline & Dispersal

The original Satu-Mare community was almost completely destroyed in the 1944 deportations, sharing the fate of Sighet and the other Maramureș communities. Of the thousands of Jews who lived there, virtually none remained. The town in Romania has no significant Jewish population today.

## Legacy Today

Satmar is the largest Hasidic dynasty in the world, with tens of thousands of adherents in Brooklyn, Kiryas Joel, Montreal, London, Antwerp, and Jerusalem (where they maintain a significant anti-Zionist presence). The dynasty split in the 1990s and 2000s between followers of two of Rabbi Yoel's nephews, a conflict that has produced enormous legal battles and occasional violence but has not diminished the dynasty's overall growth.

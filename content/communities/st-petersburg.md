# St. Petersburg (Petrograd / Leningrad)

## Overview

St. Petersburg — renamed Petrograd during World War I and Leningrad under Soviet rule, now St. Petersburg again — was the imperial capital of Russia from 1712 to 1918, and its Jewish community reflected the strange duality at the heart of Russian Jewish life: a city formally outside the Pale of Settlement yet home to some of the most influential Jews in Russian history. The Jewish community of St. Petersburg was built by bankers, physicians, lawyers, and artists who had earned the right of residence through wealth, education, or service — and who parlayed that foothold into outsized influence in finance, journalism, politics, and culture.

## Historical Background

Jews were legally prohibited from residing in St. Petersburg throughout most of the Tsarist period unless they qualified for special-category exemptions. Despite this, a Jewish community grew steadily through the nineteenth century. The first-guild merchants — wealthy enough to engage in empire-wide trade — were the earliest large category. After 1859, university graduates and skilled craftsmen were added. By 1897, approximately 16,700 Jews lived in St. Petersburg; by 1910, the community had grown to roughly 35,000, with many more living in a quasi-legal gray zone.

## The House of Günzburg

The most important family in St. Petersburg Jewish history was the Günzburg (Günzburg/Gunzburg) dynasty. Baron Joseph Evzel Günzburg (1812–1878) established the Günzburg banking house in St. Petersburg and became the preeminent figure in Russian Jewish life — a "court Jew" in the most literal sense, with access to senior government officials and the ability to intervene on behalf of Jewish communities throughout the empire. His son Baron Horace de Günzburg (1833–1909) continued this role, funding the Oriental Studies program at St. Petersburg University, supporting Jewish scholarship, and serving as the de facto spokesman for Russian Jewry with the Tsarist government. The family's palace, their philanthropy, and their connections defined the character of the Jewish elite in the capital.

## Notable Figures

Leon Trotsky (Lev Bronstein, 1879–1940), who would become one of the central leaders of the 1917 revolution and the founder of the Red Army, spent his revolutionary years in and out of St. Petersburg. The historian Simon Dubnow lived in the city for a period and later described the unique character of its Jewish community. The poet Osip Mandelstam was born in Warsaw but came of age in St. Petersburg, where the acmeist literary circle shaped his development. Moise Ginzburg (1892–1946), one of the founders of Soviet Constructivist architecture, studied and worked in the city.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The Grand Choral Synagogue of St. Petersburg, completed in 1893 in Moorish Revival style on Lermontovsky Prospekt, remains one of the most architecturally magnificent synagogues in Europe. Its construction was approved by Tsar Alexander II and required sustained lobbying by the Günzburg family over decades. The synagogue served the entire spectrum of St. Petersburg Jewish life — from wealthy bankers to students and artisans. The community also maintained a Jewish hospital, schools, and charitable societies.

## The Jewish Intelligentsia

The Jewish intelligentsia of St. Petersburg was distinctive in its deep integration into Russian high culture while maintaining Jewish identity. The journal "Voskhod" (Sunrise), the most important Russian-language Jewish publication of the late nineteenth century, was published here. The Petersburg-based Society for the Promotion of Enlightenment among the Jews (OPE) funded Jewish education and scholarship across the empire. Jewish lawyers, journalists, and academics participated in the constitutional movement of 1905 and the brief liberal flowering of the Duma period.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Jewish community of St. Petersburg made contributions to Russian culture out of all proportion to its size. Jewish musicians were central to the St. Petersburg Conservatory from its earliest years. Anton Rubinstein (1829–1894), the great pianist and composer who founded the Conservatory, came from a Jewish family (though baptized). The "Society for Jewish Folk Music," established in St. Petersburg in 1908, systematically collected and composed Jewish music in a synthesis of traditional folk melodies and Western classical form — one of the most important projects in Jewish musical history.

## Decline & Dispersal

The revolution of 1917 brought contradictory effects: formal Jewish equality was declared, but the destruction of the kehilla system and religious institutions began almost immediately. The Stalinist purges of the 1930s fell heavily on Leningrad's Jewish intellectuals. The Nazi siege of Leningrad (1941–1944) trapped a significant Jewish population along with the rest of the city's inhabitants; some 50,000 Jews died during the 900-day siege.

## Legacy Today

St. Petersburg today has a significant Jewish community with a revitalized Grand Choral Synagogue. The Jewish Museum and Tolerance Center in Moscow covers national Jewish history, but St. Petersburg maintains its own communal institutions. The extraordinary legacy of the city's Jewish intelligentsia lives in Russian literature, music, and science.

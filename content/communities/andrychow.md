# Andrychów

## Overview

Andrychów is a town in the Lesser Poland (Małopolskie) region, approximately 40 kilometers southwest of Kraków in the foothills of the Carpathian Mountains. Located in the historic region of Galicia under Austrian rule from 1772 to 1918, Andrychów's Jews were part of the distinctive Galician Jewish civilization that blended Hasidic spirituality with modernizing commerce and industry. The town's textile economy drew Jewish artisans and merchants into the fabric of industrial development.

## Historical Background

Jews settled in Andrychów in the eighteenth century under Polish rule. The town was annexed by Austria in the First Partition of Poland (1772) and became part of Austrian Galicia. Austrian rule brought both restrictions and opportunities: the 1782 Edict of Tolerance granted Jews civil rights in exchange for cultural modernization, including compulsory secular education and German-language instruction in schools.

The Andrychów region developed as a textile manufacturing center in the nineteenth century. Jewish families participated in weaving, cloth finishing, and the merchant trades connected to the textile economy. The proximity to Kraków and to the railway network integrated the town into broader commercial networks.

## Golden Age

By the interwar period, when Andrychów was part of independent Poland, its Jewish population numbered approximately 1,000–1,500 people — comprising roughly 20–25 percent of the total population. The community maintained a full complement of institutions: a synagogue, a *beit midrash*, Hasidic prayer houses, a Jewish school, Zionist youth groups, and the political organizations that characterized interwar Polish Jewish life.

The Galician character of Andrychów's Jews was marked by a distinctive blend of traditional piety and modern cultural engagement. Hasidism was strong in this region — the Belzer, Bobover, and other dynasties maintained influence — while simultaneously Hebrew and Yiddish literature, Zionist activism, and secular education flourished. This dialectic defined Galician Jewish identity in its final generations.

## Notable Figures

Andrychów did not produce figures of international renown. Its rabbis, teachers, and community leaders — names preserved in local memorial literature — guided the community through the challenges of interwar Poland: economic discrimination, rising antisemitism, and the political instability of the period.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The main synagogue of Andrychów was a brick structure in the architectural style typical of Galician synagogues — a modest but dignified building on a central street in the Jewish quarter. Separate Hasidic *shtiblach* served the followers of various rebbes. A *mikveh*, a Jewish school, a library, and charitable institutions completed the communal infrastructure. The Jewish cemetery on the town's edge contained generations of burials.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Andrychów's contribution to Jewish culture was the contribution of the Galician small town: a synthesis of deep religious commitment and engagement with the modern world. Jewish merchants, artisans, and manufacturers participated in the region's industrial development while maintaining Jewish religious observance. The community's youth movements educated young Jews in Hebrew and Zionist ideals, preparing many for eventual emigration to Palestine.

## Decline & Dispersal

The German occupation of Poland in September 1939 brought immediate persecution. In 1940 a ghetto was established in Andrychów, concentrating the Jewish population under harsh conditions. The ghetto was liquidated in 1942 or 1943; residents were deported to the Bełżec extermination camp (some 90 kilometers to the southeast). Bełżec, operational from March to December 1942, killed approximately 434,500 Jews, overwhelmingly from Galicia. The Jewish community of Andrychów was annihilated.

## Legacy Today

Andrychów's synagogue building survived in altered form. The Jewish cemetery, damaged during the war, has been partially restored. A memorial plaque commemorates the Jewish community. Descendants of Andrychów Jews settled in Israel and the Americas. Yad Vashem's Pages of Testimony preserve the names of many victims.

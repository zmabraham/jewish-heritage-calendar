# Tulchin

## Overview

Tulchin — today in the Vinnytsia Oblast of central Ukraine — was a Podolian town that became legendary in Jewish historical memory for the heroic martyrdom of its Jewish leadership during the Cossack uprising of 1648. Unlike other communities that perished in the chaos of that year, the Jews of Tulchin made a fateful choice: rather than betray their Polish neighbors to the Cossack forces, the Jewish leadership chose collective martyrdom, an act of solidarity that became one of the most frequently cited stories in Ashkenazi memory.

## Historical Background

Tulchin was a fortified town in Podolia under the rule of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. Its Jewish community, dating to the sixteenth century, was typical of Podolian kehillos: engaged in trade, crafts, leaseholding, and serving as economic intermediaries between the Polish nobility (szlachta), the Ukrainian peasantry, and the regional economy. The town's Polish garrison and fortifications gave Tulchin strategic importance.

## The 1648 Cossack Uprising

The great Cossack uprising led by Bohdan Khmelnytsky in 1648 — known in Jewish sources as "Gezeires Tach V'Tat" (the calamities of 1648–1649) — swept across Ukraine and eastern Poland, destroying hundreds of Jewish communities. Tulchin was one of the towns that faced the onslaught. The Cossack forces laid siege to the Polish garrison, demanding that the Polish defenders surrender. The Polish defenders, desperate and surrounded, extracted from the Cossacks safe passage for themselves and the Jewish residents who remained within the town walls.

According to the Jewish chronicles of the period — particularly "Megillat Eifa" and other contemporary accounts — the Jewish leaders of Tulchin gathered to make an extraordinary decision. The Cossacks had demanded that the Jews open the town gates and allow the Cossack forces to enter, which would mean the massacre of the Polish defenders. The Jewish leadership refused. They would not, even under threat of death, betray their Polish neighbors to the Cossack besiegers.

The chronicles record that the Jewish community of Tulchin — led by its rabbi, notable families, and respected figures — chose instead collective martyrdom. Rather than open the gates, they faced death together with the Polish garrison. The details vary between accounts, but the core narrative — that Tulchin's Jews refused to betray their Polish neighbors and chose martyrdom — became fixed in Jewish memory.

## Notable Figures

The Jewish martyrs of Tulchin are remembered collectively rather than individually. Their names are not recorded in the sources, but their decision became one of the archetypal stories of Jewish martyrdom (Kiddush Hashem) in Ashkenazi consciousness. The story was retold in memorial literature, laments, and historical chronicles for centuries.

## Institutions & Synagogues

Tulchin had the standard institutions of a mid-size Podolian kehilla: a synagogue, study house, cemetery, and charitable societies. The community rebuilt after 1648 and maintained Jewish life through the subsequent centuries of Polish, Russian, and eventually Soviet rule.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

The Tulchin story contributed significantly to the Jewish concept of martyrdom as an act of solidarity and refusal to collaborate with injustice. The memory of Tulchin was invoked in subsequent generations when Jews faced forced choices between collaboration with oppressive regimes and resistance. The story's emphasis on Jewish-Polish solidarity — however ambiguous this relationship was in practice — represents one of the relatively few positive memories of Jewish-Polish relations in Ashkenazi memory.

## Decline & Dispersal

The community rebuilt after 1648 but suffered repeatedly in the cycles of violence that affected Ukrainian Jewry. World War I, the Russian Revolution, and the civil war period (1918–1921) brought upheaval. The Nazi occupation of 1941–1944 saw the murder of the remaining Jewish population.

## Legacy Today

Tulchin today is a small Ukrainian town. The memory of the 1648 martyrdom is preserved in Jewish historical literature. The story continues to be cited in discussions of Jewish ethics under persecution.

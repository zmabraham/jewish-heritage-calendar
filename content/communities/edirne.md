# Edirne

## Overview

Edirne, the ancient Thracian city known to the Roman world as Hadrianopolis and to the Ottoman world as Adrianople, holds a special place in Sephardic history as the first major Ottoman city to receive Jewish refugees from the Iberian Peninsula — a generation before the main 1492 influx, Edirne's established Jewish community welcomed Sephardim fleeing the pogroms of 1391 and the persecutions of the early fifteenth century. As the Ottoman capital before the conquest of Constantinople, Edirne was where the pattern of Ottoman Jewish hospitality was established, and its Jewish community flourished under the generous terms offered by the sultans who held court there.

## Historical Origins

Jews lived in Adrianopolis through the Byzantine period, and the Ottoman conquest of the city in 1369 brought it under the same relatively tolerant Islamic governance that characterized the early Ottoman state. Sultan Murad I and his successors maintained Jewish communities as useful subjects, and the city's position as the Ottoman capital (before 1453) gave its Jewish community particular importance.

The first significant Sephardic arrivals came after the 1391 pogroms and subsequent Iberian persecutions, settling in Edirne before the main 1492 wave. These early settlers established Sephardic congregations that welcomed and integrated the much larger 1492 cohort.

## Golden Age

Edirne's golden age as a Jewish center corresponds to the period of Ottoman expansion and consolidation in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. The community produced important Talmudic scholars and maintained a significant yeshiva. The city's proximity to Istanbul, once that became the Ottoman capital, kept Edirne in the orbit of the central Jewish community.

Rabbi Joseph Karo spent time in Edirne as part of his broader trajectory through the Ottoman Sephardic world. The city's scholars maintained connections with the great centers of Salonika and Istanbul.

## Notable Figures

Rabbi Moses Almosnino (c. 1515–1580) worked in Edirne and Salonika, combining remarkable halakhic learning with a humanistic engagement with secular culture. He composed works in Ladino as well as Hebrew, contributed to philosophy and rhetoric, and served as a communal diplomat to the Ottoman court.

The community produced other scholars of note, though no single figure achieved the international standing of the great Salonika or Istanbul rabbis.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Edirne's Jewish quarter was substantial. The community maintained multiple synagogues, of which the Great Synagogue (Büyük Sinagog) is the most significant surviving monument. Built in 1907 and capable of holding 900 worshippers, it was the largest synagogue in the Balkans and the second largest in Europe at its construction. After decades of disuse and deterioration, the synagogue underwent major restoration completed in 2015, and its reopening was a significant event in Turkish-Jewish heritage.

## Cultural Life

Edirne's Jewish culture was Sephardic and Ladino-speaking, with the typical mixture of Iberian nostalgia and Ottoman accommodation that characterized the Sephardic world. The community was commercially active in the textile and grain trades and maintained a dignified scholarly tradition.

## Decline & Transformation

The twentieth century brought catastrophic decline to Edirne's Jewish community. The Balkan Wars (1912–1913), World War I, population exchanges, and the economic disruptions of the early Turkish Republic all contributed to emigration. The 1934 anti-Jewish violence in Thrace, which drove thousands from their homes in a single weekend, devastated the community. Most survivors emigrated to Istanbul or abroad.

## Legacy & Diaspora

The restored Great Synagogue of Edirne stands as a monument to the city's extraordinary Jewish past and to the possibility of heritage preservation. Edirne's role as the first major Sephardic refuge point gives it a foundational place in the history of the Ottoman Jewish experience.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Byzantine era; Sephardic community from late 14th century; peak 16th–17th centuries
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 15,000–20,000 (late 19th century)
- **Languages**: Ladino, Hebrew, Ottoman Turkish
- **Status Today**: Tiny community; Great Synagogue (1907) restored 2015 and open as heritage site

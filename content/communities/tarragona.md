# Tarragona

## Overview

Tarragona, the ancient Roman capital of Hispania Tarraconensis on the Catalan coast, claimed one of the oldest Jewish communities in the Iberian Peninsula by virtue of its Roman heritage and its role as the seat of the most powerful archbishopric in Catalonia. The community's history is characterized by both privilege and vulnerability: closely tied to royal Aragonese protection but exposed to ecclesiastical hostility, Tarragona's Jews maintained a significant presence through the medieval centuries before the catastrophes of 1391 and 1492.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Tarraco dates to the Roman period with considerable plausibility, given the city's importance as a Roman provincial capital and the well-documented presence of Jews throughout the Roman Mediterranean world. Epigraphic evidence from the Roman period has been found in the region, though dating it precisely is difficult.

Through the Visigothic period, Tarragona was an important ecclesiastical center, and its Jews would have been subject to the anti-Jewish legislation of the Visigothic councils. The Arab conquest brought improved conditions; the Catalan reconquest integrated Tarragona into the County of Barcelona's orbit.

## Golden Age

Tarragona's Jewish community flourished particularly in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, benefiting from Aragonese royal protection while navigating the proximity of the archbishop's court. The community's scholars contributed to the broader Catalan Jewish intellectual tradition, maintaining connections to Barcelona and Girona.

The Jewish quarter, the Call, was a defined area of the old city. Commercial activity centered on trade and crafts, with Jewish merchants participating in Tarragona's maritime commerce.

## Notable Figures

Tarragona's most notable Jewish contribution was collective rather than individual. The community's scholars participated in the broader Catalan Jewish intellectual tradition, corresponding with figures like Nahmanides and the Rashba.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Tarragona's Call was located near the cathedral in the historic upper city. The community maintained a synagogue and communal institutions. The proximity to the archbishop's seat created a particular institutional tension that shaped the community's legal history.

Archaeological work in Tarragona has revealed remains of the Jewish quarter, and the city's Roman antiquities include material that may have Jewish associations.

## Cultural Life

Tarragona's Jewish culture was Catalan, participating in the liturgical and legal traditions of the broader Catalan Jewish world. The city's Roman heritage created an environment where ancient monuments and living traditions coexisted — a characteristic of Tarragona that shaped both Christian and Jewish self-understanding.

## Decline & Transformation

The 1391 pogroms struck Tarragona along with the rest of Catalonia. The community was attacked and significantly reduced through forced conversions. The reconstituted community lasted until 1492, when the final expulsion ended Jewish life in the city.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Tarragona's Roman Jewish heritage gives the city a claim to one of the oldest continuously documented Jewish presences in Western Europe, even if the evidence is fragmentary. The city's heritage documentation has grown in recent years, acknowledging this ancient stratum of Jewish history.

## Key Facts
- **Founded**: Roman era (probable); established medieval community from 10th century
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 500–1,200 (13th century)
- **Languages**: Catalan, Hebrew, Latin
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; Roman-era and medieval heritage documented

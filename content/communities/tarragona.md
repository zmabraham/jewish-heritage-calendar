# Tarragona

## Overview

Tarragona, the ancient Roman capital of Hispania Tarraconensis on the Catalan coast, claims one of the oldest and most historically layered Jewish communities in the Iberian Peninsula. The medieval Arab geographer **Muhammad al-Idrisi** (1152) called it *al-Madinah al-Yahud* — "the city of the Jews" — a designation applied to only three cities in his account of Iberia, alongside Granada and Lucena. That sobriquet captures something real: Tarragona's Jewish presence, rooted in the Roman era and documented through the Visigothic, Arab, and medieval Catalan periods, represents a continuity of Jewish habitation in one place across more than a millennium. The community's history is characterized by both privilege and vulnerability — closely tied to Aragonese royal protection, but exposed to the hostility of the powerful archbishopric that made Tarragona its seat.

## Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Tarraco dates to the Roman period. Physical evidence survives in the form of a **trilingual inscription** in Hebrew, Latin, and Greek on a child's sarcophagus found in Tarragona, dated to the era of the Roman Empire and now displayed at the Sephardic Museum in Toledo — considered among the oldest physical evidence of Jewish presence in Spain. A **laver** inscribed "Peace over Israel, over ourselves, and over our children" also likely dates to the Roman period. A second trilingual Jewish inscription from Tarragona and one from Tortosa have been dated between the 2nd century BCE and the 6th century CE, confirming a substantial and organized Roman-era community.

Through the Visigothic period, Tarragona was an important ecclesiastical center, and its Jews were subject to the famously harsh anti-Jewish legislation of the Visigothic councils. Coins with Hebrew inscriptions from this period have been found in the region. The Arab conquest of 714 brought improved conditions for the Jewish population. When the Count of Barcelona reconquered the city and integrated it into the Catalan orbit, Jewish rights were formally recognized and the community began its medieval flourishing.

## Golden Age

By **1239**, the Jewish quarter — *El Call* (from Catalan for "lane" or "street of the Jewish quarter") — contained **95 houses**, established during the reign of Jaime I of Aragon. The community elected its own administrators and maintained tax arrangements parallel to those of Barcelona. *El Call* included a **synagogue**, **school**, **baths**, and **market**, centered around what is now Plaça dels Àngels. It had its own gate to the area beyond the city walls — the *Portella dels Jueus* (Gate of the Jews), a cyclopean construction still partially identifiable in the surviving medieval fabric.

Economic activities centered on commerce, industry, and brokerage, with Jewish merchants participating in Tarragona's maritime trade. The community maintained connections with the major Catalan Jewish centers of Barcelona and Girona, and its scholars corresponded with the great Sephardic authorities of the period.

## Notable Figures

**Isaac Arama** (c. 1420–1494) — one of the most important Jewish philosophers of the late medieval period — served as rabbi in Tarragona in the second half of the fifteenth century, where he maintained a yeshiva and fostered observance and learning. He is best known as the author of *Akedat Yitzhak* ("The Binding of Isaac"), a major philosophical-homiletical commentary on the Torah in 105 chapters that synthesized Aristotelian philosophy with traditional Jewish thought. The work remained influential for centuries and is still studied today. That so significant a figure served in Tarragona testifies to the community's continued vitality even as it approached its final catastrophe.

Other documented figures include **Hayyim b. Isaac**, known from a tombstone dated Nisan 1300; **Hananiah b. Simeon**, known from a 1302 tombstone; and **Samuel Benveniste**, a fourteenth-century physician and translator who contributed to medieval Catalan scientific culture. The community physician **Benjamin** is known by name from municipal records.

The Sephardic surname **Taragano** — carried by Jewish families across Europe, North Africa, and the Ottoman Empire — almost certainly derives from Tarragona, and represents the community's invisible continuation in the diaspora.

## Institutions & Sacred Spaces

*El Call* of Tarragona was a compact but well-organized community in the upper (Roman) city, near the cathedral. The layout followed the pattern of medieval Catalan Jewish quarters: narrow streets, a central synagogue, communal institutions opening onto small squares, with a gate controlling access to the broader city.

Physical remains — including the two dated tombstones — were preserved in the Diocesan Museum, the Praetorian Tower, and the College of Industrial Engineers. The building locally known as *ca la Garsa* may have Jewish origins. Modern urban demolitions over the centuries have been unkind to the Call; only a handful of streets and isolated Gothic arches survive of the once-substantial quarter.

## Cultural Life

Tarragona's Jewish culture was thoroughly Catalan, participating in the liturgical and legal traditions of the broader Catalan Jewish world. The city's ancient Roman heritage gave it a particular quality: Tarragona was a city where the weight of history — the aqueduct, the amphitheater, the forum — was palpable in daily life, and this coexistence of ancient monuments and living medieval communities shaped both Christian and Jewish self-understanding. The trilingual inscriptions of the Roman Jewish community (Hebrew, Latin, Greek) are emblematic of a culture always comfortable navigating multiple linguistic and cultural worlds.

## Decline & Transformation

The community suffered repeated attacks from ecclesiastical authority even before the mass violence of 1391. In **1313**, the Archbishop of Tarragona and the Inquisition confiscated one of the synagogues and converted it into a church. In **1322**, the archbishop confiscated Jewish property more broadly. In **1348**, in the violence accompanying the Black Death, approximately **300 Jews were killed** in Tarragona — a devastating blow. Queen Violante subsequently authorized Tarragona's surviving Jews to raise funds across other Catalan communities to rebuild a synagogue, purchase a Torah scroll and books, and redeem the cemetery.

The **1391 pogroms** struck Tarragona as part of the wider catastrophe that swept Catalonia and Aragon. The community was attacked and significantly reduced through killings and forced conversions. Tarragona's reconstituted post-1391 community survived in diminished form until **1492**, when the final expulsion ended Jewish life in the city. In a final historical irony, Tarragona served as one of the **ports of embarkation** for Jewish exiles from the Kingdom of Aragon — the ancient city that had been home to Jews for over a millennium was now the gateway through which they left forever.

## Legacy & Diaspora

Tarragona's claim to one of the oldest Jewish communities in Western Europe is supported by physical evidence — Roman-era inscriptions — that few other Iberian communities can match. The continuity from the Roman period through the medieval centuries represents a remarkable thread of Jewish presence in a single place across more than a thousand years.

The surname Taragano, carried by Sephardic families across the diaspora, preserves Tarragona's name in living form. Heritage documentation has grown in recent years, with recognition of both the Roman stratum and the medieval *Call* as part of Spain's Jewish heritage patrimony.

## Key Facts
- **Earliest evidence**: Roman era (2nd c. BCE–6th c. CE trilingual inscriptions)
- **Medieval quarter**: *El Call*, 95 houses by 1239
- **Named by al-Idrisi (1152)**: *al-Madinah al-Yahud* — "the city of the Jews"
- **Notable Figure**: Rabbi Isaac Arama (c. 1420–1494), author of *Akedat Yitzhak*
- **1348 massacre**: approximately 300 Jews killed
- **Port of embarkation**: 1492 exiles departed through Tarragona
- **Peak Population**: Estimated 500–1,200 (13th century)
- **Languages**: Catalan, Hebrew, Latin, Greek
- **Status Today**: No Jewish community; Roman and medieval heritage documented; diaspora surname *Taragano* persists

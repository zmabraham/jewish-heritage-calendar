# Dinov (Dynów)

## Overview

Dynów — known in Jewish tradition as Dinov — is a small town in the Subcarpathian region of southeastern Poland, historically part of Galicia, situated on the San River. Its Jewish community is remembered primarily through the figure of Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech Shapira of Dinov (1783–1841), the author of the Benei Yissaschar, one of the most studied works of Kabbalistic-Hasidic literature. Though the town itself was modest in size, its contribution to the Jewish library and to the mystical theology of the Hasidic movement is immense.

## Historical Background

Jews settled in Dynów in the seventeenth century as Galician Jewry spread through the smaller towns and villages of the Carpathian foothills. The community occupied a marginal but stable position in the regional economy, dealing in lumber from the abundant Carpathian forests, agricultural products, and local trade. Under Austrian Habsburgs after 1772, Dynów Jews participated in the modernizing pressures of Josephinian reform while maintaining strong attachment to traditional life and the emerging Hasidic movement.

The region of Subcarpathian Galicia became fertile ground for Hasidism in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, with multiple rabbinic courts establishing themselves in the mountain towns. Dynów gained its place on this map through the arrival and settlement of Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech.

## Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech Shapira of Dinov

Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech Shapira (1783–1841) was born in Dynów and served there as the town's rebbe from approximately 1820 until his death. He had been a disciple of some of the greatest figures of Polish-Galician Hasidism, including Rabbi Menachem Mendel of Rimanov, Rabbi Abraham Joshua Heschel of Apt (the Apter Rebbe), and the Seer of Lublin. This remarkable chain of discipleship placed him at the confluence of the major currents of early Polish Hasidism.

Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech was a prolific author of rare intellectual depth. His works combined rigorous Kabbalistic learning — particularly the Lurianic Kabbalah of Rabbi Isaac Luria — with Hasidic theology, halakhic discussion, and ethical instruction. He was equally at home in the mystical and the practical dimensions of Jewish religious life.

## The Benei Yissaschar

The Benei Yissaschar (Sons of Issachar), first published in Zolkiew in 1846 (posthumously), is his masterwork and one of the classics of Hasidic-Kabbalistic literature. Organized by the months of the Jewish calendar, each section discusses the mystical and religious significance of that month, its holidays and special Shabbatot, and the spiritual work appropriate to each season. The work is remarkable for the density of its Kabbalistic learning, drawn from the Zohar, Lurianic writings, and Hasidic masters, woven together with narrative, halakha, and devotional teaching.

The Benei Yissaschar has never gone out of print and is studied intensively in Hasidic circles worldwide, particularly in the weeks preceding major holidays when its relevant sections serve as devotional and educational preparation. It has become a standard reference work for understanding the inner meaning of the Jewish year in Hasidic practice.

Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech also wrote Derech Pikudecha (The Way of Your Commandments), a systematic analysis of the 613 commandments from a Kabbalistic perspective; Bnei Yissaschar Al HaTorah, a Torah commentary; Agra DePirka; and several other works. His total literary output was exceptional for a rebbe in a small Galician town.

## Notable Figures and the Dynów Dynasty

The Dynów dynasty continued after Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech's death. His descendants maintained the court through the nineteenth and into the twentieth century. Various branches of the Shapira family extended the Dinov legacy to other Galician towns; the Munkáč (Mukachevo) dynasty, one of the most influential Hasidic courts of interwar Hungary and Carpatho-Ruthenia, had connections to the Dinov-Shapira lineage. Rabbi Chaim Elazar Spira of Munkáč (1871–1937), one of the most forceful anti-Zionist voices of the twentieth century, represented a related branch of this tradition.

## Institutions & Synagogues

The synagogue and beit midrash of Dynów served as gathering places for the rebbe's disciples and the broader community. The town's Jewish institutions — cheder, yeshiva, charitable societies, and burial organization (chevra kadisha) — functioned through the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. By the interwar period, political modernization had touched even Dynów: Zionist organizations operated alongside traditional institutions.

## Cultural & Religious Contributions

Rabbi Tzvi Elimelech's synthesis of Kabbalah and Hasidism in an accessible, calendar-structured format was his lasting contribution to Jewish religious culture. The Benei Yissaschar demonstrated that the Kabbalistic tradition's understanding of sacred time — the idea that different periods of the year carry different spiritual energies and require different modes of divine service — could be communicated not only to scholars but to any educated Hasidic reader.

His emphasis on the mystical dimension of Jewish holidays — showing how each festival is not merely a historical commemoration but a recurrence of cosmic spiritual events — deepened popular Hasidic understanding of the Jewish year.

## Decline & Dispersal

The German occupation of Dynów in September 1939 brought immediate persecution. By 1942, the town's approximately 1,500 Jews were concentrated in a ghetto and deported to the Bełżec extermination camp. Virtually none survived. The synagogue and beit midrash were destroyed.

## Legacy Today

The Benei Yissaschar ensures that the name of Dinov resonates in every Hasidic home and study hall where the Jewish calendar is studied with mystical intention. Multiple modern editions of the work — with Hebrew commentary, English translation, and digital formats — have extended its reach to new generations. A small monument marks the site of the Dynów Jewish community's destruction.

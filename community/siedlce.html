March 28, 2026 — Siedlce Jewish Heritage Day

Siedlce

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Poland
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Siedlce was a major city in the Podlaskie region of eastern Poland whose Jewish community — roughly half the city's population in the interwar period — suffered one of the most brutal pogroms in Congress Poland in 1906 and was ultimately annihilated in the Holocaust. The 1906 Siedlce pogrom, occurring during the revolutionary upheaval of 1905–1907, was documented by Jewish and Polish witnesses and stands as a grim chapter in the history of Polish-Jewish relations.

Historical Origins

Jews settled in Siedlce in the eighteenth century as the city developed under the noble Czartoryski family, who owned the town and encouraged Jewish settlement to stimulate commerce. The Jewish community grew through the nineteenth century as Siedlce developed into a regional market and administrative center under Russian rule. By the late nineteenth century Jews comprised roughly half the city's population of approximately 30,000–40,000 residents.

The city's position on the railway line connecting Warsaw to Brest-Litovsk and Moscow made it a significant commercial hub. Jewish merchants, traders, and artisans participated in this regional commerce, connecting the city's markets to Warsaw and the broader Russian Empire.

Golden Age

The most infamous event in Siedlce's Jewish history occurred on September 7–8, 1906, during the period of revolutionary unrest that shook the Russian Empire in 1905–1907. Following a series of strikes, protests, and occasional terrorist actions by Polish and Jewish socialist activists, tensions in the city were high. When a group of police officers or soldiers was reportedly attacked or killed in August 1906, the authorities scapegoated the Jewish population.

On September 7, troops and local police — with the participation of some Polish civilians — launched a two-day pogrom against Jewish residents. Jews were beaten, raped, and murdered in the streets; Jewish property was looted and burned. The violence was systematically abetted by authorities; police were reported to have directed rioters to Jewish homes and businesses. Contemporary accounts, documented in Jewish press reports and diplomatic dispatches, estimated approximately 30 Jews killed, dozens of Jewish women raped, and hundreds injured. The pogrom shocked Jewish communities across Poland and Russia and became a reference point in Jewish memory of government-facilitated antisemitic violence.

In the interwar period (1918–1939) Siedlce's Jewish community rebuilt and numbered approximately 15,000–18,000, roughly half the city's total population. The community maintained the full complement of institutions: several synagogues including the Great Synagogue, multiple batei midrash, Hasidic prayer houses, Jewish schools (both traditional cheder and modern schools), Zionist youth organizations across the spectrum, the Bund, Agudath Israel, and a rich cultural life of theater, music, and Yiddish press.

The memory of 1906 shaped the community's consciousness of vulnerability and contributed to its support for self-defense organizations and for political movements that promised protection of Jewish rights.

The Great Synagogue of Siedlce was a grand brick structure, reflecting the community's size and importance. It was destroyed by German forces in 1939. Multiple Hasidic shtiblach served followers of the Gerer, Aleksander, and other Polish Hasidic dynasties. A yeshiva educated advanced students. Jewish schools included both traditional religious schools and modern secular-Yiddish or Hebrew institutions.

Notable Figures

  • Rabbi Yitzchak Zilberberg: served as rabbi of Siedlce in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
  • Jewish activists in the socialist movements — the Bund and Poalei Zion — were particularly strong in Siedlce given the city's working-class character and the 1905–1907 revolutionary context.

Cultural Life

Beyond the memory of the 1906 pogrom, Siedlce's Jewish community contributed to the broader culture of Podlasie: a region where Polish, Belarusian, and Jewish cultures overlapped and interacted. Jewish merchants and manufacturers participated in the regional economy; Jewish schools educated generations of children; the community's press and cultural life contributed to the Yiddish and Hebrew literary world.

Decline & Transformation

German forces entered Siedlce in September 1939. The Jewish population was subjected to persecution, property confiscation, and forced labor. A ghetto was established in 1940 and sealed in 1941. Conditions in the ghetto deteriorated severely through 1941–1942. In August 1942 the Siedlce ghetto was liquidated: approximately 15,000 Jews were transported to Treblinka and murdered. The community was annihilated.

Legacy & Diaspora

A memorial at the site of the Great Synagogue commemorates the Jewish community of Siedlce. The Jewish cemetery, though damaged, contains some surviving gravestones. A yizkor book, Sefer Siedlce, was compiled by survivors and their descendants. The 1906 pogrom is documented in historical studies of Polish antisemitism.

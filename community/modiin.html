March 6, 2026 — Modi'in Jewish Heritage Day

Modi'in

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Israel
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Modi'in holds a place of honor in Jewish national memory as the birthplace of the Maccabean revolt — the uprising that gave the world Hanukkah and preserved Judaism at a moment when it faced existential cultural annihilation. It was here, in 167 BCE, that the aged priest Mattathias and his five sons defied the Seleucid Greek king Antiochus IV Epiphanes, refused to sacrifice to pagan gods, and raised the banner of revolt against Hellenistic religious coercion. From Modi'in, the Maccabees launched the guerrilla campaign that would cleanse the Jerusalem Temple, rededicate it, and establish the Hasmonean dynasty that ruled Judea for a century. The family's ancestral tomb at Modi'in became a pilgrimage destination that drew visitors for centuries after.

Historical Origins

Modi'in is located in the foothills (Shephelah) between the coastal plain and the Judean highlands, near the main route connecting Jerusalem to the Mediterranean coast. In the 2nd century BCE, it was a modest priestly village, home to the Hasmonean family — a clan of priests (kohanim) of the Jehoiarib course (one of the twenty-four priestly divisions that served in rotation in the Jerusalem Temple).

The crisis that sparked the revolt was the Seleucid king Antiochus IV Epiphanes's decision, following a political and financial crisis in his relationship with the Jerusalem Temple establishment, to plunder the Temple, outlaw Jewish practice (Shabbat, circumcision, Torah study), and install a pagan altar in the Temple itself — the "abomination of desolation" (shikutz meshomem) mentioned in Daniel. Seleucid officials were sent to towns throughout Judea to enforce compliance.

When a royal officer arrived at Modi'in to compel the town's residents to offer pagan sacrifice, Mattathias publicly refused: "Though all the nations that are under the king's dominion obey him... yet will I and my sons and my brothers walk in the covenant of our fathers" (1 Maccabees 2:19–20). When a Jew stepped forward to sacrifice on the pagan altar, Mattathias killed him, killed the royal officer, tore down the altar, and fled with his sons to the Judean hills.

Golden Age

The Hasmonean period (167–63 BCE) represents Modi'in's golden age in terms of historical significance. From the hills above Modi'in, Judah Maccabee (Mattathias's son, surnamed "the hammer") conducted the brilliant guerrilla campaign that drove Seleucid forces from Jerusalem, recaptured the Temple, and rededicated it — an event commemorated annually as Hanukkah (the 25th of Kislev, 165 BCE). The Hasmonean state that emerged ultimately controlled all of ancient Israel from the Galilee to the Negev.

The Hasmonean family maintained deep connections to their ancestral town. Simon Maccabee, the last surviving brother, built a magnificent tomb at Modi'in for his parents and brothers — a white marble monument described in 1 Maccabees as having seven pyramids (one for each parent and five brothers), visible from the sea. This tomb became a pilgrimage site.

The primary institution associated with ancient Modi'in is the Hasmonean Tomb, the monumental funerary complex built by Simon Maccabee for his family. The exact location of this tomb has been a subject of debate and archaeological investigation. Byzantine and Crusader-era pilgrims recorded visiting "tombs of the Maccabees" in the Modi'in region.

The site of Horvat Midras nearby contains extensive rock-cut tomb complexes from the Second Temple period, and some scholars have proposed identifying it with the Hasmonean tomb, though the identification remains disputed. The modern city of Modi'in Maccabim-Reut, established in 1993, was deliberately named to evoke the Hasmonean legacy.

Notable Figures

Mattathias ben Yochanan (died c. 166 BCE), the patriarch of the Maccabean revolt, was a Hasmonean priest who chose defiance over survival. His deathbed speech to his sons (1 Maccabees 2:49–68), evoking Abraham, Joseph, Phinehas, Joshua, Caleb, David, and Elijah as models of faithfulness, became one of the great documents of Jewish patriotic literature.

Judah Maccabee (died 160 BCE), the third of Mattathias's five sons, was the military genius of the revolt. His victories at Emmaus, Beth Zur, and Jerusalem against vastly superior Seleucid forces have been studied as classics of guerrilla warfare. He is the central hero of both books of Maccabees and of the Hanukkah story. His surname "Maccabee" may derive from the Hebrew word for "hammer" (makkavet) or from the Hebrew initials of the verse "Who is like you among the gods, O Lord?" (Mi Khamokha Ba'eilim Adonai).

Simon Maccabee (died 134 BCE), the last surviving Maccabean brother, became both high priest and political leader of Judea, founding the Hasmonean dynasty that ruled until the Roman conquest under Pompey. He built the family tomb at Modi'in and maintained the ancestral connection to the family's origin.

John Hyrcanus I (reigned 134–104 BCE), Simon's son, consolidated Hasmonean rule and expanded the kingdom significantly, including the conquest of Idumea (Edom) and the forced conversion of its population — a decision with long-term consequences, as one of the Idumean converts' descendants was Herod the Great.

Cultural Life

The Maccabean revolt and the festival of Hanukkah it produced represent one of Judaism's most important cultural and theological contributions. Hanukkah's story — the rededication of the Temple, the miracle of the oil that burned for eight days when there was only enough for one, the resistance of the few against the many — became a universal symbol of religious freedom and the preservation of cultural identity against assimilation pressure.

The rabbinic transformation of Hanukkah's meaning — shifting emphasis from military victory (the Maccabee's triumph) to the miraculous oil (divine providence) — reflects the rabbis' ambivalence about glorifying the Hasmoneans, who had overreached by combining priestly and royal power. The Talmud's question "What is Hanukkah?" (Shabbat 21b) answers primarily with the miracle of the oil, not the military victory.

Modi'in thus stands at the intersection of Jewish military heroism and religious miracle — a complex legacy that Jews have interpreted differently across centuries.

Decline & Transformation

The Hasmonean dynasty ended with the Roman conquest of Pompey in 63 BCE and the subsequent rise of the Idumean Herod. The town of Modi'in continued as a Jewish village into the Roman period but lost its political significance. Byzantine pilgrims visited the site for its Maccabean associations.

Legacy & Diaspora

The modern city of Modi'in Maccabim-Reut (population approximately 100,000) was established in 1993 and is Israel's newest major city, built between Jerusalem and Tel Aviv. It is explicitly named for the Hasmonean legacy, and the International Torch Relay for Hanukkah begins at a site near Modi'in each year, with torches carried from there to communities throughout Israel and abroad. The torch relay explicitly evokes the fire of Mattathias's revolt. Modi'in is also the starting point of the annual Maccabiah Games torch relay, connecting the ancient Maccabees to the modern Jewish Olympic movement.

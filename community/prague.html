Prague

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Czech Republic
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Prague's Jewish community is among the oldest and most celebrated in European history, stretching back over a thousand years. Nestled within the city's ancient Jewish Quarter — Josefov — this kehilla produced towering scholars, mystical legends, and a cultural legacy that shaped Jewish civilization. From the Altneuschul's gothic spires to the tombstones layered upon one another in the Old Jewish Cemetery, Prague is a city where Jewish memory literally rises from the ground.

Historical Origins

Jews are attested in Prague as early as the tenth century, with a substantial community established by the eleventh century. Medieval Prague Jews lived under the protection — and periodic persecution — of Bohemian kings. The community endured repeated pogroms, most devastatingly in 1389 when thousands were massacred on Easter Sunday. Yet the community rebuilt again and again, sustained by its deep learning and communal solidarity.

Under the Habsburg emperors, Bohemia's Jews occupied a complex legal position. They were confined to the Jewish Town (later Josefov, named for Emperor Joseph II), subject to special taxes, residence restrictions, and periodic expulsion orders — most famously under Empress Maria Theresa, who expelled Prague's Jews in 1744 before allowing their return in 1748. Emperor Joseph II's Toleranzpatent of 1782 brought partial emancipation, requiring Jews to adopt German surnames, attend secular schools, and serve in the military, while dismantling some of the worst legal restrictions.

Golden Age

The late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries represent Prague Jewry's greatest flourishing. Under the tolerant rule of Emperor Rudolf II, who made Prague his imperial capital, the Jewish community thrived culturally and commercially. Rabbi Judah Loew ben Bezalel — the Maharal (c. 1520–1609) — dominated this era. A towering talmudist and kabbalist, the Maharal founded the famous study of Jewish philosophy and mysticism that gave Prague its unique intellectual character. He reportedly met with Emperor Rudolf II himself, a meeting that became legendary.

The Maharal's student Rabbi David Gans (1541–1613) was a polymath who wrote both Jewish historical chronicles (Tzemach David) and astronomical works in collaboration with Tycho Brahe and Johannes Kepler — a remarkable example of Jewish engagement with Renaissance science.

The Altneuschul (Old-New Synagogue), built around 1270, is the oldest surviving synagogue in Europe still in active use. Its Gothic brick architecture, with the distinctive steep roof and attic said to contain the remnants of the first Golem, makes it a pilgrimage site for Jews worldwide. The Pinkas Synagogue, founded in 1535, was transformed after the Shoah into a memorial inscribed with the names of 77,297 Bohemian and Moravian Jewish victims. The Maisel Synagogue, Spanish Synagogue, and High Synagogue complete the remarkable cluster of historic houses of worship in Josefov. The Old Jewish Cemetery, in use from the early fifteenth century through 1787, contains some 12,000 tombstones stacked in layers — a haunting record of a community pressed for space even in death.

Notable Figures

The Golem legend, inseparable from the Maharal, tells of a clay man animated by the sacred name of God, created to protect Prague's Jews from blood libel accusations. Whether historical kernel or pure legend, the Golem became one of the most potent symbols of Jewish self-defense and Diaspora anxiety, later inspiring Mary Shelley, Gustav Meyrink, and modern science fiction.

Rabbi David Oppenheimer (1664–1736) assembled one of the great private libraries of Jewish manuscripts and books, now housed at the Bodleian Library in Oxford. Mordechai Meisel, the immensely wealthy financier and philanthropist of Rudolf II's court, funded the construction of several synagogues and the paving of the Jewish Quarter's streets.

Franz Kafka (1883–1924), though not a religious Jew, was deeply marked by Prague's Jewish atmosphere — its bureaucratic claustrophobia, its marginality, and its uncanny doubling of identity — all central to his literary universe.

Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Cultural Life

Prague produced distinctive liturgical traditions and a rich printing culture. The Prague Hebrew press, established in the late fifteenth century, disseminated texts throughout Central Europe. The community maintained a tradition of scholarly disputation and halakhic creativity, reflected in responsa literature from figures like R. Yom Tov Lipmann Heller (author of Tosfot Yom Tov).

Decline & Transformation

Prague's Jewish community was decimated during the Holocaust. Of approximately 92,000 Jews in Bohemia and Moravia at the time of the Nazi occupation (1939), some 78,000 perished. The Terezín (Theresienstadt) concentration camp, just 60 kilometers from Prague, served as a ghetto and transit camp through which Prague Jews passed on their way to Auschwitz. Paradoxically, the Nazis preserved Prague's Jewish Quarter as a planned "museum of an extinct race," inadvertently protecting the architecture that now draws millions of visitors.

Legacy & Diaspora

Prague retains a small but active Jewish community of approximately 6,000 registered members. The Jewish Museum in Prague, comprising the historic synagogues and cemetery, is the most-visited museum in the Czech Republic. Annual ceremonies at the Pinkas Synagogue and international Holocaust remembrance events keep Prague at the center of European Jewish memory. The Maharal's grave in the Old Jewish Cemetery remains one of the most-visited Jewish pilgrimage sites in the world.

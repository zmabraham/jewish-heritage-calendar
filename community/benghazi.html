June 7, 2026 — Benghazi Jewish Heritage Day

Benghazi

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Libya
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Benghazi, Libya's second major city and the capital of the historic Cyrenaica region, was home to the second principal Jewish community in Libya. Ancient Cyrenaica (roughly eastern Libya) had one of the most significant Jewish populations of the ancient Hellenistic world — the community that launched the catastrophic revolt of 115–117 CE under Trajan — and Jewish settlement in the Benghazi region is among the oldest in North Africa. The modern community, smaller than that of Tripoli, shared its tragic fate in the 20th century, including a devastating pogrom in 1948.

Historical Origins

The Cyrenaica region of eastern Libya had an ancient Jewish presence dating to at least the 3rd century BCE, when Ptolemaic rulers encouraged Jewish settlement in their North African territories. The city of Cyrene (near modern Shahhat, inland from Benghazi) had a substantial Jewish population documented in classical sources. Jews from Cyrene appear in the New Testament (Simon of Cyrene), and Cyrene had its own synagogue in Jerusalem.

The Jewish revolt of 115–117 CE, known as the Kitos War, began in Cyrenaica under the leadership of Lukuas (or Andreas) and spread to Egypt and Cyprus. The revolt was suppressed with enormous violence by the Roman general Quintus Marcius Turbo; ancient sources (probably exaggerated) describe the near-extermination of the Cyrenaican Jewish population. Whatever the actual scale of destruction, the revolt fundamentally disrupted the ancient Cyrenaican Jewish community.

Under subsequent Roman and Byzantine rule, a Jewish community re-established itself in the Benghazi region. Medieval Arab geographers document Jewish presence. The Ottoman period saw the community reorganize as a recognized dhimmi minority in the commercial life of the port city.

Golden Age

Benghazi's Jewish quarter (hara) contained the community's synagogues, mikveh, and communal institutions. The Alliance Israélite Universelle established a school in Benghazi in the early 20th century, modernizing the community's educational profile in line with other North African communities.

The Benghazi pogrom of 1948 (coinciding with the pogrom in Tripoli during the first Arab-Israeli War) resulted in deaths, injuries, and destruction of property. Coming so soon after World War II and the Holocaust — with its devastating evidence of Jewish vulnerability — the 1948 pogrom was the decisive event that convinced most Benghazi Jews that they had no future in Libya.

Notable Figures

The Jewish community of Benghazi is less well documented in rabbinic literature than that of Tripoli, partly because its scholars published less and because the community was smaller. Local rabbis served the community without achieving the regional fame of figures like Shimon Labi of Tripoli.

In the 20th century, community leaders navigated the Italian Fascist period, the German occupation, and the catastrophic post-war pogroms with difficult decisions about resistance, accommodation, and flight.

Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Cultural Life

Benghazi's Jewish community shared the broader Libyan Jewish cultural tradition: Judeo-Libyan Arabic as the vernacular, the Tripolitanian Sephardic liturgical rite, distinctive Libyan Jewish food traditions (including specific preparations for Passover and Shabbat), and the particular saint-veneration practices of Libyan Jewry.

The ancient connection to Cyrenaica gave Benghazi Jews a sense of historical depth — awareness of belonging to one of the oldest Jewish communities in the diaspora — that shaped communal identity even in the modern period.

Decline & Transformation

Most Benghazi Jews emigrated to Israel following the 1948 pogrom, with the bulk going through the same Libyan Jewish emigration organizations that handled Tripolitan migration. The remaining small community left in 1967 and the years immediately following. As with Tripoli, the Gaddafi regime's confiscation of Jewish property and revocation of citizenship completed the destruction of any possibility of return.

Legacy & Diaspora

Benghazi Jews in Israel, particularly in cities like Netanya, Ashdod, and Rishon LeZion, maintained communal organizations and memory. The ancient connection to Cyrenaica is part of the broader consciousness of Libyan Jewish history. Archaeological work at Cyrene has documented ancient Jewish remains, including synagogue inscriptions, that connect the modern diaspora to its ancient roots.

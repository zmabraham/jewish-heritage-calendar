January 1, 2026 — Bordeaux Jewish Heritage Day

Bordeaux

Holy Land & Ancient Israel France
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Bordeaux holds a unique place in French Jewish history as home to one of the oldest Sephardic communities in Western Europe — a community built by Portuguese conversos who arrived in the sixteenth century and gradually reconstituted their Jewish identity over generations. By the eighteenth century, Bordeaux's Jewish community was the most prosperous and legally privileged in France, producing philosophers, merchants, and civic leaders who shaped the French Enlightenment. Their neighbors included Montesquieu, and their integration into French intellectual life was without parallel anywhere in pre-revolutionary Europe.

Historical Origins

The first Jews to settle in Bordeaux were Portuguese conversos — New Christians who had been forcibly baptized in Portugal in 1497 and who began arriving in the Gironde region from the early sixteenth century. Presenting themselves as "Portuguese merchants" or "New Christians," they avoided the formal exclusion of Jews from France while quietly maintaining Jewish practices in private.

The community grew steadily through the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, supported by the commercial value of their trade connections with Portugal, Brazil, and the Atlantic world. By the late seventeenth century, many had openly acknowledged their Jewish identity, and the community began to function more openly.

The key legal recognition came in 1550, when Henry II of France granted letters patent to "Portuguese merchants" allowing them to reside in France. Repeated confirmations and expansions of these rights through the seventeenth century gave Bordeaux's Jews a legal standing unlike that of Jews anywhere else in France.

Golden Age

The eighteenth century was the community's golden age. Bordeaux's Jews were formally recognized as Jews (rather than "New Christians") in royal edicts of 1723 and 1776, which granted them extensive rights: freedom of commerce, right to own property, freedom of worship, and exemption from the degrading special taxes that burdened Jews elsewhere in Europe. These privileges made Bordeaux's Sephardic community among the freest Jews in Europe before the Revolution.

Commerce was the community's lifeblood. Bordeaux's Sephardic merchants dominated the trade between France and its Atlantic colonies — particularly the sugar, tobacco, and coffee trades with Saint-Domingue, Martinique, and Guadeloupe. They also dominated trade with Portugal and Brazil, selling French wines and industrial goods in exchange for colonial produce. Jewish merchants owned ships, plantation interests, and trading companies; several were among the wealthiest men in Bordeaux.

The Grand Synagogue of Bordeaux, built in 1810 and expanded in the nineteenth century, is one of the finest neoclassical synagogue buildings in France, reflecting the community's confidence and prosperity. The community maintained a Portuguese-rite liturgy and customs that preserved the Sephardic heritage across generations.

The community's charitable institutions — welfare funds, schools, old-age homes — were models of Jewish communal organization. The Bordeaux community was highly organized and took pride in managing its own affairs with minimal recourse to external authority.

Notable Figures

Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592), the great essayist, had a Jewish grandmother — Antoinette Lopez, from a family of Portuguese conversos. Montaigne's tolerance, skepticism, and open curiosity about human diversity may owe something to his Sephardic background, though he was raised Catholic and never identified as Jewish.

Montesquieu (1689–1755), the philosopher of The Spirit of the Laws, lived near Bordeaux and had Jewish neighbors and acquaintances. His famous passage on Jews — "You complain that it would be inhuman to persecute those who are merely different from you in religion. But you yourselves persecute them..." — was directly inspired by his observation of the Bordeaux Sephardic community and their graceful integration into French civic life.

Gradis — the name of one of Bordeaux's leading Jewish merchant families — symbolizes the community's commercial achievement. Abraham Gradis (1699–1780) was among the most important merchants in all of France, with commercial networks spanning the Atlantic.

Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Cultural Life

Bordeaux's Jewish culture reflected its Sephardic and French character. The community maintained the Portuguese rite in its synagogue, used both Hebrew and Ladino (Judeo-Spanish) in devotional contexts, and French in daily life. By the eighteenth century, most community members were fully francophone, and French language and culture had penetrated deeply into Jewish domestic life.

The community produced philosophers, writers, and physicians who participated in the broader French Enlightenment. Several members of the community contributed to the Encyclopédie and engaged in Enlightenment debates. The intellectual integration of Bordeaux's Jews into French cultural life was a preview of what would happen nationally after the Revolution.

Decline & Transformation

The Revolution granted emancipation to Bordeaux's Sephardic Jews (September 1790) before the Ashkenazim received their rights in 1791 — a distinction that reflected the different legal status these communities had held under the ancien régime. The community continued to prosper through the nineteenth century, its commercial networks adapting to new economic conditions.

The Nazi occupation (1940–1944) brought persecution to Bordeaux's Jews. The Vichy government's antisemitic laws were applied here as elsewhere. Deportations to Auschwitz, though relatively smaller in absolute numbers than in Paris, devastated many families. The trial of Maurice Papon — wartime secretary-general of the Gironde prefecture — for his role in the deportation of 1,690 Bordeaux Jews (1997–1998) was one of the most significant French reckoning with wartime collaboration.

Legacy & Diaspora

Bordeaux today has a Jewish community of approximately 5,000–7,000, incorporating both the descendants of the historic Sephardic community and later Ashkenazic and North African arrivals. The Grand Synagogue remains active; the community maintains schools and cultural institutions. The converso heritage — the story of forced conversion, secret Judaism, and gradual reconversion — is commemorated as an extraordinary episode of Jewish resilience.

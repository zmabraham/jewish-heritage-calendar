Mallorca

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Spain
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Mallorca's Jewish history is one of the most dramatic and poignant in the Sephardic world. The island was home to a prosperous and cultured medieval Jewish community that was largely destroyed in a catastrophic pogrom in 1391, forcing most survivors to convert to Christianity. A smaller community persisted until 1435, when the last openly Jewish residents converted under duress. Yet remarkably, the descendants of these forced converts — known as Chuetas (from the Catalan xua, pig fat, a derogatory term) or Xuetes — maintained a separate social identity on the island for five centuries, recognized by their neighbors as descended from Jews even after generations of Catholic practice. Some Chueta families have returned to Judaism in recent decades, completing one of the most extraordinary cycles of crypto-Jewish survival in European history.

Historical Origins

Jews were present in Mallorca from Roman and early Byzantine times. Following the Christian reconquest of the island from the Almohads in 1229 by James I of Aragon, the Jewish community grew rapidly. James I actively encouraged Jewish settlement, granting the community legal protections and the right to a defined quarter — the Call — in Palma (then called Ciutat de Mallorca). The island's strategic position in the western Mediterranean made its Jewish merchants essential to the commercial networks connecting Catalonia, Genoa, North Africa, and the Levant.

The fourteenth century saw the Jewish community of Mallorca reach its peak, with a population estimated at 2,000–3,000 in a total island population of perhaps 70,000. The community produced scholars, physicians, cartographers, and merchants of international repute. The famous Majorcan School of Cartography — which produced the Catalan Atlas of 1375, one of the masterpieces of medieval cartography — was developed by the Cresques family, Jewish cartographers working in Palma.

Golden Age

The period from roughly 1250 to 1391 was the golden age of Mallorcan Jewry. The Jewish quarter in Palma was a dense urban neighborhood with multiple synagogues, a yeshiva, a mikveh, artisan workshops, and commercial establishments. Jewish merchants maintained trading relationships with correspondents in Barcelona, Genoa, Pisa, Venice, Alexandria, and Tunis.

The cartographic tradition was the community's most distinctive cultural contribution. Abraham Cresques (c. 1325–1387) and his son Jafuda Cresques produced maps of extraordinary accuracy and artistic refinement, incorporating the latest geographical knowledge from Islamic, Christian, and Jewish sources. The Catalan Atlas, commissioned for the French royal court, remains one of the finest medieval cartographic achievements. Jafuda Cresques later converted to Christianity after 1391 and, as Mestre Jacome, eventually brought his cartographic skills to Portugal, where he may have influenced the development of Portuguese exploration cartography.

The Call of Palma contained several synagogues, the most important being the Great Synagogue. After 1391, the synagogues were converted to churches; the church of Sant Miquel in Palma occupies the site of the former Great Synagogue. Archaeological investigations in the Call area have recovered material remains of the medieval Jewish quarter.

The Chueta community of Mallorca is one of the most studied cases of crypto-Jewish survival in Europe. Fifteen family names were identified as Chueta in popular tradition: among them Aguiló, Bonnín, Cortès, Fortesa, Fuster, Martí, Miró, Pinya, Pomar, Segura, Valentí, Valleriola, and Valls. These families were socially ostracized in Mallorcan society for centuries — barred from intermarrying with "old Christian" families, excluded from guilds and public offices — long after any verifiable connection to Jewish practice had been severed.

The social stigma persisted into the twentieth century. It was only after the Second Vatican Council and in the post-Franco period that Chueta families began to integrate fully into Mallorcan society. Since the 1980s, a small but significant number of Chueta-descended individuals have formally returned to Judaism, some undergoing formal conversion and others, where matrilineal descent was clear, being accepted as Jews without conversion. Several have made aliyah to Israel. This return to Judaism, after five and a half centuries, represents a unique moment in the history of Jewish peoplehood.

Notable Figures

Abraham Cresques (c. 1325–1387) — Court cartographer to Peter IV of Aragon, designated mestre de mappamondes e de brúxoles (master of world maps and compasses). His Catalan Atlas of 1375 synthesized Mediterranean, Asian, and sub-Saharan geographical knowledge into a masterwork.

Jafuda Cresques (c. 1360–after 1410) — Son of Abraham, continued his father's cartographic work until 1391, converted under duress and continued under the name Mestre Jacome in Portugal.

Rabbi Simeon ben Tsemah Duran (Rashbatz, 1361–1444) — Though he fled Mallorca after 1391 and settled in Algiers, where he became a leading figure of North African Jewry, he was born and educated in Mallorca and his early formation was shaped by the island's Jewish community.

Cultural Life

Decline & Transformation

The pogrom of August 1391 was catastrophic. Inspired by the wave of anti-Jewish violence spreading through Castile and Aragon, a mob attacked the Call of Palma, killing approximately 300 Jews and forcing the mass conversion of most of the remainder. Some Jews fled to North Africa; the majority converted. A small community of practicing Jews reconstituted itself in the following years, but faced mounting pressure. In 1435, following accusations of sorcery and host desecration, the remaining openly Jewish population of Mallorca was forced to convert en masse. Mallorca thus became — officially — an entirely Christian island.

However, the converted Jewish families formed a distinct social group, intermarrying among themselves and occupying specific streets in Palma. The Inquisition prosecuted many of them for crypto-Jewish practices in the seventeenth century, particularly in the great trials of 1678–1691, which resulted in dozens of burnings at the stake (autos-da-fé). Despite this, Chueta identity persisted.

Legacy & Diaspora

The Call of Palma has been partially restored as a heritage site, with interpretive signage and a small museum. The Can Aguiló building in the former Jewish quarter preserves architectural elements from the medieval period. The Mallorcan Jewish Heritage Foundation works to document and preserve the history of both the medieval community and the Chueta experience.

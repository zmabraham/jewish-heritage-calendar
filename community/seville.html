January 1, 2026 — Seville Jewish Heritage Day

Seville

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Spain
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Seville holds a tragic distinction in Sephardic history: it was here, in June 1391, that the wave of anti-Jewish violence that would devastate the entire Iberian Peninsula first erupted. The pogrom that began in Seville's Jewish quarter spread with terrifying speed across Castile and Aragon, killing thousands and forcing hundreds of thousands of Jews into conversion, irrevocably altering the social and religious landscape of medieval Spain. Yet before that catastrophe, Seville had been home to one of the largest and wealthiest Jewish communities in Iberia, a city whose Jewish merchants, physicians, and financiers were integral to the kingdom of Castile's economic life.

Historical Origins

Jewish settlement in Seville (ancient Hispalis) dates to Roman times, with documentary evidence strengthening through the Visigothic period. The Arab conquest of 712 brought Jews into the city's economic and administrative life. Under the Abbadid taifa dynasty of the eleventh century and subsequently under Almoravid and then Almohad rule, Seville's Jewish history was turbulent — the Almohads, as elsewhere, drove much of the Jewish population northward, creating a hiatus in the community's continuity.

Christian reconquest in 1248 under Fernando III brought the Jews back as valued partners of the new Castilian administration. Fernando allocated to the Jewish community several former mosques as synagogues, gave them a substantial quarter near the royal alcázar, and relied heavily on Jewish tax-farmers and administrators. This privileged relationship continued under Alfonso X, who employed Jewish scholars extensively in his translation projects and cultural initiatives.

Golden Age

Seville's Jewish community reached its height in the fourteenth century, when the city was the commercial capital of Castile and a major port linking the peninsula to the Atlantic trade. Jewish merchants were prominent in the wool and cloth trades, in finance, and in the collection of royal revenues. Jewish physicians served the Castilian court, and several families accumulated extraordinary wealth through tax-farming — the collection of royal and ecclesiastical revenues, a system that made Jewish collectors visible targets for popular resentment.

The community at its height probably numbered between twenty and thirty thousand, making it one of the largest in Spain. The Judería, centered in the neighborhood now known as Santa Cruz, extended over a substantial portion of the old city. Multiple synagogues served the community, and Seville's proximity to the Castilian court gave its leading families direct access to royal patronage and protection.

Seville's major synagogue, known as the Great Synagogue, was one of the largest in the peninsula. Three former synagogues were converted into churches after 1391, and their locations can still be traced in the street patterns of the Santa Cruz neighborhood. The church of Santa María la Blanca, built on the site of a synagogue, preserves the name of the original structure and some architectural elements of its predecessor.

The Judería itself — today the Barrio de Santa Cruz, one of Seville's most picturesque and touristed neighborhoods — retains its medieval street pattern, narrow alleyways, and walled courtyard houses, though every building has been transformed beyond recognition.

Notable Figures

Seville's most consequential figure in the years leading up to the catastrophe was Fernán Martínez (also known as Ferrand Martínez), the Archdeacon of Écija — not a Jew, but the man whose inflammatory preaching directly ignited the 1391 violence. His decades-long campaign against the Jewish community, calling for the destruction of synagogues and the forced conversion of Jews, created the atmosphere that made mass violence possible.

Among Jewish figures, Samuel Abravanel, patriarch of the great Abravanel family, served as treasurer to the king of Castile from Seville. Though his family would later flee to Portugal and then to Naples, their origins in Seville connect the city to one of the most distinguished dynasties of Sephardic scholarship and statecraft.

Abraham ibn Zacut, the astronomer whose astronomical tables were used by Columbus, had connections to the Castilian Jewish intellectual networks centered partly in Seville.

Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Cultural Life

Seville's Jewish community was notable for its commercial sophistication and courtly connections rather than as a center of Talmudic scholarship or philosophy in the manner of Toledo or Lucena. The city's Jewish elite were courtiers, merchants, and financiers who moved comfortably between Jewish communal life and Castilian court society. Hebrew letters were practiced, but Seville's cultural contribution was primarily economic and administrative.

The community maintained schools, bathhouses, and charitable institutions typical of a major Sephardic community. Liturgical life followed the Castilian rite, and the synagogues were well-endowed by wealthy patrons.

Decline & Transformation

The events of June 4, 1391, changed everything. Archdeacon Fernán Martínez, long restrained by higher ecclesiastical and royal authorities, found his moment in the political instability following the death of King Juan I. On that day, mobs stormed the Jewish quarter of Seville. The violence was catastrophic: hundreds were killed immediately, and faced with the choice between death and baptism, an estimated four thousand Sevillian Jews accepted conversion. The remainder fled or were killed.

The 1391 pogrom was not the end of Jewish history in Seville — a small community reconstituted itself — but it produced a new social reality: a large and growing population of conversos (New Christians) who maintained, to varying degrees, elements of Jewish practice and identity while nominally Christian. This population became the primary target of the Spanish Inquisition, which was established in Seville in 1480 — the first Inquisition tribunal in Spain, operating with particular ferocity in the city where the converso problem had its roots.

The Expulsion of 1492 completed the destruction of the remaining Jewish community.

Legacy & Diaspora

The events of 1391 in Seville had reverberations across the entire Jewish world. The mass conversions produced the Anusim (forced converts) whose descendants populated converso and later Marrano communities across Spain, Portugal, and the Americas. The Inquisition's pursuit of crypto-Jews from Seville's converso community extended across continents for centuries.

Seville's Jewish heritage is memorialized in the Barrio de Santa Cruz, where interpretive plaques mark sites of Jewish significance. The city has made efforts in recent decades to acknowledge and commemorate its complex Jewish past. Some descendants of Sevillian Jewish families, scattered across the Sephardic diaspora from Salonika to Amsterdam to Istanbul, maintained memories of their Sevillian origins in family names and traditions.

San Nicandro

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Italy
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

San Nicandro Garganico, a remote hilltop village in the Gargano promontory of Puglia (Apulia) in southern Italy, is the site of one of the most extraordinary episodes in modern Jewish history: the spontaneous mass conversion to Judaism of an entire community of illiterate Italian peasants, led by a visionary man named Donato Manduzio, in the 1930s and 1940s — without any contact with or guidance from organized Jewish community, and culminating in the community's mass emigration to the newly founded State of Israel in 1948–1949.

Historical Origins

San Nicandro is a deeply traditional, Roman Catholic agricultural village with no prior Jewish connection or history. Its residents in the early twentieth century were poor, largely illiterate sharecroppers and shepherds. The story begins with Donato Manduzio (1885–1948), a former soldier and laborer who was bedridden with a leg injury for years. During his long convalescence, with little else to do, Manduzio read the Bible — the Hebrew Bible in an Italian translation — obsessively.

What he read was not the New Testament narrative of Jesus but the raw text of the Torah and the Prophets. Manduzio experienced a series of visions and revelations in which he became convinced that the God of Israel was the true God, that the commandments of the Torah were binding, and that he and his community must follow them. Without knowledge of Judaism or contact with any Jewish person, Manduzio began practicing what he understood from the Bible: observing the Sabbath on Saturday, avoiding pork, circumcising male followers, and following the dietary and purity laws as best he could reconstruct them from the text.

Golden Age

By the late 1930s, Manduzio had gathered a community of several dozen followers — a remarkable achievement given that they were practicing a religion none of them had grown up in, in a culture saturated with Catholicism. The Italian Fascist authorities regarded the group with suspicion, and the community's efforts to establish contact with actual Jewish authorities were complicated by Mussolini's racial laws of 1938, which classified Jews as a racial category and made contact with them potentially dangerous.

Manduzio wrote a letter to the Chief Rabbi of Rome, introducing his community and asking for guidance in proper Jewish practice. The letter, written in the honest and biblical language of a self-taught peasant mystic, eventually reached Rabbi Elio Toaff, who visited the community in 1946 and was deeply moved. A formal conversion process was organized.

After World War II, with Italy liberated and Israel's independence approaching, the community — by now several hundred members — went through formal giyur (conversion) under proper rabbinical supervision. Their commitment was genuine and their Jewish practice, though unorthodox in some respects from years of self-teaching, was sincere and devout.

In 1948–1949, the San Nicandro community emigrated almost entirely to the new State of Israel, settling in villages in the Galilee and elsewhere. Donato Manduzio himself died in 1948, just before the establishment of the state he had dreamed of — he was buried in a small Jewish ceremony in San Nicandro. His community carried on, integrating into Israeli society as among its most unusual new citizens: southern Italian peasants who had chosen Judaism freely, without historical connection to the Jewish people, through pure religious conviction.

The San Nicandro community maintained a simple meeting place that served as a synagogue during the years of their Italian practice. After emigration, the physical traces of the community in the village faded. A small plaque marks the site of their activities.

Notable Figures

Donato Manduzio (1885–1948) is the central figure — peasant visionary, autodidact biblical scholar, and founder of the community. His spiritual diary and correspondence, preserved in archives, reveal a man of genuine religious depth and remarkable independence of mind.

Cultural Life

The San Nicandro story is a singular case in modern Jewish history of voluntary, unsolicited mass conversion. It raises profound questions about Jewish identity, the nature of religious conviction, and the relationship between ethnicity and faith. It also demonstrates the power of the Hebrew Bible — read without rabbinic mediation — to inspire religious transformation in an entirely unexpected cultural context.

The story has been interpreted by Jewish thinkers as a validation of the universal spiritual power of the Torah, and by anthropologists and religious historians as a fascinating case study in new religious movements.

Legacy & Diaspora

The community's descendants in Israel are a small but distinct group, integrated into Israeli society but aware of their unusual origin. Some have maintained contact with San Nicandro and periodically visit the village. The story has been told in several books and documentaries, most notably Elena Cassin's San Nicandro: The Story of a Religious Phenomenon and John Davis's historical account.

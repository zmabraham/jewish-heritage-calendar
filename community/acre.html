Acre (Akko)

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Israel
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Acre (Hebrew: Akko; Arabic: Akka) sits on the northern curve of Haifa Bay, its harbor protected by a promontory that has made it one of the most strategic ports in the eastern Mediterranean for three thousand years. For Jews, Acre occupies a strange and storied position: a city that held captive one of the greatest medieval rabbis, that hosted an important community during the Crusader period, and that served as the administrative and logistical center of Palestine under the Ottoman Turks — a Jewish minority surviving within the walls of a fortress city that had seen every empire from the Ptolemies to the British.

There is a melancholy to Acre's Jewish history, but also a tenacity. Within the forbidding stone walls of the Crusader city — discovered still standing beneath the Ottoman town only in the mid-twentieth century — there existed a Jewish community through centuries of turmoil. The memory of Rabbi Meir of Rothenburg, imprisoned here for years by the Holy Roman Emperor, haunts the city's stones like a ghostly presence.

Historical Origins

Acre appears in ancient Egyptian execration texts and in the tribute lists of Thutmose III. It was an important Phoenician city and was later taken by Alexander the Great. During the Hellenistic period, Acre was renamed Ptolemais after the Ptolemaic dynasty, and it became a major port of Greek culture and trade. Jewish presence in Hellenistic Acre is documented by Josephus and by archaeological finds; the city is mentioned in 1 Maccabees and was contested during the Maccabean Revolt.

Under Roman and Byzantine rule, Acre remained an important port and fortress. The New Testament mentions that Paul spent a day with believers at Ptolemais (Acts 21:7). The early Jewish community here, like those of other coastal cities, participated in the broader Mediterranean trade network.

Golden Age

Acre's Jewish golden age occurred under the Crusaders, despite the apparent paradox. The Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem (1099–1291) maintained a relatively tolerant policy toward Jews in Acre, recognizing the economic benefits of their presence. Acre became the capital of the Crusader Kingdom after Jerusalem's fall to Saladin in 1187, and its Jewish community swelled as Jews arrived from elsewhere in the Holy Land.

In the thirteenth century, Acre was the most important Jewish community in the Land of Israel after Jerusalem. It housed scholars, merchants, and a significant Karaite community. Letters from the Cairo Geniza describe a vibrant Jewish quarter, active synagogue life, and trade connections reaching as far as India and China. Acre was a terminus for Jewish pilgrims arriving by sea and a point of departure for those traveling overland to Jerusalem or Safed.

The Jewish quarter of Crusader Acre was located within the fortified city. While the precise locations of medieval synagogues have not been definitively identified, the Geniza letters and contemporary accounts describe active communal institutions: synagogues, a mikveh (ritual bath), study houses, and charitable associations.

The Ramchal Synagogue is named for Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto (the Ramchal, 1707–1746), the Italian kabbalist and ethicist who spent his final years in Acre. He died there in 1746 and is buried in the Jewish cemetery. The Jewish cemetery of Acre, on the slope outside the old city, contains graves from the Ottoman period and earlier, though many ancient graves are unmarked.

Notable Figures

Rabbi Meir of Rothenburg (c. 1215–1293), known as the Maharam, was the greatest Tosafist and halakhic authority of his generation. While traveling to the Holy Land, he was arrested in Lombardy by agents of the Holy Roman Emperor Rudolf I, who wanted to prevent the migration of Jewish talent from Germany. The Maharam was imprisoned in Acre fortress for seven years (c. 1286–1293), refusing to permit the Jewish community to pay his ransom lest it encourage such arrests. He died in prison. His student, Rabbi Asher ben Yehiel (the Rosh), after being ransomed, preserved the Maharam's teachings, which remain central to Ashkenazic halacha.

Nahmanides (Rabbi Moses ben Nachman, 1194–1270), the great Catalan scholar and Bible commentator, arrived in Acre in 1267 after being forced to debate Christian theologians in Barcelona and then compelled to leave Spain. He settled first in Jerusalem and later in Acre, where he reinvigorated the flagging Jewish community and established a synagogue. His letters from the Holy Land, describing its devastated condition, are among the most poignant documents of medieval Jewish literature.

Rabbi Yehuda Alharizi (c. 1165–1225), the great Spanish-Hebrew poet and translator, spent time in Acre, and his writings provide one of the most vivid literary accounts of Jewish life in the eastern Mediterranean during the Crusader period.

Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Cultural Life

Jewish Acre under the Crusaders was multilingual: Hebrew and Aramaic for religious life, Arabic for daily communication with the Muslim majority, and likely Provençal or Italian for trade with Christian merchants. The community was both Ashkenazic and Sephardic, with rabbis arriving from the Rhineland, France, Spain, and North Africa. Its commercial orientation gave it a cosmopolitan character.

The Geniza letters reveal a community actively engaged in long-distance trade: textiles, spices, and especially the India trade that passed through the Red Sea and across to the Mediterranean. Jewish merchants of Acre corresponded with colleagues in Egypt, Tunisia, Sicily, and beyond, forming a network that was both commercial and intellectual.

Decline & Transformation

Acre's golden age ended with the Mamluk conquest of 1291. The Mamluks, deeply hostile to the Crusader presence, destroyed Acre's fortifications and harbor, ending its strategic importance. The Jewish community, though diminished, survived. Under the Ottomans (1517–1917), Acre was rebuilt but never regained its pre-1291 importance as a Jewish center. The community remained small throughout the Ottoman period.

The modern period saw some revival. Napoleon's siege of 1799 and his proclamation promising to restore Palestine to the Jews briefly raised hopes; the subsequent withdrawal dashed them. In the late Ottoman period, some Jewish families settled in Acre. During the British Mandate, the fortress of Acre (now a prison) held Jewish underground fighters. The 1948 Arab-Israeli war left Acre under Israeli control, and its ancient but diminished Jewish community was supplemented by new immigrants. Today Acre is a mixed Jewish-Arab city with a population of roughly 50,000.

Legacy & Diaspora

Acre's legacy is preserved in the memory of Rabbi Meir of Rothenburg, whose imprisonment became emblematic of medieval Jewish vulnerability and moral fortitude, and in the Geniza letters that bring to life a surprisingly cosmopolitan medieval Jewish port. The Crusader city's spectacular underground ruins — the Knights Halls, the secret tunnel, the fortifications — are one of Israel's most impressive archaeological sites, but the Jewish history of those halls, streets, and houses is less often remembered. The Jewish community of Acre continues today, a small presence within the ancient walls that held the Maharam captive.

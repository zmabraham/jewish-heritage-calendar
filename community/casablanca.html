Casablanca

Holy Land & Ancient Israel Morocco
Founded: Ancient Peak Population: Unknown Historical

Overview

Casablanca was, by the mid-20th century, home to the largest urban Jewish community in North Africa and possibly in any Arab country. At its peak in the early 1950s, approximately 80,000 Jews lived in Casablanca — a figure representing the culmination of decades of internal migration from Morocco's smaller cities and towns. As the country's commercial capital and principal Atlantic port, Casablanca drew Jewish migrants seeking economic opportunity, and its Jewish community developed into one of the most diverse, modern, and institutionally sophisticated in the Arab world.

Historical Origins

Casablanca (Dar al-Baida, "White House") was a small coastal settlement until the French Protectorate transformed it into Morocco's commercial metropolis after 1912. Jews began arriving in significant numbers from the 1910s onward, primarily from Fez, Meknès, Marrakech, and smaller towns. They came seeking the economic opportunities created by French colonial development: import-export trade, manufacturing, the professions, and the expanding service sector.

The Jewish population grew rapidly: from perhaps a few thousand in 1912 to 30,000 in 1935, 60,000 in 1945, and nearly 80,000 by 1952. This rapid growth produced a community of extraordinary diversity — old Moroccan families with roots in medieval Spain, Berber-speaking Jews from the Atlas Mountains, European-educated professionals, and recent internal migrants all coexisted in a dynamic, occasionally fractious urban environment.

Golden Age

The 1930s through 1950s represent Casablanca Jewry's apex. Jewish merchants and entrepreneurs played central roles in the city's booming economy. Jewish professionals — doctors, lawyers, engineers, architects — served both Jewish and non-Jewish clients and achieved integration into the colonial educated elite unusual for Morocco's interior communities.

The Alliance Israélite Universelle had established its Casablanca schools in the early 20th century, and by the 1940s a generation of French-educated Moroccan Jews had emerged who were as fluent in the idioms of French culture as in traditional Jewish practice. This produced a complex, sometimes tense identity — simultaneously Moroccan, Jewish, and French — that would shape the community's eventual dispersal and its creative contributions in France and Israel.

At its height, Casablanca supported dozens of synagogues, multiple yeshivot, branches of virtually every Jewish charitable and Zionist organization, Hebrew-language schools, and a vibrant Jewish press. The Beth-El Synagogue and the Grand Synagogue of Casablanca were among the largest and most architecturally significant.

The Jewish community maintained the Casablanca Jewish Community Council (Consistoire Israélite de Casablanca), which managed communal affairs in coordination with the French and later Moroccan authorities. Social welfare organizations, mutual aid societies, and Zionist youth movements (Hashomer Hatzair, Betar, and others) competed for the community's loyalty.

Notable Figures

The Ha-Cohen family included prominent rabbis and communal leaders who helped manage the rapid transformation of Casablanca Jewry from a traditional to a modern community.

Rabbi Shaul Ibn Dannan (late 19th–early 20th century) was among the senior halakhic authorities who oversaw the community's religious life during the early period of growth.

Dr. León Benzaquen served as a minister in the Moroccan government after independence — one of the first Jews to hold ministerial rank in an Arab country — exemplifying the brief moment of optimism about Jewish participation in post-colonial Morocco.

André Azoulay, born in Essaouira but representing the broader Casablanca Jewish professional class, served as a senior advisor to Kings Hassan II and Mohammed VI — a remarkable example of sustained Jewish involvement in Moroccan public life.

Institutions & Sacred Spaces

Cultural Life

Casablanca's size and diversity generated cultural production of unusual richness. Hebrew and Judeo-Arabic newspapers flourished; Jewish authors wrote in French, Arabic, and Hebrew; musicians blended traditional Moroccan malhun with modern French song. The Zionist youth movements produced a generation that would become leaders of Israeli society — including military officers, academics, and political figures.

The Mimouna celebration reached its grandest expression in Casablanca, with elaborately decorated tables, musical gatherings, and Muslim-Jewish hospitality exchanges that expressed the best of Moroccan intercommunal relations.

Decline & Transformation

The 1948 Arab-Israeli War prompted the first major exodus wave, as anti-Jewish violence in several Moroccan cities (including Oujda and Jerada) and the general atmosphere of hostility led tens of thousands to emigrate to Israel. This continued through the 1950s. Moroccan independence in 1956, the 1961 closing of official emigration channels (briefly), the 1967 Six-Day War, and the 1973 war each prompted further emigration waves.

By 1970, the community had shrunk to perhaps 25,000; by 1980, under 15,000; today approximately 2,500 Jews remain in Casablanca — the largest Jewish community in any Arab country, but a shadow of its mid-century peak. Most emigrants went to France (particularly Paris and the south), Canada (Montreal), Israel, and to a lesser extent the United States.

Legacy & Diaspora

Casablanca maintains a living Jewish community with active synagogues, a Jewish day school, and communal institutions. The Beth-El Synagogue continues to hold services. The Museum of Moroccan Judaism, located in Casablanca, is the only Jewish museum in a Muslim country and houses extensive collections of religious objects, documents, and photographs. The Casablanca Jewish community, though small, benefits from King Mohammed VI's explicit commitment to protecting Morocco's Jewish heritage.
